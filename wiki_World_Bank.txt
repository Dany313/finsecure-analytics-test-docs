Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank\n==================================================\n\nGroup making loans to developing countries\nThe\nWorld Bank Group\n(\nWBG\n) is a family of five international organizations that make\nleveraged loans\nto\ndeveloping countries\n. The group is the largest development bank in the world.\nIt is headquartered in\nWashington, D.C.\n, United States. The bank's stated mission is to achieve the twin goals of ending extreme poverty and building shared prosperity.\nHistorically, within the group, the IBRD and IDA were collectively known as the\nWorld Bank\ndue to their emphasis on global lending.\nThe World Bank was established along with the\nInternational Monetary Fund\nat the 1944\nBretton Woods Conference\n. Initially, its loans helped rebuild countries devastated by\nWorld War II\n. Over time, it has shifted its focus to development, with a stated mission of eradicating extreme poverty and boosting shared prosperity.\nIt has been criticized for its\nstructural adjustment\nprograms, governance, environmental record, and policies detrimental to the social welfare.\nThe group comprises five organizations, as established over time:\nInternational Bank for Reconstruction and Development\n(IBRD), 1944\nInternational Development Association\n(IDA), 1960\nInternational Finance Corporation\n(IFC), 1956\nInternational Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes\n(ICSID), 1965\nMultilateral Investment Guarantee Agency\n(MIGA), 1988\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nHarry Dexter White\n(left) and\nJohn Maynard Keynes\n, the "founding fathers" of both the World Bank and the\nInternational Monetary Fund\n(IMF)\nThe World Bank was created at the 1944\nBretton Woods Conference\n(1–22 July 1944), along with the\nInternational Monetary Fund\n(IMF). The president of the World Bank is traditionally an\nAmerican\n.\nThe World Bank and the IMF are both based in Washington, D.C., and work closely with each other.\nThe Gold Room at the\nMount Washington Hotel\nwhere the International Monetary Fund and World Bank were established\nAlthough many countries were represented at the Bretton Woods Conference, the United States and United Kingdom were the most powerful in attendance and dominated the negotiations.\nThe intention behind the founding of the World Bank was to provide temporary loans to low-income countries that could not obtain loans commercially.\nThe bank may also make loans and demand policy reforms from recipients.\nThe agreement at Bretton Woods implied that both the World Bank and IMF would be headquartered in the United States. US Treasury Secretary\nHenry Morgenthau Jr.\nintended them to be located in\nNew York\n, but his successor\nFred M. Vinson\nunilaterally decided that they would be in\nWashington, D.C.\ninstead, noting that "the institutions would be fatally prejudiced in American opinion if they were placed in New York, since they would then come under the taint of 'international finance'".\nThe World Bank Group came into formal existence on 27 December 1946, following international ratification of the Bretton Woods agreements. The Conference also provided the foundation of the Osiander Committee in 1951, responsible for the preparation and evaluation of the\nWorld Development Report\n. Commencing operations on 25 June 1946, the bank approved its first loan on 9 May 1947 (US$250 million to France for postwar reconstruction - in real terms, the largest loan the bank has issued to date).\nIn its early years, the bank made a slow start for two reasons: it was underfunded, and there were leadership struggles between the US executive director and the president of the organization. When the\nMarshall Plan\nwent into effect in 1947, many European countries began receiving aid from other sources. Faced with this competition, the World Bank shifted its focus to non-European allies. Until 1968, its loans were earmarked for the construction of infrastructure works, such as seaports, highway systems, and power plants, that would generate enough income to enable a borrower country to repay the loan. In 1960, the\nInternational Development Association\nwas formed (as opposed to a UN fund named SUNFED), providing soft loans to developing countries.\nBefore 1974, the reconstruction and development loans the World Bank made were relatively small. Its staff was aware of the need to instill confidence in the bank.\nFiscal conservatism\nruled, and loan applications had to meet strict criteria.\nThe first country to receive a World Bank loan was France in 1947. The bank's president at the time,\nJohn McCloy\n, chose France over two other applicants, Poland and Chile. The loan was for US$250\nmillion, half the amount requested, and came with strict conditions. France had to agree to produce a balanced budget and give priority of debt repayment to the World Bank over other governments. World Bank staff closely monitored the use of the funds to ensure that the French government met the conditions. In addition, before the loan was approved, the\nUnited States Department of State\ntold the French government that its members associated with the Communist Party would first have to be removed. The French government complied and removed the\nCommunist\ncoalition government—the so-called\ntripartite\n. Within hours, the loan to France was approved.\nFrom 1974 to 1980, the bank concentrated on meeting the basic needs of people in the developing world. The size and number of loans to borrowers greatly increased, as loan targets expanded from infrastructure into social services and other sectors.\nThese changes can be attributed to\nRobert McNamara\n, who was appointed to the presidency in 1968 by\nLyndon B. Johnson\n.\nMcNamara implored bank treasurer\nEugene Rotberg\nto seek out new sources of capital outside of the northern banks that had been the primary sources of funding. Rotberg used the global bond market to increase the capital available to the bank.\nOne consequence of the period of poverty alleviation lending was the rapid rise of\ndebt of developing countries\n. From 1976 to 1980, developing world debt rose at an average annual rate of 20%.\nThe World Bank Administrative Tribunal was established in 1980, to decide on disputes between the World Bank Group and its staff where allegation of non-observance of contracts of employment or terms of appointment had not been honored.\nMcNamara was succeeded by U.S.\nPresident\nJimmy Carter\n's nominee,\nAlden W. Clausen\n, in 1980.\nClausen replaced many members of McNamara's staff and crafted a different mission emphasis. His 1982 decision to replace the bank's Chief Economist,\nHollis B. Chenery\n, with\nAnne Krueger\nwas an example of this new focus. Krueger was known for her criticism of development funding and for describing developing countries' governments as "\nrent-seeking\nstates".\nDuring the 1980s, the bank emphasized lending to service debt of developing countries, and\nstructural adjustment\npolicies designed to streamline the economies of developing nations.\nUNICEF\nreported in the late 1980s that the structural adjustment programs of the World Bank had been responsible for "reduced health, nutritional and educational levels for tens of millions of children in\nAsia\n,\nLatin America\n, and\nAfrica\n".\nMembership\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nList of World Bank members\nWorld Bank Group:\nMember states of all five WBG organizations\nMember states of four WBG organizations\nMember states of three WBG organizations\nMember states of two WBG organizations\nMember states only of the IBRD\nThe\nInternational Bank for Reconstruction and Development\n(IBRD) has 189 member countries, while the\nInternational Development Association\n(IDA) has 175. Member countries are allocated votes at the time of membership and subsequently for additional subscriptions to capital (one vote for each share of capital stock held by the member).\nOrganizational structure\n[\nedit\n]\nThe World Bank Group Building in\nWashington, D.C.\nThe World Bank Sign on the building\nTogether with four affiliated agencies created between 1957 and 1988, the IBRD is part of the World Bank Group. The group's headquarters are in\nWashington, D.C.\nIt is an international organization owned by member governments; although it makes profits, they are used to support continued efforts in\npoverty reduction\n.\nTechnically the World Bank is part of the United Nations system,\nbut its governance structure is different: each institution in the World Bank Group is owned by its member governments, which subscribe to its basic\nshare capital\n, with votes proportional to shareholding. Membership gives certain voting rights that are the same for all countries but there are also additional votes that depend on financial contributions to the organization. The president of the World Bank is nominated by the president of the United States and elected by the bank's Board of Governors.\nAs of 15 November 2009, the United States held 16.4% of total votes, Japan 7.9%, Germany 4.5%, the United Kingdom 4.3%, and France 4.3%. As changes to the bank's Charter require an 85% supermajority, the U.S. can block any major change in the bank's governing structure.\nBecause the U.S. exerts formal and informal influence over the bank as a result of its vote share, control over the presidency, and the bank's headquarters location in Washington, D.C., friends and allies of the U.S. receive more projects with more lenient terms.\nWorld Bank Group agencies\n[\nedit\n]\nThe World Bank Group consists of\nthe\nInternational Bank for Reconstruction and Development\n(IBRD), established in 1944, which provides debt financing based on sovereign guarantees;\nthe\nInternational Finance Corporation\n(IFC), established in 1956, which provides various forms of financing without sovereign guarantees, primarily to the private sector;\nthe\nInternational Development Association\n(IDA), established in 1960, which provides concessional financing (interest-free loans or grants), usually with sovereign guarantees;\nthe\nInternational Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes\n(ICSID), established in 1965, which works with governments to reduce investment risk;\nthe\nMultilateral Investment Guarantee Agency\n(MIGA), established in 1988, which provides insurance against certain types of risk, including\npolitical risk\n, primarily to the private sector.\nThe term "World Bank" generally refers to just the IBRD and IDA, whereas the term "World Bank Group" or "WBG" is used to refer to all five institutions collectively.\nThe\nWorld Bank Institute\nis the capacity development branch of the World Bank, providing learning and other capacity-building programs to member countries.\nThe IBRD has 189 member governments, and the other institutions have between 153 and 184.\nThe institutions of the World Bank Group are all run by a board of governors meeting once a year.\nEach member country appoints a governor, generally its minister of finance. Daily, the World Bank Group is run by a board of 25 executive directors to whom the governors have delegated certain powers. Each director represents either one country (for the largest countries), or a group of countries. Executive directors are appointed by their respective governments or the constituencies.\nThe agencies of the World Bank are each governed by their Articles of Agreement that serves as the legal and institutional foundation for all their work.\nThe activities of the\nIFC\nand\nMIGA\ninclude investment in the private sector and providing insurance, respectively.\nPresidency\n[\nedit\n]\nTraditionally, the bank president has been a U.S. citizen nominated by the president of the United States, the bank's largest shareholder. The nominee is subject to confirmation by the executive directors, to serve a five-year, renewable term.\nCurrent president\n[\nedit\n]\nAjay Banga\nis the current and 14th\npresident of the World Bank Group\n.\nManaging director\n[\nedit\n]\nThe World Bank Group has several Managing Directors, each with different areas of responsibility. These responsibilities include organizational strategy; budget and strategic planning; information technology; shared services; Corporate Procurement; General Services and Corporate Security; the Sanctions System; and the Conflict Resolution and Internal Justice System.\nExtractive Industries Review\n[\nedit\n]\nAfter longstanding criticisms from civil society of the bank's involvement in the oil, gas, and mining sectors, the World Bank in July 2001 launched an independent review called the\nExtractive Industries Review\n(EIR—not to be confused with\nEnvironmental Impact Report\n). The review was headed by an "Eminent Person",\nEmil Salim\n(former Environment Minister of Indonesia). Salim held consultations with a wide range of stakeholders in 2002 and 2003. The EIR recommendations were published in January 2004 in a final report, "Striking a Better Balance".\nThe report concluded that fossil fuel and mining projects do not alleviate poverty, and recommended that World Bank involvement with these sectors be phased out by 2008 to be replaced by investment in\nrenewable energy\nand\nclean energy\n. The World Bank published its Management Response to the EIR in September 2004\nafter extensive discussions with the board of directors. The Management Response did not accept many of the EIR report's conclusions, but the EIR served to alter the World Bank's policies on oil, gas, and mining in important ways, as the World Bank documented in a recent follow-up report.\nOne area of particular controversy concerned the rights of indigenous peoples. Critics point out that the Management Response weakened a key recommendation that indigenous peoples and affected communities should have to provide 'consent for projects to proceed; instead, there would be 'consultation'.\nFollowing the EIR process, the World Bank issued a revised Policy on Indigenous Peoples.\nMethods\n[\nedit\n]\nThe World Bank plays a significant role in global economic governance due to its broad mandate, its vast resource base, its frequent and regular interactions with governments as clients, and its many publications and databases.\nIn 2020, the World Bank's For example, in 1991, the bank announced that to protect against\ndeforestation\n, especially\nin the Amazon\n, it would not finance any commercial logging or infrastructure projects that harm the environment.\nPoverty reduction strategies\n[\nedit\n]\nFor the poorest developing countries in the world, the bank's assistance plans are based on\npoverty reduction strategies\n; by combining an analysis of local groups with an analysis of the country's financial and economic situation the World Bank develops a plan pertaining to the country in question. The government then identifies the country's priorities and targets for the reduction of poverty, and the World Bank instigates its aid efforts correspondingly.\nForty-five countries pledged US$25.1 billion in "aid for the world's poorest countries", aid that goes to the World Bank\nInternational Development Association\n(IDA), which distributes the loans to eighty poorer countries. The grant beneficiaries are social enterprises with projects that aim to deliver social and public services to groups with the lowest incomes.\nEfforts to reduce economic inequality\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2013 the bank adopted the concept of\nshared prosperity\nas one of the World Bank's "Twin Goals" for that year, with the other one focusing on poverty reduction, aiming to reduce the share of people in extreme poverty to 3 percent of the global population by 2030.\nThe bank defined\nshared prosperity\nas increasing the income of the bottom 40 percent of the population in each country. As a result, reducing economic inequality, in this definition, had become an integral part of the World Bank's objectives.\nThe World Bank has been criticized for not embracing the reduction of economic inequality (be it economic inequality within a country, or\ninternational inequality\nbetween countries) as a goal. World Bank officials participated in the negotiations for SDG 10 in the years prior to 2015. He argued this practice distorts prices and interest rates leading to\nmalinvestment\n, or inefficient use of capital (see\neconomic calculation problem\n). He also warned such systems raise\nmoral hazard\nby encouraging government dependency and poor economic decision-making.\nWorld Bank loan agreements can also force procurements of goods and services at uncompetitive, non-free-market, prices.\nOther critical writers, such as\nJohn Perkins\n, label the international financial institutions as 'illegal and illegitimate and a cog of coercive American diplomacy in carrying out financial terrorism.\nDefenders of the World Bank contend that no country is forced to borrow its money. The bank provides both loans and grants. Even the loans are concessional since they are given to countries that have no access to international\ncapital markets\n. Furthermore, the loans, both to poor and middle-income countries, are below market-value\ninterest rates\n. The World Bank argues that it can help development more through loans than grants because money repaid on the loans can then be lent for other projects.\nThe IFC and MIGA and their way of evaluating the social and environmental impact of their projects has also been criticized. Critics state that even though IFC and MIGA have more of these standards than the World Bank, they mostly rely on private-sector clients to monitor their implementation and miss an independent monitoring institution in this context. This is why an extensive review of the institutions' implementation strategy of social and environmental standards is demanded.\nOne of the most common criticisms of the World Bank has been the way it is governed. While the World Bank represents 188 countries, it is run by a small number of economically powerful countries. These countries (which also provide most of the institution's funding) choose the bank's leadership and senior management, and critics argue the interests of these economically powerful nations dominate the World Bank agenda.\nTitus Alexander argues that the unequal voting power of western countries and the World Bank's role in\ndeveloping countries\nmakes it similar to the South African Development Bank under apartheid, and therefore a pillar of\nglobal apartheid\n.\nIn the 1990s, the World Bank and the IMF forged the\nWashington Consensus\n, policies that included\nderegulation\nand liberalization of markets, privatization and the\ndownscaling of government\n. Though the Washington Consensus was conceived as a policy that would best promote development, it was criticized for ignoring equity, employment, and how reforms like privatization were carried out. Stiglitz argued that the Washington Consensus placed too much emphasis on GDP growth and not enough on the permanence of growth or on whether growth contributed to better living standards.\nThe\nUnited States Senate Committee on Foreign Relations\nreport criticized the World Bank and other international financial institutions for focusing too much "on issuing loans rather than on achieving concrete development results within a finite period of time" and called on the institution to "strengthen anti-corruption efforts".\nJames Ferguson\nhas argued that the main effect of many development projects carried out by the World Bank and similar organizations is not the alleviation of poverty. Instead, the projects often serve to expand the exercise of bureaucratic state power. His case studies of development projects in\nThaba-Tseka\nshow that the World Bank's characterization of the economic conditions in\nLesotho\nwas flawed, and the bank ignored the political and cultural character of the state in crafting its projects. As a result, the projects failed to help the poor but succeeded in expanding the government bureaucracy.\nCriticism of the World Bank and other organizations often takes the form of protesting, such as the\nWorld Bank Oslo 2002 Protests\n,\nthe 2007\nOctober Rebellion\n,\nand the 1999\nBattle of Seattle\n.\nSuch demonstrations have occurred all over the world, even among the Brazilian\nKayapo people\n.\nAnother source of criticism has been the tradition of having an American head the bank, implemented because the United States provides the majority of World Bank funding. "When economists from the World Bank visit poor countries to dispense cash and advice," observed\nThe Economist\nin 2012, "they routinely tell governments to reject cronyism and fill each important job with the best candidate available. It is good advice. The World Bank should take it."\nIn 2021, an independent inquiry of the World Bank's\nDoing Business\nreports by the law firm\nWilmerHale\nfound that World Bank leaders, including then-Chief Executive\nKristalina Georgieva\nand then-President\nJim Yong Kim\n,\npressured staff members of the bank to alter data to inflate the rankings for\nChina\n,\nSaudi Arabia\n,\nAzerbaijan\nand the\nUnited Arab Emirates\n.\nIn September 2023, it was revealed that the World Bank had poured billions of dollars into fossil fuel projects in 2022. Campaigners estimated that about $3.7bn in trade finance was supplied to oil and gas projects despite the World Bank's green pledges.\nAllegations of corruption\n[\nedit\n]\nThe World Bank's Integrity Vice Presidency (INT)\nis charged with the investigation of internal fraud and corruption, including complaint intake, investigation, and investigation reports.\nStructural adjustment\n[\nedit\n]\nThe effect of\nstructural adjustment\npolicies on poor countries has been one of the most significant criticisms of the World Bank.\nThe\n1979 energy crisis\nplunged many countries into economic crisis.\nThe World Bank responded with\nstructural adjustment loans\n, which distributed aid to struggling countries while enforcing policy changes in order to reduce inflation and fiscal imbalance. Some of these policies included encouraging\nproduction\n, investment and labour-intensive manufacturing, changing real\nexchange rates\n, and altering the distribution of government resources. Structural adjustment policies were most effective in countries with an institutional framework that allowed these policies to be implemented easily. For some countries, particularly in\nSub-Saharan Africa\n, economic growth regressed and inflation worsened.\nBy the late 1980s, some international organizations began to believe that structural adjustment policies were worsening life for the world's poor, due to a reduction in social spending and an increase in the price of food, as subsidies were lifted. It also have been criticized for being\nDebt-trap diplomacy\n. The World Bank changed structural adjustment loans, allowing for social spending to be maintained, and encouraging a slower change to policies such as transfer of subsidies and price rises.\nIn 1999, the World Bank and the IMF introduced the\nPoverty Reduction Strategy Paper\napproach to replace structural adjustment loans.\nFairness of assistance conditions\n[\nedit\n]\nSome critics,\nmost prominently the author\nNaomi Klein\n, are of the opinion that the World Bank Group's loans and aid have unfair\nconditions\nattached to them which reflect the interests, financial power and political doctrines (notably the\nWashington Consensus\n) of the bank and the countries that are most influential within it. Among other allegations, Klein says the Group's credibility was damaged "when it forced school fees on students in Ghana in exchange for a loan; when it demanded that Tanzania privatise its water system; when it made telecom privatisation a condition of aid for\nHurricane Mitch\n; when it demanded labour 'flexibility' in Sri Lanka in the aftermath of the\nAsian tsunami\n; when it pushed for eliminating food subsidies in post-invasion Iraq".\nA study of the period 1970–2004 found that a less-developed country would on average receive more World Bank projects during any period when it occupied one of the rotating seats on the UN Security Council.\nSovereign immunity\n[\nedit\n]\nThe World Bank requires\nsovereign immunity\nfrom countries it deals with.\nSovereign immunity waives a holder from all legal liability for their actions. It is proposed that this immunity from responsibility is a "shield which The World Bank wants to resort to, for escaping accountability and security by the people".\nAs the United States has veto power, it can prevent the World Bank from taking action against its interests.\nCronyism and Elite Capture\n[\nedit\n]\nCriticism was also leveled under the presidency of\nJim Yong Kim\n, particularly regarding financial management and staff morale. Reports of a controversial $94,000 bonus awarded to the Bank's CFO,\nBertrand Badré\n(2013–2016), at his request on top of a tax-free salary of $379,000, while significant staff cuts and austerity measures were being implemented, drew criticism from within and outside the organization. This bonus, revealed by Senior Country Officer\nFabrice Houdart\namidst a broader effort by Kim to implement cost-cutting reforms, sparked debates over transparency, ethics, and the organization's commitment to its own principles, further exacerbating concerns about trust and leadership within the World Bank. Badré renounced the bonus and left the Bank shortly after.\nThe World Bank was the subject of a scandal with its then-president\nPaul Wolfowitz\nand his aide,\nShaha Riza\n, in 2007.\nAccording to reports citing a recording of a 2018 staff meeting shared by a whistleblower, World Bank staff were informed Robert Malpass, a recent economics graduate of\nCornell University\nand the son of\nDavid Malpass\n, then US\nUnder Secretary of the Treasury for International Affairs\nand later\nPresident of the World Bank Group\n, would be hired as an analyst in July of that year. On the recording, staff were reportedly told Robert Malpass was a "prince" and an "important little fellow" who could go "running to daddy." Bank officials also believed David Malpass was more influential than then-\nUS Treasury Secretary\nSteven Mnuchin\n, who they said "has little or no clue on things."\nIn April 2018, the US Treasury had changed its position to back a $13 billion capital infusion for the bank.\nMalpass served as undersecretary of the\nUS Treasury\nin the\nTrump administration\nbefore being appointed by Trump in February 2019 to be World Bank's president. Before Malpass became president, his son Robert had joined the\nInternational Finance Corporation\n(IFC), a branch of the World Bank Group that lends money to private sector businesses and whose\nUSD\n5.5 billion funding from a USD 13 billion World Bank capital increase was secured by the US Treasury at the time that David Malpass was the Treasury's undersecretary.\nCriticism of specific loans and programs in Africa\n[\nedit\n]\nAlmost 45% of all the World Bank's resources are going to Africa, despite this the region continues to face significant financing gaps and development challenges.\nOn 9 August 2023, the World Bank announced it was suspending new loans to Uganda because it claims that a new\nanti-homosexuality act\n, enacted in May 2023, contradicts its core values on human rights. The World Bank joined the United States in imposing sanctions against Uganda over the anti-homosexuality law. Uganda dismissed the move by the World Bank as unjust and hypocritical.\nThe World Bank funded a program in Tanzania supposed to help nature conservation. The program was criticized because it led to severe violation of human rights toward the\nMaasai people\n.\nInvestments\n[\nedit\n]\nThe World Bank Group has also been criticized for investing in projects with human rights issues.\nThe\nCompliance Advisor/Ombudsman\n(CAO) criticized a loan the bank made to the palm oil company Dinant after the\n2009 Honduran coup d'état\n. There have been numerous killings of Campesinos in the region where Dinant was operating.\nOther controversial investments include loans to the\nChixoy Hydroelectric Dam\nin Guatemala while it was under military dictatorship, and to\nGoldcorp\n(then Glamis Gold) for the construction of the\nMarlin Mine\n.\nIn 2019, the\nCongressional-Executive Commission on China\nquestioned the World Bank about a loan in\nXinjiang, China\n, that was used to buy high-end security gear, including surveillance equipment.\nThe bank launched an internal investigation in response to the allegation.
