Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asset_management\n==================================================\n\nSystematic method of maintaining assets\nModern portfolio theory\nsuggests a diversified portfolio of\nshares\nand other\nasset classes\n(such as debt in\ncorporate bonds\n,\ntreasury bonds\n, or\nmoney market funds\n) will realise more predictable returns if there is prudent market regulation.\nAsset management\nis a systematic approach to the governance and realization of all value for which a group or\nentity\nis responsible. It may apply both to tangible\nassets\n(physical objects such as complex process plants or manufacturing plants, infrastructure, buildings or equipment) and to intangible assets (such as\nintellectual property\n,\ngoodwill\nor\nfinancial assets\n). Asset management is a systematic process of developing, operating, maintaining, upgrading, and disposing of assets in the most cost-effective manner (including all costs, risks, and performance attributes).\nTheory of asset management primarily deals with the\nperiodic\nmatter of improving, maintaining or in other circumstances assuring the economic and capital\nvalue\nof an asset over time.\nThe term is commonly used in engineering, the business world, and\npublic infrastructure\nsectors to ensure a coordinated approach to the\noptimization\nof costs, risks, service/performance, and sustainability. The term has traditionally been used in the financial sector to describe people and companies who manage investments on behalf of others. Those include, for example,\ninvestment managers\nwho manage the assets of a\npension fund\n.\nThe\nISO 55000\nseries of standards, developed by\nISO TC 251\n, is the international standards for asset management.\nISO 55000\nprovides an introduction to and requirements specification for a management system for asset management. The\nISO 55000\nstandard defines an asset as an "item, thing or entity that has potential or actual value to an organization". ISO 55001 specifies requirements for an asset management system within the context of the organization, and ISO 55002 gives guidelines for the application of an asset management system, in accordance with the requirements of\nISO 55001.\nBy industry\n[\nedit\n]\nFinancial asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nThe most frequent usage of the term\nportfolio manager\n(asset manager) refers to\ninvestment management\n, the sector of the\nfinancial services\nindustry that manages\ninvestment funds\nand\nsegregated client accounts\n. Asset management is part of a financial company that employs experts who manage money and handle the investments of clients. This is done either actively or passively.\nActive asset management\n: involves active tasks such as studying the client's assets to plan and look after the investments, all things are looked after by the asset managers, and recommendations are provided based on the financial health of each client. Active asset management comes at a higher price to investors because more work is involved.\nPassive asset management\n: assets are allocated to mirror a market or a sector index. Unlike active asset management, passive asset management is a lot less laborious. It is also less tailored, requires less looking after, and consequently is cheaper for investors. In recent decades, passive investment strategies such as index funds and exchange-traded funds have significantly increased in market share.(Vanguard report) Benjamin Braun suggests that, since American stock ownership is concentrated on few big asset managers which are very diversified and do not have a direct interest in the performance of the companies, this emerging "asset manager capitalism" is distinct from the earlier\nshareholder primacy\n.\nPhysical and Infrastructure asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nInfrastructure asset management\nPhysical and Infrastructure asset management is the combination of management, financial, economic, engineering, and other practices applied to physical assets to provide the best value level of service for the costs involved. It includes the management of the entire life cycle—including design, construction, commissioning, operating, maintaining, repairing, modifying, replacing, and decommissioning/disposal—of physical and infrastructure assets.\nOperation and maintenance of assets in a constrained budget environment require a prioritization scheme. For instance, the recent development of\nrenewable energy\nhas seen the rise of effective asset managers involved in the management of solar systems (solar parks, rooftops, and windmills). These teams often collaborate with financial asset managers in order to offer\nturnkey\nsolutions to investors.\nInfrastructure asset management became very important in most of the developed countries in the 21st century, since their infrastructure network was almost completed in the 20th century and they have to operate and maintain them cost-effectively.\nPhysical, or Infrastructure, Asset Management is a growing specialist engineering discipline, with many technical societies now established, including the Engineers Australia technical society of the Asset Management Council (AMC),\nthe World Partners in Asset Management (WPiAM),\nSociety for Maintenance and Reliability Professionals (SMRP),\nthe Institute of Asset Management (IAM),\nthe International Society of Engineering Asset Management (ISEAM),\nand the Global Forum on Maintenance and Asset Management (GFMAM).\nEngineering asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nEngineering asset management\nis a more recent term that describes the management of complex physical assets, a specific engineering practice that is concerned with optimizing assets, in the context of the organization's goals and objectives, using multidiscipline engineering methodologies, and\nTerotechnology\n(which includes management, engineering, and financial expertise), to balance cost, risk, and performance. Engineering asset management includes multiple engineering disciplines, including but not limited to\nmaintenance engineering\n,\nsystems engineering\n,\nreliability engineering\n,\nprocess safety management\n,\nindustrial engineering\n, and\nrisk analysis\n. Engineering asset management is a term synonymous with physical and infrastructure asset management, it describes management of more complex physical assets which require the application of specialist asset management engineering methods over their life-cycles in order to maximize value for their owners, whilst keeping risk to an acceptable level.\nNatural asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nOn October 28, 2021, political leaders in the Malaysian state of Sabah on the island of Borneo signed an agreement with the Singapore shell company Hoch Standard, without the knowledge of Indigenous communities, giving the company title to the management and marketing of “natural capital/ecosystem services” on two million hectares of a forest ecosystem for one hundred to two hundred years.\nSee also:\nNatural capital\nand\nEcosystem service\nNatural assets include things such as\nforests\n,\nmarine areas\n,\npeatlands\n, and\nfarmland\n. To manage and invest in these assets,\nNatural asset companies (NACs)\nare a new type of company designed to give private investors a vehicle for direct investment in conservation and regenerative\nland management\n. NACs license rights to the value of natural assets, including ecosystem services such as carbon retention, freshwater generation, groundwater storage, pollination, and erosion prevention. They are intended to manage designated areas, such as forests, grasslands, marine areas, or farms and ranches. NACs have an equity capital structure.\nIn September 2021, the\nNew York Stock Exchange (NYSE)\nand Intrinsic Exchange Group (IEG) jointly announced plans to introduce NACs as a publicly listed asset class.\nThe NYSE filed a listing proposal with the\nU.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)\n, intending to allow NACs to be traded publicly, but withdrew the proposal in January 2024 amid political backlash. IEG continues to advance NACs in private markets and to assist landowners and companies in setting up natural asset companies.\nCriticisms of these newly emerging forms of asset question have come from the political left and right. Right-wing critics argue NACs could be used to lock up public lands. Opposition from the left has reflected concerns that market solutions are not a responsible mode of stewardship for natural assets.\nSupporters argue that NACs incentivize landowners to conserve and improve the natural capital as it correlates to the value of the company, and investors are rewarded for gains in the value of the underlying natural assets.\nSoftware asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nSoftware asset management\nSAM is a sub-discipline of\nIT asset management\n.\nISO standard for asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nThe International Organization for\nStandardization\npublished its management system standard for asset management in 2014.\nThe\nISO 55000\nseries provides terminology, requirements, and guidance for implementing, maintaining and improving an effective asset management system. The key to forming a structure of this sort is directly connected to local governance.\nPhysical asset management: the practice of managing the entire life cycle (design, construction, commissioning, operating, maintaining, repairing, modifying, replacing, and decommissioning/disposal) of physical and infrastructure assets such as structures, production, and service plant, power, water, and waste treatment facilities, distribution networks, transport systems, buildings, and other physical assets.  The increasing availability of data from asset systems is allowing the principles of\nTotal Cost of Ownership\nto be applied to facility management of an individual system, a building, or across a campus.  Physical asset management is related to\nasset health management\n.\nInfrastructure asset management\nexpands on this theme in relation primarily to the public sector,\nutilities\n, property, and transport systems. Additionally, Asset Management can refer to shaping the future interfaces between the human, built, and natural environments through collaborative and evidence-based decision processes\nFixed assets management\n: an accounting process that seeks to track fixed assets for\nfinancial accounting\nIT asset management\n: the set of business practices that join financial, contractual, and inventory functions to support life cycle management and strategic decision making for the IT environment.\nDigital asset management\n: a form of electronic media\ncontent management\nthat includes digital assets\nEnterprise asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nEnterprise asset management\n(EAM) systems are asset\ninformation\nsystems that support the management of an organization's assets.   An EAM includes an asset registry (inventory of assets and their attributes) combined with a\ncomputerized maintenance management system\n(CMMS) and other modules (such as inventory or materials management). Assets that are geographically distributed, interconnected or networked, are often also represented through the use of\ngeographic information systems (GIS)\n.\nGIS-centric asset registry standardizes data and improves interoperability, providing users the capability to reuse, coordinate, and share information efficiently and effectively. A GIS platform combined with information of both the "hard" and "soft" assets helps to remove the traditional silos of departmental functions. While the\nhard assets\nare the typical physical assets or infrastructure assets, the soft assets might include permits, licenses, brands, patents, right-of-ways, and other entitlements or valued items.\nPublic asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nPublic asset management expands the definition of enterprise asset management (EAM) by incorporating the management of all things of value to a municipal\njurisdiction\nand its citizens' expectations. An example in which public asset management is used is land-use development and planning.\nIntellectual and non-physical asset management\n[\nedit\n]\nIncreasingly both consumers and organizations use assets, e.g. software, music, books, etc. where the user's rights are constrained by a license agreement. An asset management system would identify the constraints upon such licenses, e.g. a period. If, for example, one licenses software, often the license is for a given period. Adobe and Microsoft both offer time-based software licenses. In both the corporate and consumer worlds, there is a distinction between software ownership and the updating of software. One may own a version of the software, but not newer versions of the software. Cellular phones are often not updated by vendors, in an attempt to force a purchase of newer hardware.
