For similarly named companies, see\nApple (disambiguation) §\nBusinesses and organisations\n.\nApple Inc.\nis an American multinational technology company headquartered in\nCupertino, California\n, in\nSilicon Valley\n, and known for consumer electronics, software and online services. Founded in 1976 as Apple Computer Company by\nSteve Jobs\n,\nSteve Wozniak\nand\nRonald Wayne\n, the company was incorporated by Jobs and Wozniak as Apple Computer, Inc. the following year. Its current name was adopted in 2007 as the company expanded its focus from computers to consumer electronics. Apple is one of the\nBig Tech\ncompanies.\nThe company was founded to market Wozniak's\nApple I\npersonal computer. Its successor, the\nApple II\n, became one of the first successful mass-produced\nmicrocomputers\n. Apple introduced the\nLisa\nin 1983 and the\nMacintosh\nin 1984 as some of the first computers to use a\ngraphical user interface\nand a\nmouse\n. By 1985, internal conflicts led to Jobs leaving the company to form\nNeXT\nand Wozniak withdrawing to other ventures;\nJohn Sculley\nwas\nCEO\nfor over a decade. In the 1990s, Apple lost considerable market share in the PC industry to the lower-priced\nWintel\nduopoly of\nIntel\n-powered\nPC clones\nrunning\nWindows\n, and was reporting major losses by 1997. To overhaul its market strategy, Apple acquired NeXT, bringing Jobs back to the company. Under his leadership, Apple returned to profitability by introducing the\niMac\n,\niPod\n,\niPhone\n, and\niPad\ndevices; creating the\niTunes Store\n; launching the "\nThink different\n" advertising campaign; and opening the\nApple Store\nretail chain. Jobs resigned in 2011 for health reasons and died the same year. He was succeeded as CEO by\nTim Cook\n, who will be succeeded by\nJohn Ternus\nin September 2026.\nApple's product lineup\nincludes portable and home hardware like the iPhone, iPad,\nMac\n,\nApple Watch\n, and\nAirPods\n;\nseveral in-house operating systems\nsuch as\niOS\n,\niPadOS\n, and\nmacOS\n; and\nvarious software\nand services including\nApple Pay\nand\niCloud\n, as well as multimedia streaming services like\nApple Music\nand\nApple TV\n. Since 2011, Apple has for the most part been the world's\nlargest company by market capitalization\n, and,\nas of 2024\n, is the\nlargest manufacturing company by revenue\n, the\nfourth-largest PC vendor\n, the\nlargest vendor of tablet computers\n, and the\nlargest vendor of mobile phones\n. Apple became the first publicly traded US company to be\nvalued at over $1\ntrillion\nin 2018, and,\nas of October\n2025\n, is valued at just over $4 trillion.\nApple has received criticism\nregarding\nits contractors\n' labor conditions,\nits relationship with trade unions\n,\nits environmental practices\n, and its corporate ethics, including\nanti-competitive tactics\n, materials sourcing, and\nits acquisitions of smaller businesses\n. Nevertheless, the company has\na large following\nand enjoys\na high level of customer loyalty\n. Apple has consistently been ranked as one of the world's\nmost valuable brands\nsince the late 2000s.\nHistory\nMain article:\nHistory of Apple Inc.\n1976–1980: Founding and incorporation\nIn 1976,\nSteve Jobs\nand\nSteve Wozniak\nco-founded Apple in Jobs' parents' home in\nLos Altos, California\n.\nThe\nApple I\n, the company's first product, was sold as an assembled board without a keyboard, power supply, or case.\nApple Computer was founded as a\npartnership\non April 1, 1976, by\nSteve Jobs\n,\nSteve Wozniak\n, and\nRonald Wayne\n.\nThe company's first product was the\nApple I\n, designed by Wozniak and hand-built.\nTo finance production, Jobs sold his\nVolkswagen Bus\nand Wozniak sold his\nHP-65\ncalculator. Although neither received the full selling price, together they raised\n$1,300\n(equivalent to $7,400 in 2025)\n.\nWozniak demonstrated the first Apple I prototype at the\nHomebrew Computer Club\nin July 1976, where local computer retailer\nByte Shop\nplaced an order for 50 fully assembled computers.\nUnlike most contemporary hobbyist computers, which were sold as kits requiring assembly, the Apple I came as a pre-assembled\nmotherboard\nwith a\nCPU\n,\nRAM\n, and built-in video circuitry, allowing it to connect directly to a household television instead of requiring a costly computer terminal. Users still had to provide a case, power supply, and keyboard. It was introduced at a price of\n$666.66\n(equivalent to $3,770 in 2025)\n.\nApple Computer, Inc. was incorporated in\nCupertino, California\n,\non January 3, 1977,\nwithout Wayne, who had left and sold his share of the company back to Jobs and Wozniak for $800 twelve days after its founding.\nMultimillionaire\nMike Markkula\nprovided business expertise and invested\n$250,000\n(equivalent to $1,328,000 in 2025)\nduring the company's incorporation.\nDuring its first five years, revenue grew rapidly, doubling approximately every four months. Between September 1977 and September 1980, annual sales increased from $775,000 to\nUS$118\nmillion, an average annual growth rate of 533%.\nThe\nApple II\n, also designed by Wozniak, was introduced on April 16, 1977, at the first\nWest Coast Computer Faire\n.\nAlongside the\nCommodore PET 2001\nand the\nTRS-80\n, it formed the "\n1977 Trinity\n" of influential personal computers that established the home computer market. Unlike the Apple I, the Apple II was mass-produced as a complete personal computer housed in a plastic case with a built-in keyboard, color graphics, and an\nopen architecture\n.\nApple introduced the\nDisk II\n5\n+\n1\n⁄\n4\n-inch floppy disk drive in 1978, replacing cassette tape storage with faster and more reliable removable disks.\nThe\nApple II\n, introduced in 1977, became Apple's first commercially successful computer.\nThe Apple II became Apple's first major commercial success following the 1979 release of\nVisiCalc\n, the first widely adopted\nspreadsheet\nprogram and one of the earliest personal computer "\nkiller applications\n". Initially available exclusively for the Apple II, VisiCalc transformed the computer into a popular business tool and helped establish Apple in the growing personal computer industry.\nOn December 12, 1980, Apple became a public company through an\ninitial public offering\n(IPO) on the\nNasdaq\n. The company sold 4.6 million shares at $22 each,\nraising more than $100 million, the largest IPO since\nFord Motor Company\n's 1956 offering.\nBy the end of the first day of trading, Apple's\nmarket capitalization\nreached $1.778\nbillion and more than 300 employees and investors became millionaires.\n1980–1990: Success with Macintosh\nSee also:\nList of Mac models\nand\nTimeline of the Apple II family\nSteve Jobs\nin 1984 with the\nMacintosh\n, the first successful mass-market personal computer to feature an integral\ngraphical user interface\nand\nmouse\nIn November and December 1979, Apple employees, including Jobs and\nJef Raskin\n, visited\nXerox PARC\ntwice as part of a technology exchange negotiated by Jobs, in which\nXerox\nwas granted the right to purchase $1\nmillion of Apple's pre-IPO stock.\nThere they observed the experimental\nXerox Alto\n, which featured a\ngraphical user interface\n(GUI) navigated with a\nmouse\n, that inspired Jobs to incorporate a GUI into Apple's future products.\nApple's first GUI-based computer, the\nApple Lisa\n, was introduced in 1983. Although the Lisa was pioneering as a mass-marketed GUI computer, its high price and limited software library resulted in poor sales.\nJobs was removed from the Lisa project during its early development and joined the\nMacintosh\ndivision in 1981. The Macintosh had originally been conceived by Raskin as a low-cost,\nportable computer\n, with early contributions from Wozniak. After Wozniak stepped back following a plane crash, Jobs took over the project,\ntransforming the Macintosh into a mouse-driven computer with a GUI similar to the Lisa. Wozniak later speculated that Jobs' rivalry with the Lisa project was the driving force behind this shift in direction.\nJobs was also hostile toward the Apple II division, which at the time generated most of the company's revenue.\nIn 1984, Apple launched\nthe original Macintosh\non January 24, 1984, introducing it to the public with "\n1984\n", a\nUS$1.5\n-million television advertisement directed by\nRidley Scott\nthat aired during the third quarter of\nSuper Bowl XVIII\non January 22, 1984.\nThis was hailed as a watershed event for Apple's success\nand was called a "masterpiece" by\nCNN\nand one of the greatest TV advertisements of all time by\nTV Guide\n.\nAlthough initial Macintosh sales were strong, they declined rapidly after the first few months as reviewers criticized its limited 128\nKB of RAM, small software library, and relatively high price of\n$2,495\n(equivalent to $7,700 in 2025)\n.\nSlow Macintosh sales led to a power struggle between Jobs and chief executive officer\nJohn Sculley\n, whom Jobs had recruited from\nPepsiCo\nin 1983.\nThe board of directors instructed Sculley to limit Jobs' ability to launch further expensive forays into untested products. Rather than accept Sculley's direction, Jobs attempted to remove him as chief executive.\nJean-Louis Gassée\ninformed Sculley of Jobs' efforts to organize a\nboardroom coup\n, prompting an emergency meeting in which Apple's executive staff sided with Sculley. In May 1985, the board stripped Jobs of all operational duties.\nJobs resigned from Apple in September 1985 and founded competitor\nNeXT\n, which developed the\nNeXTSTEP\noperating system, taking several Apple employees with him.\nWozniak had already stepped away from day-to-day operations following a 1981 plane crash. He left active employment at Apple in 1985, citing frustration that the company was prioritizing the Macintosh over the Apple II division, which continued to generate a significant portion of Apple's revenue during the mid-1980s.\nHe remained an Apple shareholder and continued to represent the company in a ceremonial capacity.\nAfter the departures of Jobs and Wozniak in 1985, Sculley shifted Apple's focus toward expanding the Macintosh platform and appointed Gassée to lead Macintosh development, placing him in the role previously held by Jobs. That year, Apple introduced the\nMacintosh 512K\n, which quadrupled the memory of the original Macintosh to address one of its major limitations. Apple also released the\nLaserWriter\n, one of the first affordable\nPostScript\n-based laser printers, while\nAldus Corporation\nintroduced\nPageMaker\nin July 1985.\nThe combination of the Macintosh, LaserWriter, and PageMaker helped establish the\ndesktop publishing\nmarket.\nApple's success in desktop publishing allowed the company to pursue a premium pricing strategy,\nsometimes referred to as the "high-right policy" because of its position on a price–profitability chart. Under this strategy, Apple focused on higher-\nmargin\nproducts rather than competing on price, with executives believing that\npower users\nwould continue to pay for increased performance and capabilities. The policy was championed by Gassée, who promoted a target Macintosh profit margin of 55 percent.\nGassée also opposed proposals to license the\nMac OS\nto other manufacturers. By 1988, Gassée had become head of advanced product development and worldwide marketing and was viewed by some as a potential successor to Sculley.\nHowever, the strategy began to face challenges by the late 1980s as desktop publishing software became available on\nIBM PC compatible\ncomputers which sold at substantially lower prices. Apple lost its dominant position in desktop publishing as high prices alienated some customers. The 1989 holiday season marked the first decline in the company's sales history, contributing to a 20 percent drop in Apple's stock price.\nRelations between Sculley and Gassée deteriorated over disagreements about product strategy and pricing. In January 1990, Sculley appointed\nMichael Spindler\nas\nchief operating officer\n, reducing Gassée's influence.\nGassée left Apple at the end of 1990 and founded competitor\nBe Inc.\nin 1991, which developed the\nBeOS\noperating system, taking several Apple employees with him\n.\n1990–1997: Decline and restructuring\nMacintosh LC\nThe company pivoted its strategy and, in October 1990, introduced three lower-cost models: the\nMacintosh Classic\n, the\nMacintosh LC\n, and the\nMacintosh IIsi\n, all of which generated strong sales.\nIn 1991, Apple introduced the\nPowerBook\n, a commercially successful laptop whose\nclamshell design\ninfluenced later notebook computers. The same year, Apple introduced\nSystem 7\n, a major upgrade to the Macintosh operating system that introduced a color user interface and improved networking capabilities. The success of the lower-cost Macintosh models and the PowerBook brought increasing revenue,\nand the period from 1989 to 1991 was later described by\nMacAddict\nas the "first golden age" of the Macintosh.\nHowever, the success of the lower-cost Macintosh models also cannibalized sales of Apple's higher-end systems. In response, the company introduced several new brands, selling largely identical machines at different price points, for different markets: the high-end\nQuadra\nseries, the mid-range\nCentris\n, and the consumer-oriented\nPerforma\n. This led to widespread consumer confusion.\nApple discontinued the Apple II series in 1993 after encouraging developers and customers to transition to the Macintosh, bringing the company's longest-running product line to an end.\nNewton\nMessagePad\nwith stylus\nDuring the 1990s, Apple also introduced several consumer products, including the\nQuickTake\ndigital cameras,\nPowerCD\n, the\nPippin game console\n,\neWorld\n,\nApple Interactive Television Box\n, and the\nNewton\ntablet division. Although technologically ambitious, they were commercially unsuccessful.\nMeanwhile, Microsoft expanded the reach of\nWindows\nby focusing on inexpensive personal computers, while Apple continued to emphasize premium hardware with high profit margins.\nApple unsuccessfully sued Microsoft\nover similarities between Windows and the Lisa's operating system.\nThe consumer product flops and the rapid loss of market share to Windows sullied Apple's reputation, and in 1993 Sculley was replaced as CEO by Michael Spindler.\nPower Macintosh 6100\n, Apple's first computer with a\nPowerPC\nprocessor, introduced in 1994\nIn 1994, Apple,\nIBM\n, and\nMotorola\nformed the\nAIM alliance\nto promote the\nPowerPC platform\n. That year, Apple introduced the first\nPower Macintosh\nmodels using\nPowerPC\nprocessors.\nIn the wake of the alliance, Apple opened up to the idea of allowing Motorola and other companies to build\nMacintosh clones\n. Over the next two years, 75 distinct Macintosh clone models were introduced. However, by 1996, Apple executives were worried that the clones were cannibalizing sales of its own high-end computers, where profit margins were highest.\nIn 1996, Spindler was replaced as CEO by\nGil Amelio\n, who was hired for his reputation as a corporate rehabilitator. Amelio made big changes, including extensive layoffs and cost-cutting.\nThis period was also marked by repeated attempts to modernize the\nMacintosh operating system\n. The original\nSystem 1\nwas not built for\nmultitasking\n(running several applications at once), and although Apple introduced\ncooperative multitasking\nin System 5, the company concluded that a more modern approach was needed.\nInternal efforts, including the\nPink\nproject,\nA/UX\n, and\nCopland\n, failed to produce a suitable successor. Apple then sought to acquire an external operating system, first entering negotiations to purchase BeOS. The talks with Be collapsed when Jean-Louis Gassée demanded $300\nmillion, well above Apple's $125\nmillion offer.\nApple instead acquired NeXT, the company founded by Steve Jobs, for its NeXTSTEP operating system. Apple would purchase the company for $427 million in cash, stock, stock options, and assumed debt.\nAt the insistence of Amelio, Jobs accepted 1.5 million Apple shares as part of the deal rather than an all-cash payment, lending credibility to the acquisition.\nUnder the agreement, Jobs returned to Apple as an advisor.\n1997–2007: Return to profitability\nThe NeXT acquisition was completed on February 7, 1997.\nJobs soon became frustrated with the leadership of Amelio and Apple's board, and in June 1997 anonymously sold his 1.5 million shares, sending the company's stock to a 12-year low. The stock slide rattled the board, which ousted Amelio as CEO the following month.\nJobs became Apple's\nde facto\nleader and was formally appointed interim CEO on September 16.\nIn August 1997, Jobs secured a $150 million investment from Microsoft and a commitment to continue developing software for the Mac platform,\nan agreement widely viewed as beneficial to both companies following\nMicrosoft's antitrust settlement\nwith the U.S. Department of Justice.\nJobs ended the Macintosh clone licensing program, and in September 1997, acquired the largest clone manufacturer,\nPower Computing\n.\nIn November 1997, the\nonline Apple Store\nlaunched and adopted a build-to-order manufacturing model similar to that used by\nDell\n.\nBy the end of 1997, Jobs had returned Apple to profitability, reporting a profit of $309 million.\nThe 'four-quadrant' devices\niMac\niBook\nPower Macintosh G3\nPowerBook G3\nJobs streamlined Apple's product lineup, eliminating roughly 70 percent of existing models and introducing a simplified 'four-quadrant' product strategy consisting of one desktop and one laptop computer each for consumer and professional markets.\nThe restructuring eliminated approximately 3,000 jobs.\nThe first major product introduced under this strategy was the\niMac\n, unveiled on May 6, 1998. Designed by\nJony Ive\n, the all-in-one computer featured a distinctive translucent design enclosure, emphasized Internet connectivity (the "i" in iMac), adopted modern technologies such as\nUSB\n, and omitted legacy technologies like the\nfloppy disk\ndrive. It sold approximately 800,000 units within its first five months on the market.\nApple completed the transition to the four-quadrant lineup on July 21, 1999, with the introduction of the consumer-oriented\niBook\nlaptop. It joined the iMac and updated versions of the professional\nPower Macintosh\ndesktop and\nPowerBook\nlaptop, completing the simplified product matrix. Jobs said the smaller product lineup allowed Apple to focus more on quality and innovation.\nDuring this period, Apple also focused on building an ecosystem around digital media, similar to its earlier efforts in desktop publishing, through a series of acquisitions and product launches. Apple acquired\nMacromedia\n's Key Grip digital video editing project, which became\nFinal Cut Pro\nin April 1999.\nThe technology also contributed to the development of\niMovie\n, Apple's consumer video-editing application, released in October 1999.\nIn April 2000, Apple acquired German company\nAstarte\n, whose DVDirector DVD authoring software was repackaged as\nDVD Studio Pro\nfor professionals and adapted into\niDVD\nfor consumers.\nLater that year, Apple acquired\nSoundJam MP\nfrom\nCasady & Greene\nand relaunched it as\niTunes\n, adding a simplified interface and CD-burning capabilities.\nIn 2001, Apple made three announcements that would shape its future direction. On March 24, Apple released\nMac OS X\n, its next-generation operating system after years of failed attempts to modernize the classic Mac OS. Based on\nNeXTSTEP\n, Mac OS X combined a stable, reliable, and secure Unix foundation with a redesigned GUI. In May, Apple opened its first two\nApple Store\nretail locations in\nMcLean, Virginia\n, and\nGlendale, California\n, providing controlled spaces to showcase its products and services.\nDespite skepticism that it would be able to compete as a retailer, the stores succeeded, and Apple expanded to more than 500 locations worldwide.\nOn November 10, Apple released the\niPod\n, its first major consumer product outside the computer market in years. The portable digital audio player became a major success, selling more than 100 million units within six years.\nThe iTunes Store was highly successful in shaping the legal\nmusic downloading\nindustry; chart shows the number of songs sold from 2003 to 2010.\nIn 2003, Apple launched the\niTunes Store\n, allowing customers to purchase and\ndownload music\nfor 99¢ per song. The service quickly became the leading online music retailer, reaching more than 5 billion downloads by June 2008 and becoming the world's largest music retailer two years later.\nThe iTunes Store helped transform the music industry by shifting consumers from unauthorized file-sharing services such as\nNapster\ntoward paid digital distribution.\nIn 2002, Apple purchased\nNothing Real\nfor its advanced digital\ncompositing\napplication\nShake\n,\nand\nEmagic\nfor the music productivity application\nLogic\n. The acquisition was followed by the development of Apple's consumer-level\nGarageBand\napplication.\nThe release of\niPhoto\nthat year completed the\niLife\nsuite.\nMacBook Pro\n, Apple's first laptop with an\nIntel\nprocessor, introduced in 2006\nAt the\nWorldwide Developers Conference\nkeynote address on June 6, 2005, Jobs announced that Apple would move away from PowerPC processors, and the\nMac would transition to Intel processors\nin 2006.\nOn January 10, 2006, the new\nMacBook Pro\nand\niMac\nbecame the first Apple computers to use Intel's\nCore Duo\nCPU.
