Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_risk_management\n==================================================\n\nProtecting economic value by managing risk exposure\nFinancial risk management\nis the practice of protecting\neconomic value\nin a\nfirm\nby managing exposure to\nfinancial risk\n- principally\ncredit risk\nand\nmarket risk\n, with more specific variants as listed aside - as well as some aspects of\noperational risk\n. As for\nrisk management\nmore generally, financial risk management requires identifying the sources of risk, measuring these, and crafting plans to\nmitigate\nthem.\nSee\nFinance §\nRisk management\nfor an overview.\nFinancial risk management as a "science" can be said to have been born\nwith\nmodern portfolio theory\n, particularly as initiated by Professor\nHarry Markowitz\nin 1952 with his article, "Portfolio Selection";\nsee\nMathematical finance §\nRisk and portfolio management: the P world\n.\nThe discipline can be qualitative and quantitative; as a specialization of\nrisk\nmanagement, however, financial risk management focuses more on when and how to\nhedge\n,\noften using financial instruments to manage costly exposures to risk.\nIn the banking sector worldwide, the\nBasel Accords\nare generally adopted by internationally active banks for tracking, reporting and exposing operational, credit and market risks.\nWithin non-financial corporates,\nthe scope is broadened to overlap\nenterprise risk management\n, and financial risk management then addresses risks to the firm's overall\nstrategic objectives\n.\nInsurers\nmanage their own risks with a focus on solvency and the ability to pay claims.\nLife Insurers are concerned more with longevity and interest rate risk, while short-Term Insurers emphasize\ncatastrophe-risk\nand claims volatility.\nIn\ninvestment management\nrisk is managed through diversification and related optimization; while further specific techniques are then applied to the portfolio or to individual stocks as appropriate.\nIn all cases, the last "\nline of defence\n" against risk is\ncapital\n, "as it ensures that a firm can continue as a\ngoing concern\neven if substantial and unexpected losses are incurred".\nEconomic perspective\n[\nedit\n]\nNeoclassical\nfinance theory\nprescribes that (1) a firm should take on a project only if it increases\nshareholder\nvalue.\nFurther, the theory suggests that (2)\nfirm managers\ncannot create value for shareholders or\ninvestors\nby taking on projects that shareholders could do for themselves at the same cost; see\nTheory of the firm\nand\nFisher separation theorem\n.\nGiven these, there is therefore a fundamental debate relating to "Risk Management" and\nshareholder value\n.\nThe discussion essentially weighs the value of risk management in a market versus the cost of\nbankruptcy\nin that market: per the\nModigliani and Miller framework\n, hedging is irrelevant since diversified shareholders are assumed to not care about firm-specific risks, whereas, on the other hand hedging is seen to create value in that it reduces the\nprobability of financial distress\n.\nWhen applied to financial risk management, this implies that firm managers should not hedge risks that investors can hedge for themselves at the same cost.\nThis notion is captured in the so-called "hedging irrelevance proposition":\n"In a\nperfect market\n, the firm cannot create value by hedging a risk when the price of bearing that\nrisk\nwithin the firm is the same as the\nprice\nof bearing it outside of the firm."\nIn practice, however, financial markets are not likely to be perfect markets.\nThis suggests that firm managers likely have many opportunities to create value for shareholders using financial risk management, wherein they are able to determine which risks are cheaper for the firm to manage than for shareholders. Here,\nmarket risks\nthat result in unique risks for the firm are commonly the best candidates for financial risk management.\nApplication\n[\nedit\n]\nAs outlined, businesses are exposed, in the main, to market, credit and operational risk. A broad distinction\nexists though, between\nfinancial institutions\nand non-financial firms - and correspondingly, the application of risk management will differ. Respectively:\nFor Banks and Fund Managers, "credit and market risks are taken intentionally with the objective of earning returns, while operational risks are a\nbyproduct\nto be controlled". 
For non-financial firms, the priorities are reversed, as "the focus is on the risks associated with the business" - ie the production and marketing of the services and products\nin which expertise is held\n- and their impact on revenue, costs and cash flow, "while market and credit risks are usually of secondary importance as they are a byproduct of the main business agenda". 
(See related discussion re\nvaluing financial services firms\nas compared to other firms.)
In all cases, as above, risk capital is the last "\nline of defence\n".\nBanking\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nBank §\nCapital and risk\n,\nShadow banking system §\nRisks or vulnerability\n, and\nBanking regulation and supervision\nBanks\nand other\nwholesale institutions\nface various\nfinancial risks\nin conducting their business, and how well these risks are managed and understood is a key driver\nbehind profitability, as well as of the\nquantum of capital\nthey are required to hold.\nFinancial risk management in banking has thus grown markedly in importance since the\n2008 financial crisis\n.\n(This has given rise\nto various\ndedicated degrees\nand several\nprofessional certifications\n.)\nThe broad distinction between\nInvestment Banks\n, on the one hand, and\nCommercial\nand\nRetail Banks\non the other, carries through to the management of risk at these institutions.
Investment Banks profit from  trading -\nproprietary\nand\nflow\n-  and earn fees from\nstructuring\nand\ndeal making\n; the latter\nincludes\nlisting securities so as to raise funding in the\ncapital markets\n(and\nsupporting\nthese thereafter), as well as directly providing debt-funding for\nlarge corporate "projects"\n.
The major focus for risk managers here is therefore on\nmarket-\nand (\ncorporate\n)\ncredit risk\n.
Commercial and Retail Banks, as\ndeposit taking institutions\n, profit from the spread between\ndeposit\nand loan rates.
The focus of risk management is then on\nloan defaults\nfrom individuals or businesses (\nSMEs\n), and on having enough\nliquid assets\nto meet\nwithdrawal demands\n; market risk concerns, mainly, the impact of interest rate changes on\nnet interest margins\n.
All banks will focus also on operational risk, impacting here (at least) through\nregulatory capital\n;
(large) banks are also exposed to\nMacroeconomic\nsystematic risk\n- risks related to the aggregate economy the bank is operating in\n(see\nToo big to fail\n).\nCentral to both commercial and investment banking is the function of\nmaturity transformation\n,\nwhere institutions fund long-term assets using short-term liabilities. Commercial banks typify this by issuing\ndemand deposits\n(which can be withdrawn at any time) to fund long-dated assets like\nmortgages\n, while investment banks often finance longer-term\ntrading inventory\nor\nstructured products\nthrough short-term\nrepurchase agreements\n(repos). While this "borrowing short and lending long" strategy is profitable — normally capturing the spread between\nlower short-term rates and higher long-term rates\n— it creates an inherent mismatch on the balance sheet. This structural mismatch generates the primary risks that banks must manage - outlined in the preceding paragraph - but here especially: liquidity risk (the inability to meet short-term obligations without selling assets) and\ninterest rate risk\n(changes in the\nyield curve\naffecting asset and liability values\ndifferently\n), making\nAsset and liability management\n(ALM) a critical discipline.\nThe\nBasel Accords\nmandate the predominant risk management framework. Under\n"Pillar I"\nregulators define the minimum\nregulatory capital\nrequirements for quantifiable risks — principally\ncredit risk\n,\nmarket risk\n, and\noperational risk\nas outlined — using either standardised or approved internal‑model approaches. Under\n"Pillar II"\n, banks must conduct an internal capital adequacy assessment (\nICAAP\n) to capture all material risks, holding sufficient "\neconomic capital\n" for those.\nSome jurisdictions (or banks) complement these with additional buffers, stress testing, and\nsupervisory review\n.\nInvestment banking\n[\nedit\n]\nThe 5% Value at Risk of a hypothetical profit-and-loss\nprobability density function\nFurther information:\nInvestment banking §\nRisk management\nSee also:\nDerivative (finance) §\nRisks\n,\nInterest rate swap §\nRisks\n,\nOption (finance) §\nRisks\n,\nValue at risk §\nVaR risk management\n,\nCorporate bond §\nRisk analysis\n, and\nMulti-curve framework §\nRisk management\nFor\ninvestment banks\n- as outlined - the major focus is on credit and market risk.
Credit risk is inherent in the business of banking, but additionally, these institutions are exposed to\ncounterparty credit risk\n. Both are\nto some extent offset\nby\nmargining\nand\ncollateral\n; and the management is of the\nnet-position\n.\nRisk management here\nis, as discussed, simultaneously concerned with
(i) managing, and as necessary\nhedging\n, the various positions held by the institution - both\ntrading positions\nand\nlong term exposures\n;
and 
(ii) calculating and monitoring the resultant\neconomic capital\n, as well as the\nregulatory capital\nunder\nBasel III\n— which, importantly, covers also\nleverage\nand\nliquidity\n— with regulatory capital as a floor.\nCorrespondingly, and broadly, the analytics\nare based as follows:
For (i) on\nthe "Greeks"\n, the sensitivity of the price of a derivative to a change in its underlying factors; as well as on\nthe various other measures of sensitivity\n, such as\nDV01\nfor the sensitivity of a\nbond\nor\nswap\nto interest rates, and\nCS01\nor\nJTD\nfor exposure to\ncredit spread\n. 
For (ii) on\nvalue at risk\n, or "VaR", an estimate of how much the investment or area in question might lose as market and credit conditions deteriorate, with a given probability over a set time period, and with the bank then holding\n"economic"-\nor "\nrisk capital\n" correspondingly;\ncommon parameters\nare 99% and 95% worst-case losses - i.e. 1% and 5% - and one day and two week (\n10 day\n) horizons.\nThese calculations are mathematically sophisticated, and within\nthe domain of quantitative finance\n.\nThe regulatory capital quantum is calculated via specified formulae: risk weighting the exposures per highly standardized asset-categorizations, applying the aside frameworks, and the resultant capital — at least 12.9%\nof these\nRisk-weighted assets\n(RWA) — must then be held\nin specific "tiers"\nand is measured correspondingly via the\nvarious capital ratios\n.
In certain cases, banks are allowed to use their own estimated risk parameters here; these\n"internal ratings-based models"\ntypically result in less required capital, but at the same time\nare subject to\nstrict minimum conditions and disclosure requirements.
As mentioned, additional to the capital covering RWA, the aggregate\nbalance sheet\nwill require capital for\nleverage\nand\nliquidity\n; this is monitored via\nthe\nLR\n,\nLCR\n, and\nNSFR\nratios.\nThe\n2008 financial crisis\nexposed holes in the mechanisms used for hedging
(see\nFundamental Review of the Trading Book §\nBackground\n,\nTail risk §\nRole of the 2007–2008 financial crisis\n,\nValue at risk §\nCriticism\n, and\nBasel III §\nCriticism\n).
As such, the methodologies employed\nhave had to evolve\n, both from a modelling point of view, and in parallel, from a regulatory point of view.\nRegarding the modelling, changes corresponding to the above are:
(i) For the daily\ndirect analysis of the positions\nat the\ndesk level\n, as a standard, measurement of the Greeks\nnow inheres\nthe\nvolatility surface\n— through\nlocal-\nor\nstochastic volatility\nmodels — while re interest rates, discounting\nand analytics\nare under a "\nmulti-curve framework\n".\nDerivative pricing\nnow embeds\nconsiderations re\ncounterparty risk\nand\nfunding risk\n, amongst others,\nthrough the\nCVA\nand\nXVA\n"valuation adjustments"; these\nalso carry\nregulatory capital.
(ii) For Value at Risk, the traditional\nparametric\nand\n"Historical"\napproaches, are now supplemented\nwith the more sophisticated\nConditional value at risk\n/\nexpected shortfall\n,\nTail value at risk\n, and\nExtreme value theory\n. For the underlying mathematics, these may utilize\nmixture models\n,\nPCA\n,\nvolatility clustering\n,\ncopulas\n, and other techniques.\nExtensions to VaR include\nMargin-\n,\nLiquidity-\n,\nEarnings-\nand\nCash flow at risk\n, as well as\nLiquidity-adjusted VaR\n.
For both (i) and (ii),\nmodel risk\nis addressed\nthrough regular\nvalidation of the models\nused by the bank's various divisions; for VaR models,\nbacktesting\nis especially employed.\nRegulatory changes, are also twofold. 
The first change, entails an\nincreased emphasis\non\nbank stress tests\n.\nThese tests, essentially\na simulation\nof the\nbalance sheet\nfor a\ngiven scenario\n, are typically linked to the macroeconomics, and provide an indicator of how sensitive the bank is to changes in economic conditions, whether it is\nsufficiently capitalized\n, and of its ability to respond to market events.
The second set of changes, sometimes called "\nBasel IV\n", entails the modification of several regulatory capital standards (\nCRR III\nis the EU implementation).  In particular\nFRTB\naddresses market risk, and\nSA-CCR\naddresses counterparty risk; 
other modifications\nare also being phased in\n.\nTo\noperationalize\nthe above,\nInvestment banks\n, particularly, employ dedicated\n"Risk Groups"\n, i.e.\nMiddle Office\nteams monitoring the firm's risk-exposure to, and the profitability and structure of, its various\nbusiness units\n,\nproducts\n,\nasset classes\n, desks, and /\nor geographies\n.\nBy increasing order of aggregation and time-horizon:\nFinancial institutions will set\nlimit values for each of the Greeks\n, or other sensitivities, that their\ntraders\nmust not exceed,  and traders\nwill then hedge\n, offset, or reduce periodically if not daily; see the techniques\nlisted below\n. These limits are set given a range\nof plausible changes in prices and rates, coupled with the board-specified\nrisk appetite\nre overnight-losses.\nDesks, or areas, will similarly be\nlimited as to their VaR quantum\n(total or incremental, and under various calculation regimes), corresponding to their allocated\neconomic capital; a loss which exceeds the VaR threshold is termed a "VaR breach".\nRWA - the key Pillar I result - is correspondingly monitored from desk level\nand upward. Market-risk RWA will be monitored more frequently, with credit-risk RWA less so. While VaR is a risk-taking constraint (front office) RWA is a capital supply constraint (management + regulators); it does provide the denominator for the below ratios and feeds into other planning.\nEach area's (or desk's)\nconcentration risk will be checked\nagainst thresholds set for various types of risk, and / or re a single counterparty,\nsector\nor geography.\nLeverage will be monitored\n, at very least\nre regulatory requirements\nvia LR, the\nLeverage Ratio\n, as leveraged positions\ncould lose large amounts\nfor a relatively small move in the price of the underlying.\nRelatedly,\nliquidity risk is monitored\n: LCR, the\nLiquidity Coverage Ratio\n, measures the ability of the bank to survive a short-term stress, covering its total net cash outflows over the next 30 days with "\nhigh quality liquid assets\n"; NSFR, the\nNet Stable Funding Ratio\n, assesses its ability to finance assets and commitments within a year (addressing also,\nmaturity transformation\nrisk). Any\n"gaps"\n, also,\nmust be managed\n.\nSystemically Important Banks\nhold additional capital such that  their\ntotal loss absorbency capacity\n, TLAC, is sufficient\ngiven both RWA and leverage. (See also "MREL"\nfor EU institutions.)\nPeriodically,\nthese all are estimated under a given stress scenario —\nregulatory\nand,\noften,\ninternal\n— 
and risk capital,\ntogether with these limits\nif indicated,\nis correspondingly revisited (or optimized\n). 
The approaches taken center either on a hypothetical or\nhistorical scenario\n,\nand may apply increasingly sophisticated mathematics\nto the analysis.
More generally, these tests\nprovide estimates\nfor scenarios  beyond the VaR thresholds, thus “preparing for anything that might happen, rather than worrying about precise likelihoods".\nA reverse stress test, in fact, starts from the point at which "the institution can be considered as failing or likely to fail... and then explores scenarios and circumstances that might cause this to occur".\nEconomic Capital\n(EC) reflects the\ntotal\nrisk capital\nthat the bank requires to cover "all" its risks as a\ngoing concern\nassessed on a realistic basis, including survival in a worst-case scenario.\nThe modelling - at least once annually - must be such that\nany material risks are adequately and conservatively quantified; banks typically deploy detailed (long run) simulations and coupled stress testing. The\nbalance-sheet composition\nis naturally revisited as part of the assessment. Although essentially an internal measure, EC will be (re)viewed by the Regulator under Pillar II and, as above, is governed by\nICAAP\n, the framework for the bank's "internal capital adequacy assessment process".\nA key practice,\nincorporating and assimilating much of the above, is to assess the\nRisk-adjusted return on capital\n, RAROC, of each area (or product). Here,\n"economic profit"\nis divided by allocated-capital; and this result is then compared\nto the target-return for the area — usually, at least the\nequity holders' expected returns\non the bank stock\n— and identified under-performance can then be addressed. (See similar\nbelow\nre. DuPont analysis.)
The numerator, risk-adjusted return, is realized trading-return less a\nterm and risk appropriate funding cost\nas charged\nby Treasury\nto the business-unit under the bank's\nfunds transfer pricing\n(FTP) framework;\ndirect costs\nare (sometimes) also subtracted.\nThe denominator is the area's allocated capital, as above, increasing as a function of position risk;\nseveral allocation techniques exist.\nRAROC is calculated both\nex post\nas discussed, used for performance evaluation (and related\nbonus calculations\n),
and\nex ante\n- i.e.\nexpected return\nless\nexpected loss\n- to decide whether a particular business unit should be expanded or contracted.\nOther teams, overlapping the above Groups, are then also involved in risk management.\nCorporate Treasury\nis responsible for monitoring overall funding and capital structure; it shares responsibility for monitoring liquidity risk, and for maintaining the FTP framework.
Middle Office\nmaintains the following functions\nalso:\nProduct Control\nis primarily responsible for ensuring traders\nmark their books to fair value\n— a key protection against\nrogue traders\n— and for\n"explaining" the daily P&L\n; with the\n"unexplained" component\n, of particular interest to risk managers.
Credit Risk\nmonitors the bank's debt-clients\non an ongoing basis, re both exposure\nand performance\n; while (large) exposures are initially approved by an "\ninvestment committee\n".
In the\nFront Office\n— since counterparty and funding-risks span assets, products, and desks — specialized\nXVA-desks\nare tasked with centrally\nmonitoring and managing\noverall CVA and XVA exposure and capital, typically with oversight from the appropriate Group.\n"Stress Testing" is similarly centralized.\nPerforming the above tasks — while simultaneously ensuring that computations are consistent\nover the various\nareas, products,  teams,\nand measures\n— requires that banks maintain a significant investment\nin\nsophisticated infrastructure\n,\nfinance / risk software\n, and\ndedicated staff\n. Risk software often deployed is from\nFIS\n,\nKamakura\n,\nMurex\n,\nNumerix\n(FINCAD) and\nRefinitiv\n.
Large institutions may prefer systems developed entirely\n"in house"\n- notably\nGoldman Sachs\n(\nSecDB\n),\nJP Morgan\n(Athena),\nJane Street\n(Core),\nBarclays\n(BARX),\nBofA\n(Quartz),\nCitadel\n(Apollo),\nMorgan Stanley\n(SecMaster) - 
while, more commonly, the\npricing\nlibrary\nwill be\ndeveloped internally\n, especially as this allows for currency re new products or market features.\nCommercial and retail banking\n[\nedit\n]\nRisk taxonomy\nfor retail and commercial banks\nFurther information:\nFinancial institution §\nRegulation\n, and\nVolcker Rule\nSee also:\nBank failure\nCommercial\nand\nretail banks\nare, by nature, more conservative than Investment banks, earning steady income from lending and deposits;  their focus is more on the "\nbanking book\n" than the "\ntrading book\n".
The biggest concern here - as mentioned - is the credit risk due to\nloan defaults\nfrom individuals or businesses. Liquidity risk, in this context not having enough liquid assets to meet\nwithdrawal demands\n, is also a major focus; while interest rate risk concerns the impact of interest rate changes on\nnet interest margins\n(the spread between deposit and loan rates).\nFor these banks, regulatory oversight is often tighter due to their direct impact on the financial system. Thus they are also\nhighly regulated\nunder Basel III and national banking laws, and will also be subject to regular stress testing by central banks; and all regulations above then apply (with local exceptions; e.g. an LCR "threshold" in the US\n). Additional to these, however, they must maintain high capital and liquidity ratios to\nprotect depositors\n; see\nCAMELS rating system\n.\nGiven their business model and risk appetite,\nas outlined, various differences result vs risk management at investment banks.\nBanks here maintain specific (and often additional)\ncapital buffers\nto cover potential loan losses; reflected also in the fact that retail and commercial loans usually attract\nhigher RWA results\nthan for assets typical in investment banking. See, e.g., the\nALLL\nand\nNPL\nratios.\nAt the same time, credit exposure for these banks is to significantly more clients than at investment banks. For retail banks, "\nconsumer credit risk\n" is often diversified across a vast number of borrowers, and these employ statistical models for (ongoing\n"behavioral"\n)\ncredit scoring\nand\nprobability of default\n. Commercial banks deal with mid-sized\ncorporate loans\nand\nbonds\n, and apply\naccounting-\nand\nfinancial analysis\nto determine creditworthiness; the approach differs re investment banking in that the broad client base allows for (necessitates) automation, with\nclose monitoring\non\nan exception basis\n.\nAI\n/\nML\nis increasingly employed at all stages.\nConcentration risk, relatedly, differs in its\nmanagement\n: the concern is sector concentration as opposed to "name concentration".  Here, in calculating VaR for a credit portfolio,\nbanks will incorporate a\njoint\ndefault probability for the various sectors and / or industries.\nBoth retail and commercial banks employ strict\nliquidity management\nto ensure enough cash for\ncustomer withdrawals\n: at a minimum meeting the above NSFR and LCR requirements; but also complying with their regulator's\nreserve requirement\n. See also\nliquidity at risk\n.\nBoth use\ninterest rate hedging\n(e.g., swaps) but here, in the main, to protect their profit margin against rate fluctuations, and the resultant "margin compression";\ni.e., as opposed to addressing market risk\nper se\n. Re the latter, they will often employ the abovementioned\ncash flow at risk\nand\nearnings at risk\nmodels. They also hold specific capital for\ninterest rate risk in the banking book\n,\n"IRRBB", which deals with the risks associated with a change in interest rates, including\ninterest rate gaps\n,\nbasis risk\n, yield curve risk, and\noption risk\n.\nBanks'\nEconomic Capital\nmodels, here, are focused more on\ncredit-\nand\noperational risk\n. ICAAP applies; although allows for modelling which may be simpler, and with less stringent review by regulators.\nThe Risk Management function typically exists\nindependent of operations\n- although may sit in Treasury - and reports directly to the board.\nIts scope often extends to non-financial\noperational\nand\nreputational risk\n(monitoring for any consequent\nrun on the bank\n).
Specialised software is employed here,\nboth operationally\nand\nfor risk management and modelling\n.\nCorporate finance\n[\nedit\n]\nContribution analytics\n: Profit and Loss for units sold at current fixed costs.\nThe same,\nfor varying\n(scenario-based) Revenue levels, at current Fixed and Total costs\nFurther information:\nAsset and liability management\n,\nTreasury management\n,\nStrategic financial management\n, and\nManagerial risk accounting\nSee also:\nCorporate finance §\nFinancial risk management\nIn\ncorporate finance\n, and\nfinancial management\nmore generally,\nfinancial risk management, as above, is concerned with\nbusiness risk\n-  risks to the\nbusiness’ value\n, within the context of its\nbusiness strategy\nand\ncapital structure\n.\nThe scope here - ie in non-financial firms\n- is thus broadened\n(re banking) to overlap\nenterprise risk management\n, and financial risk management then addresses risks to the firm's overall\nstrategic objectives\n, incorporating various (all) financial aspects\nof the exposures and opportunities arising from business decisions, and their link to the firm’s\nappetite for risk\n, as well as their impact on\nshare price\n.
In many organizations, risk executives\nare therefore involved\nin strategy formulation: "the choice of which risks to undertake through the allocation of its scarce resources is the key tool available to management."\nRelatedly,\nstrategic projects\nand\nmajor corporate investments\nmust first\nundergo\nthorough analysis\n, 
with approval by an\nInvestment Committee\n.\nRe the standard framework,\nthen, the discipline largely focuses on operations, i.e. business risk, as outlined.

More specifically re business-operations, management focuses on their\nbreak even dynamics\n,\nsafety margins\n,\ncontribution margin\nand\noperating leverage\n; along with the\ncorresponding monitoring\nand\nmanagement of revenue\n,\nof costs\n, and\nof other budget elements\n.  The\nDuPont analysis\nentails a "decomposition" of the firm's\nreturn on equity\n, ROE, allowing management\nto identify and address\nspecific areas of concern,\npreempting any underperformance vs\nshareholders' required return\n.\nIn larger firms, specialist\nRisk Analysts\ncomplement this work with\nmodel-based analytics\nmore broadly;\nin some cases, employing\nsophisticated stochastic models\n,\nin, for example,\nfinancing activity prediction\nproblems, and for\nrisk analysis ahead of a major investment\n.\nFirm exposure to long term market (and business) risk is a direct result of previous\ncapital investment decisions\n. Pictured is\nKaskaskia, Illinois\n, entirely submerged during the\nGreat Flood of 1993\n.\nInsurance companies\nmake profit\nthrough\nunderwriting\n— selecting which risks to insure, charging a risk-appropriate\npremium\n, and then\npaying claims\nas they occur —\nand by investing\nthe premiums they collect from insured parties.
They will, in turn, manage their own risks\nwith a focus on solvency and the ability to pay claims:\nLife Insurers\nare concerned more with longevity risk and\ninterest rate risk\n;

For\nexpected\nclaims — i.e. those covered, on average, by the\npricing model’s\nassumptions re claim\nfrequency\nand severity —\nreserves\nare set aside (\nactuarial\n, with\nstatutory reserves\nas a floor). These will cover both known claims, reported but unpaid, as well as those which are\nincurred but not reported\n(IBNR). 
To absorb\nunexpected\nlosses, insurance companies maintain a minimum level of capital plus an additional\nsolvency margin\n. Capital requirements are based on\nthe risks an insurer faces\n, such as underwriting risk, market risk, credit risk, and operational risk, and\nare governed\nby frameworks such as\nSolvency II\n(Europe) and Risk-Based Capital\n(U.S.).
To further mitigate large-scale risks — i.e. to reduce exposure to catastrophic losses — insurers transfer portions of their risk to\nReinsurers\n.  Here, analogous to VaR for banks,\ninsurers use simulations\nto estimate potential losses at various thresholds, while stress tests assess how extreme events might impact capital and reserves under various\nscenarios. 
(\nDynamic financial analysis\n(DFA) and the\nWilkie model\nare used generally in scenario analytics, and may underpin the VaR engine.)
In parallel with all these, as above, premiums collected\nare invested\nto generate returns which will supplement\nunderwriting profits\n, and the fund is then risk-managed as follows:\nALM must ensure that investments\nalign with the timing and amount\nof expected claim payouts; while returns ("float") are defended using the techniques\ndiscussed in the next section\n.
As for banks, all models are\nregularly reviewed\n,\ncomparing,\ni.a., "Actual versus Expected".\nSpecific treatments will, as outlined, differ by insurer-profile:\nLife Insurers\ndeal with long-term risks tied to\nmortality\n,\nlongevity\n, and interest rates. Policies (e.g.,\nwhole life\n,\nannuities\n) can span decades, making them sensitive to long-term economic and\ndemographic shifts\n. Reserves are large and complex due to the long duration of liabilities, with capital models emphasizing\nlongevity risk\n,\ninterest rate risk\n, and\nlapse risk\n. Stress tests, correspondingly, focus on long-term scenarios (e.g. sustained low interest rates, or a\npandemic\nrelated spike in\nmortality\n). Reinsurance is often used for\nexcess death\nclaims. ALM here is critical, and investments will be in long-term, stable assets (\nbonds as well as equities\n) to match these\nlong-duration\nliabilities.\nShort-Term Insurers\nface more volatility\nrelative to Life companies, while claims are typically resolved within a year or two (although\ntail events\n- e.g.\nasbestos litigation\n- can linger). Thus, reserves are shorter-term but must account for high uncertainty in claim frequency and severity; IBNR may be significant, especially after large events. Capital requirements focus on underwriting risk (e.g., mispricing policies) and catastrophe risk (e.g.,\nhurricanes\n,\nearthquakes\n).  Stress tests therefore emphasize short-term catastrophic scenarios, and specialized\ncatastrophe models\nare often used. Reinsurance is widely utilized to cap exposure to catastrophes; as are\nquota-share\nor\nexcess-of-loss treaties\nre single events. Rapid claims settlement reduces reserving duration compared to life insurance, and portfolios lean toward liquid, shorter-term assets (e.g.,\ncash\n,\nshort-term bonds\n).\nIn a typical insurance company, Risk Management and the\nActuarial Function\nare separate but closely related departments, each with distinct responsibilities. In smaller companies, the lines might blur, with actuaries taking on some risk management tasks, or vice versa.  Regardless, the Head Actuary (or Chief Actuary or Appointed Actuary) has specific responsibilities, typically requiring formal "sign-off": Reserve Adequacy and Solvency and Capital Assessment, as well as Reinsurance Arrangements. The relevant calculations are usually performed with specialized software — provided e.g. by\nWTW\nand\nMilliman\n— and often using\nR\nor\nSAS\n.\nInvestment management\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nPortfolio optimization\n,\nActive management\n, and\nPassive management\nSee also:\nModern portfolio theory\n,\nPost-modern portfolio theory\n, and\nFinancial economics §\nPortfolio theory\nModern portfolio theory\nsuggests a diversified portfolio of\nshares\nand other\nasset classes\n(such as debt in\ncorporate bonds\n,\ntreasury bonds\n, or\nmoney market funds\n) will realise more predictable returns. Illustrated is a typical\ndiversified fund\n, where\nasset allocation\nis between asset classes; within each, managers may further select specific securities.\nEfficient Frontier. The\nhyperbola\nis sometimes referred to as the "Markowitz bullet", and its upward sloped portion is the efficient frontier if no risk-free asset is available. With a risk-free asset, the straight\ncapital allocation line\nis the efficient frontier.\nHere maximizing return and minimizing risk such that the portfolio is\nPareto efficient\n(Pareto-optimal points in red)\nFund managers\n, classically,\ndefine the risk of a\nportfolio\nas its\nvariance\n(or\nstandard deviation\n) — the extent to which the portfolio's\nreturn is uncertain\n— and through\ndiversification\nthe\nportfolio is optimized\nso as to achieve the lowest risk for a given targeted return,\nor equivalently the highest return for a given level of risk: this approach is known as\nmean-variance optimization\n.
(The collection of these\nrisk-efficient portfolios\nform the "\nefficient frontier\n"; see\nMarkowitz model\n.) The logic here is that returns from different assets are highly unlikely to be perfectly\ncorrelated\n, and in fact the correlation may sometimes be negative. In this way, market risk particularly, and other financial risks such as\ninflation risk\n(see below) can at least partially be moderated by forms of diversification.\nA key issue, however, is that the (assumed) relationships are (implicitly) forward looking.
As observed in the\nlate-2000s recession\n, historic relationships can break down, resulting in losses to market participants believing that diversification would provide sufficient protection (in that market, including funds that had been explicitly set up to avoid being affected in this way\n).
A related issue is that\ndiversification has costs\n: as correlations are not constant it may be necessary to\nregularly\nrebalance the portfolio\n, incurring\ntransaction costs\n, negatively impacting\ninvestment performance\n;\nand as the fund manager diversifies, so this problem compounds (and a large fund may also exert\nmarket impact\n).
See\nModern portfolio theory §\nCriticisms\n.\nThe above mean-variance optimization is implemented\n(more or less) directly\nby\nasset allocation funds\n. 
At the same time - in part given the issues outlined - alternative methods for portfolio construction have been developed,\nincluding\nnew approaches to\ndefining risk\n, and to the\noptimization itself\n.\nNotably, managers will employ\nfactor models\n— generically\nAPT\n— using\ntime series\nregression\nto design portfolios\nwith\nthe desired exposure\nto macroeconomic, market and / or fundamental\nrisk factors\n;\nrespectively: macro-,\nfactor-\n, and\nstyle\nportfolios.
The optimization, under both the mean-variance and\nfactor model approaches, may be with respect to\n(tail)\nrisk parity\n, focusing on allocation of risk, rather than allocation of capital, and employ, e.g. the\nBlack–Litterman model\nwhich modifies the above "Markowitz optimization", to incorporate the  "views" of the portfolio manager.\nAlongside these,\nDiscretionary investment management\nfunds,\ninstead, lean heavily on traditional "\nstock picking\n", employing\nfundamental analysis\nin preference to advanced\nmathematical approaches. 
(These Managers are then\nthe major consumers\nof\nsecurities research\n.)
The specific concerns will, in turn, differ\nas a function of the Manager's\ninvestment philosophy\nand\nactive strategy\n, preferring, e.g.,\nvalue-\n,\ngrowth-\nor\ndefensive stocks\nwithin her fund. 
Portfolios here are managed, also, using qualitative and subjective considerations, which include evaluations\nof company management\n,\nindustry dynamics\n, and\nmacro/political factors\n.
As discussed below, Risk Management here will, correspondingly, be largely pragmatic and heuristic, as opposed to quantitative.\nAn important requirement, regardless of approach, is that the Manager must ensure\nthat the portfolio's risk level matches\nthe investor's objectives\nand comfort zone, i.e. must ensure\nrisk tolerance\nalignment.  Correspondingly, the fund's (\nadvertised\n)\ninvestment strategy\nwill, almost necessarily, define its own risk tolerance and\nappetite\n, and hence selection and application of optimization-criteria and risk management techniques. Here, for both individuals and Funds, generally, longer time horizons allow for greater tolerance of short-term volatility, while shorter horizons require more conservative strategies.  A further generalization: portfolios constructed using mathematical-approaches are more exposed to market risk and the\nstock market cycle\n; while those constructed by\nstock picking\nare exposed, more, to\nfirm\nand sector specific risks.\nIn measuring risk quantitatively, the Manager will employ a variety\nof\nfinancial risk modeling\ntechniques — including\nvalue at risk\n,\nhistorical simulation\n,\nstress tests\n, and\nextreme value theory\n— to analyze the portfolio and to forecast the likely losses incurred for a selection of exposures and scenarios
(see\n§ Investment banking\nfor discussion).\nGuided by the analytics, and / or the above considerations, fund managers (and\ntraders\n) will implement specific\nrisk hedging\ntechniques and\nstrategies\n.\nAs appropriate, these are applied to the portfolio as a whole ("top-down") or to individual holdings  ("bottom-up"):\nTo protect the overall portfolio, fund managers\nmay sell\nthe\nstock market index future\nor\nbuy puts\non the\nstock market index option\n;\nthe respective sensitivities,\nportfolio beta\nand\noption delta\n, determine the number of hedge-contracts required.\nFor both, the logic is that the (diversified) portfolio is likely highly correlated with the\nstock index\nit is part of: thus if the portfolio-value declines, the index will have declined likewise with the derivative holder profiting correspondingly.\nFund managers may (instead) engage in "\nportfolio insurance\n", a dynamic hedging process that involves selling index futures during periods of decline and using the proceeds to offset portfolio losses.\nFund managers, or traders, may also wish to\nhedge a specific stock's price\n. Here, they may likewise\nbuy a single-stock put\n,\nor sell\na\nsingle-stock future\n. Alternative strategies may rely on assumed relationships between related stocks, employing, for example, a\n"long/short" strategy\n.\nBond portfolios\n, when e.g. a component of an\nasset-allocation fund\nor other\ndiversified portfolio\n, are typically managed similar to equity above: the fund manager will hedge her bond allocation with\nbond index\nfutures or options; with the number of contracts, a function of\nduration\n.\nManagers may also employ\nduration management\nmore generally to\n"immunize"\nthe fund against undesired\ninterest rate risk\n(often guided by a "\nDV01\ntarget"), or against failing to meet a target return objective (e.g. a\ntarget date fund\nhas an implicit liability to its investors). See below re liquidity management; also\nliability-driven investment strategy\n,\nduration gap\n,\nladdering\n.\nFor individual\nbonds\nand other\nfixed income securities\n,  specific\ncredit and interest rate risks\ncan be hedged\nusing\ninterest rate-\nand\ncredit derivatives\n(although\nwith care\n, under multi-curves\n). Sensitivities\nre interest rates\nare measured using\nduration\nand\nconvexity\nfor bonds, and\nDV01\nand\nkey rate durations\ngenerally, and an offsetting derivative-position is purchased. For\ncredit risk\n,\nsensitivities are measured via\nCS01\n, while analysts use models such as\nJarrow–Turnbull\nand\nKMV\nto estimate the (\nrisk neutral\n)\nprobability of default\n, hedging where appropriate, usually\nvia credit default swaps\n. Probabilities (\nactuarial\n) may also be obtained from\nBond credit ratings\n; then, often at a portfolio level — e.g. for credit-VaR — analysts will use a\ntransition matrix\nof these\nto estimate the probability\nand impact\nof a "credit migration",\naggregating the bond-by-bond result. Interest rate- and credit risk together, may be hedged via a\nTotal return swap\n. The\nKelly criterion\nwill suggest\n- i.e. limit - the size of a position that an investor should hold in her portfolio.\nRoy's safety-first criterion\nminimizes the probability\nof the portfolio's return falling below a minimum desired threshold.\nChance-constrained portfolio selection\nsimilarly seeks to ensure that the probability of final wealth falling below a given "safety level" is acceptable.\nManagers likewise employ the abovementioned\nfactor models\non an ongoing basis to measure exposure to the relevant\nrisk factors\n.\nAhead of an anticipated movement in any of these, the Manager may then,\nas indicated\n, reduce holdings, hedge, or purchase offsetting exposure. 
Thus a factor-based fund may "tilt" from\nmomentum\nto\nvalue\n, a style-based fund from\ncyclical\nto\ndefensive\n.
