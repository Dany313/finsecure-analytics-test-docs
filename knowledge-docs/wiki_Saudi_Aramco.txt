Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Aramco\n==================================================\n\nSaudi Arabian state-owned petroleum and oil-trade company\nNot to be confused with\nAAMCO\nor\nAmoco\n.\nSaudi Aramco\n(\nArabic\n:\nأرامكو السعودية\nʾArāmkū as-Suʿūdiyyah\n), officially the\nSaudi Arabian Oil Company\n, is a majority state-owned\npetroleum\nand\nnatural gas\ncompany, and the\nnational oil company\nof\nSaudi Arabia\n.\nAs of 2024\n, it is the fourth-\nlargest company in the world by revenue\nand is headquartered in\nDhahran\n. Saudi Aramco has both the world's largest proven crude\noil reserves\n, at more than\n270\nbillion barrels\n(43\nbillion cubic metres)\n,\nand largest daily oil production of all oil-producing companies.\nSaudi Aramco operates the world's largest single\nhydrocarbon\nnetwork, the\nMaster Gas System\n. In 2024, its oil production total was 12.7 million barrels of oil equivalent per day, and it manages over one hundred oil and gas fields in Saudi Arabia, including 288.4\ntrillion\nstandard cubic feet (scf)\nof\nnatural gas\nreserves. Along the\nEastern Province\n, Saudi Aramco most notably operates the\nGhawar Field\n(the world's largest\nonshore oil field\n) and the\nSafaniya Field\n(the world's largest\noffshore oil field\n).\nOn 11 December 2019, the company's shares commenced trading on the\nSaudi Exchange\n. The shares rose to 35.2\nSaudi riyals\n, giving it a\nmarket capitalisation\nof about US$1.88 trillion,\nand surpassed the US$2 trillion mark on the second day of trading.\nAs of November 2025 the stock trades at 25.8 SAR, giving it a market capitalisation of approximately US$1.66 trillion. This represents an US$800bn fall in value since its peak in 2022\nThis represents an US$800bn fall in value since 2022, one of the largest decreases in corporate history.\nIn 2024, Saudi Aramco was responsible for more CO\n2\nemissions than any other entity, taking into account\nScope 3\nemissions from use of the oil it sells; it was responsible for 1,653 Mt CO2 emissions, or 4.28% of global CO2 emissions.\nHistory\n[\nedit source\n]\nDammam No. 7\nstruck oil in 1938, becoming the first commercial oil well in\nSaudi Arabia\nSaudi Aramco's origins trace to the oil shortages of\nWorld War I\nand the exclusion of American companies from\nMesopotamia\nby the United Kingdom and France under the\nSan Remo Petroleum Agreement\nof 1920.\nThe US administration had popular support for an "\nOpen Door policy\n", which\nHerbert Hoover\n, secretary of commerce, initiated in 1921.\nStandard Oil of California\n(SoCal) was among those US companies seeking new sources of oil from abroad.\nIn 1932 the\nBahrain Petroleum Company\n, a wholly owned SoCal subsidiary, discovered the first oil field in the Persian Gulf outside of Iran.\nThe Saudi Arabian government granted a concession to SoCal in preference to a rival bid from the\nIraq Petroleum Company\n.\nThe concession allowed SoCal to explore for oil in Saudi Arabia. SoCal assigned this concession to a wholly owned\nsubsidiary\n, California-Arabian Standard Oil Company (CASOC). In 1936, with the company having had no success at locating oil, the\nTexas Company\n(Texaco) purchased a 50% stake of the concession.\nAfter four years of fruitless exploration, the first success came with the seventh drill site in\nDhahran\nin 1938, a well referred to as\nDammam No. 7\n.\nThis well immediately produced over\n1,500 barrels per day (240\nm\n/d)\n, giving the company confidence to continue. On 31 January 1944, the company name was changed from California-Arabian Standard Oil Co. to Arabian American Oil Co. (or Aramco).\nOn 17 March 1947,\nStandard Oil of New Jersey\n(later known as Exxon) purchased 30% and\nSocony Vacuum\n(later Mobil) purchased 10% of the company, with SoCal and Texaco retaining 30% each.\nThe newcomers were also shareholders in the Iraq Petroleum Co. and had to get the restrictions of the\nRed Line Agreement\nlifted in order to be free to enter into this arrangement.\nAramco compound in Saudi Arabia, 1954\nIn 1949, Aramco had made incursions into the\nEmirate of Abu Dhabi\n, leading to a\nborder dispute\nbetween Abu Dhabi and Saudi Arabia.\nIn 1950,\nKing Abdulaziz\nthreatened to\nnationalise\nhis country's oil facilities, thus pressuring Aramco to agree to share profits 50/50.\nA similar process had taken place with American oil companies in\nVenezuela\na few years earlier. The American government granted US Aramco member companies a\ntax break\nknown as the\ngolden gimmick\nequivalent to the profits given to King Abdulaziz. In the wake of the new arrangement, the company's headquarters were moved from New York to Dhahran.\nIn 1951, the company discovered the\nSafaniya Oil Field\n, the world's largest offshore field. In 1957, the discovery of smaller connected oil fields confirmed the\nGhawar Field\nas the world's largest onshore field.\nIn 1975, the Saudi Arabia second five-year economic plan included a Master Gas Plan. Natural gas would be used to generate power, rather than\nflaring the gas\n. The plan counted on using the\nassociated gas\n, but by 1985, Aramco was able to include a billion\nstandard cubic foot\nper day (Bscfd) of non-associated gas. This non-associated gas was produced from the Khuff\nFormation\n, which is a\nlimestone\nlayer\n650 metres (2,130\nft)\nbelow the oil-producing Arab Zone. In 1994, Aramco discovered more non-associated gas in the deeper Jawf\nsandstone\nformation and built plants in Hawiyah and\nHaradh\nto process it. This increased the capacity of the Master Gas System to 9.4 billion scfd.\nYom Kippur War\n[\nedit source\n]\nIn 1973, following US support for Israel during the\nYom Kippur War\n, the Saudi Arabian government acquired a one-quarter "participation interest" in Aramco's assets, then increased it to sixty percent in 1974.\nAramco continued to operate and manage the former Aramco assets, including its concessionary interest in certain Saudi Arab oil fields, on behalf of the Saudi Arab Government until 1988. In November 1988, a\nroyal decree\ncreated a new Saudi Arab company, the Saudi Arabian Oil Company, to take control of the former Aramco assets (or Saudi Aramco)\nand took the management and operations control of Saudi Arabia's oil and gas fields from Aramco and its partners. In 1989–90, high-quality oil and gas were discovered in three areas south of Riyadh.\nPersian Gulf War\n[\nedit source\n]\nIn September 1990, after the start of the\nPersian Gulf War\n, Aramco was expected to replace much of the oil production removed from the global market due to the embargo of Iraq and occupied Kuwait. This amounted to producing an extra 4.8 million barrels per day (Mbpd) to keep the global oil market stable. In addition, Aramco was expected to provide all of the\ncoalition\naviation and diesel needs. Aramco recommissioned 146 Harmaliyah,\nKhurais\n, and Ghawar oil wells with associated\ngas oil separation plants\n, and saltwater treatment pipeline, that had been\nmothballed\nduring the 1980s oil price collapse. Daily production increased from 5.4 Mbpd in July to 8.5 Mbpd in December 1990 after a three-month de-mothball effort.\nStarting in 1990, Aramco embarked on an expansion of crude oil sales in the Asian market. Agreements with\nSouth Korea\n, the\nPhilippines\n, and China resulted. By 2016, about 70% of Aramco's crude oil sales were to Asia.\n2000s\n[\nedit source\n]\nIn May 2001, Saudi Arabia announced the Gas Initiative, which proposed forming three joint ventures with eight\nIOCs\nfor gas exploration on pure upstream acreage. It reads, "The enterprise under this contract shall be directed and supervised by Americans who shall employ Saudi nationals as far as practicable, and in so far as the company can find suitable Saudi employees it will not employ other nationals." The first company school was started in May 1940 in the Al-Khobar home of Hijji bin Jassim, company interpreter, translator and first instructor. Al-Naimi pointed out, "From the beginning, the development of Aramco was directly tied to the betterment of Saudi Arabia." Another school was located in Dhahran in 1941, and was called the Jebel School. Boys hired into entry-level positions attended at 7\nAM for four hours, followed by four hours of work in the afternoon. In 1950, Aramco built schools for 2,400 students. In 1959, Aramco sent the first group of Saudi students to college in the States. In 1970, Aramco started hiring its first high school graduates, and in 1979 started offering college scholarships. In 1965, Zafer H. Husseini was named the first Saudi manager and in 1974, Faisal Al-Bassam was named the first Saudi vice president. One of the early students was Al-Naimi, who was named the first Saudi president of Aramco in November 1983. As Al-Naimi states, "The oil company committed itself to developing qualified Saudis to become fully educated and trained industry professionals." Al-Naimi acknowledged\nThomas Barger\n's championing of Saudization, "You, of all of Aramco's leaders, had the greatest vision when you supported the training effort of Saudi Arab employees during its early days. That visionary support and effort is bearing fruit now and many executive positions are filled by Saudis because of that effort." In 1943, 1,600 Saudis were employed at Aramco, but by 1987, nearly two-thirds of Aramco's 43,500 strong workforce were Saudis. In 1988, Al-Naimi became\nCEO\nand\nHisham Nazer\nbecame\nchairman\n, the first Saudis to hold those positions. The "pinnacle of Saudization" occurred when the\nShaybah\noil field came on line in July 1998, after a three-year effort by a team consisting of 90% Saudis. The Aramco of 2016 still maintained an\nexpatriate\nworkforce of about 15%, so Aramco can, in the words of Al-Naimi, "be sure it is getting access to the latest innovations and technical expertise."\nSaudi Aramco has emitted 59.26 billion tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent since it began operations, accounting for 4.38% of worldwide anthropogenic\nCO\n2\nemissions\nsince 1965.\nIn a letter sent to nine international banks reportedly hired by Aramco to assist it in arranging its US$2 trillion market debut, ten environmental groups warned about the listing causing a highly possible hindrance in the effort to reduce\ngreenhouse gas emissions\nand end\nhuman rights\nabuses committed by the Saudi regime.\nOn 6 November 2019, Saudi Aramco joined the\nWorld Bank\n's initiative to reduce\ngas flaring\nto zero by 2030. The firm reported flaring of less than 1% of its total raw gas production in the first half of 2019.\nGreenhouse gas emissions\n[\nedit source\n]\nAccording to environmentalists, Aramco is responsible for more than 4% of global\ngreenhouse gas emissions\nsince 1965. Most of this is from the use of the oil they sold, for example burning gasoline in car engines. In\ngreenhouse gas accounting\nsuch emissions from use of a product are called "scope 3" emissions. Aramco has no plan to limit scope 3 emissions. However the government says it will have\nnet zero\ncarbon emissions by 2060 within the country.\nIn October 2023, Saudi Aramco announced a\ndirect air capture\npilot programme in partnership with\nSiemens Energy\nto be completed in 2024.\nOn 9 December, 2024, Aramco along with\nSLB\nand\nLinde\nsigned an agreement to build a carbon capture and storage project in Jubail, Saudi Arabia. Aramco will be owning 60% of the project while SLB and Linde will hold 20% each.\nThe first phase has been estimated to be completed by the end of 2027, while capturing and storing up to 9 million metric tons of carbon dioxide each year,\nor about one half of one percent of its scope 3 emissions.\nControversies\n[\nedit source\n]\n2007 Haradh gas pipeline explosion\n[\nedit source\n]\nMain article:\n2007 Haradh, Saudi Arabia gas pipeline explosion\nOn 18 November 2007, reports came out that a natural gas pipeline explosion had taken the lives of several workers, the death toll was later determined to be 34.\nAramco asserts it was a purely maintenance-related incident.\nOrganisational culture\n[\nedit source\n]\nOn 9 December 2020, the\nFinancial Times\npublished an article about an engineer, whose family claims that Saudi Aramco was negligent in handling his COVID-19 infection.\nAccording to his family, he had been asking the company and authorities for help, three weeks before his death, but was simply asked to keep his gloves and mask on. Saudi Aramco did not formally contact his family until approximately 14 hours after his death, refused to release his body, and allegedly erased information from his mobile phone. Since 2016, Saudi Arabia has spent around $140 million on\nlobbying to influence public opinion\nand policies in the US. In an effort to keep gasoline cars competitive, Saudi Aramco is working on a device which would trap some of the carbon dioxide upon attaching it to cars running on gasoline.
