Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mastercard\n==================================================\n\nAmerican multinational financial services corporation\nMastercard Inc.\n(stylized as\nMasterCard\nfrom 1979 to 2016 and as\nmastercard\nfrom 2016) is an American\nmultinational\npayment card services\ncorporation headquartered in\nPurchase, New York\n.\nIt offers a range of payment transaction processing and other related-payment services (such as travel-related payments and bookings). Throughout the world, its principal business is to process payments between the banks of merchants and the card-issuing banks or credit unions of the purchasers who use the Mastercard-brand\ndebit\n,\ncredit\nand\nprepaid\ncards to make purchases. Mastercard has been\npublicly traded\nsince 2006.\nMastercard (originally\nInterbank\n, then\nMaster Charge\n)\nwas created by an alliance of several banks and regional bankcard associations in response to the BankAmericard issued by\nBank of America\n, which later became\nVisa\nand is still its biggest competitor. Along with Visa, Mastercard has faced numerous antitrust lawsuits. Prior to its\ninitial public offering\n, Mastercard Worldwide was a\ncooperative\nowned by the more than 25,000\nfinancial institutions\nthat issue its branded cards.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nAlthough BankAmericard's debut in September 1958 was a failure,\nit began to turn a profit by May 1961.\nBank of America deliberately kept this information secret and allowed then-widespread negative impressions to linger in order to ward off\ncompetition\n.\nThis strategy was successful until 1966, when BankAmericard's profitability had become far too big to hide.\nFrom 1960 to 1966, there were only 10 new credit cards introduced in the United States, but from 1966 to 1968, approximately 440 credit cards were introduced by banks large and small throughout the country.\nThese newcomers promptly banded together into regional bankcard associations.\nOne reason why most banks chose to join forces was that at the time, 16 states limited the ability of banks to operate through branch locations, while 15 states entirely prohibited branch banking and required unit banking.\nA unit bank can legally operate only at a single site and is thereby forced to remain very small.\nBy joining a regional bankcard association, a unit bank could quickly add a credit card to its lineup of financial products, and achieve\neconomies of scale\nby outsourcing tedious\nback office\ntasks like card servicing to the association.\nSuch associations also enabled unit banks to aggregate their customer bases and merchant networks in order to make a credit card useful for both customers and merchants; early credit cards had failed because they could only be used within a small radius around their respective issuing banks.\nIn 1966, Karl H. Hinke, an executive vice president at\nMarine Midland Bank\n, asked representatives of several other banks to meet him in\nBuffalo, New York\n.\nMarine Midland had just launched its own regional bankcard in the\nUpstate New York\nmarket after Bank of America declined its request for a BankAmericard regional license because Marine Midland was too big.\nThe result of the Buffalo meeting was that several banks and regional bankcard associations soon agreed to join forces as Interbankard, Inc.,\nwhich then became the Interbank Card Association (ICA).\nBy the end of 1967, ICA had 150 members and Hinke became ICA's chairman.\nBank of America eventually joined Mastercard as well.\n(In the 21st century, Bank of America would revive the BankAmericard brand name as a Mastercard credit card, which it remains today).\nThe Interbank branding in 1966 initially consisted only of a small unobtrusive lowercase\ni\ninside a circle in the lower right-hand corner of the front of each Interbank card; the rest of the card design was the prerogative of each issuing bank.\nThis tiny logo proved to be entirely unsatisfactory for creating nationwide\nbrand awareness\nin order to compete against the established leader, BankAmericard.\nIn 1969, Interbank developed a new national brand, "Master Charge: The Interbank Card" by combining the two overlapping yellow and orange circles of the Western States Bankcard Association with the "Master Charge" name coined by the First National Bank of Louisville, Kentucky.\nAlso in 1969,\nFirst National City Bank\njoined Interbank and merged its proprietary\nEverything Card\nprogram with Master Charge. Earlier in 1968, the ICA and\nEurocard\nstarted a strategic alliance, which effectively allowed the ICA access to the European market, and for Eurocard to be accepted on the ICA network.
