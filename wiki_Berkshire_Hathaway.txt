Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway\n==================================================\n\nAmerican multinational conglomerate holding company\nBuffett (second from left) and Munger (at right) in 1998\nBerkshire Hathaway Inc.\n(\n/\nˈ\nb\nɜːr\nk\nʃ\nər\n/\n) is an American\nmultinational\nconglomerate\nholding company\nheadquartered in\nOmaha, Nebraska\n. Originally a textile manufacturer, the company transitioned into a\nconglomerate\nstarting in 1965 under the management of chairman and CEO\nWarren Buffett\n(from 1970 to 2025) and vice chairman\nCharlie Munger\n(from 1978 to 2023).\nGreg Abel\nsucceeded Buffett as CEO at the beginning of 2026.\nBuffett personally owns 38.4% of the Class A voting shares of Berkshire Hathaway, representing a 15.1% overall economic interest in the company.\nThe company is often compared to an\ninvestment fund\n; between 1965, when Buffett gained control of the company, and 2023, the company's shareholder returns amounted to a\ncompound annual growth rate\n(CAGR) of 19.8% compared to a 10.2% CAGR for the\nS&P 500\n.\nFrom 1965 to 2023, the stock price had negative performance in only eleven years.\nIn August 2024, Berkshire Hathaway became the eighth largest U.S.\npublic company\nand the first non-technology company to be valued at over $1 trillion.\nBerkshire Hathaway is ranked 5th on the\nFortune\n500\nand 9th on the\nFortune\nGlobal 500\n.\nIn surveys taken in the 2000s and 2010s by\nBarron's\n, Berkshire was named among the "Most Respected" companies by professional investors.\nBerkshire is one of the ten largest components of the\nS&P 500\nand is on the\nlist of largest employers in the United States\n. Its\nclass A shares\nhave the highest per-share price of any public company in the world, reaching $700,000 in August 2024, because the\nboard of directors\nhas historically been opposed to\nstock splits\n.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nBerkshire Hathaway originated as a textile manufacturer formed in 1955 from the merger of\nHathaway Manufacturing Company\nand\nBerkshire Fine Spinning Associates\n. Berkshire Associates took its name from\nBerkshire County, Massachusetts\n, where it was based, and Hathaway Manufacturing was named for its founder,\nHoratio Hathaway\n.\nAfter the merger, Berkshire Hathaway had 15 plants employing over 12,000 workers with over $120\nmillion in revenue, and was headquartered in\nNew Bedford\n.\nHowever, seven of those locations were closed by the end of the 1950s, accompanied by large layoffs.\nIn 1962, Warren Buffett began buying Berkshire Hathaway stock for his\ninvestment fund\n, Buffett Partnership Ltd., at $7.50 per share, anticipating that as the company liquidated textile mills there would come a\ntender offer\nwhen he could sell the shares at a profit. A year later, Buffett's fund owned 7% of the company.\nIn 1964, Buffett offered to sell his shares back to the company for $11.50 each.\nSeabury Stanton\n, the manager of Berkshire Hathaway, told Buffett orally that he had a deal.\nA few weeks later, Warren Buffett received the tender offer in writing, but the tender offer was for only $11.375 per share. Buffett later admitted that this lower, undercutting offer made him angry.\nInstead of selling at the slightly lower price, Buffett bought more of the stock at an even higher price to take control of the company and fire Stanton; Stanton and his son resigned in 1965.\nHowever, this left Buffett's fund with a major interest in a declining textile business.\nBuffett has described buying the Berkshire Hathaway textile company as the biggest investment mistake he had ever made, denying him compounded investment returns of about $200\nbillion over the subsequent 45 years.\nHe has estimated that had he invested the same money directly in insurance businesses instead of indirectly via Berkshire Hathaway (due to what he perceived as a slight by an individual), it would have paid off several hundredfold.\nIn 1985, the company's last textile operations were shut down.\nAlso in 1962, Warren Buffett set up his office in Omaha, Nebraska, at the current Berkshire Hathaway headquarters located at\nBlackstone Plaza\n(formerly named Kiewit Plaza).\nAs of the end of 2024, the company had only 27 employees at that corporate headquarters.\nIn 1963,\nFranklin Otis Booth Jr.\ninvested $1 million in the company (\nequivalent to $10,500,000\nin 2025\n), and in 1968\nDavid Gottesman\nalso invested in the company. The increased\nmarket liquidity\nresulted in the company's inclusion in the\nS&P 500\n.\nShare price milestones\n[\nedit\n]\nShares closed over $1,000 for the first time on August 26, 1983, and over $10,000 on October 16, 1992.\nShares closed over $100,000 for the first time on October 23, 2006.\nThey first closed over $200,000 on August 14, 2014.\nShares closed over $500,000 for the first time on March 16, 2022.\nExecutive compensation\n[\nedit\n]\nThe salary for Buffett is $100,000 per year with no stock options,\nwhich is among the lowest salaries\nfor CEOs of large companies in the United States.\nBuffett's salary has not changed in 35 years.\nBuffett also receives approximately $300,000 worth of home security services from the company annually.\nAbel and Jain each receive a salary of $20 million per year, with a possible $3 million annual bonus at the discretion of Buffett.\nAnnual letters and shareholders' meetings\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nBerkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting\nBerkshire routinely features Coca-Cola products, from one of its oldest and largest investments, at its corporate events.\nBuffett's letters to shareholders are published annually.\nBerkshire's\nannual shareholders' meetings\n, nicknamed "\nWoodstock\nfor Capitalists", take place at the\nCHI Health Center Omaha\nin Omaha, Nebraska.
