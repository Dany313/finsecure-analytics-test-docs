Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hedge_fund\n==================================================\n\nPrivately pooled investment fund using diverse strategies to seek high returns\nA\nhedge fund\nis a\npooled\ninvestment fund\nthat holds\nliquid\nassets and that makes use of complex\ntrading\nand\nrisk management\ntechniques to aim to improve investment performance and insulate returns from\nmarket risk\n. Among these\nportfolio techniques\nare\nshort selling\nand the use of\nleverage\nand\nderivative instruments\n.\nIn the\nUnited States\n,\nfinancial regulations\nrequire that hedge funds be marketed only to\ninstitutional investors\nand\nhigh-net-worth individuals\n.\nHedge funds are considered\nalternative investments\n. Their ability to use\nleverage\nand more complex investment techniques distinguishes them from regulated investment funds available to the retail market, commonly known as\nmutual funds\nand\nexchange-traded funds\n(ETFs). They are also considered distinct from\nprivate equity funds\nand other similar\nclosed-end funds\nas hedge funds generally invest in relatively\nliquid assets\nand are usually\nopen-ended\n. This means they typically allow investors to invest and withdraw capital periodically based on the fund's\nnet asset value\n, whereas private-equity funds generally invest in\nilliquid assets\nand return capital only after a number of years.\nOther than a fund's regulatory status, there are no formal or fixed definitions of fund types, and so there are different views of what can constitute a "hedge fund".\nAlthough hedge funds are not subject to the many restrictions applicable to regulated funds, regulations were passed in the United States and\nEurope\nfollowing the\n2008 financial crisis\nwith the intention of increasing government oversight of hedge funds and eliminating certain regulatory gaps.\nWhile most modern hedge funds are able to employ a wide variety of\nfinancial instruments\nand risk management techniques,\nthey can be very different from each other with respect to their strategies, risks, volatility and expected return profile. It is common for hedge fund investment strategies to aim to achieve a positive\nreturn on investment\nregardless of whether markets are rising or falling ("\nabsolute return\n"). Hedge funds can be considered risky investments; the expected returns of some hedge fund strategies are less volatile than those of retail funds with high exposure to stock markets because of the use of hedging techniques. Research in 2015 showed that hedge fund activism can have significant real effects on target firms, including improvements in productivity and efficient reallocation of corporate assets. Moreover, these interventions often lead to increased labor productivity, although the benefits may not fully accrue to workers in terms of increased wages or work hours.\nA hedge fund usually pays its\ninvestment manager\na management fee (typically, 2% per annum of the net asset value of the fund) and a\nperformance fee\n(typically, 20% of the increase in the fund's net asset value during a year).\nHedge funds have existed for many decades and have become increasingly popular. They have now grown to be a substantial portion of the\nasset management\nindustry,\nwith assets totaling around $3.8 trillion as of 2021.\nEtymology\n[\nedit\n]\nThe word "hedge", meaning a line of bushes around the perimeter of a field, has long been used as a metaphor for placing limits on risk.\nEarly hedge funds sought to hedge specific investments against general market fluctuations by\nshorting\nother, similar assets.\nNowadays, however, many different investment strategies are used, many of which do not "hedge" risk.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nDuring the US\nbull market\nof the 1920s, there were numerous private\ninvestment vehicles\navailable to wealthy investors. Of that period, the best known today is the Graham-Newman Partnership, founded by\nBenjamin Graham\nand his long-time business partner Jerry Newman.\nThis was cited by\nWarren Buffett\nin a 2006 letter to the\nMuseum of American Finance\nas an early hedge fund,\nand based on other comments from Buffett, Janet Tavakoli deems Graham's\ninvestment firm\nthe first hedge fund.\nThe sociologist\nAlfred W. Jones\nis credited with coining the phrase "\nhedged\nfund"\nand is credited with creating the first hedge fund structure in 1949.\nJones referred to his fund as being "hedged", a term then commonly used on\nWall Street\nto describe the management of\ninvestment risk\ndue to changes in the financial markets.\nJones also developed the popular 2-and-20 structure of hedge funds, in which hedge funds charged investors a management fee of 2% on total assets and a 20% fee on realized gains.\nIn the 1970s, hedge funds specialized in a single strategy with most fund managers following the\nlong/short equity\nmodel. Many hedge funds closed during the\nrecession of 1969–1970\nand the\n1973–1974 stock market crash\ndue to heavy losses. They received renewed attention in the late 1980s.\nCumulative hedge fund and other risk asset returns (1997–2012)\nDuring the 1990s, the number of hedge funds increased significantly with the\n1990s stock market rise\n,\nthe aligned-interest compensation structure (\ni.e.\n, common financial interests), and the promise of above average returns\nas likely causes. Over the next decade, hedge fund strategies expanded to include credit arbitrage,\ndistressed debt\n,\nfixed income\n,\nquantitative\n, and multi-strategy.\nUS\ninstitutional investors\n, such as pension and\nendowment funds\n, began allocating greater portions of their\nportfolios\nto hedge funds.\nDuring the first decade of the 21st century, hedge funds gained popularity worldwide, and, by 2008, the worldwide hedge fund industry held an estimated US$1.93\ntrillion in\nassets under management\n(AUM).\nHowever, the\n2008 financial crisis\ncaused many hedge funds to restrict investor withdrawals and their popularity and AUM totals declined.\nAUM totals rebounded and in April 2011 were estimated at almost $2 trillion.\nAs of February\n2011\n, 61% of worldwide investment in hedge funds came from institutional sources.\nIn June 2011, the hedge fund management firms with the greatest AUM were\nBridgewater Associates\n(US$58.9\nbillion),\nMan Group\n(US$39.2\nbillion),\nPaulson & Co.\n(US$35.1\nbillion),\nBrevan Howard\n(US$31\nbillion), and\nOch-Ziff\n(US$29.4\nbillion).\nBridgewater Associates had $70 billion in assets under management\nas of March\n2012\n.\nAt the end of that year, the 241 largest hedge fund firms in the United States collectively held $1.335 trillion.\nIn April 2012, the hedge fund industry reached a record high of US$2.13 trillion total assets under management.\nIn the middle of the 2010s, the hedge fund industry experienced a general decline in the "old guard" fund managers. Dan Loeb called it a "hedge fund killing field" due to the classic long/short falling out of favor because of unprecedented easing by\ncentral banks\n. The US\nstock market\ncorrelation became untenable to\nshort sellers\n.\nThe hedge fund industry today has reached a state of maturity that is consolidating around the larger, more established firms such as Citadel, Elliot, Millennium, Bridgewater, and others. The rate of new fund start ups is now outpaced by fund closings.\nIn July 2017, hedge funds recorded their eighth consecutive monthly gain in returns with assets under management rising to a record $3.1 trillion.\nNotable hedge fund managers\n[\nedit\n]\nTom Steyer\n, hedge-fund manager of\nFarallon Capital\n.\nGeorge Soros\n, fund manager of\nQuantum Group of Funds\nRay Dalio\n, fund manager of\nBridgewater Associates\nJohn Meriwether\nof\nLong-Term Capital Management\n, most successful returns from 27% to 59% through 1993 to 1998 until its collapse and liquidation.\nGeorge Soros\nof\nQuantum Group of Funds\nRay Dalio\nof\nBridgewater Associates\n, the world's largest hedge fund firm with US$160 billion in\nassets under management\nas of 2017\nSteve Cohen\nof\nPoint72 Asset Management\n, formerly known as SAC Capital Advisors\nJohn Paulson\nof\nPaulson & Co.\n, whose hedge funds as of December 2015 had $19 billion assets under management\nDavid Tepper\nof\nAppaloosa Management\nPaul Tudor Jones\nof\nTudor Investment Corporation\nDaniel Och\nof\nOch-Ziff Capital Management Group\nwith more than $40 billion in assets under management in 2013\nIsrael Englander\nof\nMillennium Management, LLC\nLeon Cooperman\nof Omega Advisors\nMichael Platt\nof\nBlueCrest Capital Management\n(UK), Europe's third-largest hedge-fund firm\nJames Dinan\nof\nYork Capital Management\nStephen Mandel\nof\nLone Pine Capital\nwith $26.7 billion under management as of June 2015\nLarry Robbins\nof Glenview Capital Management with $9.2 billion of assets under management as of July 2014\nGlenn Dubin\nof\nHighbridge Capital Management\nPaul Singer\nof\nElliott Management Corporation\n, an activist hedge fund with more than US$23 billion in assets under management in 2013,\nand a portfolio worth $8.1 billion as of the first quarter of 2015\nDavid E. A fund may employ a single strategy or multiple strategies for flexibility,\nrisk management\n, or diversification.\nThe hedge fund's\nprospectus\n, also known as an\noffering memorandum\n, offers potential investors information about key aspects of the fund, including the fund's investment strategy, investment type, and\nleverage\nlimit.\nThe elements contributing to a hedge fund strategy include the hedge fund's approach to the market, the particular instrument use, the\nmarket sector\nthe fund specializes in (\ne.g.\n, healthcare), the method used to select investments, and the amount of diversification within the fund. There are a variety of market approaches to different\nasset classes\n, including\nequity\n,\nfixed income\n,\ncommodity\n, and\ncurrency\n. Instruments used include equities, fixed income,\nfutures\n,\noptions\n, and\nswaps\n. Strategies can be divided into those in which investments can be selected by managers, known as "discretionary/qualitative", or those in which investments are selected using a computerized system, known as "systematic/quantitative".\nThe amount of diversification within the fund can vary; funds may be multi-strategy, multi-fund, multi-market, multi-manager, or a combination.\nSometimes hedge fund strategies are described as "\nabsolute return\n" and are classified as either "\nmarket neutral\n" or "directional". Market neutral funds have less correlation to overall market performance by "neutralizing" the effect of market swings whereas directional funds utilize trends and inconsistencies in the market and have greater exposure to the market's fluctuations.\nGlobal macro\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nGlobal macro\nHedge funds using a global macro investing strategy take large\npositions\nin share, bond, or currency markets in anticipation of global\nmacroeconomic events\nin order to generate a\nrisk-adjusted return\n.\nGlobal macro fund managers use macroeconomic ("big picture") analysis based on global market events and trends to identify opportunities for investment that would profit from anticipated price movements. While global macro strategies have a large amount of flexibility (due to their ability to use leverage to take large positions in diverse investments in multiple markets), the timing of the implementation of the strategies is important in order to generate attractive, risk-adjusted returns.\nGlobal macro is often categorized as a directional investment strategy.\nGlobal macro strategies can be divided into discretionary and systematic approaches. Discretionary trading is carried out by investment managers who identify and select investments, whereas\nsystematic trading\nis based on\nmathematical models\nand executed by\nsoftware\nwith limited human involvement beyond the programming and updating of the software. These strategies can also be divided into\ntrend\nor counter-trend approaches depending on whether the fund attempts to profit from following\nmarket trend\n(long or short-term) or attempts to anticipate and profit from reversals in trends.\nWithin global macro strategies, there are further sub-strategies including "systematic diversified", in which the fund trades in diversified markets, or sector specialists such as "systematic currency", in which the fund trades in\nforeign exchange markets\nor any other sector specialisation.\nOther sub-strategies include those employed by\ncommodity trading advisors\n(CTAs), where the fund trades in\nfutures\n(or\noptions\n) in\ncommodity\nmarkets or in swaps.\nThis is also known as a "managed future fund".\nCTAs trade in commodities (such as gold) and financial instruments, including\nstock indices\n. They also take both long and short positions, allowing them to make profit in both market upswings and downswings.\nMost global macro managers tends to be a CTA from a regulatory perspective and the main divide is between systematic and discretionary strategies. A classification framework for CTA/Macro Strategies can be found in the reference.\nDirectional\n[\nedit\n]\nSchematic representation of short selling in two steps. The short seller\nborrows shares\nand immediately sells them. The short seller then expects the price to decrease, when the seller can profit by purchasing the shares to return to the lender.\nDirectional investment strategies use market movements, trends, or inconsistencies when picking stocks across a variety of markets. Computer models can be used, or fund managers will identify and select investments. These types of strategies have a greater exposure to the fluctuations of the overall market than do market neutral strategies.\nDirectional hedge fund strategies include US and international\nlong/short equity\nhedge funds, where\nlong equity\npositions are hedged with\nshort sales\nof equities or equity\nindex\noptions\n.\nWithin directional strategies, there are a number of sub-strategies. "\nEmerging markets\n" funds focus on emerging markets such as China and India,\nwhereas "sector funds" specialize in specific areas including technology, healthcare, biotechnology, pharmaceuticals, energy, and basic materials. Funds using a "fundamental growth" strategy invest in companies with more\nearnings\ngrowth than the overall\nstock market\nor relevant sector, while funds using a "\nfundamental value\n" strategy invest in undervalued companies.\nFunds that use\nquantitative\nand\nfinancial signal processing\ntechniques for equity\ntrading\nare described as using a "quantitative directional" strategy.\nFunds using a "\nshort bias\n" strategy take advantage of declining equity prices using short positions.\nEvent-driven\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nEvent-driven investing\nEvent-driven strategies concern situations in which the underlying investment opportunity and risk are associated with an event.\nAn event-driven investment strategy finds investment opportunities in corporate transactional events such as consolidations,\nacquisitions\n,\nrecapitalizations\n,\nbankruptcies\n, and\nliquidations\n. Managers employing such a strategy capitalize on\nvaluation\ninconsistencies in the market before or after such events, and take a position based on the predicted movement of the\nsecurity\nor securities in question. Large\ninstitutional investors\nsuch as hedge funds are more likely to pursue event-driven investing strategies than traditional equity investors because they have the expertise and resources to analyze corporate transactional events for investment opportunities.\nCorporate transactional events generally fit into three categories:\ndistressed securities\n,\nrisk arbitrage\n, and\nspecial situations\n.\nDistressed securities\ninclude such events as restructurings,\nrecapitalizations\n, and\nbankruptcies\n.\nA distressed securities investment strategy involves investing in the bonds or loans of companies facing bankruptcy or severe financial distress, when these\nbonds\nor\nloans\nare being traded at a\ndiscount\nto their value. Hedge fund managers pursuing the distressed debt investment strategy aim to capitalize on depressed bond prices. Hedge funds purchasing distressed debt may prevent those companies from going bankrupt, as such an acquisition deters\nforeclosure\nby banks.\nWhile event-driven investing, in general, tends to thrive during a\nbull market\n, distressed investing works best during a\nbear market\n.\nRisk arbitrage\nor\nmerger arbitrage\nincludes such events as\nmergers\n, acquisitions, liquidations, and\nhostile takeovers\n.\nRisk arbitrage typically involves buying and selling the stocks of two or more merging companies to take advantage of market discrepancies between acquisition price and stock price. The risk element arises from the possibility that the merger or acquisition will not go ahead as planned; hedge fund managers will use research and analysis to determine if the event will take place.\nSpecial situations are events that impact the value of a company's stock, including the\nrestructuring\nof a company or corporate transactions including\nspin-offs\n, share\nbuy backs\n, security issuance/repurchase, asset sales, or other catalyst-oriented situations. To take advantage of special situations the hedge fund manager must identify an upcoming event that will increase or decrease the value of the company's equity and equity-related instruments.\nOther event-driven strategies include credit arbitrage strategies, which focus on corporate\nfixed income\nsecurities; an activist strategy, where the fund takes large positions in companies and uses the ownership to participate in the management; a strategy based on predicting the final approval of new\npharmaceutical drugs\n; and legal catalyst strategy, which specializes in companies involved in major lawsuits.\nRelative value\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nRelative value (economics)\nRelative value arbitrage strategies take advantage of relative discrepancies in price between securities. The price discrepancy can occur due to mispricing of securities compared to related securities, the\nunderlying security\nor the market overall. Hedge fund managers can use various types of analysis to identify price discrepancies in securities, including mathematical,\ntechnical\n, or\nfundamental\ntechniques.\nRelative value is often used as quantities of equities and bonds, investment in hedge funds may provide diversification and reduce the overall portfolio risk.\nManagers of hedge funds often aim to produce returns that are relatively\nuncorrelated\nwith market indices and are consistent with investors' desired level of risk.\nWhile\nhedging\ncan reduce some risks of an investment it usually increases others, such as\noperational risk\nand\nmodel risk\n, so overall risk is reduced but cannot be eliminated. According to a report by the Hennessee Group, hedge funds were approximately one-third less volatile than the\nS&P 500\nbetween 1993 and 2010.\nRisk management\n[\nedit\n]\nInvestors in hedge funds are, in most countries, required to be qualified investors who are assumed to be aware of the\ninvestment risks\n, and accept these risks because of the potential\nreturns\nrelative to those risks. Fund managers may employ extensive\nrisk management\nstrategies in order to protect the fund and investors. According to the\nFinancial Times\n, "big hedge funds have some of the most sophisticated and exacting risk management practices anywhere in asset management."\nHedge fund managers that hold a large number of investment positions for short periods are likely to have a particularly comprehensive risk management system in place, and it has become usual for funds to have independent risk officers who assess and manage risks but are not otherwise involved in trading.\nA variety of different measurement techniques and models are used to estimate risk according to the fund's leverage, liquidity, and investment strategy.\nNon-normality of returns, volatility clustering and trends are not always accounted for by conventional risk measurement methodologies and so in addition to\nvalue at risk\nand similar measurements, funds may use integrated measures such as\ndrawdowns\n.\nSee\nFinancial risk management §\nInvestment management\ngenerally.\nIn addition to assessing the market-related risks that may arise from an investment, investors commonly employ\noperational due diligence\nto assess the risk that error or\nfraud\nat a hedge fund might result in a loss to the investor. Considerations will include the organization and management of operations at the hedge fund manager, whether the investment strategy is likely to be sustainable, and the fund's ability to develop as a company.\nTransparency, and regulatory considerations\n[\nedit\n]\nSince hedge funds are private entities and have few public\ndisclosure\nrequirements, this is sometimes perceived as a\nlack of transparency\n.\nAnother common perception of hedge funds is that their managers are not subject to as much regulatory oversight and/or\nregistration\nrequirements as other financial investment managers, and more prone to manager-specific idiosyncratic risks such as style drifts, faulty operations, or fraud.\nNew regulations introduced in the US and the EU as of 2010 required hedge fund managers to report more information, leading to greater transparency.\nIn addition, investors, particularly institutional investors, are encouraging further developments in hedge fund risk management, both through internal practices and external regulatory requirements.\nThe increasing influence of institutional investors has led to greater transparency: hedge funds increasingly provide information to investors including valuation methodology, positions, and leverage exposure.\nRisks shared with other investment types\n[\nedit\n]\nHedge funds share many of the same types of risk as other investment classes, including\nliquidity risk\nand manager risk.\nLiquidity\nrefers to the degree to which an asset can be bought and sold or converted to cash; similar to private-equity funds, hedge funds employ a\nlock-up period\nduring which an investor cannot remove money.\nManager risk refers to those risks which arise from the management of funds. As well as specific risks such as\nstyle drift\n, which refers to a fund manager "drifting" away from an area of specific expertise, manager risk factors include\nvaluation risk\n, capacity risk,\nconcentration risk\n, and\nleverage risk\n.\nValuation risk refers to the concern that the\nnet asset value\n(NAV) of investments may be inaccurate;\ncapacity risk can arise from placing too much money into one particular strategy, which may lead to fund performance deterioration;\nand concentration risk may arise if a fund has too much exposure to a particular investment, sector, trading strategy, or group of\ncorrelated\nfunds.\nThese risks may be managed through defined controls over\nconflict of interest\n,\nrestrictions on allocation of funds,\nand set exposure limits for strategies.\nMany investment funds use\nleverage\n, the practice of\nborrowing\nmoney, trading on\nmargin\n, or using\nderivatives\nto obtain market exposure in excess of that provided by investors' capital. Although leverage can increase potential returns, the opportunity for larger gains is weighed against the possibility of greater losses.\nHedge funds employing leverage are likely to engage in extensive risk management practices.\nIn comparison with\ninvestment banks\n, hedge fund leverage is relatively low; according to a\nNational Bureau of Economic Research\nworking paper, the average leverage for investment banks is 14.2, compared to between 1.5 and 2.5 for hedge funds.\nSome types of funds, including hedge funds, are perceived as having a greater\nappetite for risk\n, with the intention of maximizing returns,\nsubject to the\nrisk tolerance\nof investors and the fund manager. Managers will have an additional incentive to increase risk oversight when their own capital is invested in the fund.\nFees and remuneration\n[\nedit\n]\nFees paid to hedge funds\n[\nedit\n]\nHedge fund management firms typically charge their funds both a\nmanagement fee\nand a\nperformance fee\n.\nManagement fees are calculated as a percentage of the fund's\nnet asset value\nand typically range from 1% to 4% per annum, with 2% being standard.\nThey are usually expressed as an annual percentage, but calculated and paid monthly or quarterly. Management fees for hedge funds are designed to cover the operating costs of the manager, whereas the performance fee provides the manager's profits. However, due to\neconomies of scale\nthe management fee from larger funds can generate a significant part of a manager's profits, and as a result some fees have been criticized by some public pension funds, such as\nCalPERS\n, for being too high.\nThe\nperformance fee\nis typically 20% of the fund's profits during any year, though performance fees range between 10% and 50%. Performance fees are intended to provide an incentive for a manager to generate profits.\nPerformance fees have been criticized by\nWarren Buffett\n, who believes that because hedge funds share only the profits and not the losses, such fees create an incentive for high-risk investment management. Performance fee rates have fallen since the start of the\ncredit crunch\n.\nAlmost all hedge fund performance fees include a "\nhigh water mark\n" (or "loss carryforward provision"), which means that the performance fee only applies to net profits (\ni.e.\n, profits after losses in previous years have been recovered). This prevents managers from receiving fees for volatile performance, though a manager will sometimes close a fund that has suffered serious losses and start a new fund, rather than attempt to recover the losses over a number of years without a performance fee.\nSome performance fees include a "\nhurdle\n", so that a fee is only paid on the fund's performance in excess of a\nbenchmark\nrate (\ne.g.\n,\nLIBOR\n) or a fixed percentage.\nThe hurdle is usually tied to a benchmark rate such as Libor or the one-year Treasury bill rate plus a spread.\nA "soft" hurdle means the performance fee is calculated on all the fund's returns if the hurdle rate is cleared. A "hard" hurdle is calculated only on returns above the hurdle rate.\nBy example the manager sets a hurdle rate equal to 5%, and the fund return 15%, incentive fees would only apply to the 10% above the hurdle rate.\nA hurdle is intended to ensure that a manager is only rewarded if the fund generates returns in excess of the returns that the investor would have received if they had invested their money elsewhere.\nSome hedge funds charge a redemption fee (or withdrawal fee) for early withdrawals during a specified period of time (typically a year), or when withdrawals exceed a predetermined percentage of the original investment.\nThe purpose of the fee is to discourage short-term investing, reduce turnover, and deter withdrawals after periods of poor performance. Unlike management fees and performance fees, redemption fees are usually kept by the fund and redistributed to all investors.\nRemuneration of portfolio managers\n[\nedit\n]\nHedge fund management firms are often owned by their\nportfolio managers\n, who are therefore entitled to any profits that the business makes. As management fees are intended to cover the firm's operating costs, performance fees (and any excess management fees) are generally distributed to the firm's owners as profits. Funds do not tend to report compensation, and so published lists of the amounts earned by top managers tend to be estimates based on factors such as the fees charged by their funds and the capital they are thought to have invested in them.\nMany managers have accumulated large stakes in their own funds and so top hedge fund managers can earn extraordinary amounts of money, perhaps up to $4 billion in a good year.\nEarnings at the very top are higher than in any other sector of the financial industry,\nand collectively the top 25 hedge fund managers regularly earn more than all 500 of the chief executives in the\nS&P 500\n.\nMost hedge fund managers are remunerated much less, however, and if performance fees are not earned then small managers at least are unlikely to be paid significant amounts.\nIn 2011, the top manager earned $3 billion, the tenth earned $210 million, and the 30th earned $80 million.\nIn 2011, the average earnings for the 25 highest-compensated hedge fund managers in the United States was $576 million\nwhile the mean total compensation for all hedge fund investment professionals was $690,786 and the median was $312,329. The same figures for hedge fund CEOs were $1,037,151 and $600,000, and for chief investment officers were $1,039,974 and $300,000, respectively.\nOf the 1,226 people on the\nForbes\nWorld's Billionaires List for 2012,\n36 of the financiers listed "derived significant chunks" of their wealth from hedge fund management.\nAmong the richest 1,000 people in the United Kingdom, 54 were hedge fund managers, according to the\nSunday Times\nRich List for 2012.\nA portfolio manager risks losing his past compensation if he or she engages in\ninsider trading\n. Supp. 2d 356 (S.D.N.Y. 2013), applying New York's\nfaithless servant\ndoctrine, the court held that a hedge fund's portfolio manager engaging in insider trading in violation of his company's code of This allows fund managers to avoid attempting a valuation of the underlying investments, which may not always have a readily available\nmarket value\n.\nSide pockets were widely used by hedge funds during the\n2008 financial crisis\namidst a flood of withdrawal requests. However, summaries of individual hedge fund performance are occasionally available in industry journals\nand databases.\nOne estimate is that the average hedge fund returned 11.4% per year,\nrepresenting a 6.7% return above overall market performance before fees, based on performance data from 8,400 hedge funds.\nAnother estimate is that between January 2000 and December 2009 hedge funds outperformed other investments and were substantially less volatile, with stocks falling an average of 2.62% per year over the decade and hedge funds rising an average of 6.54% per year; this was an unusually volatile period with both the 2001-2002\ndot-com bubble\nand a\nrecession beginning mid 2007\n.\nHowever, more recent data show that hedge fund performance declined and underperformed the market from about 2009 to 2016.\nHedge funds performance is measured by comparing their returns to an estimate of their risk.\nCommon measures are the\nSharpe ratio\n,\nTreynor measure\nand\nJensen's alpha\n.\nThese measures work best when returns follow\nnormal distributions\nwithout\nautocorrelation\n, and these assumptions are often not met in practice.\nNew performance measures have been introduced that attempt to address some of theoretical concerns with traditional indicators, including: modified\nSharpe ratios\n;\nthe\nOmega ratio\nintroduced by Keating and Shadwick in 2002;\nAlternative Investments Risk Adjusted Performance (AIRAP) published by Sharma in 2004;\nand Kappa developed by Kaplan and Knowles in 2004.\nSector-size effect\n[\nedit\n]\nThere is a debate over whether\nalpha\n(the manager's skill element in performance) has been diluted by the expansion of the hedge fund industry. Two reasons are given. First, the increase in traded volume may have been reducing the\nmarket anomalies\nthat are a source of hedge fund performance. Second, the remuneration model is attracting more managers, which may dilute the talent available in the industry.\nHedge fund indices\n[\nedit\n]\nIndices\nplay a central and unambiguous role in traditional asset markets, where they are widely accepted as representative of their underlying portfolios. Equity and debt\nindex fund\nproducts provide investable access to most\ndeveloped markets\nin these asset classes.\nHedge fund indices are more problematic. The typical hedge fund is not traded on exchange, will accept investments only at the discretion of the manager, and does not have an obligation to publish returns. Despite these challenges, Non-investable, Investable, and Clone indices have been developed.\nNon-investable indices\n[\nedit\n]\nNon-investable indices are indicative in nature and aim to represent the performance of some database of hedge funds using some measure such as mean, median, or weighted mean from a hedge fund database. The databases have diverse selection criteria and methods of construction, and no single database captures all funds. This leads to significant differences in reported performance between different indices.\nAlthough they aim to be representative, non-investable indices suffer from a lengthy and largely unavoidable list of\nbiases\n. Funds' participation in a database is voluntary, leading to\nself-selection bias\nbecause those funds that choose to report may not be typical of funds as a whole. For example, some do not report because of poor results or because they have already reached their target size and do not wish to raise further money.\nThe short lifetimes of many hedge funds mean that there are many new entrants and many departures each year, which raises the problem of\nsurvivorship bias\n. If we examine only funds that have survived to the present, we will overestimate past returns because many of the worst-performing funds have not survived, and the observed association between fund youth and fund performance suggests that this bias may be substantial.\nWhen a fund is added to a database for the first time, all or part of its historical data is recorded ex-post in the database. It is likely that funds only publish their results when they are favorable, so that the average performances displayed by the funds during their incubation period are inflated. This is known as "instant history bias" or "\nbackfill bias\n".\nInvestable indices\n[\nedit\n]\nInvestable indices are an attempt to reduce these problems by ensuring that the return of the index is available to shareholders. To create an investable index, the index provider selects funds and develops structured products or derivative instruments that deliver the performance of the index. When investors buy these products the index provider makes the investments in the underlying funds, making an investable index similar in some ways to a fund of hedge funds portfolio.\nTo make the index investable, hedge funds must agree to accept investments on the terms given by the constructor. To make the index liquid, these terms must include provisions for redemptions that some managers may consider too onerous to be acceptable. This means that investable indices do not represent the total universe of hedge funds. Most seriously, they under-represent more successful managers, who typically refuse to accept such investment protocols.\nHedge fund replication\n[\nedit\n]\nThe most recent addition to the field approaches the problem in a different manner. Instead of reflecting the performance of actual hedge funds, they take a statistical approach to the analysis of historic hedge fund returns and use this to construct a model of how hedge fund returns respond to the movements of various investable financial assets. This model is then used to construct an investable portfolio of those assets. This makes the index investable, and in principle, they can be as representative as the hedge fund database from which they were constructed. However, these clone indices rely on a statistical modelling process. Such indices have too short a history to state whether this approach will be considered successful.\nClosures\n[\nedit\n]\nIn March 2017, HFR – a hedge fund research data and service provider – reported that there were more hedge-fund closures in 2016 than during the 2009 recession. Such risk may arise following a destabilizing event or events affecting a group of\nfinancial institutions\nlinked through investment activity.\nOrganizations such as the\nEuropean Central Bank\nhave charged that hedge funds pose systemic risks to the financial sector,\nand following the failure of hedge fund\nLong-Term Capital Management\n(LTCM) in 1998 there was widespread concern about the potential for systemic risk if a hedge fund failure led to the failure of its counterparties. One of the attractive features of hedge funds (in particular\nmarket neutral\nand similar funds) is that they sometimes have a modest correlation with traditional assets such as equities. This means that hedge funds have a potentially quite valuable role in investment portfolios as diversifiers, reducing overall portfolio risk.\nHowever, there are at least three reasons why one might not wish to allocate a high proportion of assets into hedge funds. These reasons are:\nHedge funds are highly individual, making it hard to estimate the likely returns or risks.\nHedge funds' correlation with other assets tends to rise during stressful market events, making them much less useful for diversification in bad times than they may appear in good times.\nHedge fund returns are reduced considerably by the high fees that are typically charged.\nSeveral studies have suggested that hedge funds are sufficiently diversifying to merit inclusion in investor portfolios, but this is disputed for example by Mark Kritzman who performed a mean-variance optimization calculation on an opportunity set that consisted of a stock index fund, a bond index fund, and ten hypothetical hedge funds.\nThe optimizer found that a mean-variance efficient portfolio did not contain any allocation to hedge funds, largely because of the impact of performance fees. To demonstrate this, Kritzman repeated the optimization using an assumption that the hedge funds took no performance fees. The result from this second optimization was an allocation of 74% to hedge funds.\nHedge funds tend to perform poorly during equity\nbear markets\n, just when an investor needs part of their portfolio to add value.\nFor example, in January–September 2008, the Credit Suisse/Tremont Hedge Fund Index returned -9.87%.\nAccording to the same index series, even "dedicated short bias" funds returned −6.08% in September 2008, when\nLehman Brothers\ncollapsed.\nSee also\n[\nedit\n]\nActivist shareholder\nAlternative investment\nNaked Capitalism\nCorporate governance\nFund governance\nInvestment banking\nList of hedge funds\nVulture fund\nNotes\n[\nedit\n]\n1\n2\n3\n4\nGerald T.
