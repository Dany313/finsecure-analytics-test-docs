Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_technology\n==================================================\n\nSubset of technologies used in finance\nNew York City\n, the world's principal fintech and\nfinancial center\nFinancial technology\n(abbreviated as\nfintech\n) refers to the application of innovative technologies to products and services in the\nfinancial industry\n. This broad term encompasses a wide array of technological advances in financial services, including\nmobile banking\n, online lending platforms, digital payment systems,\nrobo-advisors\n, and\nblockchain\n-based applications such as\ncryptocurrencies\n.\nFinancial technology companies include both\nstartups\nand established technology and financial firms that aim to improve, complement, or replace traditional financial services.\nEvolution\n[\nedit\n]\nThe evolution of financial technology spans more than a century, marked by significant technological innovations that have reshaped the financial industry. While the application of technology to finance has deep historical roots, the term "financial technology" emerged in the late 20th century and gained prominence in the 1990s.\nThe earliest documented use of the term dates back to 1967, appearing in an article in\nThe Boston Globe\ntitled "Fin-Tech New Source of Seed Money." This piece reported on a startup investment company established by former executives of\nComputer Control Company\n, aimed at providing venture capital and industry expertise to startups in the financial technology industry.\nHowever, the term didn't gain popularity until the early 1990s when Citicorp Chairman John Reed used it to describe the Financial Services Technology Consortium. This project, initiated by\nCitigroup\n, was designed to promote technological cooperation in the financial sector, marking a pivotal moment in the industry's collaborative approach to innovation.\nThe financial technology ecosystem includes various types of companies. While startups developing new financial technologies or services are often associated with financial technology, the sector also encompasses established technology companies expanding into financial services and traditional\nfinancial institutions\nadopting new technologies. This diverse landscape has led to innovations across multiple financial sectors, including\nbanking\n,\ninsurance\n,\ninvestment\n, and\npayment systems\n.\nFinancial technology applications span a wide range of financial services. These include\ndigital banking\n,\nmobile payments\nand\ndigital wallets\n,\npeer-to-peer lending\nplatforms,\nrobo-advisors\nand\nalgorithmic trading\n,\ninsurtech\n,\nblockchain\nand\ncryptocurrency\n,\nregulatory technology\n, and\ncrowdfunding\nplatforms.\nThe evolution of financial technology often involves the crossover of telecommunications engineering into finance. For example, early experimentation with\nNFC\npayment standards in the 2010s led some developers to explore distributed ledgers as a backend for scalable mobile payments, attempting to resolve the throughput limitations of traditional banking infrastructure without relying on centralized intermediaries.\nImpact on financial inclusion\n[\nedit\n]\nThere is growing empirical evidence that FinTech innovations, especially digital payment systems,\nmobile money\nand online financial platforms, significantly contribute to financial inclusion.\nA 2025 systematic review found that such technologies improve access to financial services in developing and underserved communities by reducing barriers to banking and enabling secure, affordable transactions.\nDespite overall positive effects on financial inclusion, digital financial services may exclude people with limited internet access, low digital or financial literary, or lower confidence using online services. It also raises consumer-protection concerns around fraud, cybersecurity and data privacy.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nFoundations\n[\nedit\n]\nThe late 19th century laid the groundwork for early financial technology with the development of the\ntelegraph\nand\ntransatlantic cable systems\n. These innovations transformed the transmission of financial information across borders, enabling faster and more efficient communication between financial institutions.\nA significant milestone in electronic money movement came with the establishment of the\nFedwire\nFunds Service by the\nFederal Reserve Banks\nin 1918. This early electronic funds transfer system used\ntelegraph\nlines to facilitate secure transfers between member banks, marking one of the first instances of electronic money movement.\nThe 1950s ushered in a new era of consumer financial services.\nDiners Club International\nintroduced the first universal\ncredit card\nin 1950, a pivotal moment that would reshape consumer spending and credit. This innovation paved the way for the launch of\nAmerican Express\ncards in 1958 and the BankAmericard (later Visa) in 1959, further expanding the credit card industry.\nDigital revolution\n[\nedit\n]\nThe 1960s and 1970s marked the beginning of the shift from analog to digital finance, with several groundbreaking developments that shaped the future of financial technology.\nIn 1967,\nBarclays\nintroduced the world's first\nATM\nin\nLondon\n, expanding access to cash and basic banking services. Inspired by\nvending machines\n, the ATM marked a significant step towards self-service banking.\nFinancial technology infrastructure continued to evolve with the establishment of the Inter-bank Computer Bureau in the\nUK\nin 1968. This development laid the groundwork for the country's first automated\nclearing house\nsystem, eventually evolving into\nBACS\n(Bankers' Automated Clearing Services) to facilitate electronic funds transfers between banks.\nThe world of\nsecurities trading\nwas transformed in 1971 with the establishment of\nNASDAQ\n, the world's first digital\nstock exchange\n. NASDAQ's electronic quotation system represented a significant leap forward from the traditional\nopen outcry\nsystem used in stock exchanges.\nTwo years later, the founding of the\nSWIFT\n(Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) standardized and secured communication between financial institutions globally. SWIFT's messaging system became the global standard for international money and security transfers.\nThe introduction of electronic fund transfer systems, such as the\nACH\n(Automated Clearing House) in the\nUnited States\n, facilitated faster and more efficient money transfers. The ACH network allowed for\ndirect deposits\n,\npayroll\npayments, and electronic bill payments, significantly reducing the need for\npaper checks\n.\nRise of digital financial services\n[\nedit\n]\nBloomberg Terminal\nMuseum in\nManhattan\nThe 1980s and 1990s witnessed significant developments in financial technology, with the rise of digital financial services and the early stages of\nonline banking\n. A major breakthrough came when\nMichael Bloomberg\nfounded Innovative Market Systems (later\nBloomberg L.P.\n) and introduced the\nBloomberg Terminal\n. This innovation transformed how financial professionals accessed and analyzed market data, providing real-time financial market data, analytics, and news to financial institutions worldwide.\nOnline banking emerged in the early 1980s, with the\nBank of Scotland\noffering the first UK online banking service called Homelink. This service allowed customers to view statements, transfer money, and pay bills using their\ntelevisions\nand\ntelephones\n.\nThe late 1980s saw the development of\nEDI\n(Electronic Data Interchange) standards, allowing businesses to exchange financial documents electronically and streamlining\nB2B\n(business-to-business) transactions.\nA significant milestone in consumer digital banking came in 1994 when\nStanford Federal Credit Union\nlaunched the first\nInternet banking\nwebsite. This service initially allowed members to check account balances online, with bill pay functionality added in 1997.\nHowever, it was not until 1999 that the first state-chartered,\nFDIC\n-insured institution operating primarily online was established. First Internet Bank, founded by David Becker, marked a new era in online-only banking.\nDot-com era\n[\nedit\n]\nThe late 1990s and early 2000s marked a significant turning point in the evolution of financial technology, as numerous innovations emerged during the\ndot-com boom\n. One notable development was the rise of online trading platforms, with\nE-Trade\n, founded in 1982, leading the charge. In 1992, E-Trade became one of the first financial services companies to offer online trading to consumers, revolutionizing the way individuals interacted with the\nstock market\n.\nAnother pivotal moment was the founding of\nPayPal\nin 1998. PayPal's success in creating a secure and user-friendly online payment system demonstrated the viability of digital payment solutions and paved the way for numerous subsequent financial technology startups.\nThe early 2000s also saw the emergence of innovative business models in the financial services industry. WebBank, established in 1997, began offering a "rent-a-charter" model in 2005, providing the necessary banking infrastructure and regulatory compliance for financial technology startups to offer banking services without obtaining their own charters. This model would later prove crucial in enabling the growth of numerous financial technology companies.\nPost-2008 financial crisis\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\n2008 financial crisis\nserved as a catalyst for the rapid growth of the financial technology industry, as declining trust in traditional financial institutions created opportunities for innovative, technology-driven solutions. The early days of the post-crisis era saw the emergence of digital currencies, with\ne-Gold\nserving as a precursor to the development of\nBitcoin\n. While e-Gold, which allowed users to create accounts denominated in grams of gold and enable instant transfers, ultimately faced legal challenges and closure, it laid the foundation for future digital currencies.\nThe invention of Bitcoin in 2008 by an anonymous creator using the pseudonym\nSatoshi Nakamoto\nmarked a turning point in the evolution of digital currencies and\ndecentralized finance\n. Bitcoin's use of\nblockchain\ntechnology sparked a wave of development in the field of\ncryptocurrencies\n, opening up new possibilities for secure, transparent, and decentralized financial systems.\nAs the financial technology landscape continued to evolve, new payment processing companies entered the market, offering developer-friendly\nAPIs\nthat dramatically simplified online payment integration. By lowering the barriers to entry for\ne-commerce\nand online financial services, these companies played a crucial role in enabling the growth of new financial technology startups and driving innovation in the sector.\nThe partner banking model, which emerged in the early 2000s, gained significant traction in the post-crisis era. This model expanded beyond its initial "rent-a-charter" concept, evolving into more comprehensive partnerships between traditional banks and financial technology companies. These collaborations allowed for rapid innovation and market entry, as financial technology leveraged the regulatory compliance and infrastructure of established banks while bringing their own technological expertise and customer-centric approaches. This further accelerated the growth of the financial technology sector, enabling the proliferation of digital-first financial services.\nThe maturation of this model paved the way for the rise of\nneobanks\n, which challenged traditional banking paradigms by offering fully digital experiences, redefining customer expectations in the banking sector.\nThe increasing adoption of\nsmartphones\ndrove the development of mobile-first financial technology solutions. Square's introduction of a mobile card reader in 2009 enabled small businesses to accept\ncredit card\npayments using smartphones, democratizing access to payment processing and highlighting the transformative potential of mobile technology in the financial services industry.\nThe evolution of mobile payment systems continued with the launch of\nGoogle Wallet\nin 2011 and\nApple Pay\nin 2014, which further popularized mobile payments and demonstrated the growing consumer demand for convenient, secure, and user-friendly payment solutions.\nThis period also saw the rise of peer-to-peer (P2P) payment applications. These platforms revolutionized how individuals transfer money, enabling quick and easy transactions between users. By allowing fast, direct transfers through mobile devices, P2P payment apps significantly reduced the friction in personal financial transactions, making it simpler for people to split bills, share costs, or send money to friends and family.\nAccelerated growth of digital finance\n[\nedit\n]\nThe global\nCOVID-19 pandemic\n, which began in early 2020, had a profound impact on the financial technology industry, accelerating the adoption of digital financial services and highlighting the importance of technology in ensuring the resilience and accessibility of financial systems. As lockdowns and\nsocial distancing\nmeasures forced businesses and consumers to rely more heavily on digital channels, financial technology solutions experienced a surge in demand.\nMobile-first financial technology applications saw unprecedented growth during this period. Many trading platforms reported significant increases in new user accounts, with some seeing millions of new funded accounts added in the early months of the pandemic.\nSimilarly, payment and money transfer apps experienced substantial user growth, with some platforms more than doubling their monthly active users over a three-year period, indicating a massive shift towards digital financial services.\nThe events of 2020 also exposed the limitations of traditional financial institutions in meeting the needs of consumers and businesses in times of crisis. Financial technology companies, with their agile and technology-driven business models, were better positioned to respond to the challenges posed by the rapidly changing environment, offering innovative solutions for remote banking, contactless payments, and digital lending.\nDuring this period,\nventure capital\nvaluations for financial technology companies soared, driven by low interest rates and a booming\nstock market\n. The surge in financial technology investments was marked by significant capital inflows, leading to higher valuations and more frequent exits via\nIPOs\nand\nSPACs\n. Several prominent financial technology companies achieved record-breaking valuations, further underscoring the sector's growth and investor confidence.\nThe shift towards digital financial services during this period also accelerated the adoption of blockchain technology and cryptocurrencies. As\ncentral banks\naround the world explored the possibility of issuing\ndigital currencies\n, the interest in\ndecentralized finance\nand\nnon-fungible tokens\ngrew, opening up new avenues for innovation in the financial technology sector.\nAfrica\n[\nedit\n]\nThe financial technology landscape in Africa is on the rise, with active companies reaching 1,263 in 2024, a significant increase from 1,049 in 2022 and 450 in 2020.\nNigeria leads the financial technology sector, accounting for 28% of all financial technology companies on the continent, and hosting eight of Africa's nine fintech unicorns.\nBy early 2025, Nigeria was home to more than 430 fintech companies, a 70% increase year-on-year, with fintech accounting for 72% of the country's total equity funding.\nAfrican fintech startups collectively raised over US$3 billion in disclosed funding in 2025, a 33% year-on-year increase, as the sector shifted from rapid scaling toward sustainable growth and profitability.\nMiddle East\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nUnited Arab Emirates\nhas emerged as the leading fintech hub in the\nMiddle East and North Africa\n(MENA) region. The UAE hosts approximately a quarter of all fintech companies in the MENA region, supported by two financial free zones with independent regulatory frameworks: the\nDubai International Financial Centre\n(DIFC), regulated by the\nDubai Financial Services Authority\n(DFSA), and the\nAbu Dhabi Global Market\n(ADGM), regulatedby the Financial Services Regulatory Authority (FSRA).\nBy 2025, the DIFC had grown to host over 1,500 fintech, artificial intelligence, and innovation firms, ranking ninth among global fintech hubs according to the Global Financial Centres Index, and first in the Middle East.\n2020s\n[\nedit\n]\nThe 2020s marked a new phase in financial technology, defined by the mainstream adoption of artificial intelligence across financial services. Beginning in 2023, generative AI tools were adopted by banks, payment processors, and investment platforms to automate customer service, credit decision-making, fraud detection, and portfolio management.\nThe rapid integration of AI into financial infrastructure also drew regulatory attention. In May 2026, the\nInternational Monetary Fund\nwarned that AI-powered cyberattacks posed systemic risks to global financial stability, noting that advanced models could dramatically reduce the time and cost needed to identify and exploit vulnerabilities across interconnected banking systems, with extreme cyber incidents capable of triggering funding strains and disrupting broader markets.\nThe\nFederal Reserve\n's Spring 2026 Financial Stability Report found that 50% of market contacts identified AI as a potential systemic shock to financial markets, up from 9% the previous year, representing a more than fivefold increase in perceived AI-related systemic risk in twelve months.\nThe decade also saw significant fintech expansion in emerging markets. In Africa, active fintech companies reached 1,263 in 2024, up from 450 in 2020, with Nigeria accounting for 28% of all companies on the continent. In the\nGCC\n, regulatory sandboxes established by the\nDubai Financial Services Authority\nand\nAbu Dhabi Global Market\naccelerated fintech licensing, enabling a new generation of digital investment platforms to reach retail investors across the region.\nIndustry landscape\n[\nedit\n]\nThe financial technology industry includes a diverse range of financial services and technologies that can be categorized into several key areas. Many companies operate across multiple areas or create new niches that blur these distinctions.\nRevenue models\n[\nedit\n]\nFinancial technology companies utilize various revenue models, often combining multiple approaches to diversify income streams.\nTransaction fees form a primary source of income for many financial technology businesses, particularly payment processors and cryptocurrency exchanges. These companies typically charge a percentage of each processed transaction. Some companies have expanded this model to include premium fees for services like instant payouts, catering to merchants who require immediate access to funds.\nInterchange fees\nrepresent another significant revenue stream, particularly for firms offering payment cards.\nGAO report image explaining interchange fees\nSubscription and\nfreemium\nmodels allow companies to offer basic services at no cost while charging for advanced features or premium tiers.\nThis approach is common among digital banks and financial management platforms. In the business-to-business (B2B) sector, usage-based pricing is prevalent, especially for API services. Financial technology infrastructure providers often charge based on the volume of API calls or transactions processed, enabling other businesses to access specialized financial services without developing them internally.\nInterest-based revenue is crucial for many financial technology companies, particularly in the banking and lending sectors. Digital banks and investment platforms typically earn interest on customer deposits and cash balances. Lending platforms often combine interest revenue with loan sales, selling portions of their loan portfolios to other institutions or investors.\nData-driven revenue models, while potentially lucrative, have faced increasing scrutiny and regulation. Some firms engage in data monetization, selling aggregated or anonymized user data to third parties. However, this practice has raised privacy concerns and regulatory challenges.\nA less controversial approach involves leveraging user data for targeted advertising and lead generation, earning revenue through product recommendations and referral fees while providing free services to users.\nSome revenue models, such as payment for order flow (PFOF) used by certain brokerage firms, occupy a regulatory gray area. While PFOF allows for commission-free trades, potentially benefiting retail investors, it has faced scrutiny due to concerns about conflicts of interest and best execution practices.\nInfrastructure\n[\nedit\n]\nModern financial technology depends on a shared layer of technical infrastructure that enables applications, banks, and\nthird-party providers\nto exchange data and initiate transactions securely. Central to this infrastructure are standardised application programming interfaces (APIs), which allow software systems to communicate with one another without exposing underlying code or data directly. The security of these integrations is governed by protocols such as\nOAuth 2.0\nand\nOpenID\nConnect, and in financial services specifically by the Financial-grade API (FAPI) standard — a security profile developed by the OpenID Foundation that defines how APIs handling sensitive financial data must authenticate clients, manage consent, and ensure non-repudiation of transactions.\nOpen banking\nrepresents the most significant regulatory formalisation of this API infrastructure to date. Under open banking frameworks, banks are required to expose customer account data and payment services to authorised third parties via standardised APIs, subject to explicit customer consent. The model was first mandated at scale by the\nEuropean Union's\nrevised Payment Services Directive (\nPSD2\n) in 2018 and the UK's\nOpen Banking Standard\n, established following a 2016 Competition and Markets Authority ruling.\nComparable frameworks have since been adopted globally, including the Financial Data Exchange (FDX) standard in the United States and the Consumer Data Right (CDR) in Australia, though these differ in terminology, technical standards, and implementation model.\nBy 2024, the global open banking market was valued at approximately $31.6 billion and is projected to reach $135 billion by 2030.\nControversies\n[\nedit\n]\nAs financial technology companies seek to disrupt traditional\nfinancial services\n, some have been criticized for prioritizing growth over\ncompliance\n, security, and\nconsumer protection\n.\nIn a notable controversy,\ncryptocurrency exchange\nFTX\ncollapsed in November 2022, facing accusations of deceptive practices, improper handling of client assets, and insufficient risk controls.\nSam Bankman-Fried\n, FTX's founder and CEO, was later convicted of\nwire fraud\n,\nconspiracy\n, and\nmoney laundering\n.\nSee also\n[\nedit\n]\nArtificial intelligence in finance\nFinancial technology in Australia\nSmart contract\nTrade finance technology\nBanking as a service\nPayments as a service\nReferences and notes\n[\nedit\n]\n↑\n"The Global Financial Centres Index 37"\n.
