Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_at_risk\n==================================================\n\nEstimated potential loss for an investment under a given set of conditions\nNot to be confused with\nValuation risk\n.\nThe 5% Value at Risk of a hypothetical profit-and-loss probability density function\nValue at risk\n(\nVaR\n) is a measure of the risk of loss of investment/capital. It estimates how much a set of investments might lose (with a given probability), given normal market conditions, in a set time period such as a day. VaR is typically used by firms and regulators in the financial industry to gauge the amount of assets needed to cover possible losses.\nFor a given\nportfolio\n,\ntime horizon\n, and\nprobability\np\n, the\np\nVaR can be defined informally as the maximum possible loss during that time after excluding all worse outcomes whose combined probability is at most\np\n. This assumes\nmark-to-market\npricing, and no trading in the portfolio.\nFor example, if a portfolio of stocks has a one-day 5% VaR of $1 million, that means that there is a 0.05 probability that the portfolio will fall in value by $1 million or more over a one-day period if there is no trading. Informally, a loss of $1 million or more on this portfolio is expected on 1 day out of 20 days (because of 5% probability).\nMore formally,\np\nVaR is defined such that the probability of a loss greater than VaR is (at most)\np\nwhile the probability of a loss less than VaR is (at least)\n1-p\n. A loss which exceeds the VaR threshold is termed a "VaR breach".\nFor a fixed\np\n, the\np\nVaR does not assess the magnitude of loss when a VaR breach occurs and therefore is considered by some to be a questionable metric for risk management. For instance, assume someone makes a bet that flipping a coin seven times will not give seven heads. The terms are that he wins $100 if this does not happen (with probability 127/128) and he loses $12,700 if it does happen (with probability 1/128). That is, the possible loss amounts are $0 or $12,700. The 1% VaR is then $0, because the probability of any loss at all is 1/128 which is less than 1%. They are, however, exposed to a possible loss of $12,700 which can be expressed as the\np\nVaR for any\np ≤ 0.78125% (1/128)\n.\nVaR has four main uses in\nfinance\n:\nrisk management\n, financial\ncontrol\n,\nfinancial reporting\nand computing\nregulatory capital\n. VaR is sometimes used in non-financial applications as well.\nHowever, it is a controversial risk management tool.\nImportant related ideas are economic capital,\nbacktesting\n,\nstress testing\n,\nexpected shortfall\n, and\ntail conditional expectation\n.\nDetails\n[\nedit\n]\nCommon parameters for VaR are 1% and 5% probabilities and one day and two week horizons, although other combinations are in use.\nThe reason for assuming normal markets and no trading, and to restricting loss to things measured in\ndaily accounts\n, is to make the loss\nobservable\n. In some extreme financial events it can be impossible to determine losses, either because market prices are unavailable or because the loss-bearing institution breaks up. Some longer-term consequences of disasters, such as lawsuits, loss of market confidence and employee morale and impairment of brand names can take a long time to play out, and may be hard to allocate among specific prior decisions. VaR marks the boundary between normal days and extreme events. Institutions can lose far more than the VaR amount; all that can be said is that they will not do so very often.\nThe probability level is about equally often specified as one minus the probability of a VaR break, so that the VaR in the example above would be called a one-day 95% VaR instead of one-day 5% VaR. This generally does not lead to confusion because the probability of VaR breaks is almost always small, certainly less than 50%.\nAlthough it virtually always represents a loss, VaR is conventionally reported as a positive number. A negative VaR would imply the portfolio has a high probability of making a profit, for example a one-day 5% VaR of negative\n$1 million\nimplies the portfolio has a 95% chance of making more than\n$1 million\nover the next day.\nAnother inconsistency is that VaR is sometimes taken to refer to profit-and-loss at the end of the period, and sometimes as the maximum loss at any point during the period. The original definition was the latter, but in the early 1990s when VaR was aggregated across trading desks and time zones, end-of-day valuation was the only reliable number so the former became the\nde facto\ndefinition. As people began using multiday VaRs in the second half of the 1990s, they almost always estimated the distribution at the end of the period only. It is also easier theoretically to deal with a point-in-time estimate versus a maximum over an interval. Therefore, the end-of-period definition is the most common both in theory and practice today.\nVarieties\n[\nedit\n]\nThe definition of VaR is\nnonconstructive\n; it specifies a\nproperty\nVaR must have, but not how to compute VaR. Moreover, there is wide scope for interpretation in the definition.\nThis has led to two broad types of VaR, one used primarily in\nrisk management\nand the other primarily for risk measurement. The distinction is not sharp, however, and hybrid versions are typically used in financial\ncontrol\n,\nfinancial reporting\nand computing\nregulatory capital\n.\nTo a\nrisk manager\n, VaR is a system, not a number. The system is run periodically (usually daily) and the published number is compared to the computed price movement in opening positions over the time horizon. There is never any subsequent adjustment to the published VaR, and there is no distinction between VaR breaks caused by input errors (including\nIT\nbreakdowns,\nfraud\nand\nrogue trading\n), computation errors (including failure to produce a VaR on time) and market movements.\nA\nfrequentist\nclaim is made that the long-term frequency of VaR breaks will equal the specified probability, within the limits of sampling error, and that the VaR breaks will be\nindependent\nin time and independent of the level of VaR. This claim is validated by a\nbacktest\n, a comparison of published VaRs to actual price movements. In this interpretation, many different systems could produce VaRs with equally good backtests, but wide disagreements on daily VaR values.\nFor risk measurement a number is needed, not a system. A\nBayesian probability\nclaim is made that given the information and beliefs at the time, the\nsubjective probability\nof a VaR break was the specified level. VaR is adjusted after the fact to correct errors in inputs and computation, but not to incorporate information unavailable at the time of computation.\nIn this context, "backtest" has a different meaning. Rather than comparing published VaRs to actual market movements over the period of time the system has been in operation, VaR is retroactively computed on scrubbed data over as long a period as data are available and deemed relevant. The same position data and pricing models are used for computing the VaR as determining the price movements.\nAlthough some of the sources listed here treat only one kind of VaR as legitimate, most of the recent ones seem to agree that risk management VaR is superior for making short-term and tactical decisions in the present, while risk measurement VaR should be used for understanding the past, and making medium term and strategic decisions for the future. When VaR is used for\nfinancial control\nor\nfinancial reporting\nit should incorporate elements of both. For example, if a\ntrading desk\nis held to a VaR limit, that is both a risk-management rule for deciding what risks to allow today, and an input into the risk measurement computation of the desk's risk-adjusted\nreturn\nat the end of the reporting period.\nIn governance\n[\nedit\n]\nVaR can also be applied to\ngovernance\nof endowments, trusts, and pension plans. Essentially, trustees adopt portfolio Values-at-Risk metrics for the entire pooled account and the diversified parts individually managed. Instead of probability estimates they simply define maximum levels of acceptable loss for each. Doing so provides an easy metric for oversight and adds accountability as managers are then directed to manage, but with the additional constraint to avoid losses within a defined risk parameter. VaR utilized in this manner adds relevance as well as an easy way to monitor risk measurement control far more intuitive than Standard Deviation of Return. Use of VaR in this context, as well as a worthwhile critique on board governance practices as it relates to investment management oversight in general can be found in\nBest Practices in Governance.\nSEC Rule 18f-4 requires certain funds that use derivatives to comply with a relative VaR test as part of the fund’s leverage risk management framework. A fund relying on the rule generally must comply with an outer limit on fund leverage risk based on value-at-risk, or “VaR.” This outer limit is based on a relative VaR test that compares the fund’s VaR to the VaR of a “designated reference portfolio” for that fund. A fund generally can use either an index that meets certain requirements or the fund’s own securities portfolio (excluding derivatives transactions) as its designated reference portfolio. If the fund’s derivatives risk manager reasonably determines that a designated reference portfolio would not provide an appropriate reference portfolio for purposes of the relative VaR test, the fund would be required to comply with an absolute VaR test. This sometimes leads to confusion. Sources earlier than 1995 usually emphasize the risk measure, later sources are more likely to emphasize the metric.\nThe VaR risk measure defines risk as\nmark-to-market\nloss on a fixed portfolio over a fixed time horizon. There are many alternative risk measures in finance. Given the inability to use mark-to-market (which uses market prices to define loss) for future performance, loss is often defined (as a substitute) as change in\nfundamental value\n. For example, if an institution holds a\nloan\nthat declines in market price because\ninterest\nrates go up, but has no change in cash flows or credit quality, some systems do not recognize a loss. Also some try to incorporate the\neconomic\ncost of harm not measured in daily\nfinancial statements\n, such as loss of market confidence or employee morale, impairment of brand names or lawsuits.\nRather than assuming a static portfolio over a fixed time horizon, some risk measures incorporate the dynamic effect of expected trading (such as a\nstop loss order\n) and consider the expected holding period of positions.\nThe VaR risk metric summarizes the\ndistribution\nof possible losses by a\nquantile\n, a point with a specified probability of greater losses. A common alternative metric is\nexpected shortfall\n.\nVaR risk management\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nFinancial risk management § Investment banking\nSupporters of VaR-based risk management claim the first and possibly greatest benefit of VaR is the improvement in\nsystems\nand modeling it forces on an institution. In 1997,\nPhilippe Jorion\nwrote\n:\n[T]he greatest benefit of VAR lies in the imposition of a structured methodology for critically thinking about risk. Institutions that go through the process of computing their VAR are forced to confront their exposure to financial risks and to set up a proper risk management function. Thus the process of getting to VAR may be as important as the number itself.\nPublishing a daily number, on-time and with specified\nstatistical\nproperties holds every part of a trading organization to a high objective standard. Robust backup systems and default assumptions must be implemented. Positions that are reported, modeled or priced incorrectly stand out, as do data feeds that are inaccurate or late and systems that are too-frequently down. Anything that affects profit and loss that is left out of other reports will show up either in inflated VaR or excessive VaR breaks. "A risk-taking institution that\ndoes not\ncompute VaR might escape disaster, but an institution that\ncannot\ncompute VaR will not."\nThe second claimed benefit of VaR is that it separates risk into two regimes. Inside the VaR limit, conventional\nstatistical\nmethods are reliable. Relatively short-term and specific data can be used for analysis. Probability estimates are meaningful because there are enough data to test them. In a sense, there is no true risk because these are a sum of many\nindependent\nobservations with a left bound on the outcome. For example, a casino does not worry about whether red or black will come up on the next roulette spin. Risk managers encourage productive risk-taking in this regime, because there is little true cost. People tend to worry too much about these risks because they happen frequently, and not enough about what might happen on the worst days.\nOutside the VaR limit, all bets are off. Risk should be analyzed with\nstress testing\nbased on long-term and broad market data.\nProbability statements are no longer meaningful.\nKnowing the distribution of losses beyond the VaR point is both impossible and useless. The risk manager should concentrate instead on making sure good plans are in place to limit the loss if possible, and to survive the loss if not.\nOne specific system uses three regimes.\nOne to three times VaR are normal occurrences. Periodic VaR breaks are expected. The loss distribution typically has\nfat tails\n, and there might be more than one break in a short period of time. Moreover, markets may be abnormal and trading may exacerbate losses, and losses taken may not be measured in daily\nmarks\n, such as lawsuits, loss of employee morale and market confidence and impairment of brand names. An institution that cannot deal with three times VaR losses as routine events probably will not survive long enough to put a VaR system in place.\nThree to ten times VaR is the range for\nstress testing\n. Institutions should be confident they have examined all the foreseeable events that will cause losses in this range, and are prepared to survive them. These events are too rare to estimate probabilities reliably, so risk/return calculations are useless.\nForeseeable events should not cause losses beyond ten times VaR. If they do they should be\nhedged\nor insured, or the business plan should be changed to avoid them, or VaR should be increased. It is hard to run a business if foreseeable losses are orders of magnitude larger than very large everyday losses. It is hard to plan for these events because they are out of scale with daily experience.\nAnother reason VaR is useful as a metric is due to its ability to compress the riskiness of a portfolio to a single number, making it comparable across different portfolios (of different assets). Within any portfolio it is also possible to isolate specific positions that might better hedge the portfolio to reduce, and minimise, the VaR.\nComputation methods\n[\nedit\n]\nVaR can be estimated either parametrically (for example,\nvariance\n-\ncovariance\nVaR or\ndelta\n-\ngamma\nVaR) or nonparametrically (for examples, historical\nsimulation\nVaR or\nresampled\nVaR).\nNonparametric methods of VaR estimation are discussed in Markovich\nand Novak.\nA comparison of a number of strategies for VaR prediction is given in Kuester et al.\nA McKinsey report\npublished in May 2012 estimated that 85% of large banks were using\nhistorical simulation\n. The other 15% used\nMonte Carlo methods\n(often applying\na PCA decomposition\n).\nBacktesting\n[\nedit\n]\nBacktesting is the process to determine the accuracy of VaR forecasts vs. actual portfolio profit and losses.
A key advantage to VaR over most other measures of risk such as\nexpected shortfall\nis the availability of several\nbacktesting\nprocedures for validating a set of VaR forecasts. Early examples of backtests can be found in Christoffersen (1998),\nlater generalized by Pajhede (2017),\nwhich models a "hit-sequence" of losses greater than the VaR and proceed to tests for these "hits" to be independent from one another and with a correct probability of occurring. E.g. a 5% probability of a loss greater than VaR should be observed over time when using a 95% VaR, these hits should occur independently.\nA number of other backtests are available which model the time between hits in the hit-sequence, see Christoffersen and Pelletier (2004),\nHaas (2006),\nTokpavi et al. (2014).\nand Pajhede (2017)\nAs pointed out in several of the papers, the asymptotic distribution is often poor when considering high levels of coverage, e.g. a 99% VaR, therefore the parametric bootstrap method of Dufour (2006)\nis often used to obtain correct size properties for the tests. Backtest toolboxes are available in Matlab,\nor\nR\n—though only the first implements the parametric bootstrap method.\nThe second pillar of\nBasel II\nincludes a\nbacktesting\nstep to validate the VaR figures.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nThe problem of risk measurement is an old one in\nstatistics\n,\neconomics\nand\nfinance\n. Financial risk management has been a concern of regulators and financial executives for a long time as well. Retrospective analysis has found some VaR-like concepts in this history. But VaR did not emerge as a distinct concept until the late 1980s. The triggering event was the stock market\ncrash of 1987\n. This was the first major financial crisis in which a lot of academically-trained\nquants\nwere in high enough positions to worry about firm-wide survival.\nThe crash was so unlikely given standard\nstatistical\nmodels, that it called the entire basis of\nquant\nfinance into question. A reconsideration of history led some quants to decide there were recurring crises, about one or two per decade, that overwhelmed the statistical assumptions embedded in models used for\ntrading\n,\ninvestment management\nand\nderivative\npricing. These affected many markets at once, including ones that were usually not\ncorrelated\n, and seldom had discernible economic cause or warning (although after-the-fact explanations were plentiful).\nMuch later, they were named "\nBlack Swans\n" by\nNassim Taleb\nand the concept extended far beyond\nfinance\n.\nIf these events were included in\nquantitative analysis\nthey dominated results and led to strategies that did not work day to day. If these events were excluded, the profits made in between "Black Swans" could be much smaller than the losses suffered in the crisis. Institutions could fail as a result.\nVaR was developed as a systematic way to segregate extreme events, which are studied qualitatively over long-term history and broad market events, from everyday price movements, which are studied quantitatively using short-term data in specific markets. It was hoped that "Black Swans" would be preceded by increases in estimated VaR or increased frequency of VaR breaks, in at least some markets. The extent to which this has proven to be true is controversial.\nAbnormal markets and trading were excluded from the VaR estimate in order to make it observable.\nIt is not always possible to define loss if, for example, markets are closed as after\n9/11\n, or severely illiquid, as happened several times in 2008.\nLosses can also be hard to define if the risk-bearing institution fails or breaks up.\nA measure that depends on traders taking certain actions, and avoiding other actions, can lead to\nself reference\n.\nThis is risk management VaR. It was well established in\nquantitative trading\ngroups at several financial institutions, notably\nBankers Trust\n, before 1990, although neither the name nor the definition had been standardized. There was no effort to aggregate VaRs across trading desks.\nThe financial events of the early 1990s found many firms in trouble because the same underlying bet had been made at many places in the firm, in non-obvious ways. Since many trading desks already computed risk management VaR, and it was the only common risk measure that could be both defined for all businesses and aggregated without strong assumptions, it was the natural choice for reporting firmwide risk.\nJ. P. Morgan\nCEO\nDennis Weatherstone\nfamously called for a "4:15 report" that combined all firm\nrisk\non one page, available within 15 minutes of the market close.\nRisk measurement VaR was developed for this purpose. Development was most extensive at\nJ. P. Morgan\n, which published the methodology and gave free access to estimates of the necessary underlying parameters in 1994. This was the first time VaR had been exposed beyond a relatively small group of\nquants\n. Two years later, the methodology was spun off into an independent for-profit business now part of RiskMetrics Group (now part of\nMSCI\n).\nIn 1997, the\nU.S. Securities and Exchange Commission\nruled that public corporations must disclose quantitative information about their\nderivatives\nactivity. Major\nbanks\nand dealers chose to implement the rule by including VaR information in the notes to their\nfinancial statements\n.\nWorldwide adoption of the\nBasel II Accord\n, beginning in 1999 and nearing completion today, gave further impetus to the use of VaR. VaR is the preferred\nmeasure\nof\nmarket risk\n, and concepts similar to VaR are used in other parts of the accord.\nCriticism\n[\nedit\n]\nVaR has been controversial since it moved from trading desks into the public eye in 1994. A famous 1997\ndebate\nbetween\nNassim Taleb\nand Philippe Jorion set out some of the major points of contention. Taleb claimed VaR:\nIgnored 2,500 years of experience in favor of untested models built by non-traders\nWas charlatanism because it claimed to estimate the risks of rare events, which is impossible\nGave false confidence\nWould be exploited by traders\nIn 2008\nDavid Einhorn\nand\nAaron Brown\ndebated VaR in\nGlobal Association of Risk Professionals Review\n.\nEinhorn compared VaR to "an\nairbag\nthat works all the time, except when you have a car accident". He further charged that VaR:\nLed to excessive risk-taking and leverage at financial institutions\nFocused on the manageable risks near the center of the distribution and ignored the tails\nCreated an incentive to take "excessive but remote risks"\nWas "potentially catastrophic when its use creates a false sense of security among senior executives and watchdogs."\nNew York Times\nreporter\nJoe Nocera\nwrote an extensive piece\nRisk Mismanagement\non January 4, 2009, discussing the role VaR played in the\n2008 financial crisis\n. After interviewing risk managers (including several of the ones cited above) the article suggests that VaR was very useful to risk experts, but nevertheless exacerbated the crisis by giving false security to bank executives and regulators. A powerful tool for professional risk managers, VaR is portrayed as both easy to misunderstand, and dangerous when misunderstood.\nTaleb in 2009 testified in Congress asking for the banning of VaR for a number of reasons. One was that tail risks are non-measurable. Another was that for\nanchoring\nreasons VaR leads to higher risk taking.\nVaR is not\nsubadditive\n:\nVaR of a combined portfolio can be larger than the sum of the VaRs of its components.\nFor example, the average bank branch in the United States is robbed about once every ten years. A single-branch bank has about 0.0004% chance of being robbed on a specific day, so the risk of robbery would not figure into one-day 1% VaR. It would not even be within an order of magnitude of that, so it is in the range where the institution should not worry about it, it should insure against it and take advice from insurers on precautions. The whole point of insurance is to aggregate risks that are beyond individual VaR limits, and bring them into a large enough portfolio to get statistical predictability. It does not pay for a one-branch bank to have a security expert on staff.\nAs institutions get more branches, the risk of a robbery on a specific day rises to within an order of magnitude of VaR. At that point it makes sense for the institution to run internal stress tests and analyze the risk itself. It will spend less on insurance and more on in-house expertise. For a very large banking institution, robberies are a routine daily occurrence. Losses are part of the daily VaR calculation, and tracked statistically rather than case-by-case. A sizable in-house security department is in charge of prevention and control, the general risk manager just tracks the loss like any other cost of doing business.
As portfolios or institutions get larger, specific risks change from low-probability/low-predictability/high-impact to statistically predictable losses of low individual impact. That means they move from the range of far outside VaR, to be insured, to near outside VaR, to be analyzed case-by-case, to inside VaR, to be treated statistically.\nVaR is a static measure of risk. By definition, VaR is a particular characteristic of the probability distribution of the underlying (namely, VaR is essentially a quantile). For a dynamic measure of risk, see Novak,\nch. 10.\nThere are common abuses of VaR:\nAssuming that plausible losses will be less than some multiple (often three) of VaR. Losses can be extremely large.\nReporting a VaR that has not passed a\nbacktest\n. Regardless of how VaR is computed, it should have produced the correct number of breaks (within\nsampling error\n) in the past.
