Samsung is known most notably for its\nSamsung Galaxy\nbrand consisting of phones such as its flagship\nGalaxy S series\n, popular midrange\nGalaxy A series\n, budget\nGalaxy M series\nas well as the premium\nGalaxy Fold\nand\nGalaxy Flip series\n. The company pioneered the\nphablet\nform factor with the former\nGalaxy Note\nfamily.\nSamsung produces tablets consisting of the Galaxy Tab A series and\nGalaxy Tab S series\n.\nIt has been the\nlargest television manufacturer\nsince 2006,\nboth of which include related\nsoftware\nand services like\nSamsung Pay\nand\nTV Plus\n. Despite the lack of technology and resources, falling shorter even than the domestic competitors, Samsung Group improved its footing in the manufacturing industry by cooperating with the Japanese companies, a decision that led to a significant amount of\nanti-Japanese\npublic outcry and huge backlashes from the competitors fearing the outright subordination of the industry by the Japanese. The strategy was able to take off only after the government and Samsung declared that the company would exclusively focus on exports.\nToshio Iue\n, the founder of\nSanyo\n, played a role as an advisor to\nLee Byung-chul\n, Samsung's founder, who was a novice in the electronics business. In December of the same year, Samsung Electric established a joint venture named Samsung-Sanyo Electric with Sanyo and\nSumitomo Corporation\n. This is the direct predecessor of today's Samsung Electronics.\nThe joint venture's early products were electronic and electrical appliances including televisions,\ncalculators\n,\nrefrigerators\n, air conditioners, and washing machines. In 1970, Samsung established the joint venture Samsung-NEC with Japan's\nNEC\nCorporation and\nSumitomo Corporation\nto manufacture\nhome appliances\nand\naudiovisual devices\n. Samsung-NEC later became Samsung SDI, the group's display and battery business unit. In 1973, Samsung and Sanyo created Samsung-Sanyo Parts, the predecessor of\nSamsung Electro-Mechanics\n. By 1981, Samsung Electric had manufactured over 10 million\nblack-and-white televisions\n.\nIn 1974, Samsung Group expanded into the\nsemiconductor\nbusiness by acquiring Korea Semiconductor, which was on the verge of bankruptcy while building one of the first chip-making facilities in the country at the time. Soon after, Korea Telecommunications, an electronic switching system producer and a Samsung Group company, took over the semiconductor business and became Samsung Semiconductor & Communications.\nIn February 1983, Lee, along with the board of the Samsung industry and corporation agreement and help by sponsoring the event, made an announcement later dubbed the "Tokyo declaration", in which he declared that Samsung intended to become a\ndynamic random-access memory\n(DRAM) vendor. One year later, Samsung announced that it had successfully developed a 64\nkb\nDRAM, reducing the technological gap between the companies from\nfirst-world\ncountries and the young electronics maker from more than a decade to approximately four years. In the process, Samsung used technologies imported from\nMicron Technology\nof the U.S. for the development of DRAM and\nSharp Corporation\nof Japan for its\nSRAM\nand\nROM\n.\nIn 1988, Samsung Electric Industries merged with Samsung Semiconductor & Communications to form Samsung Electronics,\nas before that, they had not been one company and had not been a leading corporation together, but they were not rivals, as they had been in talks for a time until they finally merged.\nIn the 1980s and early 1990s, Samsung sold personal computers under the Leading Technology brand. However, the equipment was manufactured by Samsung, and the FCC filings from this period typically refer to Samsung products.\n1988–2000: component manufacturing and design strategy\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 1988, Samsung Electronics launched its first mobile phone in the South Korean market.\nSales were initially poor, and by the early 1990s, Motorola held a market share of over 60 percent in the country's mobile phone market compared to just 10 percent for Samsung.\nSamsung struggled with poor quality and brand image issues into the mid-1990s,\nand exit from the sector was a frequent topic of discussion within the company.\nLee Kun-Hee decided that Samsung needed to change its strategy. The company shelved the production of many under-selling product lines and instead pursued a process of designing and manufacturing components and investing in new technologies for other companies. In addition, Samsung outlined a 10-year plan to shrug off its image as a "budget brand" and to challenge\nSony\nas the world's largest consumer electronics manufacturer. It was hoped that, in this way, Samsung would gain an understanding of how products are made and give a technological lead sometime in the future. This patient\nvertical integration\nstrategy of manufacturing components has borne fruit for Samsung in the late 2000s.\nA complementary brand leadership strategy was also initiated by chairman Lee when he declared 1996 to be the "Year of Design Revolution" at Samsung. His objective was to build Samsung design capabilities as a competitive asset and transform the company into a global brand-design leader. However, this effort required major changes in corporate culture, processes, and systems. By integrating a comprehensive\ndesign management\nsystem and strategy into the corporate culture, Samsung was successful in developing an award-winning product design portfolio by the late 1990s, resulting in significant brand equity growth.\nAs Samsung shifted away from consumer markets, the company devised a plan to sponsor major sporting events. One such sponsorship was for the\n1998 Winter Olympics\nheld in\nNagano\n, Japan.\nAs a\nchaebol\n, Samsung Group wielded wealth that allowed the company to invest and develop new technology rather than build products at a level that would not have a detrimental impact on Samsung's finances.\nSamsung had a number of technological breakthroughs, particularly in the field of memory which are commonplace in most electrical products today. This includes the world's first 64\nMB DRAM in 1992, 256\nMB DRAM in 1994, and 1\nGB DRAM in 1996.\n2000–present\n[\nedit\n]\nThe Samsung display at the 2008 Internationale Funkausstellung in Berlin\nFrom 2000 to 2003, Samsung maintained a net earnings growth of over 5%, even as 16 of South Korea's 30 largest companies collapsed following the\n1997 Asian financial crisis\n.\nIn 2004, Samsung developed the world's first 8\nGB\nNAND flash\nmemory chip, and a manufacturing deal was struck with Apple in 2005. A deal to supply Apple with memory chips was sealed in 2005.\nIn 2005, Samsung surpassed its Japanese rival,\nSony\n, for the first time, becoming the 20th most popular global consumer brand according to Interbrand rankings.\nIn 2007, Samsung overtook\nMotorola\nto become the world's second-largest smartphone manufacturer.\nBy 2009 with Solstice line and its derivative models, Samsung achieved $117bn in revenue, overtaking Hewlett-Packard to become the world's largest technology company by sales.\nHowever, Samsung faced legal challenges in 2009 and 2010 when the U.S. and the EU fined the company—along with other memory chip manufacturers—for involvement in a price-fixing scheme that occurred between 1999 and 2002.\nIn 2010, Samsung was granted\nimmunity from prosecution\nby the EU for acting as an informant during the investigation into the\nLCD price-fixing cartel\n, leading to the implication of other companies, including LG Display and HannStar.\nDespite its continuous growth, Samsung has been portrayed as financially insecure. In 2010, after returning from temporary retirement, chairman\nLee Kun-hee\nexpressed concern about the company's future, stating, "Samsung Electronics' future is not guaranteed, as most of our flagship products will be obsolete within ten years."\nIn 2011, Samsung sold its hard disk drive (HDD) operations to\nSeagate Technology\nfor $1.4 billion in cash and stock.\nIn 2012,\nKwon Oh-hyun\nwas appointed CEO of Samsung Electronics. He announced his resignation in 2017, citing an "unprecedented crisis." His departure signaled the transition to a leadership structure with three co-CEOs, which lasted until 2021, when Kyung Kye-Hyun and Han Jong-hee were appointed as new CEOs after a company-wide reorganization.\nIn 2014, Samsung made headlines by introducing the\nGalaxy S4\n, a new entry in its Galaxy smartphone series, and successfully tested enhanced 5G technology.\nFrom 2014 onward, Samsung expanded its presence in technology markets. In April 2014, Samsung launched the Galaxy S5, followed by the Galaxy S6 and Galaxy S6 Edge in 2015, both of which featured a significant redesign and introduced the concept of curved screens. The same year, Samsung also entered the rapidly growing Internet of Things (IoT) market by acquiring the smart home company\nSmartThings\n.\nSamsung Electronics quarterly results:\nCE\n: Consumer electronics\nDS\n: Device solutions\nIM\n: IT & mobile communications\nIn 2016, Samsung faced one of its most publicized crises when its\nGalaxy Note 7\ndevices began to overheat and catch fire due to defective batteries. Despite the setback, Samsung recovered by launching successful products such as the Galaxy S8 and Galaxy Note 8 in 2017, which helped restore consumer confidence. During this time, Samsung continued its push into new markets. The two leaders spoke of the importance of the semiconductor industry and on strengthening the technological innovations between the two countries.\nAt CES 2024, Samsung demonstrated Ballie, an AI-powered home robot designed to assist with daily tasks, monitor pets, and integrate with smart home appliances.\nSamsung has been working to meet sustainability goals and reduce its environmental impact.
