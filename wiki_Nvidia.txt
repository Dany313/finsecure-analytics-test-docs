For the educational platform, see\nI-Ready\n.\nNvidia Corporation\n(\n/\nɛ\nn\nˈ\nv\nɪ\nd\ni\nə\n/\nen-\nVID\n-ee-ə\n) is an American\nmultinational\ntechnology company headquartered in\nSanta Clara, California\n. The company develops\ngraphics processing units\n(GPUs),\nsystems on chips\n(SoCs), and\napplication programming interfaces\n(APIs) for\ndata science\n,\nhigh-performance computing\n,\nartificial intelligence\n(AI), and mobile and automotive applications.\nFounded in 1993 by\nJensen Huang\n,\nChris Malachowsky\n, and\nCurtis Priem\n, Nvidia has been widely described as a\nBig Tech\ncompany.\nOriginally focused on GPUs for video games, Nvidia broadened their use into other markets, including\nartificial intelligence\n(AI), professional visualization, and\nsupercomputing\n. The company's product lines include\nGeForce\nGPUs for gaming and creative workloads, and professional GPUs for\nedge computing\n, scientific research, and industrial applications. As of the first quarter of 2025, Nvidia held a 92% share of the discrete desktop and laptop GPU market.\nIn the early-to-mid 2000s, the company invested over a billion dollars to develop\nCUDA\n, a software platform and API (Application Programming Interfaces) that enabled GPUs to run massively\nparallel programs\nfor a broad range of compute-intensive applications.\nAs a result, as of 2025, Nvidia controlled more than 80% of the market for GPUs used in training and deploying AI models,\nand provided chips for over 75% of the world's\nTOP500\nsupercomputers.\nThe company has also expanded into gaming hardware and services, with products such as the\nShield Portable\n,\nShield Tablet\n, and\nShield TV\n, and operates the\nGeForce Now\ncloud gaming service.\nFurthermore, it has developed the\nTegra\nline of mobile processors for smartphones, tablets, and automotive infotainment systems.\nIn 2023, Nvidia became the seventh U.S. company to reach a\nUS$1 trillion valuation\n.\nIn 2025, amid increased demand for\nAI data center\nhardware in the midst of the\nAI boom\n, Nvidia became the first company in the world to surpass US$4 trillion\nand US$5 trillion in\nmarket capitalization\n.\nBecause of its market capitalization and role in major technology indexes, Nvidia has been selected as one of\nBloomberg\n's "\nMagnificent Seven\n", the seven biggest companies on the stock market in these regards.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nFounding\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nDenny's\nroadside diner in\nSan Jose, California\n, where Nvidia's three co-founders agreed to start the company in late 1992\nNvidia's former headquarters which was home to the company through most of its pre-AI period (still in use)\nAerial view of Endeavor, the first of the two new Nvidia headquarters buildings, in\nSanta Clara, California\n, in 2017\nEntrance of Endeavor headquarters building in 2018\nNvidia was founded on April 5, 1993,\nby Jensen Huang, a\nTaiwanese-American\nelectrical engineer who was previously the director of CoreWare at\nLSI Logic\nand a\nmicroprocessor\ndesigner at\nAMD\n;\nChris Malachowsky\n, an engineer who worked at\nSun Microsystems\n; and\nCurtis Priem\n, who was previously a senior staff engineer and graphics chip designer at\nIBM\nand Sun Microsystems.\nIn late 1992, the three men agreed to start the company in a meeting at a\nDenny's\nroadside diner on Berryessa Road in East\nSan Jose\n.\nAt the time, Malachowsky and Priem were frustrated with Sun's management and were looking to leave, but Huang was on "firmer ground",\nin that he was already running his own division at LSI.\nThe three co-founders discussed a vision of the future which was so compelling that Huang decided to leave LSI\nand become the chief executive officer of their new startup.\nThe three co-founders envisioned graphics-based processing as the best trajectory for tackling challenges that had eluded general-purpose computing methods.\nAs Huang later explained: "We also observed that video games were simultaneously one of the most computationally challenging problems and would have incredibly high sales volume. Those two conditions don't happen very often. Video games was our\nkiller app\n- a flywheel to reach large markets funding huge\nR&D\nto solve massive computational problems."\nThe first problem was who would quit first. Huang's wife, Lori, did not want him to resign from LSI unless Malachowsky resigned from Sun at the same time, and Malachowsky's wife, Melody, felt the same way about Huang.\nPriem broke that deadlock by resigning first from Sun, effective December 31, 1992.\nAccording to Priem, this put pressure on Huang and Malachowsky to not leave him to "flail alone", so they gave notice too.\nHuang left LSI and "officially joined Priem on February 17", which was also Huang's 30th birthday, while Malachowsky left Sun in early March.\nIn early 1993, the three founders began working together on their new startup in Priem's townhouse in\nFremont, California\n.\nWith $40,000 in the bank (\nequivalent to $89,000\nin 2025\n), the company was born.\nThe company subsequently received $20 million of venture capital funding from\nSequoia Capital\n,\nSutter Hill Ventures\n, and others.\nDuring the late 1990s, Nvidia was one of 70 startup companies pursuing the idea that graphics acceleration for video games was the path to the future.\nOnly two survived: Nvidia and\nATI Technologies\n, the latter of which merged into AMD.\nNvidia initially had no name.\nPriem's first idea was "Primal Graphics", a\nsyllabic abbreviation\nof two of the founders' last names, but that left out Huang.\nThey soon discovered it was impossible to create a workable name with syllables from all three founders' names, after considering "Huaprimal", "Prihuamal", "Malluapri", etc.\nThe next idea came from Priem's idea for the name of Nvidia's first product.\nPriem originally wanted to call it the "GXNV", as in the "next version" of the GX graphics chips which he had worked on at Sun.\nThen Huang told Priem to "drop the GX", resulting in the name "NV".\nPriem made a list of words with the letters "NV" in them.\nAt one point, Malachowsky and Priem wanted to call the company NVision, but that name was already taken by a manufacturer of toilet paper.\nBoth Priem\nand Huang have taken credit for coming up with the name Nvidia,\nfrom "\ninvidia\n", the Latin word for "envy".\nAfter the company outgrew Priem's townhouse, its original headquarters office was in\nSunnyvale, California\n.\nFirst graphics accelerator\n[\nedit\n]\nNvidia's first graphics accelerator, the\nNV1\n, was designed to process\nquadrilateral\nprimitives\n(\nforward texture mapping\n), a feature that set it apart from competitors, who preferred triangle primitives.\nHowever, when\nMicrosoft\nintroduced the\nDirectX\nplatform, it chose not to support any other graphics software and announced that its\nDirect3D\nAPI would exclusively support triangles.\nAs a result, the NV1 failed to gain traction in the market.\nNvidia had also entered into a partnership with\nSega\nto supply the graphics chip for the\nDreamcast\nconsole and worked on the project for about a year. However, Nvidia's technology was already lagging behind competitors. This placed the company in a difficult position: continue working on a chip that was likely doomed to fail or abandon the project, risking financial collapse.\nIn a pivotal moment, Sega's president,\nShoichiro Irimajiri\n, visited Huang in person to inform him that Sega had decided to choose another vendor for the Dreamcast. However, Irimajiri believed in Nvidia's potential and persuaded Sega's management to invest $5 million into the company. Huang later reflected that this funding was all that kept Nvidia afloat, and that Irimajiri's "understanding and generosity gave us six months to live".\nIn 1996, Huang laid off more than half of Nvidia's employees-reducing headcount from 100 to 40-and focused the company's remaining resources on developing a graphics accelerator product optimized for processing triangle primitives: the\nRIVA 128\n.\nBy the time the RIVA 128 was released in August 1997, Nvidia had only enough money left for one month's payroll.\nThe sense of impending failure became so pervasive that it gave rise to Nvidia's unofficial company motto: "Our company is thirty days from going out of business."\nHuang began internal presentations to Nvidia staff with those words for many years.\nNvidia sold about a million RIVA 128 units within four months,\nand used the revenue to fund development of its next generation of products.\nIn 1998, the release of the\nRIVA TNT\nhelped solidify Nvidia's reputation as a leader in graphics technology.\nPublic company\n[\nedit\n]\nNvidia went public on January 22, 1999.\nInvesting in Nvidia after it had already failed to deliver on its contract turned out to be Irimajiri's best decision as Sega's president. After Irimajiri left Sega in 2000, Sega sold its Nvidia stock for $15 million.\nIn late 1999, Nvidia released the\nGeForce 256\n(NV10), its first product expressly marketed as a GPU, which was most notable for introducing onboard\ntransformation and lighting\n(T&L) to consumer-level 3D hardware. Running at 120\nMHz and featuring four-pixel pipelines, it implemented advanced video acceleration,\nmotion compensation\n, and hardware sub-picture\nalpha blending\n. The GeForce outperformed existing products by a wide margin.\nBecause its products were performing well, Nvidia won the contract to develop the graphics hardware for Microsoft's Xbox game console, which included a $200 million advance. However, the project took many of its best engineers away from other projects. In the short term this did not matter, and the\nGeForce 2 GTS\nshipped in the summer of 2000.
