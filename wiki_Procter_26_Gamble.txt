For other uses, see\nPG\n.\nLogo used from 1992 to 2002.\nPrimary logo used since 2002 on P&G branded products, formerly used as a corporate logo until 2013.\nThe Procter & Gamble Company\n(\nP&G\n) is an American multinational\nconsumer goods\ncorporation\nincorporated and headquartered in\nCincinnati\n, Ohio.\nThe company operates five divisions:\nBeauty (18% of 2024 revenues), which includes\nHead & Shoulders\n,\nHerbal Essences\n,\nPantene\n, Rejoice,\nOlay\n,\nOld Spice\n, Safeguard,\nSecret\n, SK-II, and Native;\nGrooming (8% of 2024 revenues), which includes\nBraun\n,\nGillette\n, and Venus;\nHealth Care (14% of 2024 revenues), which includes\nCrest\n,\nOral-B\n,\nMetamucil\n,\nNeurobion\n,\nPepto-Bismol\n, and\nVicks\n;\nFabric & Home Care (36% of 2024 revenues), which includes\nAriel\n,\nDowny\n,\nGain\n,\nTide\n,\nCascade\n,\nDawn\n,\nFairy\n,\nFebreze\n,\nMr. Clean\n, and\nSwiffer\n;\nBaby, Feminine & Family Care (24% of 2024 revenues), which includes\nLuvs\n,\nPampers\n,\nAlways\n,\nTampax\n,\nBounty\n,\nCharmin\n, and Puffs.\nThe company owns brands that are in many cases the global brand leader in their category.\nMany of the brands have a\nmarket share\ngreater than 25%.\nThe company generates 48% of its sales in the United States and 52% of its sales in other countries.\nThe company manufactures 90% of its domestically sold merchandise in the United States.\nThe company is ranked 51st on the\nFortune\n500\nand 60th on the\nForbes\nGlobal 2000\n.\nThe company was founded in 1837 by\nWilliam Procter\nand\nJames Gamble\n.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nOrigins\n[\nedit\n]\nCandlemaker\nWilliam Procter\n, born in England, and soap maker\nJames Gamble\n, born in\nIreland\n, both emigrated to the US from the United Kingdom. They initially settled in\nCincinnati\n, Ohio, and met when they married sisters Olivia and Elizabeth Norris.\nAlexander Norris, their father-in-law, persuaded them to become business partners, and in 1837, Procter & Gamble was created.\nFrom 1858 to 1859, sales reached $1\nmillion. By that point, about 80 employees worked for Procter & Gamble. During the\nAmerican Civil War\n, the company won contracts to supply the\nUnion Army\nwith soap and candles. In addition to the increased profits experienced during the war, the military contracts introduced soldiers from all over the country to Procter & Gamble's products.\nIn the 1880s, Procter & Gamble began to market a new product, an inexpensive soap that floated in water.\nThe company called the soap\nIvory\n.\nWilliam Arnett Procter, William Procter's grandson, began a\nprofit-sharing\nprogram for the company's\nworkforce\nin 1887. By giving the workers a stake in the company, he correctly assumed that they would be less likely to go on strike.\nThe company began to build factories in other locations in the United States because the demand for products had outgrown the capacity of the Cincinnati facilities. The company's leaders began to diversify its products as well, and in 1911 the company began producing\nCrisco\n, a\nshortening\nmade of\nvegetable oils\nrather than\nanimal fats\n.\nBeginning in the 1880s, P&G advertised its wares in full-page advertisements in many general-interest magazines. By 1921, it had become a major international corporation with a diversified line of soaps, toiletries, and food products; in that year, its annual advertising budget reached $1 million. In the 1920s, P&G advertised its products on the new medium of radio and, from 1932 forward, was one of the biggest sponsors of daytime serials, which soon became known as\nsoap operas\n.\nIn the television era, P&G sponsored and produced some twenty soap operas across six decades; the division went on a hiatus after the end of\nAs the World Turns\nin 2010 before\nThe Gates\nlaunched on CBS in 2025 as a co-production with P&G.\nInternational expansion\n[\nedit\n]\nThe company moved into other countries, both in terms of manufacturing and product sales, becoming an international corporation with its 1930 acquisition of the\nThomas Hedley Co.\n,\nbased in\nNewcastle upon Tyne\n, England. Thomas Hedley was a company local to\nNewcastle upon Tyne\n, and was the start of P&Gs expansion from its American operations. After this acquisition, P&G moved into Hedley's Newcastle City Road site and had their UK Headquarters at Hedley House in Newcastle upon Tyne, until quite recently, when they moved to The Heights,\nBrooklands\n. It continued its UK operations by opening up a Manchester factory in 1933 (which expanded rapidly; 100% expansion by 1936), and constructing a London plant in 1937, however, it was Tyneside where P&G was solidly based. By 1948 these offices were proving inadequate for an expanding post-war business, and in 1953 P&G moved its UK administrative centre to purpose-built offices in\nGosforth\n, Newcastle. The building was named Hedley House, in remembrance of the roots of P&G in Tyneside. Numerous new products and brand names were introduced over time, and Procter & Gamble began branching out into new areas. The company introduced\nTide\nlaundry\ndetergent\nin 1946,\nPrell\nshampoo in 1947\nand Joy, the first liquid synthetic detergent in 1949.\nIn 1955, Procter & Gamble began selling the first toothpaste to contain\nfluoride\n, known as\nCrest\n.\nBranching out once again in 1957, the company purchased\npaper mills\nfrom\nCharmin\nand began manufacturing\ntoilet paper\nand other\ntissue paper\nproducts. The Hedley Research Laboratories on Whitley Road,\nLongbenton\nwere officially opened on June 11 by\nHugh Percy, 10th Duke of Northumberland\n. This site became known as Newcastle Innovation Centre (NIC) and\nas of 2013\nwas led by Charles Bragg. It cost £500,000 to build and equip. The site has been upgraded many times in the 50 years since its opening and currently focuses on the development of laundry detergents and machine dishwashing products for all parts of the world. Once again focusing on laundry, Procter & Gamble began making\nDowny\n-branded\nfabric softener\nin 1960 and Bounce fabric softener sheets in 1972.\nFrom 1957 to 1968, Procter & Gamble owned\nClorox\n, the leading American manufacturer of liquid bleach; however, the\nFederal Trade Commission\nchallenged the acquisition, and the U.S. Supreme Court decided against P&G in April 1967.\nOne of the most revolutionary products to come out on the market was the company's disposable\nPampers\ndiaper, first test-marketed in 1961, the same year Procter & Gamble came out with\nHead & Shoulders\n.\nPrior to this point,\ndisposable diapers\nwere not popular, although\nJohnson & Johnson\nhad developed a product called Chux. Babies always wore cloth diapers, which were leaky and labor-intensive to wash. Pampers provided a convenient alternative, albeit at the environmental cost of more waste requiring\nlandfilling\n. Amid the recent concerns parents have voiced on the ingredients in diapers, Pampers launched Pampers Pure collection in 2018, which is a "natural" diaper alternative.\nIn 1962 Shultons established an\nOld Spice\nplant at the\nNorthumberland\ntown of\nSeaton Delaval\n. When Shultons was acquired by Procter & Gamble in 1990, it became another part of P&G's presence on\nTyneside\n. The plant now manufactures many fragrances that P&G produce, both under its own brands and under licence. It is also the site for the 'company shop', where P&G staff and retirees can purchase P&G goods for a reduced price.\nIn 2000, the Gosforth offices were closed 43 years after their opening. This was done as part of a big corporate restructuring within P&G globally, and the UK's administrative centre became the Brooklands complex in\nWeybridge\n,\nSurrey\n. However, one of three global business service centres was established at\nCobalt Business Park\nin\nNorth Tyneside\n. P&G has one building on the park, offering financial services to P&G companies in Europe, the Middle East and Africa. As has been the tradition, one pays homage to Thomas Hedley Co. and is called New Hedley House.\nFurther developments\n[\nedit\n]\nProcter & Gamble acquired a number of other companies that diversified its product line and significantly increased profits. These acquisitions included\nFolgers Coffee\n, Norwich Eaton Pharmaceuticals (the makers of\nPepto-Bismol\n), Richardson-Vicks, Noxell (\nNoxzema\n), Shulton's\nOld Spice\n,\nMax Factor\n, the\nIams\nCompany, and\nPantene\n, among others. In 1994, the company made headlines for big losses resulting from leveraged positions in\ninterest rate derivatives\n, and subsequently sued\nBankers Trust\nfor fraud; this placed its management in the unusual position of testifying in court that they had entered into transactions that they were not capable of understanding. In 1996, P&G again made headlines when the\nFood and Drug Administration\napproved a new product developed by the company,\nOlestra\n. Also known by its brand name "Olean", Olestra is a lower-calorie substitute for fat in cooking\npotato chips\nand other snacks.\nIn January 2005, P&G announced the acquisition of\nGillette\n, forming the largest consumer goods company and placing\nUnilever\ninto second place.\nThis added brands such as Gillette razors,\nDuracell\n,\nBraun\n, and\nOral-B\nto its stable. The acquisition was approved by the\nEuropean Union\nand the\nFederal Trade Commission\n, with conditions to a spinoff of certain overlapping brands. The companies officially merged on October 1, 2005. In 2008, P&G branched into the record business with its sponsorship of Tag Records, as an endorsement for\nTAG Body Spray\n.\nP&G's dominance in many categories of consumer products necessitates its corporate strategists to account for the likelihood of one of its products\ncannibalizing the sales of another\nthrough\nbrand management\n.\nIn August 2009,\nWarner Chilcott\nacquired P&G's prescription-drug business for $3.1\nbillion.\nP&G exited the food business in 2012 when it sold its\nPringles\nsnack food business to\nKellogg's\nfor $2.75\nbillion after the $2.35\nbillion deal with former suitor\nDiamond Foods\nfell short.\nThe company had previously sold\nJif peanut butter\n, Crisco shortening and oils, and\nFolgers\ncoffee in separate transactions to fellow Ohio-based company\nSmucker's\n.\nIn April 2014, the company sold its\nIams\npet food business in all markets excluding Europe to\nMars, Inc.\nfor $2.9\nbillion.\nIt sold the European Iams business to\nSpectrum Brands\nin December 2014.\nRestructuring\n[\nedit\n]\nOn August 1, 2014, P&G announced it was streamlining the company, dropping and selling off around 100 brands from its product portfolio in order to focus on the remaining 65 brands,\nwhich produced 95% of the company's profits.\nA.G. Lafley\n, the company's chairman and CEO until October 2015, said the future P&G would be "a much simpler, much less complex company of leading brands that's easier to manage and operate".\nIn March 2015, the company divested its\nVicks\nVapoSteam U.S. liquid inhalant business to\nHelen of Troy\n, part of a brand-restructuring operation. Most people have these bacteria living in their bodies as harmless\ncommensals\nin places such as the nose, skin, and vagina. The disease can strike anyone, not only women, but the disease is often associated with\ntampons\n. In 1980, 814 menstrual-related TSS cases were reported; 38 deaths resulted from the disease. The majority of women in these cases were documented as using super-absorbent synthetic tampons, particularly the\nRely\ntampon created by Procter & Gamble.\nUnlike other tampons made of cotton and rayon, Rely used carboxymethylcellulose and compressed beads of\npolyester\nfor absorption.\nIn the summer of 1980, the\nCenters for Disease Control\nreleased a report explaining how these bacterial mechanisms were leading to TSS. They also stated that the Rely tampon was associated with TSS more than any other brand of tampon. In September 1980, Procter & Gamble voluntarily recalled its Rely brand of tampons from the market.\nSince the 1980s, reported cases of TSS have dramatically decreased.\nOther products\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2002, P&G was sued for its ads falsely suggesting to the consumers that the drug\nPrilosec\ncould cure heartburn in a day.\nIn December 2005, the Pharmaceutical division of P&G was involved in a dispute over research involving its osteoporosis drug\nActonel\n.
