Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company\n==================================================\n\nAmerican multinational beverage corporation\nThe Coca-Cola Company\nis an American\nmultinational corporation\nfounded in January 1892, headquartered in\nAtlanta\n, Georgia. It manufactures, sells and markets soft drinks including\nCoca-Cola\n, other\nnon-alcoholic beverage\nconcentrates and syrups, and alcoholic beverages. Its stock is listed on the\nNew York Stock Exchange\nand is a component of the\nDJIA\nand the\nS&P 500\nand\nS&P 100\nindices.\nCoca-Cola was developed in May 1886 by\npharmacist\nJohn Stith Pemberton\n. At the time it was introduced, the product contained the\nstimulants\ncocaine\nfrom\ncoca\nleaves and\ncaffeine\nfrom\nkola nuts\n, which together acted\nsynergistically\n. The coca and the kola are the source of the product name and led to Coca-Cola's promotion as a "healthy tonic". Pemberton had been severely wounded in the\nAmerican Civil War\nand had become addicted to the pain medication\nmorphine\n. At the time, cocaine was being promoted as a "cure" for opioid addiction, so he developed the beverage as a\npatent medicine\nin an effort to control his addiction.\nIn 1889, the formula and brand were sold for $2,300 (\nequivalent to $73,000\nin 2024\n) to\nAsa Griggs Candler\n, who incorporated The Coca-Cola Company in Atlanta in 1892.\nThe company has operated a\nfranchised\ndistribution system since 1889.\nThe company largely produces syrup concentrate, which is then sold to various\nbottlers\nthroughout the world who hold exclusive territories.\nA study published in\nScience Advances\nin 2024 found that The Coca-Cola Company was responsible for 11% of global branded\nplastic pollution\n(by count), which was a greater share of plastic pollution than any other company.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nCoca-Cola §\nHistory\nIn July 1886, pharmacist\nJohn Stith Pemberton\nfrom\nColumbus, Georgia\n, invented the original Coca-Cola drink, which was advertised as helpful in the relief of headaches and to be placed on sale primarily in drugstores as a medicinal beverage.\nPemberton had made many mixing experiments and reached his goal during the month of May, but the new product was as yet unnamed and uncarbonated.\nPemberton's bookkeeper,\nFrank Mason Robinson\n, is credited with naming the product and creating its logo.\nRobinson chose the name Coca-Cola because of its two main ingredients (coca leaves and kola nuts) and because it is an alliteration. John Pemberton had taken a break and left Robinson to make, promote, and sell Coca-Cola on his own. Robinson promoted the drink with the limited budget that he had and succeeded.\nAmerican businessman\nAsa Griggs Candler\npurchased the Coca-Cola formula and brand, forming The Coca-Cola Company in Atlanta in 1892. By 1895, Coca-Cola was being sold nationwide.\nIn 1919, the company was sold to\nErnest Woodruff\n's Trust Company of Georgia,\nafter which it was soon reincorporated under the\nDelaware General Corporation Law\nthat same year.\nCoca-Cola's first ad read, "Coca-Cola. Delicious! Refreshing! Exhilarating! Invigorating!"\nCandler was one of the first businessmen to use merchandising in his advertising strategy.\nAs of 1948, Coca-Cola had claimed about 60% of its market share.\nBy 1984, The Coca-Cola Company's market share decreased to 21.8% due to new competitors.\nThe company's 29-foot headquarters building in Atlanta, known as One Coca-Cola Plaza, was designed by\nFABRAP\nand completed in 1980.\nAcquisitions\n[\nedit\n]\nCoca-Cola acquired\nMinute Maid\nin 1960 for an undisclosed amount.\nIn 1982, it acquired the movie studio\nColumbia Pictures\nfor $692\nmillion.\nCoca-Cola then launched a series of entertainment takeovers, namely\nMerv Griffin Enterprises\nand\nEmbassy Communications\nin the mid-1980s, forming the Entertainment Business Sector, which would later merge with\nTri-Star Pictures\nto start out Columbia Pictures Entertainment, with CPE holding a stake in the company.\nCoca-Cola sold Columbia to\nSony\nfor $3\nbillion in 1989.\nIn 1986, Coke sold off two assets, namely Presto Products and Winker-Flexible Products, to an investment group led by E.O. Weinberg\nBottlers\n[\nedit\n]\nCoca-Cola\nIn general, The Coca-Cola Company and its subsidiaries only produce syrup concentrate, as well as source beverage bases, including coffee beans, tea leaves, juices, etc., which are then sold to various\nbottlers\nthroughout the world who hold a local Coca-Cola franchise.\nCoca-Cola bottlers, who hold territorially exclusive contracts with the company, produce the finished product in packages from the concentrate and beverage base, in combination with filtered water and sweeteners. The bottlers then sell, distribute, and merchandise the Coca-Cola product to retail stores, vending machines, restaurants, and food service distributors.\nOutside the United States, these bottlers also control the fountain business, while in the U.S., the company bypasses bottlers by manufacturing and selling fountain syrups directly to authorized fountain wholesalers and some fountain retailers.\nSince the 1980s, the company has actively encouraged the consolidation of bottlers, with the company often owning a share of these "anchor bottlers".\nIn January 2006, the company formed the Bottling Investments Group (BIG) to strategically invest in select bottling operations, temporarily taking them under Coca-Cola ownership and utilizing the leadership and resources of the company. Also, the company has accelerated re-franchising both company-owned bottlers and independent bottlers to consolidate their operations and move away from the capital-intensive and low-margin business of bottling, while maintaining minor share ownership of these consolidated bottlers and securing the right to nominate directors and executives.\nIn September 2015, the company announced the sale of several production plants and territories to large independent bottlers and the creation of the Coca-Cola National Product Supply System, which controls 95% of the territory in the United States.\nAs a result of these re-franchisings and bottler consolidations, large-scale multinational bottlers and a small number of U.S.-based bottlers now dominate the manufacturing and distribution of the company's products except in the territories managed by BIG bottlers.\nLarge-scale multinational bottlers include:\nCoca-Cola Europacific Partners PLC\n: Headquartered in the UK, it operates in 29 countries in Western Europe, Oceania, Indonesia, and Papua New Guinea. Residents of Samoa have seen an increase in plastic pollution since this switch has been made. Alternatives to plastic, such as aluminum, have also been overlooked, with Coca-Cola releasing a new plastic bottle slightly bigger than a can size.\nThe magazine\nForbes\nhas labeled Coca-Cola as the world's most polluting brand.\nThe company's global chief executive stated that "Coca-Cola has no plans to reduce its use of plastic bottles"\nand opposes\nbottle bill\nlegislation,\nas consumers prefer the plastic bottles that "reseal and are lightweight".\nA research study published in 2025 showed that Coca-Cola, together with\nPepsiCo\nand\nNestlé\ncontributed 14 to 21 million tons of plastic waste to aquatic environments in the time span 2000 to 2023.\nIn 2023, Coca-Cola's packaging composition included 47.7% plastic, 26% aluminum, and 10.4% glass. The company's efforts towards increasing the reuse of packaging have been limited, with only 1.2% of its packaging being reusable in the previous year.\nAdditionally, Coca-Cola has increased its use of virgin plastics by approximately 6% since 2019.\nShifting sustainability targets\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2018, Coca-Cola pledged to use 50% recycled materials in its packaging and to recycle the equivalent of 100% of its packaging by 2030.\nThat same year, the company started to sell reusable bottles in Brazil, which customers could return to the\npoint of sale\nfor a discount of subsequent purchases. The initiative was expanded to several other South American countries by 2022.\nIn 2019, the company's Swedish branch transitioned to solely using plastic bottles consisting of recycled material.\nIn December 2024, Coca-Cola announced changes to its sustainability goals, including revising its target to recycle the equivalent of 100% of its packaging by 2030 to a goal of recycling the equivalent of 70-75% of its packaging by 2035. Coca-Cola also revised their goal of having packaging produced with 50% recycled material by 2030 to being produced with 35-40% recycled material by 2035. The company acknowledged that it will not meet the goals as initially set.\nEnergy usage of corn syrup manufacturing\n[\nedit\n]\nCoca-Cola's use of\nhigh-fructose corn syrup\nis less sustainable when compared to producing sugary drinks with sugar cane. High-fructose corn syrup is carbon intensive due to the substantial amounts of fossil fuels required to power heavy machinery. The energy-intensive process of turning corn into high-fructose corn syrup expends large amounts of carbon, particularly during corn growth. Burning fossil fuels releases\nCO\n2\nand\nCH\n4\nwhich is the main source of energy for producing high-fructose corn syrup. The environmental impact of corn production can be attributed to three primary effects: field emissions, irrigation, and grain decay.\nWater usage\n[\nedit\n]\nCoca-Cola has also been under fire for depleting water sources through their high water usages. Documents from the government's water ministry reveal water levels remained stable from 1995 until 2000, when Coca-Cola was first operational.
