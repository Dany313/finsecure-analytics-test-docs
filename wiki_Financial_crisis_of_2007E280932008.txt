Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008\n==================================================\n\nWorldwide economic crisis\nThe\nbankruptcy of Lehman Brothers\n(\nheadquarters\npictured), the fourth-largest U.S. investment bank (behind\nGoldman Sachs\n,\nMorgan Stanley\n, and\nMerrill Lynch\n), on September 15, 2008, is often considered the climax of the 2008 financial crisis.\nThe\nTED spread\n, an indicator of perceived\ncredit risk\nin the financial system, increased significantly during the crisis. It spiked sharply in August 2007, remained volatile for a year, and spiked even higher in September 2008 to reach a record 4.65% on October 10, 2008.\nA major worldwide\nfinancial crisis\ncentered in the United States took place in 2008.  The causes included excessive\nspeculation\non property values by both homeowners and\nfinancial institutions\n, leading to the\n2000s United States housing bubble\n. This was exacerbated by\npredatory lending\nfor\nsubprime mortgages\nand by deficiencies in\nregulation\n.\nCash out refinancings\nhad fueled an increase in consumption that could no longer be sustained when home prices declined. The first phase of the crisis was the\nsubprime mortgage crisis\n, which began in early 2007, as\nmortgage-backed securities\n(MBS) tied to U.S.\nreal estate\n, and a vast web of\nderivatives\nlinked to those MBS collapsed in value. A\nliquidity crisis\nspread to global institutions by mid-2007 and climaxed with the\nbankruptcy of Lehman Brothers\nin September 2008, which triggered a\nstock market crash\nand\nbank runs\nin several countries.\nThe crisis exacerbated the\nGreat Recession\n, a\nglobal recession\nthat began in late-2007, as well as the\nUnited States bear market of 2007–2009\n. It was also a contributor to the\n2008–2011 Icelandic financial crisis\nand the\neuro area crisis\n.\nDuring the 1990s, the\nU.S. Congress\nhad passed legislation that intended to expand\naffordable housing\nthrough looser financing rules, and in 1999, parts of the\n1933 Banking Act\n(Glass–Steagall Act) were\nrepealed\n, enabling institutions to mix low-risk operations, such as\ncommercial banking\nand\ninsurance\n, with higher-risk operations such as\ninvestment banking\nand\nproprietary trading\n.\nAs the\nFederal Reserve\n("Fed") lowered the\nfederal funds rate\nfrom 2000 to 2003, institutions increasingly targeted low-income homebuyers, largely belonging to\nracial minorities\n, with high-risk loans;\nthis development went unattended by regulators.\nAs interest rates rose from 2004 to 2006, the cost of\nmortgages\nrose and the demand for housing fell; in early 2007, as more U.S. subprime mortgage holders began\ndefaulting\non their repayments, lenders went bankrupt, culminating in the bankruptcy of\nNew Century Financial\nin April. As demand and prices continued to fall, the\nfinancial contagion\nspread to global\ncredit markets\nby August 2007, and\ncentral banks\nbegan injecting\nliquidity\n. In March 2008,\nBear Stearns\n, the fifth-largest U.S. investment bank, was sold to\nJPMorgan Chase\nin a "\nfire sale\n" backed by Fed financing.\nIn response to the growing crisis, governments around the world deployed massive\nbailouts\nof financial institutions and used\nmonetary policy\nand\nfiscal policies\nto prevent an\neconomic collapse\nof the\nglobal financial system\n.\nBy July 2008,\nFannie Mae\nand\nFreddie Mac\n, companies which together owned or guaranteed half of the U.S. housing market, verged on collapse; the\nHousing and Economic Recovery Act of 2008\nenabled the federal government to seize them on September 7.\nLehman Brothers\n(the fourth-largest U.S. investment bank) filed for the largest bankruptcy in U.S. history on September 15, which was followed by a Fed bail-out of\nAmerican International Group\n(the country's largest insurer) the next day, and the seizure of\nWashington Mutual\nin the largest bank failure in U.S. history on September 25. On October 3, Congress passed the\nEmergency Economic Stabilization Act\n, authorizing the\nTreasury Department\nto purchase\ntoxic assets\nand bank stocks through the $700 billion\nTroubled Asset Relief Program\n(TARP). The Fed began a program of\nquantitative easing\nby buying treasury bonds and other assets, such as MBS, and the\nAmerican Recovery and Reinvestment Act\n, signed in February 2009 by newly elected president\nBarack Obama\n, included a range of measures intended to preserve existing jobs and create new ones. These initiatives combined, coupled with actions taken in other countries, ended the worst of the\nGreat Recession\nby mid-2009.\nAssessments of the crisis's impact in the U.S. vary, but suggest that some 8.7 million jobs were lost, causing unemployment to rise from 5% in 2007 to a high of 10% in October 2009. The percentage of citizens living in poverty rose from 12.5% in 2007 to 15.1% in 2010. The\nDow Jones Industrial Average\nfell by 53% between October 2007 and March 2009, and some estimates suggest that one in four households lost 75% or more of their\nnet worth\n. In 2010, the\nDodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act\nwas passed, overhauling financial regulations.\nIt was opposed by many\nRepublicans\n, and it was weakened by the\nEconomic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act\nin 2018. The\nBasel III\ncapital and liquidity standards were also adopted by countries around the world.\nBackground\n[\nedit\n]\nWorld map showing\nreal GDP growth rates\nfor 2009 (countries in brown were in recession)\nShare in GDP of U.S. financial sector since 1860\nThe crisis exacerbated the\nGreat Recession\n, a\nglobal recession\nthat began in mid-2007.\nIt was also followed by the\neuro area crisis\n, which began with the start of the\nGreek government-debt crisis\nin late 2009, and the\n2008–2011 Icelandic financial crisis\n, which involved the\nbank failure\nof all three of the major banks in Iceland and, relative to the size of its economy, was the largest economic collapse suffered by any country in history.\nIt was among the five worst financial crises the world had experienced and led to a loss of more than $2 trillion from the global economy.\nU.S. home mortgage debt relative to\nGDP\nincreased from an average of 46% during the 1990s to 73% during 2008, reaching $10.5 (~$\n15\ntrillion\nin\n2024\n) trillion.\nThe increase in\ncash out refinancings\n, as home values rose, fueled an increase in consumption that could no longer be sustained when home prices declined.\nMany financial institutions owned investments whose value was based on home mortgages such as\nmortgage-backed securities\n, or\ncredit derivatives\nused to insure them against failure, which declined in value significantly.\nThe\nInternational Monetary Fund\nestimated that large U.S. and European banks lost more than $1 trillion on\ntoxic assets\nand from bad loans from January 2007 to September 2009.\nLack of investor confidence in bank\nsolvency\nand declines in credit availability led to plummeting stock and\ncommodity\nprices in late 2008 and early 2009.\nThe crisis rapidly spread into a global economic shock, resulting in several\nbank failures\n.\nEconomies worldwide slowed during this period since credit tightened and international trade declined.\nHousing markets suffered and unemployment soared, resulting in\nevictions\nand\nforeclosures\n. Several businesses failed.\nFrom its peak in the second quarter of 2007 at $61.4 trillion, household wealth in the United States fell $11 trillion, to $50.4 trillion by the end of the first quarter of 2009, resulting in a decline in consumption, then a decline in business investment.\nIn the fourth quarter of 2008, the quarter-over-quarter decline in real GDP in the U.S. was 8.4%.\nThe U.S. unemployment rate peaked at 11.0% in October 2009, the highest rate since 1983 and roughly twice the pre-crisis rate. The average hours per work week declined to 33, the lowest level since the government began collecting the data in 1964.\nThe economic crisis started in the U.S. but spread to the rest of the world.\nU.S. consumption accounted for more than a third of the growth in global consumption between 2000 and 2007 and the rest of the world depended on the U.S. consumer as a source of demand.\nToxic securities were owned by corporate and institutional investors globally. Derivatives such as\ncredit default swaps\nalso increased the linkage between large financial institutions. The\nde-leveraging\nof financial institutions, as assets were sold to pay back obligations that could not be refinanced in frozen credit markets, further accelerated the solvency crisis and caused a decrease in international trade. Reductions in the growth rates of\ndeveloping countries\nwere due to falls in trade, commodity prices, investment and\nremittances\nsent from migrant workers (example: Armenia\n). States with fragile political systems feared that investors from Western states would withdraw their money because of the crisis.\nAs part of\nnational fiscal policy response to the Great Recession\n, governments and central banks, including the\nFederal Reserve\n, the\nEuropean Central Bank\nand the\nBank of England\n, provided then-unprecedented trillions of dollars in\nbailouts\nand\nstimulus\n, including expansive\nfiscal policy\nand\nmonetary policy\nto offset the decline in consumption and lending capacity, avoid a further collapse, encourage lending, restore faith in the integral\ncommercial paper\nmarkets, avoid the risk of a\ndeflationary spiral\n, and provide banks with enough funds to allow customers to make withdrawals.\nIn effect, the central banks went from being the "\nlender of last resort\n" to the "lender of only resort" for a significant portion of the economy. In some cases the Fed was considered the "buyer of last resort".\nDuring the fourth quarter of 2008, these central banks purchased US$2.5 trillion (~$\n3.57\ntrillion\nin\n2024\n)  of government debt and troubled private assets from banks. This was the largest liquidity injection into the credit market, and the largest monetary policy action in world history. Following a model initiated by the\n2008 United Kingdom bank rescue package\n,\nthe governments of European nations and the United States guaranteed the debt issued by their banks and raised the capital of their national banking systems, ultimately purchasing $1.5 trillion newly issued\npreferred stock\nin major banks.\nThe\nFederal Reserve\ncreated then-significant amounts of new currency as a method to combat the\nliquidity trap\n.\nBailouts came in the form of trillions of dollars of loans, asset purchases, guarantees, and direct spending.\nSignificant controversy accompanied the bailouts, such as in the case of the\nAIG bonus payments controversy\n, leading to the development of a variety of "decision making frameworks", to help balance competing policy interests during times of financial crisis.\nAlistair Darling\n, the U.K.'s\nChancellor of the Exchequer\nat the time of the crisis, stated in 2018 that Britain came within hours of "a breakdown of law and order" the day that\nRoyal Bank of Scotland\nwas bailed-out.\nInstead of financing more domestic loans, some banks instead spent some of the stimulus money in more profitable areas such as investing in emerging markets and foreign currencies.\nIn July 2010, the\nDodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act\nwas enacted in the United States to "promote the financial stability of the United States".\nThe\nBasel III\ncapital and liquidity standards were adopted worldwide.\nSince the 2008 financial crisis, consumer regulators in America have more closely supervised sellers of credit cards and home mortgages in order to deter anticompetitive practices that led to the crisis.\nAt least two major reports on the causes of the crisis were produced by the U.S. Congress: the\nFinancial Crisis Inquiry Commission\nreport, released January 2011, and a report by the\nUnited States Senate Homeland Security Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations\nentitled\nWall Street and the Financial Crisis: Anatomy of a Financial Collapse\n, released April 2011.\nIn total, 47 bankers served jail time as a result of the crisis, over half of whom were from Iceland, where the crisis was the most severe and led to the collapse of all three major Icelandic banks.\nIn April 2012,\nGeir Haarde\nof Iceland became the only politician to be convicted as a result of the crisis.\nIn the aftermath of the crisis, U.S. authorities intensified enforcement actions against financial misconduct, including high-profile insider trading prosecutions such as that of\nRaj Rajaratnam\n, who was convicted in 2011.\nOnly one banker in the United States served jail time as a result of the crisis,\nKareem Serageldin\n, a banker at\nCredit Suisse\nwho was sentenced to 30 months in jail and returned $24.6 million in compensation for manipulating bond prices to hide $1 billion of losses.\nNo individuals in the United Kingdom were convicted as a result of the crisis.\nIn\nSEC v. Goldman Sachs\n,\nGoldman Sachs\npaid a $550 million civil penalty and acknowledged that its marketing materials for CDO contained incomplete information, settling\nsecurities fraud\ncharges after allegedly anticipating the financial crisis and selling toxic investments to its clients.\nWith fewer resources to risk in\ncreative destruction\n, the number of patent applications was flat, compared to exponential increases in patent application in prior years.\nShare of income going to the top 1% of earners, one measure of economic inequality in the US from 1913 to 2008.\nTypical American families did not fare well, nor did the "wealthy-but-not-wealthiest" families just beneath the pyramid's top.\nHowever, half of the poorest families in the United States did not have wealth declines at all during the crisis because they generally did not own financial investments whose value could fluctuate. The Federal Reserve surveyed 4,000 households from 2007 to 2009, and found that the total wealth of 63% of all Americans declined in that period and 77% of the richest families had a decrease in total wealth, while only 50% of those on the bottom of the pyramid suffered a decrease.\nTimeline\n[\nedit\n]\nSee also:\nGlobal financial crisis in September 2008\n,\nGlobal financial crisis in October 2008\n,\nGlobal financial crisis in November 2008\n,\nGlobal financial crisis in December 2008\n,\nGlobal financial crisis in 2009\n,\nUnited States bear market of 2007–2009\n,\nDodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act\n,\nRegulatory responses to the subprime crisis\n, and\nSubprime mortgage crisis solutions debate\nThe following is a timeline of the major events of the financial crisis, including government responses, and the subsequent economic recovery.\nPre-2007\n[\nedit\n]\nCost of housing by state from the year 2000 to 2022\nApril 28, 2004: Meeting is held in the basement of the Federal Reserve with the Gang of Five (Goldman Sachs, Lehman Brothers, Merrill Lynch, Bear Stearns and Morgan Stanley) and the five members of the Securities and Exchange Commission to create an exemption for the five largest investment banks in the U.S. from having to adhere to the\nNet Capital Rule\n.\nMay 19, 2005: Fund manager\nMichael Burry\nclosed a\ncredit default swap\nagainst subprime mortgage bonds with\nDeutsche Bank\nvalued at $60 million – the first such CDS. warned of the impending real estate collapse.\nThe\nAustrian School\nregarded the crisis as a vindication and classic example of a predictable credit-fueled bubble caused by laxity in monetary supply.\nThere were other economists that did warn of a pending crisis.\nThe former Governor of the\nReserve Bank of India\n,\nRaghuram Rajan\n, had predicted the crisis in 2005 when he became chief economist at the\nInternational Monetary Fund\n. In 2005, at a celebration honoring\nAlan Greenspan\n, who was about to retire as chairman of the\nUS Federal Reserve\n, Rajan delivered a controversial paper that was critical of the financial sector.\nIn that paper, Rajan "argued that disaster might loom".\nRajan argued that financial sector managers were encouraged to "take risks that generate severe adverse consequences with small probability but, in return, offer generous compensation the rest of the time. These risks are known as tail risks. But perhaps the most important concern is whether banks will be able to provide liquidity to financial markets so that if the tail risk does materialize, financial positions can be unwound and losses allocated so that the consequences to the real economy are minimized."\nStock trader and financial risk engineer\nNassim Nicholas Taleb\n, author of the 2007 book\nThe Black Swan\n, spent years warning against the breakdown of the banking system in particular and the economy in general owing to their use of and reliance on bad risk models and reliance on forecasting, and framed the problem as part of "robustness and fragility".\nHe also took action against the establishment view by making a big financial bet on banking stocks and making a fortune from the crisis ("They didn't listen, so I took their money").\nAccording to\nDavid Brooks\nfrom\nThe New York Times\n, "Taleb not only has an explanation for what's happening, he saw it coming."\nPopular articles published in the mass media have led the general public to believe that the majority of economists have failed in their obligation to predict the financial crisis. For example, an article in\nThe New York Times\nnoted that economist\nNouriel Roubini\nwarned of such crisis as early as September 2006, and stated that the profession of economics is bad at predicting recessions.\nAccording to\nThe Guardian\n, Roubini was ridiculed for predicting a collapse of the housing market and worldwide recession, while\nThe New York Times\nlabelled him "Dr. Doom".\nIn a 2012 article in the journal\nJapan and the World Economy\n, Andrew K. Rose and Mark M. Spiegel used a Multiple Indicator Multiple Cause (MIMIC) model on a cross-section of 107 countries to evaluate potential causes of the 2008 crisis. The authors examined various economic indicators, ignoring\ncontagion effects\nacross countries. The authors concluded: "We include over sixty potential causes of the crisis, covering such categories as: financial system policies and conditions; asset price appreciation in real estate and equity markets; international imbalances and foreign reserve adequacy; macroeconomic policies; and institutional and geographic features. Despite the fact that we use a wide number of possible causes in a flexible statistical framework, we are unable to link most of the commonly cited causes of the crisis to its incidence across countries. This negative finding in the cross-section makes us skeptical of the accuracy of 'early warning' systems of potential crises, which must also predict their timing."\nIndyMac\n[\nedit\n]\nThe first visible institution to run into trouble in the United States was the Southern California–based\nIndyMac\n, a spin-off of\nCountrywide Financial\n. Before its failure, IndyMac Bank was the largest\nsavings and loan association\nin the Los Angeles market and the seventh-largest\nmortgage loan\noriginator in the United States.\nThe failure of IndyMac Bank on July 11, 2008, was (until the crisis precipitated even larger failures) the fourth-largest\nbank failure\nin United States history,\nand the second-largest failure of a regulated\nthrift\n.\nIndyMac Bank's parent corporation was IndyMac Bancorp until the FDIC seized IndyMac Bank.\nIndyMac Bancorp filed for\nChapter 7 bankruptcy\nin July 2008.\nIndyMac Bank was founded as Countrywide Mortgage Investment in 1985 by\nDavid S. Loeb\nand\nAngelo Mozilo\nas a means of\ncollateralizing\nCountrywide Financial\nloans too big to be sold to Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae. In 1997, Countrywide spun off IndyMac as an independent company run by Mike Perry, who remained its CEO until the downfall of the bank in July 2008.\nThe primary causes of its failure were largely associated with its business strategy of originating and securitizing\nAlt-A\nloans on a large scale. This strategy resulted in rapid growth and a high concentration of risky assets. From its inception as a savings association in 2000, IndyMac grew to the seventh largest savings and loan and ninth largest originator of mortgage loans in the United States. During 2006, IndyMac originated over $90 billion (~$\n134\nbillion\nin\n2024\n) of mortgages.\nIndyMac's aggressive growth strategy, use of Alt-A and other nontraditional loan products, insufficient underwriting, credit concentrations in residential real estate in the\nCalifornia\nand\nFlorida\nmarkets—states, alongside Nevada and Arizona, where the\nhousing bubble\nwas most pronounced—and heavy reliance on costly funds borrowed from a\nFederal Home Loan Bank\n(FHLB) and from brokered deposits, led to its demise when the mortgage market declined in 2007.\nIndyMac often made loans without verification of the borrower's income or assets, and to borrowers with poor credit histories. Appraisals obtained by IndyMac on underlying collateral were often questionable as well. As an Alt-A lender, IndyMac's business model was to offer loan products to fit the borrower's needs, using an extensive array of risky option-\nadjustable-rate mortgages\n(option ARMs), subprime loans, 80/20 loans, and other nontraditional products. Ultimately, loans were made to many borrowers who simply could not afford to make their payments. The thrift remained profitable only as long as it was able to sell those loans in the\nsecondary mortgage market\n. IndyMac resisted efforts to regulate its involvement in those loans or tighten their issuing criteria: see the comment by Ruthann Melbourne, Chief Risk Officer, to the regulating agencies.\nOn May 12, 2008, in the "Capital" section of its last 10-Q,\nIndyMac\nrevealed that it may not be well capitalized in the future.\nIndyMac reported that during April 2008,\nMoody's\nand\nStandard & Poor's\ndowngraded the ratings on a significant number of\nMortgage-backed security\n(MBS) bonds—including $160 million (~$\n228\nmillion\nin\n2024\n) issued by IndyMac that the bank retained in its MBS portfolio. IndyMac concluded that these downgrades would have harmed its risk-based capital ratio as of June 30, 2008. Had these lowered ratings been in effect on March 31, 2008, IndyMac concluded that the bank's capital ratio would have been 9.27% total risk-based. IndyMac warned that if its regulators found its capital position to have fallen below "well capitalized" (minimum 10% risk-based capital ratio) to "adequately capitalized" (8–10% risk-based capital ratio) the bank might no longer be able to use brokered deposits as a source of funds.\nSenator\nCharles Schumer\n(D-NY) later pointed out that brokered deposits made up more than 37% of IndyMac's total deposits, and asked the\nFederal Deposit Insurance Corporation\n(FDIC) whether it had considered ordering IndyMac to reduce its reliance on these deposits.\nWith $18.9 billion in total deposits reported on March 31,\nSenator Schumer would have been referring to a little over $7 billion in brokered deposits. While the breakout of maturities of these deposits is not known exactly, a simple averaging would have put the threat of brokered deposits loss to IndyMac at $500 million a month, had the regulator disallowed IndyMac from acquiring new brokered deposits on June 30.\nIndyMac was taking new measures to preserve capital, such as deferring interest payments on some preferred securities. Dividends on common shares had already been suspended for the first quarter of 2008, after being cut in half the previous quarter. The company still had not secured a significant capital infusion nor found a ready buyer.\nIndyMac reported that the bank's risk-based capital was only $47 million above the minimum required for this 10% mark. But it did not reveal some of that $47 million (~$\n67\nmillion\nin\n2024\n) capital it claimed it had, as of March 31, 2008, was fabricated.\nWikinews has related news:\nIndyMac Bank placed into conservatorship by US Government\nWhen home prices declined in the latter half of 2007 and the secondary mortgage market collapsed, IndyMac was forced to hold $10.7 billion (~$\n15.5\nbillion\nin\n2024\n) of loans it could not sell in the secondary market. Its reduced liquidity was further exacerbated in late June 2008 when account holders withdrew $1.55 billion (~$\n2.21\nbillion\nin\n2024\n) or about 7.5% of IndyMac's deposits.\nThis\nbank run\non the thrift followed the public release of a letter from Senator Charles Schumer to the FDIC and OTS. The letter outlined the Senator's concerns with IndyMac. Some worried depositors began to withdraw money.\nOn July 7, 2008, IndyMac announced on the company blog that it:\nHad failed to raise\ncapital\nsince its May 12, 2008, quarterly earnings report;\nHad been notified by bank and thrift regulators that IndyMac Bank was no longer deemed "well-capitalized".\nIndyMac announced the closure of both its retail lending and wholesale divisions, halted new loan submissions, and cut 3,800 jobs.\nOn July 11, 2008, citing liquidity concerns, the FDIC put IndyMac Bank into\nconservatorship\n. Telephone and Internet account access was restored when the bank reopened.\nThe FDIC guarantees the funds of all insured accounts up to US$100,000, and declared a special advance dividend to the roughly 10,000 depositors with funds in excess of the insured amount, guaranteeing 50% of any amounts in excess of $100,000.\nYet, even with the pending sale of Indymac to IMB Management Holdings, an estimated 10,000 uninsured depositors of Indymac are still at a loss of over $270 million.\nWith $32 billion in assets, IndyMac Bank was one of the largest bank failures in American history.\nIndyMac Bancorp filed for\nChapter 7 bankruptcy\non July 31, 2008.\nInitially the companies affected were those directly involved in home construction and mortgage lending such as Northern Rock and\nCountrywide Financial\n, as they could no longer obtain financing through the credit markets.
