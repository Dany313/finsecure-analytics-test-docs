Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operational_risk\n==================================================\n\nRisk of disrupting business operations\nOperational risk\nis the risk of losses caused by flawed or failed processes, policies, systems or events that disrupt business operations.\nEmployee errors, criminal activity such as fraud, and physical events are among the factors that can trigger operational risk. The process to manage operational risk is known as\noperational risk management\n. The definition of operational risk, adopted by the European\nSolvency II Directive\nfor insurers, is a variation adopted from the\nBasel II\nregulations for banks: "The risk of a change in value caused by the fact that actual losses, incurred for inadequate or failed internal processes, people and systems, or from external events (including legal risk), differ from the expected losses".\nThe scope of operational risk is then broad, and can also include other classes of risks, such as\nfraud\n,\nsecurity\n,\nprivacy protection\n,\nlegal risks\n, physical (e.g. infrastructure shutdown) or environmental risks. Operational risks similarly may impact broadly, in that they can affect client satisfaction, reputation and shareholder value, all while increasing business volatility.\nPreviously, in\nBasel I\n, operational risk was\nnegatively defined\n: namely that operational risk are all risks which are\nnot\nmarket risk\nand not\ncredit risk\n. Some banks have therefore also used the term operational risk synonymously with\nnon-financial risks\n.\nIn October 2014, the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision proposed a revision to its operational risk capital framework that sets out a new standardized approach to replace the basic indicator approach and the standardized approach for calculating operational\nrisk capital\n.\nContrary to other risks (e.g.\ncredit risk\n,\nmarket risk\n,\ninsurance risk\n) operational risks are usually not willingly incurred nor are they revenue driven. Moreover, they are not diversifiable and cannot be laid off. This means that as long as people, systems, and processes remain imperfect, operational risk cannot be fully eliminated.
Operational risk is, nonetheless, manageable as to keep losses within some level of\nrisk tolerance\n(i.e. the amount of risk one is prepared to accept in pursuit of his objectives), determined by balancing the costs of improvement against the expected benefits.
Wider trends such as globalization, the expansion of the internet and the rise of social media, as well as the increasing demands for greater corporate accountability worldwide, reinforce the need for proper\nrisk management\n.\nThus\noperational risk management\n(ORM) is a specialized discipline within risk management. 
It constitutes the continuous-process of risk assessment, decision making, and implementation of risk controls, resulting in the acceptance, mitigation, or avoidance of the various operational risks. 
ORM somewhat overlaps\nquality management\nand the\ninternal audit\nfunction.\nBackground\n[\nedit\n]\nUntil\nBasel II\nreforms to banking supervision, operational risk was a residual category reserved for risks and uncertainties which were difficult to quantify and manage in traditional ways\n– the "other risks" basket.\nSuch regulations institutionalized operational risk as a category of regulatory and managerial attention and connected operational risk management with good\ncorporate governance\n.\nBusinesses in general, and other institutions such as the military, have been aware, for many years, of hazards arising from operational factors, internal or external. The primary goal of the military is to fight and win wars in quick and decisive fashion, and with minimal losses. For the military and the businesses of the world alike, operational risk management is an effective process for preserving resources by anticipation.\nTwo decades (from 1980 to the early 2000s) of\nglobalization\nand\nderegulation\n(\ne.g.\nBig Bang (financial markets)\n), combined with the increased sophistication of\nfinancial services\naround the world, introduced additional complexities into the activities of banks, insurers, and firms in general and therefore their risk profiles.\nSince the mid-1990s, the topics of market risk and credit risk have been the subject of much debate and research, with the result that financial institutions have made significant progress in the identification, measurement, and management of both these forms of risk.\nThe\n2008 financial crisis\nand\nsubprime mortgage crisis\nexposed significant limitations in existing approaches to measuring market and credit risk. In response, regulators introduced new requirements worldwide, including\nBasel III\nfor banks and Solvency II for insurers.\nEvents such as the\nSeptember 11 terrorist attacks\n, rogue trading losses at\nSociété Générale\n,\nBarings\n,\nAIB\n,\nUBS\n, and\nNational Australia Bank\nserve to highlight the fact that the scope of\nrisk management\nextends beyond merely\nmarket\nand\ncredit risk\n.\nThese reasons underscore banks' and supervisors' growing focus upon the identification and measurement of operational risk.\nThe list of risks (and, more importantly, the scale of these risks) faced by banks today includes fraud, system failures, terrorism, and employee compensation claims. These types of risk are generally classified under the term 'operational risk'.\nThe identification and measurement of operational risk is a real and live issue for modern-day banks, particularly since the decision by the\nBasel Committee on Banking Supervision\n(BCBS) to introduce a capital charge for this risk as part of the new capital adequacy framework (\nBasel II\n).\nDefinition\n[\nedit\n]\nThe Basel Committee defines operational risk in Basel II and Basel III as:\nThe risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, people and systems or from external events. This definition includes legal risk, but excludes strategic and reputational risk.\nThe Basel Committee recognizes that operational risk is a term that has a variety of meanings and therefore, for internal purposes, banks are permitted to adopt their own definitions of operational risk, provided that the minimum elements in the Committee's definition are included.\nScope exclusions\n[\nedit\n]\nThe Basel II definition of operational risk excludes, for example, strategic risk – the risk of a loss arising from a poor strategic business decision.\nOther risk terms are seen as potential consequences of operational risk events.  For example,\nreputational risk\n(damage to an organization through loss of its reputation or standing) can arise as a consequence (or impact) of operational failures – as well as from other events.\nEvent types\n[\nedit\n]\nThe following lists the seven official Basel II event types with some examples for each category:\nInternal Fraud – misappropriation of assets, tax evasion, intentional\nmismarking\nof positions,\nbribery\nExternal Fraud – theft of information, hacking damage, third-party theft and forgery\nEmployment Practices and Workplace Safety – discrimination, workers compensation, employee health and safety\nClients, Products, and Business Practice –\nmarket manipulation\n, antitrust, improper trade, product defects, fiduciary breaches, account churning\nDamage to Physical Assets – natural disasters, terrorism, vandalism\nBusiness Disruption and Systems Failures –  utility disruptions, software failures, hardware failures\nExecution, Delivery, and Process Management – data entry errors, accounting errors, failed mandatory reporting, negligent loss of client assets\nVendor risk\n[\nedit\n]\nVendor risk\nrefers to the risk caused by the dependency of one's services or products on a lower-level service or product sourced from a particular vendor.\nIt includes the risks of\nthe vendor no longer providing the required product or service,\nsubstantially increasing the cost of such or\nmaking modifications to the provided product or service such that new versions of the product no longer meet one's functional or non-functional requirements.\nDifficulties\n[\nedit\n]\nIt is relatively straightforward for an organization to set and observe specific, measurable levels of market risk and credit risk because models exist which attempt to predict the potential impact of market movements, or changes in the cost of credit. These models are only as good as the underlying assumptions, and a large part of the\n2008 financial crisis\narose because the valuations generated by these models for particular types of investments were based on incorrect assumptions.\nBy contrast, it is relatively difficult to identify or assess levels of operational risk and its many sources.  Historically organizations have accepted operational risk as an unavoidable cost of doing business.  Many now though collect data on operational losses – for example through system failure or fraud – and are using this data to model operational risk and to calculate a capital reserve against future operational losses.  In addition to the Basel II requirement for banks, this is now a requirement for European insurance firms who are in the process of implementing Solvency II, the equivalent of Basel II for the insurance sector.\nMethods for calculating operational risk capital\n[\nedit\n]\nBasel II and various supervisory bodies of the countries have prescribed various soundness standards for\noperational risk management\nfor banks and similar financial institutions. To complement these standards, Basel II has given guidance to 3 broad methods of capital calculation for operational risk:\nBasic Indicator Approach\n– based on annual revenue of the Financial Institution\nStandardized Approach\n– based on annual revenue of each of the broad business lines of the Financial Institution\nAdvanced Measurement Approaches\n– based on the internally developed risk measurement framework of the bank adhering to the standards prescribed (methods include IMA, LDA, Scenario-based, Scorecard etc.)\nThe operational risk management framework should include identification, measurement, monitoring, reporting, control and mitigation frameworks for operational risk.\nThere are a number of methodologies to choose from when modeling operational risk, each with its advantages and target applications. The ultimate choice of the methodology/methodologies to use in your institution depends on a number of factors, including:\nTime sensitivity for analysis;\nResources desired and/or available for the task;\nApproaches used for other risk measures;\nExpected use of results (e.g., allocating capital to business units, prioritizing control improvement projects, satisfying regulators that your institution is measuring risk, providing an incentive for better management of operational risk, etc.);\nSenior management understanding and commitment; and\nExisting complementary processes, such as self-assessment\nStandardised Measurement Approach (Basel III)\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nBasel Committee on Banking Supervision\n(BCBS) has proposed the "\nStandardised Measurement Approach\n" (SMA) as a method of assessing operational risk as a replacement for all existing approaches, including AMA. The objective is to provide stable, comparable and risk-sensitive estimates for the operational risk exposure and is effective January 1, 2022.\nThe SMA puts weight on the internal loss history (losses of the last 10 years must be considered).
