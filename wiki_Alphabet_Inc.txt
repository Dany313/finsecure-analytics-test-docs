For the airport code, see\nLinguère Airport\n.\nAlphabet Inc.\nis an American multinational technology\nconglomerate\nholding company\nheadquartered in\nMountain View, California\n. It was created through a\nrestructuring\nof\nGoogle\non October 2, 2015,\nand became the parent holding company of Google and several former Google subsidiaries.\nAlphabet is listed on the large-cap section of the\nNasdaq\nunder the ticker symbols GOOGL and GOOG; both classes of stock are components of major\nstock market indices\nsuch as the\nS&P 500\nand\nNasdaq-100\n. Alphabet has been described as a\nBig Tech\ncompany.\nThe establishment of Alphabet Inc. was prompted by a desire to make the core Google business "cleaner and more accountable" while allowing greater autonomy to group companies that operate in businesses other than Internet services.\nFounders\nLarry Page\nand\nSergey Brin\nannounced their resignation from their executive posts in December 2019, with the CEO role to be filled by\nSundar Pichai\n, who is also the CEO of Google. Page and Brin remain employees, board members, and\ncontrolling shareholders\nof Alphabet Inc.\nAlphabet Inc. has faced numerous legal and ethical controversies, including a 2017 lawsuit against\nUber\nover stolen\nself-driving\ntechnology, a 2020 privacy settlement over\nGoogle+\ndata exposure, and multiple antitrust actions from the United States, France, and Japan. It has also been accused of labor law violations related to worker organizing and was forced to file for\nbankruptcy in Russia\nafter its bank account was seized in 2022. In 2023, the company was widely criticized for\nmass layoffs\nthat impacted 12,000 employees, many of whom discovered their termination only upon losing account access.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\n‹\nThe\ntemplate\nEvolution of Google\nis being\nconsidered for deletion\n.\n›\nOn August 11, 2015, Google announced plans to create a new public\nholding company\n, Alphabet Inc. Google co-founder and CEO\nLarry Page\nmade this announcement in a blog post on Google's official blog.\nAlphabet was created to restructure Google by moving subsidiaries from Google to Alphabet, thus narrowing Google's scope. The new holding company would consist of Google as well as other businesses including\nX Development\n,\nCalico\n,\nNest\n,\nVerily\n,\nFiber\n,\nCapitalG\n, and\nGV\n.\nSundar Pichai\n, the company's Product Chief, became the new chief executive officer of Google, replacing Page, who transitioned to the role of running Alphabet, along with co-founder Sergey Brin.\nAlphabet is the world's\nsecond-largest technology company by revenue\n, after\nNvidia\n, the largest technology company by profit, and the world's\nmost valuable companies\n.\nIn his announcement, Page stated that the planned holding company would allow for "more management scale, as we can run things independently that aren't very related" to Google. He clarified that, as a result of the new holding company, Google would be "a bit slimmed down, with the companies that are pretty far afield of our main internet products contained in Alphabet instead".\nHe further stated that the motivation behind the reorganization is to make Google "cleaner and more accountable and better" and that he wanted to improve "the transparency and oversight of what we're doing".\nFormer executive\nEric Schmidt\nrevealed in the conference in 2017 the inspiration for this structure came from\nWarren Buffett\nand his management structure of\nBerkshire Hathaway\na decade ago.\nSchmidt said he encouraged Page and Brin to meet with Buffett in Omaha to see how Berkshire Hathaway was a holding company made of subsidiaries with strong CEOs who were trusted to run their businesses.\nBefore it became a subsidiary of Alphabet, Google Inc. was first structured as the owner of Alphabet.\nThe roles were reversed after a placeholder subsidiary was created for the ownership of Alphabet, at which point the newly formed subsidiary was merged with Google. Google's stock was then converted to Alphabet's stock. Under the\nDelaware General Corporation Law\n(where Alphabet is incorporated), a holding company reorganization such as this can be done without a vote of shareholders, as this reorganization was.\nThe restructuring process was completed on October 2, 2015.\nAlphabet retains Google Inc.'s stock price history and continues to trade under Google Inc.'s former ticker symbols "GOOG" and "GOOGL".\nOn December 3, 2019, Page and Brin jointly announced that they would step down from their respective roles, remaining as employees and still the majority vote on the board of directors.\nSundar Pichai\n, the CEO of Google, assumed the CEO role at Alphabet while retaining the same at Google.\nThe firm completed a\nstock split\nin mid-2022.\nOn January 20, 2023, Pichai wrote a letter to all employees announcing that the company would be\nlaying off\nabout 12,000 jobs, or 6% of its global workforce. In the letter, Pichai wrote, "Over the past two years we've seen periods of dramatic growth. To match and fuel that growth, we hired for a different economic reality than the one we face today."\nIn January 2024, Waymo, the autonomous driving division of Alphabet Inc., which operates extensively in San Francisco, filed an application with the California Public Utilities Commission to expand service in Los Angeles. Such a license would allow the company to make full use of its fleet in the city instead of test drives by invitation.\nIn August 2024, following the lawsuit filed by the United States Department of Justice in 2020, a\nUnited States district court\nfound Alphabet guilty of violating anti-trust law. This marked the first anti-trust ruling against a U.S. company in 24 years. Alphabet has appealed the ruling.\nOn 10 December 2024, Alphabet's shares rose about 5% after the company unveiled its new quantum computing chip, Willow. The chip solved a complex problem in five minutes, a task that would take a classical computer longer than the age of the universe. Willow reduces error rates in quantum computing and can correct them in real time. Alphabet's stock was up 25% for the year, marking its best day since April 2024.\nOn 15 September 2025, Alphabet became the fourth company—trailing\nNvidia\n,\nMicrosoft\n, and\nApple\n—to top $3 trillion in market cap.\nAt the time, shares were up more than 32% for the year amid optimism for AI adoption and a more-favorable-than-expected antitrust ruling that did not require the company to divest its\nChrome\nbrowser platform.\nAccording to an analysis by\nBridgewater Associates\n, Alphabet, along with Amazon, Meta and Microsoft are expected to collectively invest about $650 billion to scale up AI-related infrastructure in 2026.\nOn June 1, 2026, Alphabet announced plans to raise $80 billion through a series of equity offerings to fund the expansion of its AI infrastructure. As part of the initiative, Berkshire Hathaway agreed to invest $10 billion in Alphabet through a private placement.\nStructure\n[\nedit\n]\nAlphabet Inc. is the parent of a diverse set of subsidiaries.\nAs of September\n1,\n2017\n, its equity is held by a subsidiary known as\nXXVI Holdings, Inc.\n(referring to the Roman numeral of 26, the number of letters in the alphabet), so that they can be valued and legally separated from Google. At the same time, it was announced that Google would be reorganized as a\nlimited liability company\n, Google LLC.\nEric Schmidt\nsaid at an\nInternet Association\nevent in 2015 that there may eventually be more than 26 Alphabet subsidiaries. He also said that he was currently meeting with the CEOs of the current and proposed Alphabet subsidiaries. He said, "You'll see a lot coming."\nWhile many companies or divisions formerly a part of Google became subsidiaries of Alphabet, Google remains the umbrella company for Alphabet's Internet-related businesses. These include widely used products and services long associated with Google, such as the\nAndroid operating system\n,\nYouTube\n, and\nGoogle Search\n, which remain direct components of Google.\nFormer subsidiaries include\nNest Labs\n, which was merged into Google in February 2018,\nand\nChronicle Security\n, which was merged with Google Cloud in June 2019.\nSidewalk Labs\nwas absorbed into Google in 2021 following CEO\nDaniel L. Doctoroff\n's departure from the company due to a suspected\nALS\ndiagnosis.\nIn January 2021,\nLoon LLC\nCEO\nAlastair Westgarth\nmentioned in a blog post\nthat the company would be shutting down, citing lack of a scalable and sustainable business model. In July 2021, Alphabet announced\nIntrinsic\n, a new\nrobotics\nsoftware company spun out of X.\nIn November 2021, Alphabet announced a new company named\nIsomorphic Labs\n, using artificial intelligence for\ndrug discovery\nand headed by DeepMind CEO\nDemis Hassabis\n.\nOwnership\n[\nedit\n]\nAlphabet is mainly owned by institutional investors, who own over 60% of shares. We also like that it means alpha‑bet (\nAlpha\nis investment return above benchmark), which we strive for!"\nIn a 2018 talk, Schmidt disclosed that the original inspiration for the name came from the location of Google's Hamburg office's street address at the time: ABC-Straße.\nAlphabet uses the domain\nabc.xyz\nwith the\n.xyz\ntop-level domain\n(TLD), which was introduced in 2014. It does not own the domain\nalphabet.com\n, which is owned by a\nfleet management\ndivision of\nBMW\n. Following the announcement, BMW said it would be "necessary to examine the legal trademark implications" of the proposals. Google's\nmission statement\n, from the outset, was "to organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful",\nand its unofficial slogan is "\nDon't be evil\n".\nIn October 2015, a related motto was adopted in the Alphabet corporate\ncode of conduct\n: "Do the right thing".\nThe original motto was retained in the code of conduct of Google, now a subsidiary of Alphabet.\nFinances\n[\nedit\n]\nThe key trends of Alphabet Inc. are (as of the financial year ending December 31):\nAs per its 2017 annual report, 86% of Alphabet's revenues came from performance advertising (through user clicks using\nAdSense\nand\nGoogle Ads\n) and brand advertising.\nOf these, 53% came from its international operations. This translated to a total revenue of US$110,855\nmillion in 2017 and a net income of US$12,662\nmillion. On February 1, 2016, Alphabet Inc. surpassed\nApple\nto become the world's most valuable publicly traded company until February 3, 2016, when Apple surged back over Alphabet to retake the position. Experts cited Apple's lack of innovation as well as increasing Chinese competition as reasons for the poor performance.\nAs of 2019\n, Alphabet is ranked No. 15 on the\nFortune\n500\nrankings of the largest United States corporations by total revenue.\nOn January 16, 2020, Alphabet became the fourth U.S. company to reach a $1\ntrillion\nmarket value\nentering the\ntrillion dollar companies club\nfor the first time. In October 2022, Alphabet recorded the weakest quarterly growth, with fewer sales in nearly a decade. The possible global recession, the strong U.S. dollar, and the pandemics all contributed to the slowed economy.\nIn 2022, Alphabet was the company with the\nsecond-highest expenditure\non\nresearch and development\nworldwide, with R&D expenditure amounting to US$39.5\nbillion.\nIn 2023, Alphabet was ranked 7th in the Global 2000 (World's Largest Public Companies).\nOn April 26, 2024, Alphabet surpassed a market valuation of $2 trillion for the first time. This surge follows the announcement of the company's first-ever dividend payout and a significant $70 billion\nstock buyback\nprogram. The company's first-quarter earnings also exceeded analyst expectations, further contributing to the positive investor sentiment.\nAs of June 2024, the company is one of the 10 largest components of the\nMSCI KLD 400 Social Index\n.\nOn September 15, 2025, Alphabet became the fourth company to reach a $3 trillion market valuation.\nInvestments and acquisitions\n[\nedit\n]\nInvestments\n[\nedit\n]\nIn November 2017, Alphabet Inc. led a Series A round of $71\nmillion along with\nAndreessen Horowitz\nand\n20th Century Studios\nin music startup\nUnitedMasters\n, founded by\nSteve Stoute\n.\nIn addition to funding startups, Alphabet also invests in more mature companies, including publicly traded companies like\nUber\nand privately held companies like\nMedium\n.\nAcquisitions\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nList of mergers and acquisitions by Alphabet\nAn analysis of the company's investments in 2017 suggested that it was the most active investor in that period, outdoing the capital arm of\nIntel\nand also its own best customer. Alphabet, Inc. acquired seven of its own capital-backed startups in the 2017 financial year, with\nCisco\nsecond having acquired six of the company's previous investments.\nLawsuits and controversies\n[\nedit\n]\nSee also:\nCriticism of Google\n,\nGoogle litigation\n,\nAntitrust cases against Google by the European Union\n,\nCensorship by Google\n, and\nPrivacy concerns regarding Google\nIn 2017, Alphabet Inc. sued\nUber\nover technology similar to Alphabet's proprietary\nself-driving car\ntechnology. Alphabet's\nautonomous vehicle\ntechnology had been under development for a decade by Alphabet's\nWaymo\n(self-driving vehicle division). The\nproprietary technology\nis related to 14,000 documents believed to have been downloaded and stolen by a former Waymo engineer, subsequently employed by Uber.\nThe lawsuit was settled in February 2018, with Uber agreeing not to use the self-driving technology in dispute and also agreed to provide Waymo with an equity stake of 0.34%, equating to around $245\nmillion at the firm's early 2018 value.\nIn October 2018, a class action lawsuit was filed against Google and Alphabet due to "non-public"\nGoogle+\naccount data being exposed as a result of a privacy bug that allowed app developers to gain access to the private information of users. The litigation was settled in July 2020 for $7.5 million with a payout to claimants of at least $5 each, with a maximum of $12 each.\nIn October 2020, the\nUnited States Department of Justice\nfiled an antitrust lawsuit against Alphabet, alleging anti-competitive practices.\nOn 2 December 2020, the\nNational Labor Relations Board\nfiled a complaint that claimed Alphabet Inc conducted unlawful monitoring and questioning of several workers at Google. The employees in question were fired for unionization attempts and protesting company policies. The board also alleges that Google unlawfully placed employees on administrative leave in retribution. Alphabet Inc has denied any wrongdoing and said it acted legally.\nOn June 7, 2021, Alphabet Inc. announced it had settled an\nantitrust\nsuit with the French\nAutorité de la concurrence\nwith a payment of $270 million. The settlement amounted to less than 0.7% of Alphabet Inc.'s yearly earnings.\nOn June 12, 2021, it was announced that Japan would launch an antitrust probe into Alphabet Inc. and Apple Inc. to determine whether their dealings with Japanese smartphone makers violate current antitrust measures or could necessitate new ones.\nIn May 2022, Russian authorities seized Google's Russian bank account,\nforcing it to file for bankruptcy one month later due to the inability to pay vendors and staff. However, free services such as Google Search, YouTube, Gmail, Maps, Android and Play were to remain available.\nIn 2023, the company was criticized for conducting mass lay-offs without informing employees before they arrived to work, including many long-tenured and recently promoted employees. Around 12,000 jobs were cut, which reduced the company's workforce by 6%.\nAccording to various posts on social media, several Google employees discovered they had been terminated after they were unable to access their accounts and confirming it through news articles discussing the mass layoffs.\nIn June 2025, a United Nations report on\ncorporations complicit\nin the\nGaza genocide\nshowed that Alphabet Inc. was one of several companies "central to\nIsrael\n's\nsurveillance apparatus\nand the\nongoing Gaza destruction\n."\nOn September 23, 2025, Alphabet announced that it would reinstate YouTube creators that were banned for spreading\nmisinformation about COVID-19\nand the\n2020 U.S. presidential election\n.\nWithin the context of the\nsuspension of Jimmy Kimmel\nand debates about\nfree speech in the United States\n, Vice President\nJD Vance\ndefended Kimmel's suspension and instead cited a letter sent by Alphabet legal counsel Daniel F. Donovan to\nU.S. House Judiciary Committee\nchairman\nJim Jordan\n, claiming the\nBiden administration\npressured YouTube to remove "non-violative user-generated content" containing misinformation about the COVID-19 pandemic and the 2020 election, and announced that it would reinstate content creators previously banned due to the cited content;\nhowever, such claims have not formally been proven, and the\nU.S. Supreme Court\ndismissed in 2024 the\nFirst Amendment\ncase\nMurthy v. Missouri\n(which claimed the Biden administration had pressured social media companies to censor conservative views, government criticism, and COVID-19 misinformation), ruling 6–3 that neither the attorneys general of Missouri and Louisiana nor other respondents had standing to bring the case.
