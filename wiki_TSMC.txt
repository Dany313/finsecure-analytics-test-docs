Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TSMC\n==================================================\n\nTaiwanese semiconductor foundry company\nNot to be confused with\nTaiwan Semiconductor Company Limited\n(TSC).\nTaiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company Limited\n(\nTaiwan Semiconductor\nor\nTSMC\n)\nis a Taiwanese multinational\nsemiconductor\ncontract manufacturing and design company. Headquartered in\nHsinchu Science Park\n, it is one of the world's largest non-U.S. companies by\nmarket capitalisation\n. It holds a commanding majority in the semiconductor foundry market, accounting for approximately 70% of the global market share.\nAs the leading\ndedicated\ncontract chipmaker\n, it serves as the main supplier for\nNvidia\n,\nApple\n,\nBroadcom\n, and\nQualcomm\n.\nThe company has grown rapidly due to the\nartificial intelligence boom\nand its importance in the creation of advanced chips needed for AI.\nIts role as a supply chain\nbottleneck\nraises concerns that a disruption could cause another\nworldwide chip shortage\n.\nAlthough the\ngovernment of Taiwan\nis the largest individual shareholder,\nthe majority of TSMC is owned by foreign investors.\nIn 2025, the company was ranked 38th in the\nForbes\nGlobal 2000\n.\nTaiwan's exports of integrated circuits amounted to $184 billion in 2022, nearly 25 percent of Taiwan's\nGDP\n. TSMC constitutes about 30 percent of the\nTaiwan Stock Exchange\n's main index.\nTaiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) was established in 1987 as a joint venture between Taiwan’s government, the Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI), and private investors.\nMorris Chang\nfounded the company to commercialise semiconductor research originally developed at ITRI, becoming the world’s first dedicated semiconductor foundry.\nWhen Chang retired in 2018, after 31 years of TSMC leadership, Mark Liu became chairman and\nC. C. Wei\nbecame Chief Executive.\nIt has been listed on the\nTaiwan Stock Exchange\nsince 1993; in 1997 it became the first Taiwanese company to be listed on the\nNew York Stock Exchange\n. Since 1994, TSMC has had a\ncompound annual growth rate\n(CAGR) of 17.4 percent in revenue and a CAGR of 16.1 percent in earnings.\nMost\nfabless\nsemiconductor companies such as\nAMD\n, Apple,\nARM\n, Broadcom,\nMarvell\n,\nMediaTek\n, Qualcomm, and Nvidia are customers of TSMC, as are emerging companies such as\nAllwinner Technology\n,\nHiSilicon\n, Spectra7, and\nUNISOC\n.\nProgrammable logic device\ncompanies\nXilinx\nand previously\nAltera\nalso make or made use of TSMC's foundry services.\nSome\nintegrated device manufacturers\nthat have their own\nfabrication facilities\n, such as\nIntel\n,\nNXP\n,\nSTMicroelectronics\n, and\nTexas Instruments\n, outsource some of their production to TSMC.\nTSMC has a global capacity of about thirteen million 300\nmm-equivalent wafers per year as of 2020 and produces chips for customers with process nodes from 2 microns to\n3 nanometres\n. TSMC was the first foundry to market\n7-nanometre\nand\n5-nanometre\n(used by the 2020\nApple A14\nand\nM1\nSoCs\n, the MediaTek\nDimensity 8100\n, and\nAMD Ryzen 7000\nseries processors) production capabilities, and the first to commercialise\nASML\n's\nextreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography\ntechnology in high volume.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 1986,\nLi Kwoh-ting\n, representing the\nExecutive Yuan\n, invited Morris Chang to serve as the president of the\nIndustrial Technology Research Institute\n(ITRI) and offered him a blank cheque to build Taiwan's chip industry. At that time, the Taiwanese government wanted to develop its semiconductor industry, but its high investment and high risk nature made it difficult to find investors.\nTexas Instruments\nand\nIntel\nturned down Chang. Only\nPhilips\nwas willing to sign a joint venture contract with Taiwan to put up $58\nmillion, transfer its production technology, and license intellectual property in exchange for a 27.6 percent stake in TSMC. Alongside generous tax benefits, the Taiwanese government, through the National Development Fund, Executive Yuan, provided another 48.3 percent of the startup capital for TSMC. 20% of the capital was raised from eight private companies (ranging from Formosa Plastics Group’s 5%, CAPCO’s 5% to Taiyuan Textile’s 1%) which were owned by some of the island's wealthiest families, who owned firms that specialised in plastics, textiles, and chemicals. These wealthy Taiwanese were directly "asked" by the government to invest. The rest of the 4.1% capital came from Taiwan's then ruling party’s investment arm: Kuomintang Central Investment Co.\nFrom the company's onset, TSMC operated primarily as a project of the Taiwanese state, as opposed to a completely private company.\nIts first CEO was James E. Dykes, who left after a year and Morris Chang became the CEO.\nAlthough Philips initially held a 27.5% stake in TSMC, its influence extended beyond financial investment. In addition to capital, Philips played a crucial role by transferring semiconductor manufacturing technology, intellectual property, and patents to TSMC, enabling the company to scale more rapidly. Philips also provided TSMC's first CEO, James E. Dykes, who had previously worked at Philips North America. This partnership represented an early example of the "fab-light" strategy, as Philips gradually reduced its in-house semiconductor manufacturing and relied more on external foundries like TSMC. Over the following decades, Philips steadily divested its stake in TSMC and shifted its primary focus to healthcare technology.\nSince then, the company has continued to grow, albeit subject to the cycles of demand. In 2011, the company planned to increase research and development expenditures by almost 39 percent to\nNT$\n50\nbillion to fend off growing competition.\nThe company also planned to expand capacity by 30 percent in 2011 to meet strong market demand.\nIn May 2014, TSMC's board of directors approved capital appropriations of US$568\nmillion to increase and improve manufacturing capabilities after the company forecast higher than expected demand.\nIn August 2014, TSMC's board of directors approved additional capital appropriations of US$3.05\nbillion.\nIn 2011, it was reported that TSMC had begun trial production of the\nA5\nSoC and\nA6\nSoCs for Apple's\niPad\nand\niPhone\ndevices.\nAccording to reports in May 2014,\nApple sourced its\nA8\nand\nA8X\nSoCs from TSMC.\nApple then sourced the\nA9\nSoC with both TSMC and Samsung (to increase volume for\niPhone 6S\nlaunch) and the\nA9X\nexclusively with TSMC, thus resolving the issue of sourcing a chip in two different\nmicroarchitecture\nsizes. As of 2014, Apple was TSMC's most important customer.\nIn October 2014,\nARM\nand TSMC announced a new multi-year agreement for the development of ARM based 10\nnm\nFinFET\nprocessors.\nOver the objection of the\nTsai Ing-wen administration\n, in March 2017, TSMC invested US$3 billion in\nNanjing\nto develop a manufacturing subsidiary there.\nIn 2020, TSMC became the first semiconductor company in the world to sign up for the\nRE100\ninitiative, pledging to use 100 percent\nrenewable energy\nby 2050.\nTSMC accounts for roughly 5 percent of the energy consumption in Taiwan, even exceeding that of the capital city Taipei. This initiative was thus expected to accelerate the transformation to renewable energy in the country.\nFor 2020, TSMC had a net income of US$17.60\nbillion on a consolidated revenue of US$45.51\nbillion, an increase of 57.5 percent and 31.4 percent respectively from the 2019 level of US$11.18\nbillion net income and US$34.63\nbillion consolidated revenue.\nIts\nmarket capitalisation\nwas over $550\nbillion in April 2021. TSMC's revenue in the first quarter of 2020 reached US$10\nbillion,\nwhile its market capitalisation was US$254\nbillion.\nTSMC's market capitalisation reached a value of NT$1.9\ntrillion (US$63.4\nbillion) in December 2010.\nIt was ranked 70th in the\nFT Global 500\n2013 list of the world's most highly valued companies with a capitalisation of US$86.7\nbillion,\nwhile reaching US$110\nbillion in May 2014.\nIn March 2017, TSMC's market capitalisation surpassed that of semiconductor giant\nIntel\nfor the first time, hitting NT$5.14\ntrillion (US$168.4\nbillion), with Intel's at US$165.7\nbillion.\nOn 27 June 2020, TSMC briefly became the world's 10th most valuable company, with a market capitalisation of US$410\nbillion.\nTo mitigate business risks in the event of war between Taiwan and the People's Republic of China, since the beginning of the 2020s, TSMC has expanded its geographic operations, opening new fabs in Japan and the United States, with further plans for expansion into Germany.\nIn July 2020, TSMC confirmed it would halt the shipment of silicon wafers to Chinese telecommunications equipment manufacturer\nHuawei\nand its subsidiary\nHiSilicon\nby 14 September.\nIn November 2020, officials in\nPhoenix, Arizona\nin the United States approved TSMC's plan to build a $12\nbillion chip plant in the city. The decision to locate a plant in the US came after the\nTrump administration\nwarned about the issues concerning the world's electronics made outside of the U.S.\nIn 2021, news reports claimed that the facility might be tripled to roughly a $35\nbillion investment with six factories.\nSee\nTSMC §\nArizona\nfor more details.\nIn June 2021, following nearly a year of public controversy surrounding its\nCOVID-19 vaccine shortage\n,\nwith only about 10 percent of its 23.5\nmillion population vaccinated;\nTaiwan agreed to allow TSMC and\nFoxconn\nto jointly negotiate purchasing\nCOVID-19 vaccines\non its behalf.\nIn July 2021,\nBioNTech\n's Chinese sales agent\nFosun Pharma\nannounced that the two technology manufacturers had reached an agreement to purchase 10 million\nBioNTech COVID-19 vaccines\nfrom Germany.\nTSMC and Foxconn pledged to each buy five million doses for up to $175\nmillion,\nfor donation to Taiwan's vaccination programme.\nDue to the\n2020–2023 global semiconductor shortage\n, Taiwanese competitor\nUnited Microelectronics\nraised prices approximately 7–9 percent, and prices for TSMC's more mature processors were raised by about 20 percent.\nIn November 2021, TSMC and\nSony\nannounced that TSMC would be establishing a new subsidiary named\nJapan Advanced Semiconductor Manufacturing\n(JASM) in\nKumamoto\n, Japan. The subsidiary manufactures 22- and 28-nanometre processes. The initial investment was approximately $7\nbillion, with Sony investing approximately $500\nmillion for a less than 20 percent stake. Construction of the fabrication plant started in 2022, with production beginning two years later in 2024.\nIn February 2022, TSMC, Sony Semiconductor Solutions, and\nDenso\nannounced that Denso would take a more than 10 percent equity stake in JASM with a US$0.35\nbillion investment, amid a scarcity of chips for automobiles.\nTSMC will also enhance JASM's capabilities with 12/16 nanometre FinFET process technology in addition to the previously announced 22/28 nanometre process and increase monthly production capacity from 45,000 to 55,000 12-inch wafers.\nThe total capital expenditure for JASM's Kumamoto fab is estimated to be approximately US$8.6\nbillion.\nThe Japanese government wants JASM to supply essential chips to Japan's electronic device makers and auto companies as trade friction between the United States and China threatens to disrupt\nsupply chains\n.\nThe fab is expected to directly create about 1,700 high-tech professional jobs.\nIn July 2022, TSMC announced the company had posted a record profit in the second quarter, with net income up 76.4 percent year-over-year. The company saw steady growth in the automotive and data centre sectors with some weakness in the consumer market. Some of the\ncapital expenditures\nare projected to be pushed up to 2023.\nIn the third quarter of 2022,\nBerkshire Hathaway\ndisclosed purchase of 60 million shares in TSMC, acquiring a $4.1\nbillion stake, making it one of its largest holdings in a technology company.\nHowever, Berkshire sold off 86.2 percent of its stake by the next quarter citing geopolitical tensions as a factor.\nIn February 2024, TSMC\nshares\nhit a record high, with the high on the trading day reaching\nNT$\n709 and closing at NT$697 (+8%). This was influenced by the increase in the price target on chip designer\nNvidia\n. TSMC currently manufactures\n3-nanometre chips\nand plans to start\n2-nanometre\nmass production in 2025.\nIt is included in the\nFTSE4Good Index\n, being the only Asian company in the top ten.\nIn October 2024, TSMC informed the\nUnited States Department of Commerce\nabout a potential breach of\nexport controls\nin which one of its most advanced chips was sent to Huawei via another company with ties to the Chinese government.\nIn June 2025, TSMC announced the establishment of the TSMC-UTokyo Lab (TSMC-University of\nTokyo Laboratory\n), marking its first joint research laboratory with an overseas university.\nIn August 2025, TSMC reported that two current employees and six former employees had illegally acquired trade secrets related to the company's leading\n2nm process\n.\nOne of them went on to work for\nTokyo Electron\n, a partner of Japan's semiconductor effort\nRapidus\n, but has since been terminated.\nA number of suspects have been arrested by Taiwanese authorities.\nIn January 2026, reported a 35% increase in fourth-quarter profit. Wei and Mark Liu (2013–2018); co-CEO's\nBusiness trends\n[\nedit\n]\nThe key trends for TSMC are (as of the financial year ending 31 December):\nTSMC and the rest of the foundry industry are exposed to the\ncyclical industrial dynamics\nof the semiconductor industry. TSMC must ensure its production capacity to meet strong customer demand during upturns; however, during downturns, it must contend with excess capacity because of weak demand and the high fixed costs associated with its manufacturing facilities.\nAs a result, the company's financial results tend to fluctuate with a cycle time of a few years. This is more apparent in earnings than revenues because of the general trend of revenue and capacity growth. TSMC's business has generally also been seasonal, with a peak in Q3 and a low in Q1.\nIn 2014, TSMC was at the forefront of the foundry industry for high-performance, low-power applications,\nleading major smartphone chip companies, such as\nQualcomm\n,\nMediatek\n,\nand\nApple\n,\nto place an increasing amount of orders.\nWhile the competitors in the foundry industry (primarily\nGlobalFoundries\nand\nUnited Microelectronics Corporation\n) have encountered difficulties ramping leading-edge 28\nnm capacity,\nthe leading\nintegrated device manufacturers\nsuch as\nSamsung\nand\nIntel\nthat seek to offer foundry capacity to third parties were also unable to match the requirements for advanced mobile applications.\nFor most of 2014, TSMC saw a continuing increase in revenues due to increased demand, primarily due to chips for smartphone applications. TSMC raised its financial guidance in March 2014 and posted 'unseasonably strong' first-quarter results.\nFor Q2 2014, revenues came in at NT$183\nbillion, with 28\nnm technology business growing more than 30 percent from the previous quarter.\nLead times for chip orders at TSMC increased due to a tight capacity situation, putting fabless chip companies at risk of not meeting their sales expectations or shipment schedules,\nand in August 2014 it was reported that TSMC's production capacity for the fourth quarter of 2014 was already almost fully booked, a scenario that had not occurred for many years, which was described as being due to a ripple-effect due to TSMC landing CPU orders from Apple.\nHowever, monthly sales for 2014 peaked in October, decreasing by 10 percent in November due to cautious inventory adjustment actions taken by some of its customers.\nTSMC's revenue for 2014 saw growth of 28 percent over the previous year, while TSMC forecasted that revenue for 2015 would grow by 15 to 20 percent from 2014, thanks to strong demand for its 20\nnm process, new 16\nnm FinFET process technology as well as continuing demand for 28\nnm, and demand for less advanced chip fabrication in its 200mm fabs.\nIn 2019, TSMC was ranked fourth in the\nMEMS\nfield, behind leader Silex Microsystems.\nIn 2021, TSMC was ranked third in the\nMEMS\nfield.\nOwnership\n[\nedit\n]\nAround 56 percent of TSMC shares are held by the general public and around 38 percent are held by institutions.
