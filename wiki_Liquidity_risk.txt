Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquidity_risk\n==================================================\n\nType of financial risk\nNot to be confused with\nLiquidity at risk\n.\nLiquidity risk\nis a\nfinancial risk\nthat for a certain period of time a given\nfinancial asset\n,\nsecurity\nor\ncommodity\ncannot be traded quickly enough in the market without\nimpacting\nthe market price.\nTypes\n[\nedit\n]\nMarket liquidity\n– An asset cannot be sold due to lack of liquidity in the market – essentially a sub-set of market risk.\nThis can be accounted for by:\nWidening bid–ask spread\nMaking explicit liquidity reserves\nLengthening holding period for\nvalue at risk\n(VaR) calculations\nFunding liquidity\n– Risk that liabilities:\nCannot be met when they fall due\nCan only be met at an uneconomic price\nCan be name-specific or systemic\nCauses\n[\nedit\n]\nLiquidity risk arises from situations in which a party interested in trading an\nasset\ncannot do it because nobody in the\nmarket\nwants to trade for that asset. Liquidity risk becomes particularly important to parties who are about to hold or currently hold an asset, since it affects their ability to trade.\nManifestation of liquidity risk is very different from a drop of price to zero. In case of a drop of an asset's price to zero, the market is saying that the asset is worthless. However, if one\nparty\ncannot find another party interested in trading the asset, this can potentially be only a problem of the\nmarket\nparticipants with finding each other.\nThis is why liquidity risk is usually found to be higher in emerging markets or low-volume markets.\nLiquidity risk is\nfinancial risk\ndue to uncertain\nliquidity\n. An institution might lose liquidity if its\ncredit rating\nfalls, it experiences sudden unexpected cash outflows, or some other event causes counterparties to avoid trading with or lending to the institution. A firm is also exposed to liquidity risk if markets on which it depends are subject to loss of liquidity.\nMarket and funding liquidity risks compound each other as it is difficult to sell when other investors face funding problems and it is difficult to get funding when the collateral is hard to sell.\nLiquidity risk also tends to compound other risks. If a trading organization has a position in an illiquid asset, its limited ability to liquidate that position at short notice will compound its market risk. Suppose a firm has offsetting cash flows with two different counterparties on a given day. If the\ncounterparty\nthat owes it a\npayment\ndefaults, the firm will have to raise cash from other sources to make its payment. Should it be unable to do so, it too will default. Here, liquidity risk is compounding\ncredit risk\n.\nA position can be hedged against market risk but still entail liquidity risk. This is true in the above credit risk example—the two payments are offsetting, so they entail credit risk but not\nmarket risk\n. Another example is the 1993\nMetallgesellschaft\ndebacle. Futures contracts were used to hedge an over-the-counter finance (OTC) obligation. It is debatable whether the hedge was effective from a market risk standpoint, but it was the\nliquidity crisis\ncaused by staggering\nmargin calls\non the futures that forced\nMetallgesellschaft\nto unwind the positions.\nAccordingly, liquidity risk has to be managed in addition to market, credit and other risks. Because of its tendency to compound other risks, it is difficult or impossible to isolate liquidity risk. In all but the simplest of circumstances, comprehensive metrics of liquidity risk do not exist. Certain techniques of\nasset liability management\ncan be applied to assessing liquidity risk. A simple test for liquidity risk is to look at future net cash flows on a day-by-day basis. Any day that has a sizeable negative net cash flow is of concern. Such an analysis can be supplemented with\nstress testing\n. Look at net cash flows on a day-to-day basis assuming that an important counterparty defaults.\nAnalyses such as these cannot easily take into account contingent cash flows, such as cash flows from derivatives or mortgage-backed securities. If an organization's cash flows are largely contingent, liquidity risk may be assessed using some form of scenario analysis. A general approach using scenario analysis might entail the following high-level steps:\nConstruct multiple scenarios for market movements and defaults over a given period of time\nAssess day-to-day cash flows under each scenario.\nBecause\nbalance sheets\ndiffer so significantly from one organization to the next, there is little standardization in how such analyses are implemented.\nRegulators are primarily concerned about systemic implications of liquidity risk.\nPricing\n[\nedit\n]\nRisk-averse investors naturally require higher expected return as compensation for liquidity risk. The liquidity-adjusted CAPM pricing model therefore states that, the higher an asset's market-liquidity risk, the higher its required return.\nA common method for estimating the upper bound for a security illiquidity discount is by using a Lookback option, where the premia is equal to the difference between the maximum value of a security during a restricted trading period and its value at the end of the period.\nWhen the method is extended for corporate debt it is shown that liquidity risk increases with a bond credit risk.\nMeasures of liquidity risk\n[\nedit\n]\nLiquidity gap\n[\nedit\n]\nCulp defines the liquidity gap as the net liquid assets of a firm. The excess value of the firm's liquid assets over its volatile liabilities. A company with a negative liquidity gap should focus on their cash balances and possible unexpected changes in their values.\nAs a static measure of liquidity risk, it gives no indication of how the gap would change with an increase in the firm's marginal funding cost.\nElasticity\n[\nedit\n]\nCulp denotes the change of net of assets over funded liabilities that occurs when the\nliquidity premium\non the bank's marginal funding cost rises by a small amount as the liquidity risk elasticity. For banks this would be measured as a spread over Libor, for nonfinancial the LRE would be measured as a spread over commercial paper rates.\nProblems with the use of liquidity risk elasticity are that it assumes parallel changes in funding spread across all maturities and that it is only accurate for small changes in funding spreads.\nMeasures of asset liquidity\n[\nedit\n]\nBid–ask spread\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nbid–ask spread\nis used by market participants as an asset liquidity measure. To compare different products the ratio of the spread to the product's bid price can be used. The smaller the ratio the more liquid the asset is.\nThis spread is composed of operational, administrative, and processing costs as well as the compensation required for the possibility of trading with a more informed trader.\nMarket depth\n[\nedit\n]\nHachmeister refers to\nmarket depth\nas the amount of an asset that can be bought and sold at various bid–ask spreads.\nSlippage\nis related to the concept of market depth. Knight and Satchell mention a flow trader needs to consider the effect of executing a large order on the market and to adjust the bid–ask to spread accordingly. They calculate the liquidity cost as the difference of the execution price and the initial execution price.\nImmediacy\n[\nedit\n]\nImmediacy refers to the time needed to successfully trade a certain amount of an asset at a prescribed cost.\nResilience\n[\nedit\n]\nHachmeister identifies the fourth dimension of liquidity as the speed with which prices return to former levels after a large transaction. Unlike the other measures, resilience can only be determined over a period of time, i.e., resilience is the capacity to recover.\nManagement\n[\nedit\n]\nSee also:\nLiquidity regulation §\nResponse to the 2008 financial crisis\n, and\nFinancial risk management §\nBanking\nLiquidity-adjusted value at risk\n[\nedit\n]\nLiquidity-adjusted VAR incorporates exogenous liquidity risk into\nValue at Risk\n. It can be defined at VAR + ELC (Exogenous Liquidity Cost). The ELC is the worst expected half-spread at a particular confidence level.\nAnother adjustment, introduced in the 1970s with a regulatory precursor to today's VAR measures,\nis to consider VAR over the period of time needed to liquidate the portfolio. VAR can be calculated over this time period. The\nBIS\nmentions "... a number of institutions are exploring the use of liquidity adjusted VAR, in which the holding periods in the risk assessment are adjusted by the length of time required to unwind positions."\nLiquidity at risk\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nLiquidity at risk\nAlan Greenspan\n(1999) discusses management of\nforeign exchange reserves\nand suggested a measure called\nliquidity at risk\n. A country's liquidity position under a range of possible outcomes for relevant financial variables (exchange rates, commodity prices, credit spreads, etc.) is considered. It might be possible to express a standard in terms of the probabilities of different outcomes. For example, an acceptable debt structure could have an average maturity—averaged over estimated distributions for relevant financial variables—in excess of a certain limit. In addition, countries could be expected to hold sufficient liquid reserves to ensure that they could avoid new borrowing for one year with a certain ex ante probability, such as 95 percent of the time.\nScenario analysis-based contingency plans\n[\nedit\n]\nThe FDIC discuss liquidity risk management and write "Contingency funding plans should incorporate events that could rapidly affect an institution’s liquidity, including a sudden inability to securitize assets, tightening of collateral requirements or other restrictive terms associated with secured borrowings, or the loss of a large depositor or counterparty."\nGreenspan's liquidity at risk concept is an example of scenario-based liquidity risk management.\nDiversification of liquidity providers\n[\nedit\n]\nIf several liquidity providers are on call, then if any of those providers increases its costs of supplying liquidity, the impact of this is reduced. The American Academy of Actuaries wrote "While a company is in good financial shape, it may wish to establish durable, ever green (i.e., always available) liquidity lines of credit. The credit issuer should have an appropriately high credit rating to increase the chances that the resources will be there when needed."\nDerivatives\n[\nedit\n]\nBhaduri, Meissner and Youn discuss five derivatives created specifically for hedging liquidity risk.:\nWithdrawal option: a put of the illiquid underlying at the market price.\nBermudan-style return put option: right to put the option at a specified strike.\nReturn swap: swap the underlying's return for LIBOR paid periodically.\nReturn swaption: option to enter into the return swap.\nLiquidity option: "knock-in"\nbarrier option\n, where the barrier is a liquidity metric.\nCase studies\n[\nedit\n]\nAmaranth Advisors LLC – 2006\n[\nedit\n]\nAmaranth Advisors\nlost roughly $6bn in the\nnatural gas\nfutures market in September 2006. Amaranth had taken a concentrated, leveraged, and undiversified position in its natural gas strategy. Amaranth's positions were staggeringly large, representing around 10% of the global market in natural gas futures.\nChin Carini notes that firms need to manage liquidity risk explicitly. The inability to sell a futures contract at or near the latest quoted price is related to one's concentration in the security. In Amaranth's case, the concentration was far too high and there were no natural counterparties when they needed to unwind the positions.\nChin Carini (2006) argues that part of the loss Amaranth incurred was due to asset illiquidity. Regression analysis on the 3 week return on natural gas future contracts from August 31, 2006, to September 21, 2006, against the excess open interest suggested that contracts whose open interest was much higher on August 31, 2006, than the historical normalized value, experienced larger negative returns.\nNorthern Rock – 2007\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nNationalisation of Northern Rock\nNorthern Rock\nsuffered from funding liquidity risk in September 2007 following the subprime crisis. The firm suffered from liquidity issues despite being solvent at the time, because maturing loans and deposits could not be renewed in the short-term money markets.\nIn response, the\nFSA\nnow places greater supervisory focus on liquidity risk especially with regard to "high-impact retail firms".\nLTCM – 1998\n[\nedit\n]\nLong-Term Capital Management (LTCM)\nwas bailed out by a consortium of 14 banks in 1998 after being caught in a cash-flow crisis when economic shocks resulted in excessive\nmark-to-market\nlosses and\nmargin calls\n. The fund suffered from a combination of funding and asset liquidity issues. The asset liquidity issue arose from LTCM's failure to account for liquidity becoming more valuable, as it did following the crisis. Since much of its balance sheet was exposed to liquidity risk premium, its short positions would increase in price relative to its long positions. This was essentially a massive, unhedged exposure to a single risk factor.\nLTCM had been aware of funding liquidity risk. Indeed, they estimated that in times of severe stress, cuts on AAA-rated commercial mortgages would increase from 2% to 10%, and similarly for other securities. In response to this, LTCM had negotiated long-term financing with margins fixed for several weeks on many of their collateralized loans. Due to an escalating liquidity spiral, LTCM could ultimately not fund its positions in spite of its numerous measures to control funding risk.\nSee also\n[\nedit\n]\nLiquidity (disambiguation)\nDeposit risk\nTreasury management\nReferences\n[\nedit\n]\n1\n2\n3\n4\nBrunnermeier, Markus K.; Pedersen, Lasse Heje (2009).
