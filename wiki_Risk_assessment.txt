As individual risks aggregate into portfolios or larger systems, risk engineers deploy statistical models and simulation techniques to uncover dependencies and potential cascade effects. This is of particular importance as school programs and others weigh the benefits of youth and adult participation in various outdoor learning activities against the inherent and other hazards present in those activities. Schools, corporate entities seeking team-building experiences, parents/guardians, and others considering outdoor experiences expect or require\norganizations to assess the hazards and risks of different outdoor activities—such as sailing, target shooting, hunting, mountaineering, or camping—and select activities with acceptable risk profiles.\nOutdoor education, wilderness adventure, and other outdoor-related organizations should, and are in some jurisdictions required, to conduct risk assessments before offering programs for commercial purposes.\nSuch organizations are given guidance on how to provide their risk assessments.\nRisk assessments for led outdoor activities form only one component of a comprehensive risk management plan, as many risk assessments use a basic linear-style thinking that does not employ more modern risk management practice employing complex socio-technical systems theory.\nEnvironment\n[\nedit source\n]\nEnvironmental Risk Assessment\n(ERA) aims to assess the effects of stressors, usually chemicals, on the local environment. A risk is an integrated assessment of the likelihood and severity of an undesired event. In ERA, the undesired event often depends on the chemical of interest and on the risk assessment scenario.\nThis undesired event is usually a detrimental effect on organisms, populations or\necosystems\n. Current ERAs usually compare an exposure to a no-effect level, such as the Predicted Environmental Concentration/\nPredicted No-Effect Concentration\n(PEC/PNEC) ratio in Europe. Although this type of ratio is useful and often used in regulation purposes, it is only an indication of an exceeded apparent threshold.\nNew approaches start to be developed in ERA to quantify this risk and to communicate effectively on it with both the managers and the general public.\nEcological risk assessment is complicated by the fact that there are many nonchemical stressors that substantially influence ecosystems, communities, and individual plants and animals, as well as across landscapes and regions.\nDefining the undesired (adverse) event is a political or policy judgment, further complicating applying traditional risk analysis tools to ecological systems.  Much of the policy debate surrounding ecological risk assessment is over defining precisely what is an adverse event.\nBiodiversity\n[\nedit source\n]\nBiodiversity Risk Assessments evaluate risks to\nbiological diversity\n, especially the risk of\nspecies\nextinction\nor the risk of\necosystem collapse\n. The units of assessments are the biological (species,\nsubspecies\nor\npopulations\n) or ecological entities (\nhabitats\n,\necosystems\n, etc.), and the risks are often related to human actions and interventions (threats and pressures). Regional and national protocols have been proposed by multiple academic or governmental institutions and working groups,\nbut global standards such as the\nRed List of Threatened Species\nand the\nIUCN Red List of Ecosystems\nhave been widely adopted, and are recognized or proposed as official indicators of progress toward international policy targets and goals, such as the\nAichi targets\nand the\nSustainable Development Goals\n.\nLaw\n[\nedit source\n]\nRisk assessments are used in numerous stages during the legal process and are developed to measure a wide variety of items, such as recidivism rates, potential pretrial issues, probation/parole, and to identify potential interventions for defendants.\nClinical psychologists, forensic psychologists, and other practitioners are responsible for conducting risk assessments.\nDepending on the risk assessment tool, practitioners are required to gather a variety of background information on the defendant or individual being assessed. This information includes their previous criminal history (if applicable) and other records (i.e. Demographics, Education, Job Status, Medical History), which can be accessed through direct interview with the defendant or on-file records.\nIn the pre-trial stage, a widely used risk assessment tool is the Public Safety Assessment,\nwhich predicts failure to appear in court, likelihood of a new criminal arrest while on pretrial release, and likelihood of a new violent criminal arrest while on pretrial release. Multiple items are observed and taken into account based on which aspect of the PSA is being focused, and like all other actuarial risk assessments, each item is assigned a weighted amount to produce a final score.\nDetailed information such as transparency on the items the PSA factors and how scores are distributed are accessible online.\nFor\ndefendants\nwho have been incarcerated, risk assessments are used to determine their likelihood of\nrecidivism\nand inform sentence length decisions. Risk assessments also aid parole/probation officers in determining the level of supervision a probationer should be subjected to and what interventions could be implemented to improve offender risk status.\nThe\nCorrectional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions\n(COMPAS) is a risk assessment too designed to measure pretrial release risk, general recidivism risk, and violent recidivism risk.
