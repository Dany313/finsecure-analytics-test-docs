Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interest_rate_risk\n==================================================\n\nFinancial loss due to interest rate changes\nInterest rate risk\nrefers to the potential for financial loss due to fluctuations in\ninterest rates\n.  It will, in turn, impact differently re\nmarket risk\n, i.e. impacting instruments such as Bonds, re banks and re insurers.\nMarket risk\n[\nedit\n]\nFluctuating\ninterest rates\nexpose\nbond\nowners to risk. How much interest rate risk a bond has depends on how sensitive its price is to interest rate changes in the market. The sensitivity depends on two things, the bond's time to maturity, and the coupon rate of the bond.\nInterest rate risk analysis is almost always based on simulating movements in one or more\nyield curves\nusing the\nHeath-Jarrow-Morton framework\nto ensure that the yield curve movements are both consistent with current market yield curves and such that no riskless\narbitrage\nis possible.  The Heath-Jarrow-Morton framework was developed in the early 1991 by David Heath of Cornell University, Andrew Morton of Lehman Brothers, and\nRobert A. Jarrow\nof Kamakura Corporation and Cornell University.\nThere are a number of standard calculations for measuring the impact of changing interest rates on a portfolio consisting of various assets and liabilities.  The most common techniques include:\nMarking to market\n, calculating the net market value of the assets and liabilities, sometimes called the "market value of portfolio equity"\nStress testing this market value by shifting the yield curve in a specific way.\nCalculating the\nvalue at risk\nof the portfolio\nCalculating the multiperiod cash flow or financial accrual income and expense for N periods forward in a deterministic set of future yield curves\nDoing step 4 with random yield curve movements and measuring the probability distribution of cash flows and financial accrual income over time.\nMeasuring the mismatch of the\ninterest sensitivity gap\nof assets and liabilities, by classifying each asset and liability by the timing of interest rate reset or maturity, whichever comes first.\nAnalyzing\nDuration\n,\nConvexity\n,\nDV01\nand\nKey Rate Duration\n.\nBanks\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nFinancial risk management §\nBanking\n, and\nInterest rate risk in the banking book\nThe assessment of interest rate risk is a very large topic at banks, thrifts, saving and loans, credit unions, and other finance companies, and among their regulators. The widely deployed\nCAMELS rating system\nassesses a financial institution's:\nC\napital adequacy,\nA\nssets,\nM\nanagement,\nE\narnings,\nL\niquidity, and\nS\nensitivity to market risk. A large portion of the Sensitivity in CAMELS is\ninterest rate risk\n. Much of what is known about assessing interest rate risk has been developed by the interaction of financial institutions with their regulators since the 1990s. Interest rate risk is the largest part of the sensitivity analysis in the CAMELS system for most banking institutions. When a bank receives a poor CAMELS rating (a 4 or 5 on the ratings scale), it is indicative of taking excessive risks and it's likely the bank is in an overall troubled condition and may be included on the\nFDIC problem bank list\n.\nSee the\nSensitivity section\nof the CAMELS rating system for a substantial list of links to documents and examiner manuals, issued by financial regulators, that cover many issues in the analysis of interest rate risk.\nIn addition to being subject to the CAMELS system, the largest banks are often subject to prescribed stress testing. The assessment of interest rate risk is typically informed by some type of stress testing. See:\nStress test (financial)\n,\nList of bank stress tests\n,\nList of systemically important banks\n,\nInterest rate risk in the banking book\n.\nInsurers\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nInsurance §\nInsurers' business model\n,\nFinancial risk management §\nInsurance\n, and\nIFRS 17\nFor\ninsurers\n,\ninterest rate risk is particularly significant as it affects both their\ninvestment portfolios\n(assets) and their obligations to policyholders (liabilities), impacting overall profitability and solvency. Further,\nthe mismatch\nbetween the\ndurations\nof\nassets and liabilities\ncan lead to cashflow and operational challenges in meeting obligations. Specific treatments will differ by insurer-profile:\nLife insurers\nare particularly exposed to interest rate risk because they hold long-term liabilities from\nlife insurance\npolicies and annuities. Changes in interest rates can impact the pricing of new products and the valuation of existing policies, as lower rates typically reduce investment income, resulting in tighter margins. Moreover, life insurers may face challenges in managing their\nasset-liability matching over long time horizons\nespecially in a prolonged low-interest-rate environment.\nShort-term insurers (such as\nproperty-\nand\ncasualty companies\n) also face interest rate risk but to a lesser extent. Their liabilities are typically shorter in duration, which may allow for quicker adjustments in investment strategies to respond to interest rate changes. However, sudden interest rate shifts can still affect the returns on investments supporting\nclaim reserves\n, thereby impacting the balance sheet, as well as underwriting performance.\nRisk management\nstrategies here include\nasset-liability management\n(ALM) techniques that aim to align the cash flows and\ndurations\nof assets and liabilities (respectively:\ncashflow matching\nand\nimmunization\n). Insurers may utilize derivatives and other financial instruments to\nhedge against rate fluctuations\n, along with diversifying their investment portfolios to mitigate exposure.
