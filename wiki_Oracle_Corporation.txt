Some new employees worked as receptionists or distributed coffee until more suitable positions became available.\nOracle in the late 1980s began selling\nenterprise software\nrunning on the database, starting with\nfinancial software\n, then manufacturing. The sales people then booked the value of future license sales in the current quarter, thereby increasing their bonuses.\nThis became a problem when the future sales subsequently failed to materialize. Oracle eventually had to restate its earnings twice, and also settled (out of court)\nclass-action\nlawsuits arising from its having overstated its earnings. Ellison stated in 1992 that Oracle had made "an incredible business mistake".\nHumphries described the cause ("building for years") as a combination of technical problems that benefited rivals, and the sales force using\nside letters\nand other improper tactics to meet the company's longstanding goal of doubling revenue each year. Ellison hired ("bring in adults", Jerry Held said) new executives\nJeff Henley\nand\nRaymond J. Lane\n, who helped the company recover from what Ken Jacobs later described as a "near death experience", in which Oracle required an investment from a Japanese steel company to meet payroll.\nCompetition\n[\nedit\n]\nThe relational database industry was unusually concentrated. Besides being headquartered near each other in the\nSan Francisco Bay area\n, database technology experts attended the same universities and served on the same standards committees (with\nJim Gray\nserving as neutral arbiter as companies tried to get competing proposals enacted).\nSales and marketing people were both fierce rivals and often moved between the companies, even as engineers socialized with each other. The smaller, later-founded database vendors viewed Oracle as their top enemy,\nwith Bob MacDonald of\nInformix Software\ncalling Oracle "the\nevil empire\n", while Humphries described his company as "the\nKlingons\nof" the bay.\nHeld later described his company's philosophy as "it's not good enough for Oracle to win. It was important for somebody else to lose".\nIngres\nwas its first major competitor; Oracle's strategy of focusing on one opponent at a time. Even publicly complimenting other rivals "to take the pressure off", Held said, while effective, caused it to neglect\nSybase\nas a threat. Conversely, when Sybase had technical problems "it was: how do you put every ounce of product marketing, sales effort and focus on that".\nIBM\n's endorsement of SQL in\nDb2\nin the early 1980s benefited Oracle and forced rivals like Ingres and\nInformix Corporation\nto adopt SQL to compete.\nOracle acknowledged IBM as the standard while emphasizing its own superiority, touting "total IBM SQL compatibility" while IBM's software ran "\nonly on IBM mainframes\n".\nBy the mid-1980s the company described its database as "the last DBMS", bragging that Oracle now had larger database revenue than\ndBASE\nmaker\nAshton-Tate\n,\nand stating that Microsoft, Ashton-Tate, and Sybase's planned\nMicrosoft SQL Server\n"jumped on Oracle's SQL bandwagon".\nMacDonald credited Oracle with "being marketing oriented before any of the competitors ... pushing the envelope on selling the future way ahead of the rest of us".\nStu Schuster of Sybase said "Larry taught us a lot about marketing". While other database companies' brochures emphasized technical features, Oracle advertisements showed an Oracle jet fighter shooting down Ashton-Tate and Borland biplanes;\ndBASE, IBM, and Db2 were among the many competitors Oracle marketing criticized by name.\nConversely, another Oracle ad quoted Microsoft's\nBill Gates\n, Sun's\nScott McNealy\n, Hewlett-Packard's\nJohn A. Young\n, and\nApple Computer\n's\nJohn Sculley\nas agreeing with Ellison on Oracle's database breakthroughs.\nLarry Rowe of Ingres said "you could never come up with a strategy to beat Oracle because whatever you said today, two days later Ellison was saying it with more marketing dollars".\nJacobs said, by contrast:\nIt really surprises me when people say Oracle's a great marketing machine because by definition, if you think a company has great marketing, they don't. If you think they have great technology then they do have a great marketing machine. Larry's belief has fundamentally always been that marketing isn't critical; he's famous for saying, "If you're not building the product and you're not selling it, tell me what it is you do." Because those are the things that he felt were important. So marketing has never been a focus.\nThe "benchmark wars" began in the early 1980s;\nRoger Sippl\nof Informix said that when his company's product beat Oracle on 27 of 30\nbenchmarks\n, advertisements titled "Oracle wins again!" appeared citing the three it won,\nand Ellison allegedly called Dave DeWitt at the\nUniversity of Wisconsin\nafter he developed a benchmark unfavorable to Oracle, threatening to get the professor fired.\nSchuster and Held recalled "a not very pleasant environment because it was so directly competitive". They and Sippl described "a vicious cycle" of companies battling each other over, for example, whose\ndistributed database\nhad the best\ntwo-phase commit\n:\nThe ads weren't to let me show you how my product helps you solve your business problem, Mr. Customer. It's let me tell you how much better I am than the guy down the street to the point of taking out billboards.\nEven IBM participated in the "billboard wars".\nBy 1995 Oracle had 44% of the $2.4 billion relational database market; Sybase had 17% and Informix had 16%. Informix claimed that Oracle had hired away Informix engineers to disclose important trade secrets about an upcoming product. Informix finally dropped its lawsuit against Oracle in 1997.\nHeld said\nAnd, in one year, we kind of turned the focus from Sybase to Informix, and basically they never knew what hit them.
