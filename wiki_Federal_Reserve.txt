For other uses, see\nThe Fed (disambiguation)\n.\nThe\nFederal Reserve System\n(often shortened to the\nFederal Reserve\n, or simply\nthe Fed\n) is the\ncentral banking\nsystem of the\nUnited States\n. It was created on December 23, 1913, with the enactment of the\nFederal Reserve Act\n, after a series of\nfinancial panics\n(particularly the\nPanic of 1907\n) led to the desire for central control of the monetary system in order to alleviate financial crises.\nAlthough an instrument of the U.S. government, the Federal Reserve System considers itself "an independent central bank because its monetary policy decisions do not have to be approved by the president or by anyone else in the executive or legislative branches of government, it does not receive funding appropriated by Congress, and the terms of the members of the board of governors span multiple presidential and congressional terms."\nOver the years, events such as the\nGreat Depression\nin the 1930s and the\nGreat Recession\nduring the 2000s have led to the expansion of the roles and responsibilities of the Federal Reserve System.\nCongress\nestablished three key objectives for\nmonetary policy\nin the Federal Reserve Act: maximizing employment, stabilizing prices, and moderating long-term interest rates.\nThe first two objectives are sometimes referred to as the Federal Reserve's dual mandate.\nIts duties have expanded over the years, and include supervising and\nregulating banks\n, maintaining the stability of the financial system, and providing financial services to\ndepository institutions\n, the U.S. government, and foreign official institutions.\nThe Fed also conducts research into the economy and provides numerous publications, such as the\nBeige Book\nand the\nFRED database\n.\nThe Federal Reserve System is composed of several layers. It is governed by the presidentially appointed\nboard of governors\nor Federal Reserve Board (FRB). Twelve regional\nFederal Reserve Banks\n, located in cities throughout the nation, regulate and oversee privately owned commercial banks.\nNationally chartered commercial banks are required to hold stock in, and can elect some board members of, the Federal Reserve Bank of their region.\nThe\nFederal Open Market Committee\n(FOMC) sets monetary policy by adjusting the target for the\nfederal funds rate\n, which generally influences\nmarket interest rates\nand, in turn, US economic activity via the\nmonetary transmission mechanism\n. The FOMC consists of all seven members of the board of governors and the twelve regional Federal Reserve Bank presidents, though only five bank presidents vote at a time: the president of the\nNew York Fed\nand four others who rotate through one-year voting terms. There are also various advisory councils.\nIt has a structure unique among central banks, and is also unusual in that the\nUnited States Department of the Treasury\n, an entity outside of the central bank, prints the\ncurrency\nused.\nThe federal government sets the salaries of the board's seven governors, and it receives all the system's annual profits after dividends on member banks' capital investments are paid, and an account surplus is maintained. In 2015, the Federal Reserve earned a net income of $100.2 billion and transferred $97.7 billion to the U.S. Treasury,\nand 2020 earnings were approximately $88.6 billion with remittances to the U.S. Treasury of $86.9 billion.\nThe Federal Reserve\nhas been criticized\nfor its approach to managing\ninflation\n, perceived lack of transparency, and its role in economic downturns.\nFigures such as\nMilton Friedman\nand\nRon Paul\nhave argued that its actions, such as\nexpansionary policies\nand bailouts, contribute to inflation,\nasset bubbles\n, and\nmoral hazard\n.\nCritics have also noted that the shift from the\ngold standard\nto\nfiat currency\nhas led to long-term inflation and financial instability, with some calling for the\nFed's abolition\nor greater accountability through audits.\nPurpose\n[\nedit\n]\nBefore the founding of the Federal Reserve System, the United States underwent several financial crises. A particularly severe crisis in 1907 led Congress to enact the\nFederal Reserve Act\nin 1913. The primary declared motivation for creating the Federal Reserve System was to address\nbanking panics\n.\nOther stated purposes, are "to furnish an elastic currency, to afford means of rediscounting\ncommercial paper\n, [and] to establish a more effective supervision of banking in the United States".\nToday, the purposes of the Federal Reserve include the responsibilities to:\nAddress the problem of\nbanking panics\nServe as the\ncentral bank\nfor the United States\nBalance private interests of banks and the centralized responsibility of government\nSupervise and regulate banking institutions\nProtect the credit rights of consumers\nConduct\nmonetary policy\nby influencing\nmarket interest rates\nto achieve the goals of\nMaximum employment\nStable prices, interpreted as an\ninflation\nrate of 2 percent per year on average\nModerate long-term interest rates\nMaintain the stability of the financial system and contain\nsystemic risk\nin financial markets\nProvide financial services to depository institutions, the U.S. government, and foreign official institutions\nFacilitate the exchange of payments among regions and the operation of the nation's payments system\nRespond to local liquidity needs\nStrengthen U.S. standing in the world economy\nFractional-reserve bank\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nBank run\nand\nFractional-reserve banking\nBanks usually invest the majority of the funds received from depositors. However, banking institutions in the United States are required to hold reserves\n‍\n—\n‌\namounts of currency and deposits in other banks\n‍\n—\n‌\nequal to a fraction of the amount of the bank's deposit liabilities owed to customers. This practice is called\nfractional-reserve banking\n. On rare occasions, too many of the bank's customers will withdraw their savings such that the bank cannot continue operating on its own; this is called a\nbank run\n. Bank runs can lead to a multitude of social and economic problems. The Federal Reserve System was designed as an attempt to prevent or minimize the occurrence of bank runs, and possibly act as a\nlender of last resort\nwhen a bank run occurs. Many economists, following\nNobel\nlaureate\nMilton Friedman\n, believe that the Federal Reserve inappropriately refused to lend money to small banks during the bank runs of 1929; Friedman argued that this contributed to the\nGreat Depression\n.\nCheck clearing system\n[\nedit\n]\nBefore the establishment of the Federal Reserve, during times of economic uncertainty, some banks refused to\nclear checks\nfrom certain other banks, which led to large-scale\nbank failure\nin the early 20th-century. Hence, a national check-clearing system was created in the Federal Reserve System.\nThe Federal Reserve can physically accept and transport cheques.\nLender of last resort\n[\nedit\n]\nIn the United States of America, the Federal Reserve serves as the\nlender of last resort\nto those institutions that cannot obtain credit elsewhere, institutions the collapse of which would have serious implications for the economy. It took over this role from the private sector\nclearing houses\nwhich operated during the\nFree Banking Era\n. The availability of liquidity is intended to prevent bank runs.\nFluctuations\n[\nedit\n]\nReserve Banks provide liquidity to banks to meet short-term needs stemming from seasonal fluctuations in deposits or unexpected withdrawals through its\ndiscount window\nand\ncredit operations\n. Longer-term liquidity may also be provided in exceptional circumstances. The rate the Fed charges banks for these loans is called the discount rate (officially the primary credit rate). By making these loans, the Fed serves as a buffer against unexpected day-to-day fluctuations in reserve demand and supply. This contributes to the effective functioning of the banking system, alleviates pressure in the reserves market, and reduces the extent of unexpected movements in the interest rates.\nFor example, on September 16, 2008, the Federal Reserve Board authorized an $85\nbillion loan to stave off the bankruptcy of international insurance giant\nAmerican International Group\n(AIG).\nCentral bank\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nCentral bank\nObverse of a Federal Reserve\n$1 note\nissued in 2021\nAs the\ncentral bank\nof the United States, the Fed serves as a\nbanker's bank\nand as the government's bank. As the banker's bank, it helps to assure the safety and efficiency of the payments system. As the government's bank or\nfiscal agent\n, the Fed processes a variety of financial transactions involving trillions of dollars. The\nU.S. Treasury\nkeeps a checking account with the Federal Reserve, through which incoming federal tax deposits and outgoing government payments are handled. As part of this service relationship, the Fed sells and redeems\nU.S. government securities\nsuch as savings bonds and Treasury bills, notes and bonds. It also issues the nation's\ncoin\nand\npaper currency\n. The U.S. Treasury, through its\nBureau of the Mint\nand\nBureau of Engraving and Printing\n, actually produces the nation's cash supply and, in effect, sells the paper currency to the Federal Reserve Banks at manufacturing cost, and the coins at face value. The Federal Reserve Banks then distribute it to other financial institutions in various ways.\nDuring the\nFiscal Year\n2020, the Bureau of Engraving and Printing delivered 57.95\nbillion notes at an average cost of 7.4 cents per note.\nFederal funds\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nFederal funds\nFederal funds, officially\nFederal Reserve Deposits\n, are the reserve balances that private banks keep at their local Federal Reserve Bank.\nThese balances are the namesake reserves of the Federal Reserve System. The purpose of keeping funds at a Federal Reserve Bank is to have a mechanism for private banks to lend funds to one another. This market for funds plays an important role in the Federal Reserve System as it is the basis for its\nmonetary policy\nwork. Monetary policy is put into effect partly by influencing how much interest the private banks charge each other for the lending of these funds. Federal reserve accounts contain federal reserve credit, which can be converted into\nfederal reserve notes\n.\nBank regulator\n[\nedit\n]\nThe Federal Reserve regulates private banks. The system was designed out of a compromise between the competing philosophies of privatization and government regulation. In 2006\nDonald L. Kohn\n, vice chairman of the board of governors, summarized the history of this compromise:\nAgrarian and progressive interests, led by William Jennings Bryan, favored a central bank under public, rather than banker, control. However, the vast majority of the nation's bankers, concerned about government intervention in the banking business, opposed a central bank structure directed by political appointees.

The legislation that Congress ultimately adopted in 1913 reflected a hard-fought battle to balance these two competing views and created the hybrid public-private, centralized-decentralized structure that we have today.\nIn the structure of the Federal Reserve System, private banks elect members of the board of directors at their regional Federal Reserve Bank while the members of the board of governors are selected by the\npresident of the United States\nand confirmed by the\nUnited States Senate\n.\nGovernment regulation and supervision\n[\nedit\n]\nBen Bernanke\n(lower right), former chairman of the Federal Reserve Board of Governors, at a\nHouse Financial Services Committee\nhearing on February 10, 2009. The Board and, under delegated authority, the Federal Reserve Banks, supervise approximately 900 state member banks and 5,000 bank holding companies. Other federal agencies also serve as the primary federal supervisors of commercial banks; the\nOffice of the Comptroller of the Currency\nsupervises national banks, and the\nFederal Deposit Insurance Corporation\nsupervises\nstate banks\nthat are not members of the Federal Reserve System.\nSome regulations issued by the Board apply to the entire banking industry, whereas others apply only to member banks, that is,\nstate banks\nthat have chosen to join the Federal Reserve System and national banks, which by law must be members of the System. The Chair has formal responsibilities in the international arena as well.\nThe Federal Banking Agency Audit Act, enacted in 1978 as Public Law 95-320 and 31 U.S.C. section 714 establish that the board of governors of the Federal Reserve System and the Federal Reserve banks may be audited by the\nGovernment Accountability Office\n(GAO).\nThe GAO has authority to audit check-processing, currency storage and shipments, and some regulatory and bank examination functions – though there are restrictions to what the GAO may audit.\nRegulatory and oversight responsibilities\n[\nedit\n]\nThe board of directors of each Federal Reserve Bank District also has regulatory and supervisory responsibilities. The act subjects all depository institutions, not just member commercial banks, to reserve requirements and grants them equal access to Reserve Bank payment services. The twelve Federal Reserve Banks provide banking services to depository institutions and to the federal government. For depository institutions, they maintain accounts and provide various payment services, including collecting checks, electronically transferring funds, and distributing and receiving currency and coin. For the federal government, the Reserve Banks act as fiscal agents, paying Treasury checks; processing electronic payments; and issuing, transferring, and redeeming U.S. government securities.\nThe Federal Reserve plays a role in the nation's retail and wholesale payments systems by providing financial services to depository institutions. Retail payments are generally for relatively small-dollar amounts and often involve a depository institution's retail clients. The data was released on March 31, 2011.\nMonetary policy\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nMonetary policy of the United States\nand\nFractional-reserve banking\nThe term "\nmonetary policy\n" refers to the actions undertaken by a central bank, such as the Federal Reserve, to influence economic activity (the\noverall demand\nfor goods and services) to help promote national economic goals. The\nFederal Reserve Act of 1913\ngave the Federal Reserve authority to set monetary policy in the United States. The Fed's mandate for monetary policy is commonly known as the dual mandate of promoting maximum employment and stable prices, the latter being interpreted as a stable inflation rate of 2 percent per year on average. The Fed's monetary policy influences economic activity by influencing the general level of\ninterest rates\nin the economy, which again via the\nmonetary transmission mechanism\naffects households' and firms' demand for goods and services and in turn employment and inflation.\nUnemployment\nvs\nInflation\nvs\nInverted yield curve\nUnemployment rate\nInflation\nCPI\n10 year\nbond\nminus 2 year bond\ninverted yield curve\nInterbank lending\n[\nedit\n]\nThe Federal Reserve sets monetary policy by influencing the\nfederal funds rate\n(FFR), which is the rate of interbank lending of\nreserve balances\n. The rate that banks charge each other for these loans is determined in the\ninterbank market\n, and the Federal Reserve influences this rate through the "tools" of monetary policy described in the\nTools\nsection\nbelow. The federal funds rate is a short-term interest rate that the FOMC focuses on, which affects the longer-term interest rates throughout the economy. The Federal Reserve explained the implementation of its monetary policy in 2021:\nThe FOMC has the ability to influence the federal funds rate–and thus the cost of short-term interbank credit–by changing the rate of interest the Fed pays on reserve balances that banks hold at the Fed. A bank is unlikely to lend to another bank (or to any of its customers) at an interest rate lower than the rate that the bank can earn on reserve balances held at the Fed. And because overall reserve balances are currently abundant, if a bank wants to borrow reserve balances, it likely will be able to do so without having to pay a rate much above the rate of interest paid by the Fed.\nChanges in the target for the federal funds rate affect overall financial conditions through various channels, including subsequent changes in the market interest rates that commercial banks and other lenders charge on short-term and longer-term loans, and changes in\nasset prices\nand in currency\nexchange rates\n, which again affects\nprivate consumption\n,\ninvestment\nand\nnet export\n. By easening or tightening the stance of monetary policy, i.e. lowering or raising its target for the federal funds rate, the Fed can either spur or restrain growth in the overall US demand for goods and services.\nTools\n[\nedit\n]\nThere are four main tools of monetary policy that the Federal Reserve uses to implement its monetary policy:\nInterest on reserve balances (IORB)\nInterest paid on funds that banks hold in their reserve balance accounts at their Federal Reserve Bank.\nIORB is the primary tool for moving the federal funds rate within the target range.\nOvernight reverse repurchase agreement (ON RRP) facility\nThe Fed's standing offer to many large nonbank financial institutions to deposit funds at the Fed and earn interest. Acts as a supplementary tool for moving the FFR within the target range.\nOpen market operations\nPurchases and sales of U.S. Treasury and federal agency securities. Used to maintain an ample supply of reserves.\nDiscount window\nThe Fed's lending to banks at the discount rate.\nHelps put a ceiling on the FFR.\nFederal funds rate\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nFederal funds rate\nThe Federal Reserve System implements\nmonetary policy\nlargely by targeting the\nfederal funds rate\n. This is the\ninterest rate\nthat banks charge each other for overnight loans of\nfederal funds\n, which are the reserves held by banks at the Fed. This rate is actually determined by the market and is not explicitly mandated by the Fed. The Fed therefore tries to align the effective federal funds rate with the targeted rate, mainly by adjusting its IORB rate.\nThe Federal Reserve System usually adjusts the federal funds rate target by 0.25% or 0.50% at a time.\nInterest on reserve balances\n[\nedit\n]\nThe interest on reserve balances (IORB) is the interest that the Fed pays on funds held by commercial banks in their reserve balance accounts at the individual Federal Reserve System banks. It is an administrated interest rate (i.e. set directly by the Fed as opposed to a\nmarket interest rate\nwhich is determined by the forces of supply and demand).\nAs banks are unlikely to lend their reserves in the FFR market for less than they get paid by the Fed, the IORB guides the effective FFR and is used as the primary tool of the Fed's monetary policy.\nOpen market operations\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nOpen market operations\nOpen market operations are done through the sale and purchase of\nUnited States Treasury securities\n, or "Treasurys". The Federal Reserve buys Treasurys both directly and via\nprimary dealers\n, which have accounts at depository institutions.\nThe Federal Reserve's objective for open market operations has varied over the years. During the 1980s, the focus gradually shifted toward attaining a specified level of the\nfederal funds rate\n(the rate that banks charge each other for overnight loans of federal funds, which are the reserves held by banks at the Fed), a process that was largely complete by the end of the decade.\nUntil the\n2008 financial crisis\n, the Fed used open market operations as its primary tool to adjust the supply of reserve balances in order to keep the federal funds rate around the Fed's target.\nThis regime is also known as a limited reserves regime.\nAfter the\n2008 financial crisis\n, the Federal Reserve has adopted a so-called ample reserves regime where open market operations leading to modest changes in the supply of reserves are no longer effective in influencing the FFR. Instead the Fed uses its administered rates, in particular the IORB rate, to influence the FFR.\nHowever, open market operations are still an important maintenance tool in the overall framework of the conduct of monetary policy as they are used for ensuring that reserves remain ample.\nRepurchase agreements\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nRepurchase agreement\nTo smooth temporary or cyclical changes in the money supply, the desk engages in\nrepurchase agreements\n(repos) with its primary dealers. Repos are essentially secured, short-term lending by the Fed. On the day of the transaction, the Fed deposits money in a primary dealer's reserve account, and receives the promised securities as\ncollateral\n. When the transaction matures, the process unwinds: the Fed returns the collateral and charges the primary dealer's reserve account for the principal and accrued interest. The term of the repo (the time between settlement and maturity) can vary from 1 day (called an overnight repo) to 65 days.\nDiscount window and discount rate\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nDiscount window\nThe Federal Reserve System also directly sets the\ndiscount rate\n, which is the interest rate for "discount window lending", overnight loans that member banks borrow directly from the Fed. Funds placed in term deposits are removed from the accounts of participating institutions for the life of the term deposit and thus drain reserve balances from the banking system. The bursting of the\nUnited States housing bubble\nprompted the Fed to buy mortgage-backed securities for the first time in November 2008. The first new tool, called the\nTerm Auction Facility\n, was added on December 12, 2007. It was announced as a temporary tool,\nbut remained in place for a prolonged period of time.\nCreation of the second new tool, called the\nTerm Securities Lending Facility\n, was announced on March 11, 2008.\nThe main difference between these two facilities was that the Term Auction Facility was used to inject cash into the banking system whereas the Term securities Lending Facility was used to inject\ntreasury securities\ninto the banking system.\nCreation of the third tool, called the\nPrimary Dealer Credit Facility\n(PDCF), was announced on March 16, 2008.\nThe PDCF was a fundamental change in Federal Reserve policy because it enabled the Fed to lend directly to\nprimary dealers\n, which was previously against Fed policy.\nThe differences between these three facilities was described by the Federal Reserve:\nThe Term  auction Facility program offers term funding to depository institutions via a bi-weekly auction, for fixed amounts of credit. In 1775, the Continental Congress, as well as the states, began issuing paper currency, calling the bills "\nContinentals\n".\nThe Continentals were backed only by future tax revenue, and were used to help finance the Revolutionary War. Overprinting, as well as British counterfeiting, caused the value of the Continental to diminish quickly. This experience with paper money led the United States to strip the power to issue Bills of Credit (paper money) from a draft of the new Constitution on August 16, 1787,\nas well as banning such issuance by the various states, and limiting the states' ability to make anything but gold or silver coin legal tender on August 28.\nIn 1791, the government granted the\nFirst Bank of the United States\na charter to operate as the U.S. central bank until 1811.\nThe First Bank of the United States came to an end under\nPresident Madison\nwhen Congress refused to renew its charter. The\nSecond Bank of the United States\nwas established in 1816, and lost its authority to be the central bank of the U.S. twenty years later under\nPresident Jackson\nwhen its charter expired. Both banks were based upon the Bank of England.\nUltimately, a third national bank, known as the Federal Reserve, was established in 1913 and still exists to this day.\nFirst Central Bank, 1791 and Second Central Bank, 1816\n[\nedit\n]\nThe first U.S. institution with central banking responsibilities was the\nFirst Bank of the United States\n, chartered by Congress and signed into law by President\nGeorge Washington\non February 25, 1791, at the urging of\nAlexander Hamilton\n. This was done despite strong opposition from\nThomas Jefferson\nand\nJames Madison\n, among numerous others. The charter was for twenty years and expired in 1811 under President Madison, when Congress refused to renew it.\nIn 1816, however, Madison revived it in the form of the\nSecond Bank of the United States\n. Years later, early renewal of the bank's charter became the primary issue in the reelection of President\nAndrew Jackson\n. After Jackson, who was opposed to the central bank, was reelected, he pulled the government's funds out of the bank. Jackson was the only President to completely pay off the national debt\nbut his efforts to close the bank contributed to the\nPanic of 1837\n. The bank's charter was not renewed in 1836, and it would fully dissolve after several years as a private corporation.\nFrom 1837 to 1862, in the\nFree Banking Era\nthere was no formal central bank. From 1846 to 1921, an\nIndependent Treasury System\nruled.
From 1863 to 1913, a system of national banks was instituted by the 1863\nNational Banking Act\nduring which a series of bank panics, in\n1873\n,\n1893\n, and\n1907\noccurred.\nCreation of Third Central Bank, 1907–1913\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nHistory of the Federal Reserve System\nThe main motivation for the third central banking system came from the\nPanic of 1907\n, which caused a renewed desire among legislators, economists, and bankers for an overhaul of the monetary system.\nDuring the last quarter of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th century, the United States economy went through a series of\nfinancial panics\n.\nAccording to many economists, the previous national banking system had two main weaknesses: an\ninelastic\ncurrency and a lack of liquidity.\nIn 1908, Congress enacted the\nAldrich–Vreeland Act\n, which provided for an emergency currency and established the\nNational Monetary Commission\nto study banking and currency reform.\nThe National Monetary Commission returned with recommendations which were repeatedly rejected by Congress. A revision crafted during a secret meeting on\nJekyll Island\nby Senator Aldrich and representatives of the nation's top finance and industrial groups later became the basis of the\nFederal Reserve Act\n.\nFederal Reserve Act, 1913\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nFederal Reserve Act\nNewspaper clipping, December 24, 1913\nThe head of the bipartisan National Monetary Commission was financial expert and Senate\nRepublican\nleader\nNelson Aldrich\n. Aldrich set up two commissions – one to study the American monetary system in depth and the other, headed by Aldrich himself, to study the European central banking systems and report on them.\nIn early November 1910, Aldrich met with five well known members of the New York banking community to devise a central banking bill.\nPaul Warburg\n, an attendee of the meeting and longtime advocate of central banking in the U.S., later wrote that Aldrich was "bewildered at all that he had absorbed abroad and he was faced with the difficult task of writing a highly technical bill while being harassed by the daily grind of his parliamentary duties".\nAfter ten days of deliberation, the bill, which would later be referred to as the "Aldrich Plan", was agreed upon. It had several key components, including a central bank with a Washington-based headquarters and fifteen branches located throughout the U.S. in geographically strategic locations, and a uniform elastic currency based on gold and commercial paper. Aldrich believed a central banking system with no political involvement was best, but was convinced by Warburg that a plan with no public control was not politically feasible.\nThe compromise involved representation of the public sector on the board of directors.\nAldrich's bill met much opposition from politicians. Critics charged Aldrich of being biased due to his close ties to wealthy bankers such as\nJ. P. Morgan\nand\nJohn D. Rockefeller Jr.\n, Aldrich's son-in-law. Most Republicans favored the Aldrich Plan,\nbut it lacked enough support in Congress to pass because rural and western states viewed it as favoring the "eastern establishment".\nIn contrast, progressive Democrats favored a reserve system owned and operated by the government; they believed that public ownership of the central bank would end Wall Street's control of the American currency supply.\nConservative Democrats fought for a privately owned, yet decentralized, reserve system, which would still be free of Wall Street's control.\nThe original Aldrich Plan was dealt a fatal blow in 1912, when Democrats won the White House and Congress.\nNonetheless, President\nWoodrow Wilson\nbelieved that the Aldrich plan would suffice with a few modifications. The plan became the basis for the Federal Reserve Act, which was proposed by Senator\nRobert Owen\nin May 1913. The primary difference between the two bills was the transfer of control of the board of directors (called the Federal Open Market Committee in the Federal Reserve Act) to the government.\nThe bill passed Congress on December 23, 1913,\non a mostly partisan basis, with most Democrats voting "yea" and most Republicans voting "nay".\nThe House voted on December 22, 1913, with 298 voting yes to 60 voting no. The Senate voted 43–25 on December 23, 1913.\nPresident\nWoodrow Wilson\nsigned the bill later that day.\nFederal Reserve era, 1913–present\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nHistory of the Federal Reserve\nKey laws affecting the Federal Reserve have been:\nFederal Reserve Act\n, 1913\nGlass–Steagall Act\n, 1933\nBanking Act of 1935\nEmployment Act of 1946\nFederal Reserve-Treasury Department Accord of 1951\nBank Holding Company Act of 1956\nand the amendments of 1970\nFederal Reserve Reform Act of 1977\nInternational Banking Act of 1978\nFull Employment and Balanced Growth Act\n(1978)\nDepository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act\n(1980)\nFinancial Institutions Reform, Recovery and Enforcement Act of 1989\nFederal Deposit Insurance Corporation Improvement Act of 1991\nGramm–Leach–Bliley Act\n(1999)\nFinancial Services Regulatory Relief Act\n(2006)\nEmergency Economic Stabilization Act\n(2008)\nDodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act\n(2010)\nThe Banking Act of 1935 created the modern structure of the Federal Reserve and placed monetary decisions beyond presidential control, thus enshrining the\nindependence\nof the Federal Reserve.\nSecond Trump presidency\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nTrump v.
