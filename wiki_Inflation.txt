The common measure of inflation is the\ninflation rate\n, the annualized percentage change in a general\nprice index\n.\nChanges in inflation are widely attributed to increases in the\nmoney supply\n, fluctuations in\nreal\ndemand\nfor goods and services (also known as\ndemand shocks\n, including changes in\nfiscal\nor\nmonetary policy\n), changes in available supplies such as during\nenergy crises\n(also known as\nsupply shocks\n), significant decreases in\ninterest rates\nset by the\ncentral bank\n, or changes in inflation expectations, which may be self-fulfilling.\nModerate inflation affects economies in both positive and negative ways. The negative effects would include an increase in the\nopportunity cost\nof holding money; uncertainty over future inflation, which may discourage investment and savings; and, if inflation were rapid enough, shortages of\ngoods\nas consumers begin\nhoarding\nout of concern that prices will increase in the future. Positive effects include reducing unemployment due to\nnominal wage rigidity\n,\nallowing the central bank greater freedom in carrying out monetary policy, encouraging loans and investment instead of money hoarding, and avoiding the inefficiencies associated with deflation.\nToday, most economists favour a low and steady rate of inflation. Low (as opposed to zero or\nnegative\n) inflation reduces the likelihood of economic\nrecessions\nby enabling the labor market to adjust more quickly and reduces the risk that a\nliquidity trap\nprevents\nmonetary policy\nfrom stabilizing the economy, while also avoiding the costs associated with high inflation.\nThe task of keeping the rate of inflation low and stable is usually given to central banks that control monetary policy, normally through the setting of interest rates and by carrying out\nopen market operations\n.\nTerminology\n[\nedit\n]\nThe term originates from the Latin\ninflare\n(to blow into or inflate). Conceptually, inflation refers to the general trend of prices, not changes in any specific price. For example, if people choose to buy more cucumbers than tomatoes, cucumbers consequently become more expensive and tomatoes less expensive. These changes are not related to inflation; they reflect a shift in tastes. Inflation is related to the value of currency itself. When currency was linked with gold, if new gold deposits were found, the price of gold and the value of currency would fall, and consequently, the prices of all other goods would become higher.\nClassical economics\n[\nedit\n]\nBy the nineteenth century, economists categorised three separate factors that cause a rise or fall in the price of goods: a change in the\nvalue\nor production costs of the good, a change in the\nprice of money\nwhich then was usually a fluctuation in the\ncommodity\nprice of the metallic content in the currency, and\ncurrency depreciation\nresulting from an increased supply of currency relative to the quantity of redeemable metal backing the currency. Following the proliferation of private\nbanknote\ncurrency printed during the\nAmerican Civil War\n, the term "inflation" started to appear as a direct reference to the\ncurrency depreciation\nthat occurred as the quantity of redeemable banknotes outstripped the quantity of metal available for their redemption. At that time, the term inflation referred to the\ndevaluation\nof the currency, and not to a rise in the price of goods.\nThis relationship between the over-supply of banknotes and a resulting\ndepreciation\nin their value was noted by earlier classical economists such as\nDavid Hume\nand\nDavid Ricardo\n, who would go on to examine and debate what effect a currency devaluation has on the price of goods.\nRelated concepts\n[\nedit\n]\nOther economic concepts related to inflation include:\ndeflation\n–\na fall in the general price level;\ndisinflation\n–\na decrease in the rate of inflation;\nhyperinflation\n–\nan out-of-control inflationary spiral;\nstagflation\n–\na combination of inflation, slow economic growth and high unemployment;\nreflation\n–\nan attempt to raise the general level of prices to counteract deflationary pressures;\nasset price inflation\n–\na general rise in the prices of financial assets without a corresponding increase in the prices of goods or services;\nand\nagflation\n–\nan advanced increase in the\nprice for food\nand industrial agricultural crops when compared with the general rise in prices.\nMore specific forms of inflation refer to sectors whose prices vary semi-independently from the general trend. By diluting the silver with other metals, the government could issue more coins without increasing the amount of silver used to make them. When the cost of each coin is lowered in this way, the government profits from an increase in\nseigniorage\n.\nThis practice would increase the money supply but at the same time the relative value of each coin would be lowered. For example, if the price of a can of corn changes from $0.90 to $1.00 over the course of a year, with no change in quality, then this price difference represents inflation. This single price change would not, however, represent general inflation in an overall economy. Overall inflation is measured as the price change of a large "basket" of representative goods and services. This is the purpose of a\nprice index\n, which is the combined price of a "basket" of many goods and services. The combined price is the sum of the weighted prices of items in the "basket". A weighted price is calculated by multiplying the\nunit price\nof an item by the number of that item the average consumer purchases. Weighted pricing is necessary to measure the effect of individual unit price changes on the economy's overall inflation. The\nconsumer price index\n, for example, uses data collected by surveying households to determine what proportion of the typical consumer's overall spending is spent on specific goods and services, and weights the average prices of those items accordingly. Those weighted average prices are combined to calculate the overall price. To better relate price changes over time, indexes typically choose a "base year" price and assign it a value of 100. Index prices in subsequent years are then expressed in relation to the base year price.\nWhile comparing inflation measures for various periods one has to take into consideration the\nbase effect\nas well.\nInflation measures are often modified over time, either for the relative weight of goods in the basket, or in the way in which goods and services from the present are compared with goods and services from the past. Basket weights are updated regularly, usually every year, to adapt to changes in consumer behavior. Sudden changes in consumer behavior can still introduce a weighting bias in inflation measurement. For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic it has been shown that the basket of goods and services was no longer representative of consumption during the crisis, as numerous goods and services could no longer be consumed due to government containment measures ("lock-downs").\nOver time, adjustments are also made to the type of goods and services selected to reflect changes in the sorts of goods and services purchased by 'typical consumers'. New products may be introduced, older products disappear, the quality of existing products may change, and consumer preferences can shift. Different segments of the population may naturally consume different "baskets" of goods and services and may even experience different inflation rates. It is argued that companies have put more innovation into bringing down prices for wealthy families than for poor families.\nInflation numbers are often\nseasonally adjusted\nto differentiate expected cyclical cost shifts. For example, home heating costs are expected to rise in colder months, and seasonal adjustments are often used when measuring inflation to compensate for cyclical energy or fuel demand spikes. Inflation numbers may be averaged or otherwise subjected to statistical techniques to remove\nstatistical noise\nand\nvolatility\nof individual prices.\nWhen looking at inflation, economic institutions may focus only on certain kinds of prices, or\nspecial indices\n, such as the\ncore inflation\nindex which is used by central banks to formulate\nmonetary policy\n.\nMost inflation indices are calculated from weighted averages of selected price changes. This necessarily introduces distortion, and can lead to legitimate disputes about what the true inflation rate is. This problem can be overcome by including all available price changes in the calculation, and then choosing the\nmedian\nvalue.\nIn some other cases, governments may intentionally report false inflation rates; for instance, during the presidency of\nCristina Kirchner\n(2007–2015) the\ngovernment of Argentina\nwas criticised for manipulating economic data, such as inflation and GDP figures, for political gain and to reduce payments on its inflation-indexed debt.\nOfficial vs. true vs. perceived inflation\n[\nedit\n]\nThe true inflation is one percentage point lower than the official one, according to research. Moreover, the response of inflationary expectations to monetary policy can influence the division of the effects of policy between inflation and unemployment (see\nmonetary policy credibility\n).\nCauses\n[\nedit\n]\nHistorical approaches\n[\nedit\n]\nTheories of the origin and causes of inflation have existed since at least the 16th century. Two competing theories, the\nquantity theory of money\nand the\nreal bills doctrine\n, appeared in various guises during century-long debates on recommended central bank behaviour. In the 20th century,\nKeynesian\n,\nmonetarist\nand\nnew classical\n(also known as\nrational expectations\n) views on inflation dominated post-World War II\nmacroeconomics\ndiscussions, which were often heated intellectual debates, until some kind of synthesis of the various theories was reached by the end of the century.\nBefore 1936\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nReal bills doctrine\nThe\nprice revolution\nfrom ca. 1550–1700 caused several thinkers to present what is now considered to be early formulations of the\nquantity theory of money\n(QTM). Other contemporary authors attributed rising price levels to the debasement of national coinages. Later research has shown that also growing output of\nCentral European\nsilver mines and an increase in the\nvelocity of money\nbecause of innovations in the payment technology, in particular the increased use of\nbills of exchange\n, contributed to the price revolution.\nAn alternative theory, the\nreal bills doctrine\n(RBD), originated in the 17th and 18th century, receiving its first authoritative exposition in\nAdam Smith\n's\nThe Wealth of Nations\n.\nIt asserts that banks should issue their money in exchange for short-term real bills of adequate value. As long as banks only issue a dollar in exchange for assets worth at least a dollar, the issuing bank's assets will naturally move in step with its issuance of money, and the money will hold its value. Should the bank fail to get or maintain assets of adequate value, then the bank's money will lose value, just as any financial security will lose value if its asset backing diminishes. The real bills doctrine (also known as the backing theory) thus asserts that inflation results when money outruns its issuer's assets. The quantity theory of money, in contrast, claims that inflation results when money outruns the economy's production of goods.\nDuring the 19th century, three different schools debated these questions: The\nBritish Currency School\nupheld a quantity theory view, believing that the\nBank of England\n's issues of bank notes should vary one-for-one with the bank's gold reserves. In contrast to this, the\nBritish Banking School\nfollowed the real bills doctrine, recommending that the bank's operations should be governed by the needs of trade: Banks should be able to issue currency against bills of trading, i.e. "real bills" that they buy from merchants. A third group, the Free Banking School, held that competitive private banks would not overissue, even though a monopolist central bank could be believed to do it.\nThe debate between currency, or quantity theory, and banking schools during the 19th century prefigures current questions about the credibility of money in the present. In the 19th century, the banking schools had greater influence in policy in the United States and Great Britain, while the\ncurrency schools\nhad more influence "on the continent", that is in non-British countries, particularly in the\nLatin Monetary Union\nand the\nScandinavian Monetary Union\n.\nDuring the Bullionist Controversy during the\nNapoleonic Wars\n,\nDavid Ricardo\nargued that the Bank of England had engaged in over-issue of bank notes, leading to commodity price increases. In the late 19th century, supporters of the quantity theory of money led by\nIrving Fisher\ndebated with supporters of\nbimetallism\n. Later,\nKnut Wicksell\nsought to explain price movements as the result of real shocks rather than movements in money supply, resounding statements from the real bills doctrine.\nIn 2019, monetary historians\nThomas M. Humphrey\nand\nRichard Timberlake\npublished "Gold, the Real Bills Doctrine, and the Fed: Sources of Monetary Disorder 1922–1938".\nKeynes and the early Keynesians\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nKeynesian Revolution\nFurther information:\nKeynes's theory of wages and prices\nJohn Maynard Keynes in his 1936 main work\nThe General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money\nemphasized that wages and prices were\nsticky\nin the short run, but gradually responded to\naggregate demand\nshocks. These could arise from many different sources, e.g. autonomous movements in investment or fluctuations in private wealth or interest rates.\nEconomic policy could also affect demand,\nmonetary policy\nby affecting interest rates and\nfiscal policy\neither directly through the level of\ngovernment final consumption expenditure\nor indirectly by changing\ndisposable income\nvia tax changes.\nThe various sources of variations in aggregate demand will cause cycles in both output and price levels. Initially, a demand change will primarily affect output because of the price stickiness, but eventually prices and wages will adjust to reflect the change in demand. Consequently, movements in real output and prices will be positively, but not strongly, correlated.\nKeynes' propositions formed the basis of\nKeynesian economics\nwhich came to dominate macroeconomic research and economic policy in the first decades after World War II.\nOther Keynesian economists developed and reformed several of Keynes' ideas. Importantly,\nAlban William Phillips\nin 1958 published indirect evidence of a negative relation between inflation and unemployment, confirming the Keynesian emphasis on a positive correlation between increases in real output (normally accompanied by a fall in unemployment) and rising prices, i.e. inflation. Phillips' findings were confirmed by other empirical analyses and became known as a\nPhillips curve\n. It quickly became central to macroeconomic thinking, apparently offering a stable trade-off between\nprice stability\nand employment. The curve was interpreted to imply that a country could achieve low unemployment if it were willing to tolerate a higher inflation rate or vice versa.\nThe Phillips curve model described the U.S. experience well in the 1960s but failed to describe the\nstagflation experienced in the 1970s\n.\nMonetarism\n[\nedit\n]\nInflation\nDeflation\nM2 money supply\nincreases Year/Year\nInflation and the growth of money supply (M2)\nFurther information:\nMonetarism\nDuring the 1960s the Keynesian view of inflation and macroeconomic policy altogether were challenged by\nmonetarist\ntheories, led by\nMilton Friedman\n.\nFriedman famously stated that:\nInflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon.\nHe revived the\nquantity theory of money\nby\nIrving Fisher\nand others, making it into a central tenet of monetarist thinking, arguing that the most significant factor influencing inflation or deflation is how fast the\nmoney supply\ngrows or shrinks.\nThe quantity theory of money, simply stated, says that any change in the amount of money in a system will change the price level. This theory begins with the\nequation of exchange\n:\nM\nV\n=\nP\nQ\n,\n{\displaystyle MV=PQ,}\nwhere\nM\n{\displaystyle M}\nis the nominal quantity of money;\nV\n{\displaystyle V}\nis the\nvelocity of money\nin final expenditures;\nP\n{\displaystyle P}\nis the general price level;\nQ\n{\displaystyle Q}\nis an index of the\nreal value\nof final expenditures.\nIn this formula, the general price level is related to the level of real economic activity (\nQ\n), the quantity of money (\nM\n) and the velocity of money (\nV\n). The formula itself is simply an uncontroversial\naccounting identity\nbecause the velocity of money (\nV\n) is defined residually from the equation to be the ratio of final nominal expenditure (\nP\nQ\n{\displaystyle PQ}\n) to the quantity of money (\nM\n).\nMonetarists assumed additionally that the velocity of money is unaffected by monetary policy (at least in the long run), that the real value of output is also\nexogenous\nin the long run, its long-run value being determined independently by the productive capacity of the economy, and that money supply is exogenous and can be controlled by the monetary authorities. Under these assumptions, the primary driver of the change in the general price level is changes in the quantity of money.\nConsequently, monetarists contended that monetary policy, not fiscal policy, was the most potent instrument to influence aggregate demand, real output and eventually inflation. This was contrary to Keynesian thinking which in principle recognized a role for monetary policy, but in practice believed that the effect from interest rate changes to the real economy was slight, making monetary policy an ineffective instrument, preferring fiscal policy.\nConversely, monetarists considered fiscal policy, or government spending and taxation, as ineffective in controlling inflation.\nFriedman also took issue with the traditional Keynesian view concerning the Phillips curve. He, together with\nEdmund Phelps\n, contended that the trade-off between inflation and unemployment implied by the Phillips curve was only temporary, but not permanent. If politicians tried to exploit it, it would eventually disappear because higher inflation would over time be built into the economic expectations of households and firms.\nThis line of thinking led to the concept of\npotential output\n(sometimes called the "natural gross domestic product"), a level of GDP where the economy is stable in the sense that inflation will neither decrease nor increase. This level may itself change over time when institutional or natural constraints change. It corresponds to the Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment,\nNAIRU\n, or the "natural" rate of unemployment (sometimes called the "structural" level of unemployment).\nIf GDP exceeds its potential (and unemployment consequently is below the NAIRU), the theory says that inflation will\naccelerate\nas suppliers increase their prices. If GDP falls below its potential level (and unemployment is above the NAIRU), inflation will\ndecelerate\nas suppliers attempt to fill excess capacity, cutting prices and undermining inflation.\nRational expectations theory\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nRational expectations\nIn the early 1970s,\nrational expectations theory\nled by economists like\nRobert Lucas\n,\nThomas Sargent\nand\nRobert Barro\ntransformed macroeconomic thinking radically. They held that economic actors look rationally into the future when trying to maximize their well-being, and do not respond solely to immediate\nopportunity costs\nand pressures.\nIn this view, future expectations and strategies are important for inflation as well. One implication was that agents would anticipate the likely behaviour of central banks and base their own actions on these expectations. A central bank having a reputation of being "soft" on inflation will generate high inflation expectations, which again will be self-fulfilling when all agents build expectations of future high inflation into their nominal contracts like wage agreements. On the other hand, if the central bank has a reputation of being "tough" on inflation, then such a policy announcement will be believed and inflationary expectations will come down rapidly, thus allowing inflation itself to come down rapidly with minimal economic disruption. The implication is that\ncredibility\nbecomes very important for central banks in fighting inflation.\nNew Keynesians\n[\nedit\n]\nEvents during the 1970s proved Milton Friedman and other critics of the traditional Phillips curve right: The relation between the inflation rate and the unemployment rate broke down. That is, when the general level of prices rise, each monetary unit can buy fewer goods and services in aggregate. The effect of inflation differs on different sectors of the economy, with some sectors being adversely affected while others benefitting. For example, with inflation, those segments in society which own physical assets, such as property, stock etc., benefit from the price/value of their holdings going up, when those who seek to acquire them will need to pay more for them. Their ability to do so will depend on the degree to which their income is fixed. For example, increases in payments to workers and pensioners often lag behind inflation, and for some people income is fixed. Also, individuals or institutions with cash assets will experience a decline in the purchasing power of the cash. Increases in the price level (inflation) erode the real value of money (the functional currency) and other items with an underlying monetary nature.\nDebtors who have debts with a fixed nominal rate of interest will see a reduction in the "real" interest rate as the inflation rate rises. The real interest on a loan is the nominal rate minus the inflation rate. The formula\nR = N-I\napproximates the correct answer as long as both the nominal interest rate and the inflation rate are small. The correct equation is\nr = n/i\nwhere\nr\n,\nn\nand\ni\nare expressed as\nratios\n(e.g. 1.2 for +20%, 0.8 for −20%). As an example, when the inflation rate is 3%, a loan with a nominal interest rate of 5% would have a real interest rate of approximately 2% (in fact, it's 1.94%). Any unexpected increase in the inflation rate would decrease the real interest rate. Banks and other lenders adjust for this inflation risk either by including an inflation risk premium to fixed interest rate loans or lending at an adjustable rate.\nNegative\n[\nedit\n]\nInflation is illustrated by the contrast between what\nR$\n100 could buy in 2010 and in 2022, observed by\nLula\nduring a meeting with women in Brasilândia.\nHigh or unpredictable inflation rates are regarded as harmful to an overall economy.
