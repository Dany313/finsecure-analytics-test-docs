For other uses, see\nBank of the United States (disambiguation)\n.\nThe\nBank of America Corporation\n(\nBank of America\n; often abbreviated\nBAC\nor\nBofA\n) is an American multinational\nbank\nand\nfinancial services\nholding company\nheadquartered at the\nBank of America Corporate Center\nin\nCharlotte, North Carolina\n, with\ninvestment banking\nand auxiliary headquarters in\nManhattan\n. The bank was formed by the merger of\nNationsBank\nand\nBank of America\nin 1998. It is the\nsecond-largest banking institution\nin the United States and the second-largest bank in the world by\nmarket capitalization\n, both after\nJPMorgan Chase\n.\nBank of America is one of the\nBig Four\nbanking institutions of the United States,\nand one of eight\nsystemically important financial institutions\nin the United States. It serves about 10 percent of all American bank deposits, in competition with JPMorgan Chase,\nCitigroup\n, and\nWells Fargo\n. Its primary financial services revolve around\ncommercial banking\n,\nwealth management\n, and investment banking.\nThrough mergers, the oldest branch of the Bank of America franchise dates back to 1784, when\nMassachusetts Bank\nwas chartered, becoming the first federally chartered joint-stock-owned bank in the United States. Another branch of its history goes back to the American-based\nBank of Italy\n, founded by\nAmadeo Pietro Giannini\nin 1904, which provided various banking services to\nItalian immigrants\nwho\nfaced service discrimination\nat the time.\nHeadquartered in\nSan Francisco\n, California, Giannini acquired\nBanca d'America e d'Italia\nin 1922 and eventually did business as Bank of America.\nIn the 1950s, the passage of landmark federal banking legislation facilitated rapid growth, quickly establishing prominent shares for the present bank's predecessors. After suffering significant losses during the\n1998 Russian financial crisis\n, BankAmerica, as it was then known, was acquired by the Charlotte-based\nNationsBank\nfor $62 billion. Following what was then the\nlargest bank acquisition in history\n, the Bank of America Corporation was founded. Through a series of mergers and acquisitions, it built upon its commercial banking business by establishing\nMerrill Lynch\nfor wealth management and\nBank of America Merrill Lynch\nfor investment banking in 2008 and 2009, respectively, and since renamed BofA Securities.\nBoth Bank of America and Merrill Lynch Wealth Management retain large market shares in their respective offerings. As of 2018, the\ninvestment bank\nwas considered within the "\nbulge bracket\n" as the world's\nthird-largest investment bank\n.\nIts wealth management unit manages $1.08 trillion in\nassets under management\n(AUM) as the\nsecond-largest wealth manager in the world\n, after\nUBS\n.\nIn commercial banking, Bank of America has operations, but does not necessarily maintain retail branches in all 50 states of the United States,\nWashington, D.C.\n, and over 40 other countries.\nIts commercial banking footprint encapsulates 46 million consumer and small-business relationships at 4,600 banking centers and 16,000\nautomated teller machines\n(ATMs).\nThe bank's large market share, business activities, and economic impact have led to numerous lawsuits and investigations regarding both mortgages and financial disclosures dating back to the\n2008 financial crisis\n.\nIts corporate practices of servicing the\nmiddle class\nand wider banking community have yielded a substantial market share since the early 20th century.\nAs of August\n2018\n, Bank of America has a $313.5 billion\nmarket capitalization\n, making it the\n13th largest company in the world\n. As the sixth-largest American public company, it garnered $102.98\nbillion in sales\nas of June\n2018\n.\nBank of America was ranked number 25 on the 2020\nFortune\n500\nrankings of the largest US corporations by total revenue.\nLikewise, Bank of America was also ranked No. 6 on\nForbes\n'\n2023 Global 2000 rankings.\nBank of America was named the "World's Best Bank" by\nEuromoney Institutional Investor\nin its 2018 Awards for Excellence.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nBank of America, Los Angeles\n, was founded in California in 1923. P. Monnette\n. The following year,\nC. Giannini and Monnette headed the resulting company, serving as co-chairs.\nExpansion in California\n[\nedit\n]\nEntrance to Bank of America,\nMarket Street\nand\nPowell Street\nand Eddy Street,\nSan Francisco\nin 1943\nGiannini introduced branch banking shortly after California's 1909 legislation allowed for branch banking in the state, establishing the bank's first branch outside San Francisco in 1909 in San Jose. By 1929, the bank had 453 banking offices in California with aggregate resources of over US$1.4\nbillion.\nThere is a replica of the 1909 Bank of Italy branch bank in\nHistory Park\nin San Jose, and the 1925\nBank of Italy Building\nis an important\ndowntown\nlandmark. Giannini sought to build a national bank, expanding into most of the western states as well as into the\ninsurance industry\n, under the aegis of his holding company,\nTransamerica Corporation\n.\nIn 1953, regulators succeeded in forcing the separation of\nTransamerica Corporation\nand Bank of America under the\nClayton Antitrust Act\n.\nThe passage of the\nBank Holding Company Act of 1956\nprohibited banks from owning\nnon-banking subsidiaries\nsuch as insurance companies. Bank of America and Transamerica were separated, with the latter company continuing in the insurance sector. However, federal banking regulators prohibited Bank of America's interstate banking activity, and Bank of America's domestic banks outside California were forced into a separate company that eventually became\nFirst Interstate Bancorp\n, later acquired by\nWells Fargo and Company\nin 1996. Only in the 1980s, with a change in federal banking legislation and regulation, could Bank of America again expand its domestic consumer banking activity outside California.\nNew technologies also enabled the direct linking of\ncredit cards\nwith individual bank accounts. In 1958, the bank introduced the BankAmericard, which was renamed to\nVisa\nin 1977.\nA coalition of regional bankcard associations introduced Interbank in 1966 to compete with BankAmericard. Interbank became Master Charge in 1966 and then\nMastercard\nin 1979.\nFrom February 1970 through September 1971, there were 66 attacks on Bank of America branches in California, including 53 bombings or fire-bombings, and 13 arson fires. BankAmerica continued to operate its new subsidiary as Seafirst rather than Bank of America until the 1998 merger with NationsBank.\nBankAmerica experienced huge losses in 1986 and 1987 due to the placement of a series of bad loans in the\nThird World\n. (Tom) Clausen\n, Clausen was appointed to replace Armacost.\nThe losses resulted in a huge decline of BankAmerica stock, making it vulnerable to a hostile\ntakeover\n.\nFirst Interstate Bancorp\nof Los Angeles (which had originated from banks once owned by BankAmerica), launched such a bid in the fall of 1986, although BankAmerica rebuffed it, mostly by selling operations.\nIt sold its FinanceAmerica subsidiary to\nChrysler\nand the brokerage firm\nCharles Schwab and Co.\nback to\nMr. Schwab\n. It also sold\nBank of America and Italy\nto\nDeutsche Bank\n. By the time of the\n1987 stock-market crash\n, BankAmerica's share price had fallen to $8, but by 1992 it had rebounded mightily to become one of the biggest gainers of that half-decade.\nBankAmerica's next big acquisition came in 1992. The company acquired Security Pacific Corporation and its subsidiary\nSecurity Pacific National Bank\nin California and other banks in\nArizona\n,\nIdaho\n,\nOregon\n, and\nWashington\n, which Security Pacific had acquired in a series of acquisitions in the late 1980s. BankAmerica moved its national lending department to Chicago in an effort to establish a financial beachhead in the region.\nThese mergers helped BankAmerica Corporation to once again become the largest U.S. bank holding company in terms of deposits, but the company fell to second place in 1997 behind North Carolina's fast-growing\nNationsBank Corporation\n, and to third in 1998 behind\nFirst Union\nCorp.\nOn the capital markets side, the acquisition of Continental Illinois helped BankAmerica to build a leveraged finance origination- and distribution business, which allowed the firm's existing broker-dealer, BancAmerica Securities (originally named BA Securities), to become a full-service franchise.\nIn addition, in 1997, BankAmerica acquired\nRobertson Stephens\n, a San Francisco–based investment bank specializing in high technology for $540\nmillion.\nRobertson Stephens was integrated into BancAmerica Securities, and the combined subsidiary was renamed "BancAmerica Robertson Stephens".\nMerger of NationsBank and BankAmerica\n[\nedit\n]\nBank of America's logo used from 1998 to 2018\nIn 1997, BankAmerica lent investment management firm\nD.
