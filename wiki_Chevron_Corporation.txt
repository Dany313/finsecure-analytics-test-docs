Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chevron_Corporation\n==================================================\n\nAmerican multinational energy corporation\nChevron Corporation\nis an American multinational\nenergy corporation\npredominantly specializing in\noil and gas\n. The second-largest\ndirect descendant of Standard Oil\n, and originally known as the\nStandard Oil Company of California\n(shortened to\nSocal\nor\nCalSo\n), it is active in more than 180 countries.\nFounded originally in\nSouthern California\nduring the 1870s,  after the\nbreakup of Standard Oil\n, Socal grew quickly on its own by continuing to acquire companies and partnering with others both inside and outside of California, eventually becoming one of the\nBig Oil\ncompanies that dominated the global petroleum industry from the mid-1940s to the 1970s. In 1985, Socal merged with the Pittsburgh-based\nGulf Oil\nand rebranded as Chevron; the newly merged company later merged with\nTexaco\nin 2001.\nChevron is one of the largest companies in the world and the second-largest oil company based in the United States by revenue, only behind fellow Standard Oil descendant\nExxonMobil\n. Within oil and gas, Chevron is\nvertically integrated\nand is involved in\nhydrocarbon exploration\n,\nproduction\n,\nrefining\n, marketing and transport, chemicals manufacturing and sales, and\npower generation\n. Chevron manufactures and sells fuels, lubricants, additives, and petrochemicals, primarily in\nWestern North America\n,  the US\nGulf Coast\n, Southeast Asia, South Korea and Australia. In 2018, the company produced an average of\n791,000 barrels (125,800\nm\n)\nof net oil-equivalent per day in United States.\nChevron ranked 10th on the\nFortune\n500\nin 2023. The company is also the last-remaining oil-and-gas component of the\nDow Jones Industrial Average\nsince the exit of\nExxonMobil\nfrom the index in 2020.\nChevron has been subject to\nnumerous controversies\nrelating to its activities involving\nclimate change\n. In particular, one of its most widely covered incidents on social media was its continued denial of ecological damage near the\nLago Agrio oil field\nand its involvements against\nSteven Donziger\n, a controversy rooted and inherited from\nTexaco\nwhen Chevron acquired it in 2001. Chevron has also received negative attention for numerous other oil extraction projects, especially within the Los Angeles metropolitan area and in the Eastern Mediterranean.\nIn 2024, Chevron was responsible for 512 Mt of CO\n2\nemissions, which was 1.33% of global CO\n2\nemissions.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nPredecessors\n[\nedit\n]\nStar Oil and Pacific Coast Oil Company\n[\nedit\n]\nOne of Chevron's early predecessors, "Star Oil", discovered oil at the\nPico Canyon Oilfield\nin the\nSanta Susana Mountains\nnorth of Los Angeles in 1876. The 25 barrels of oil per day well marked the discovery of the Newhall Field, and is considered by\ngeophysicist\nMarius Vassiliou\nas the beginning of the modern oil industry in California.\nEnergy analyst\nAntonia Juhasz\nhas said that while Star Oil's founders were influential in establishing an oil industry in California, Union Mattole Company discovered oil in the state eleven years prior.\nIn September 1879,\nCharles N. Felton\n,\nLloyd Tevis\n, George Loomis and others created the "Pacific Coast Oil Company", which acquired the assets of Star Oil\nwith $1 million in funding.\nPacific Coast Oil eventually became the largest oil interest in California, and in 1900,\nJohn D. Rockefeller\n's\nStandard Oil\nacquired Pacific Coast Oil  for $761,000.\nIn 1906, the Pacific Coast acquired the business operations and assets of the\nStandard Oil Company (Iowa)\n. At this time, Pacific renamed itself the Standard Oil Company (California).\nTexaco\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nTexaco §\nHistory\nSince the acquisition of the Pacific Coast Oil Company by Standard Oil, the Standard descendant had traditionally worked closely with\nTexaco\nfor 100 years, before acquiring Texaco outright in 2001. Originally known as the Texas Fuel Company (later the Texas Company), Texaco was founded in\nBeaumont, Texas\n,  as an oil-equipment vendor by "Buckskin Joe". The founder's nickname came from being harsh and aggressive.\nTexas Fuel worked closely with Chevron. In 1936, it formed a joint venture with California Standard named\nCaltex\n, to drill and produce oil in\nSaudi Arabia\n.\nAccording to energy analyst and activist shareholder\nAntonia Juhasz\n,\nthe Texas Fuel Company and California Standard were often referred to as the "terrible twins" for their cutthroat business practices.\nFormation of the Chevron name\n[\nedit\n]\nA Chevron station branded under the Standard name in\nParadise, Nevada\n, pictured in 2009\nIn 1911, the federal government broke Standard Oil into several pieces under the\nSherman Antitrust Act\n. One of those pieces, Standard Oil Co. (California), went on to become Chevron. It became part of the "\nSeven Sisters\n", which dominated the world oil industry in the early 20th century. In 1926, the company changed its name to Standard Oil Co. of California (SOCAL).\nBy the terms of the breakup of Standard Oil, at first Standard of California could use the Standard name only within its original geographic area of the Pacific coast states, plus Nevada and Arizona; outside that area, it had to use another name.\nToday, Chevron is the owner of the\nStandard Oil\ntrademark in 16 states in the western and southeastern United States. Since\nAmerican trademark law\noperates under a use-it-or-lose-it rule,\nthe company owns and operates one Standard-branded Chevron station in each state of the area.\nHowever, though Chevron (as CalSo) acquired\nKyso\nin the 1960s, its status in Kentucky is unclear after Chevron withdrew its brand from retail sales from Kentucky in July 2010.\nThe 'Chevron' name came into use for some of its retail products in the 1930s. The name "Calso" was also used from 1946 to 1955, in states outside its native West Coast territory.\nStandard Oil Company of California ranked 75th among United States corporations in the value of\nWorld War II\nmilitary production contracts.\nIn 1933,\nSaudi Arabia\ngranted California Standard a concession to find oil, which led to the discovery of oil in 1938. In 1948, California Standard discovered the world's largest oil field in Saudi Arabia,\nGhawar Field\n.\nCalifornia Standard's subsidiary, California-Arabian Standard Oil Company, grew over the years and became the Arabian American Oil Company (ARAMCO) in 1944. In 1973, the Saudi government began buying into ARAMCO. By 1980, the company was entirely owned by the Saudis, and in 1988, its name was changed to Saudi Arabian Oil Company—\nSaudi Aramco\n.\nStandard Oil of California and\nGulf Oil\nmerged in 1984,\nwhich was the largest merger in history at that time.\nTo comply with\nU.S. antitrust law\n, California Standard divested many of Gulf's operating subsidiaries, and sold some Gulf stations in the eastern United States and a Philadelphia refinery which has since closed.  Among the assets sold off were Gulf's retail outlets in Gulf's home market of\nPittsburgh\n, where Chevron lacks a retail presence but does retain a regional headquarters there as of 2013, partially for\nMarcellus Shale\n-related drilling.\nThe same year, Standard Oil of California also took the opportunity to change its legal name to\nChevron Corporation\n, since it had already been using the well-known "Chevron" retail brand name for decades. Chevron would sell the Gulf Oil trademarks for the entire U.S. to\nCumberland Farms\n, the parent company of\nGulf Oil LP\n, in 2010 after Cumberland Farms had a license to the Gulf trademark in the Northeastern United States since 1986.\nIn 1996, Chevron transferred its natural gas gathering, operating and marketing operation to\nNGC Corporation\n(later Dynegy) in exchange for a roughly 25% equity stake in NGC.\nIn a merger completed February 1, 2000, Illinova Corp. became a wholly owned subsidiary of Dynegy Inc. and Chevron's stake increased up to 28%.\nHowever, in May 2007, Chevron sold its stake in the company for approximately $985\nmillion, resulting in a gain of $680\nmillion.\nAcquisitions and diversification\n[\nedit\n]\nChevron gas station design used until 2006\n2000s\n[\nedit\n]\nThe early 2000s saw Chevron engage in many mergers, acquisitions, and sales, the first largest of which was the $45 billion acquisition of\nTexaco\n, announced on October 15, 2000. The acquisition created the second-largest oil company in the United States and the world's fourth-largest publicly traded oil company with a combined market value of approximately $95\nbillion.\nCompleted on October 9, 2001, Chevron temporarily renamed itself to\nChevronTexaco\nbetween 2001 and 2005; after the company reverted its name to Chevron, Texaco became used as a brand by the company for some of its fueling stations.\n2005 also saw Chevron purchase\nUnocal Corporation\nfor $18.4\nbillion, increasing the company's petroleum and natural gas reserves by about 15%.\nBecause of Unocal's large South East Asian geothermal operations, Chevron became a large producer of\ngeothermal energy\n.\nThe deal did not include Unocal's former retail operations including the\nUnion 76\ntrademark, as it had sold that off to\nTosco Corporation\nin 1997.\nThe 76 brand is owned by\nPhillips 66\n, unaffiliated with Chevron.\nChevron and the\nLos Alamos National Laboratory\nstarted a cooperation in 2006, to improve the recovery of hydrocarbons from\noil shale\nby developing a\nshale oil extraction\nprocess named\nChevron CRUSH\n.\nIn 2006, the\nUnited States Department of the Interior\nissued a research, development and demonstration lease for Chevron's demonstration oil shale project on public lands in Colorado's\nPiceance Basin\n.\nIn February 2012, Chevron notified the\nBureau of Land Management\nand the Department of Reclamation, Mining and Safety that it intended to divest this lease.\nIn 2008, Chevron Limited, a subsidiary of Chevron, sold its equity distributor business in the UK to GB Oils Limited for £21.9 million.\n2010s\n[\nedit\n]\nStarting in 2010, Chevron began to reduce its retail footprint and expand in domestic natural gas. In July 2010, Chevron ended retail operations in the Mid-Atlantic United States by removing the Chevron and Texaco names from 1,100\nstations.\nIn 2011, Chevron acquired Pennsylvania-based Atlas Energy Inc. for $3.2\nbillion in cash and an additional $1.1\nbillion in existing debt owed by Atlas.\nThree months later, Chevron acquired drilling and development rights for another 228,000\nacres in the Marcellus Shale from Chief Oil & Gas LLC and Tug Hill, Inc.\nIn September 2013,\nTotal S.A.\nand its joint-venture partner agreed to buy Chevron's retail distribution business in Pakistan for an undisclosed amount.\nIn October 2014, Chevron announced that it would sell a 30 percent holding in its Canadian oil shale holdings to\nKuwait\n's state-owned oil company\nKuwait Oil Company\nfor a fee of $1.5\nbillion.\nDespite these sales, Chevron continued to explore acquisitions, a trend which had reinvigorated in 2019 and extended throughout the\nCOVID-19 pandemic\n. In April 2019, Chevron announced its intention to acquire Anadarko Petroleum in a deal valued at $33\nbillion, but decided to focus on other acquisitions shortly afterwards when a deal could not be reached.\nDespite the failed acquisition of Anadarko, Chevron did acquire\nNoble Energy\nfor $5\nbillion in July 2020.\nChevron sold its\nNorth Sea\noperations in May 2019 to\nIthaca Energy\nfor $2 billion.\nChevron was not spared from the pandemic, however, as Chevron announced reductions of 10–15% of its workforce due to both the pandemic and a\n2020 oil price war between Russia and Saudi Arabia\n.\nDuring the pandemic, Chevron considered a merger with rival ExxonMobil in 2020 during the early stages of the COVID-19 pandemic that drove oil demand sharply down. It would have been one of the largest corporate mergers in history, and a combined Chevron and ExxonMobil (dubbed "Chexxon" by\nReuters\n) would have been the second-largest oil company in the world, trailing only\nSaudi Aramco\n.\nLater in the pandemic, Chevron began requiring some employees, namely expatriate employees, those working overseas, and workers on U.S.-flagged ships, to receive COVID-19 vaccinations after having some key operations, the off-shore platforms off the\nGulf of Mexico\nand\nPermian Basin\nfor example. The requirement will begin for workers off the Gulf of Mexico on the first of November.\n2020s\n[\nedit\n]\nIn the 2020s, Chevron's primary focus was on alternative energy solutions, gradual pullouts from Africa and Southeast Asia, and an increased focus on the Americas with a lessened albeit still present interest in natural gas. Chevron in February 2020 joined Marubeni Corporation and WAVE Equity Partners in investing in Carbon Clean Solutions, a company that provides portable carbon capture technology for the oil field and other industrial facilities.\nTwo years later, Chevron announced that it will acquire\nRenewable Energy Group\n, a biodiesel production company based in\nAmes, Iowa\n. The acquisition was completed just under four months later on June 13.\nIn the Americas, Chevron acquired natural gas company Beyond6, LLC (B6) and its network of 55\ncompressed natural gas\nstations across the United States from\nMercuria\nin November 2022. The relaxed restrictions, however, came with severe limitations, including provisions which prohibited Chevron from selling to Russian or Iranian-affiliated agencies and from allowing any direct profits to go to Venezuelan oil company\nPDVSA\n.\nOn November 29, 2022, Venezuelan Government Petroleum Minister\nTarek El Aissami\nmet in\nCaracas\n, Venezuela, with the president of Chevron, Javier La Rosa. In the case of Petroboscán, current production is nil and, in Petropiar, current records indicate close to 50,000 barrels per day.\nOn March 20, 2023, Tareck El Aissami resigned from his government post amid serious corruption allegations. Moreover, El Aissami, a longtime Maduro ally, has a $10mn US government reward on his head for allegedly facilitating drug trafficking from Venezuela. He played a key role in helping Nicolas Maduro's government dodge US economic sanctions, using his\nSyrian\nand\nLebanese\nparentage to open up new business channels to\nIran\nand\nTurkey\n.\nOn January 5, 2022, Chevron temporarily decreased production in\nKazakhstan\n's\nTengiz Field\ndue to the\n2022 Kazakh protests\n, which were motivated by heavy oil price increases.\nLater that month, Chevron also announced it would end all operations in\nMyanmar\n, citing rampant human rights abuses and deteriorating rule of law since the\n2021 Myanmar coup d'état\n.\nA statement released by the company on its website stated while Chevron was committed to an orderly exit which ensured it could still provide energy to Southeast Asia, Chevron remained firmly opposed to the human rights violations committed by the current\nmilitary rule in Myanmar\n.\nAlso in 2022, Chevron was reported to explore the sale of stakes in three fields located in\nEquatorial Guinea\n. It was suggested by\nReuters\nthat the sales are intended to attract smaller oil companies.\nChevron, however, did not do business in the 2020s without controversy and regulatory obstacles.\nChevron Phillips Chemical\n, a company jointly owned by Chevron and\nPhillips 66\n, agreed to pay $118 million in March 2022 as a result of violating the\nClean Air Act\nat three of its chemical production plants in Texas. According to the\nUnited States Department of Justice\nand\nUnited States Environmental Protection Agency\n, Chevron and Phillips failed to properly\nflare\nat the plants, causing excess air pollution. The companies agreed to add pollution control systems to the plants as well.\nDespite the major oil and gas companies, including Chevron, reporting sharp rises in interim revenues and profits due to the\n2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine\n, the world's-largest oil companies received immense backlash for such profits. In addition, days before the company reported its full year earnings, Chevron increased its dividend and announced a $75 billion\nstock buyback program\n, a move which attracted a heated response from the Biden administration as well as from news commentators within the United States.\nThe 2020s also saw efforts by Chevron to expand into the clean energy industry. Chevron has been fined by the governments of Angola, for oil spills within its waters, and the United States through its\nEPA\nfor violations of the US Clean Air Act and pollutive activities arising out of its\nRichmond Refinery\nin California.\nOn multiple instances, authorities in oil-heavy countries have fired rounds onto protestors against Chevron.\nEnvironmental activists have held annual anti-Chevron protests, usually within a week of Chevron's annual meeting of shareholders.\nOne case heavily promoted on\nsocial media\nis about work done in the\nLago Agrio oil field\nby\nTexaco\n, which Chevron acquired in 2001. Texaco dumped 18 billion tons of toxic waste and spilled\n17\nmillion US gallons (64,000,000\nL)\nof petroleum.\nTexaco's activities were the subject of an Ecuadorian court case brought by\nSteven Donziger\n, which claimed an $18 billion and later reduced $9.5 billion judgment.\nChevron has played a significant role in the exploitation of natural gas resources in the Eastern Mediterranean, including the\nLeviathan\nand\nTamar\nfields off\nIsrael\n's coast. The company has financially benefited from Israel's control over maritime resources to which Palestinians have been denied access due to the\nblockade of the Gaza Strip\nand lack of maritime sovereignty.\nA major point of contention involves Texaco's operations in the\nEcuadorian Amazon\nfrom the 1960s until 1992. Critics\nallege that during this period, the company deliberately discharged approximately 72 billion liters (18 billion gallons) of toxic waste directly into the rainforest's rivers and lands as a cost-saving measure instead of utilizing proper disposal methods. This contamination is reported to have devastated local ecosystems, poisoned water sources used by indigenous communities for drinking and sustenance, and led to widespread health issues, including illnesses and physical ailments. Some experts and affected parties have referred to this environmental damage as the "Amazon Chernobyl". It is claimed that these were not accidental spills but rather the result of systematically engineered dumping practices. Local communities initially reported changes in water taste but were allegedly dismissed by the company, which purportedly suggested the water contained beneficial "vitamins".\nThe environmental damage in Ecuador led to one of the largest environmental lawsuits in history (\nAguinda v. Texaco, Inc.\n). In 2011, an Ecuadorian court found Chevron liable and ordered the company to pay $9.5 billion in damages to the affected communities.\nChevron refused to pay the judgment, removed its assets from Ecuador, and vowed to fight the ruling indefinitely, with a company lawyer stating it would contest it "until hell freezes over, and then fight it out on the ice". Chevron allegedly lobbied the US government to impose sanctions on Ecuador following the ruling. In 2018, an international tribunal in\nThe Hague\nruled in favor of Chevron, stating that the 2011 Ecuador Supreme Court ruling had been obtained through fraud, bribery and corruption.\nThe majority of Chevron's crude oil from the Amazon is processed in California at the El Segundo refinery.\nSteven Donziger\n, an American lawyer who represented the Ecuadorian\nplaintiffs\nfor decades, became a central figure in the subsequent legal battles. Chevron pursued legal action against Donziger in the United States, accusing him of obtaining the Ecuadorian judgment through bribery and fraud. A key witness in Chevron's US case against Donziger, Alberto Guerra, later said under oath in an international tribunal that Chevron had paid him substantial sums (reportedly over $1 million and ongoing benefits) to provide false testimony against Donziger, requiring extensive coaching over 50 days.\nThe US case against Donziger was overseen by Judge Lewis A. Kaplan, who critics note had financial ties to Chevron. When Donziger appealed Kaplan's order to turn over his computer and phone citing attorney-client privilege, Kaplan referred the matter for criminal contempt charges. After the US Attorney's office declined to prosecute, Judge Kaplan appointed a private law firm,\nGibson Dunn & Crutcher\n(which has represented Chevron), to prosecute Donziger in the court's name – an unusual move described as the first corporate criminal prosecution in US history. Donziger was subsequently placed under house arrest for 993 days while awaiting trial on the misdemeanor charge. He was ultimately convicted of contempt of court and served time in prison, receiving a sentence critics claim exceeded the legal maximum for the offense. As a result of these legal battles, Donziger was disbarred, had his bank accounts frozen, and reportedly faces ongoing restrictions preventing him from working, holding accounts, or leaving the US. Chevron also sued Donziger for $60 billion, described as the largest amount sought from an individual in US history. Critics\nassert that Chevron's actions against Donziger were intended to make an example of him and deter other lawyers from challenging the company over environmental damages worldwide.\nConcerns have also been raised about Chevron's oil extraction activities in urban settings like Los Angeles. Observers note the presence of active oil wells located directly adjacent to homes and schools, particularly in lower-income areas. In wealthier neighborhoods, such as\nBeverly Hills\n, Chevron allegedly camouflages its drilling operations within large, windowless structures designed to look like ordinary office buildings or service facilities, hiding numerous wells from public view. One such disguised oil pump at\nBeverly Hills High School\n, dubbed the "Tower of Hope," reportedly drew attention only after it caught fire. Furthermore, Chevron is accused of improperly abandoning depleted wells in the city, leaving behind toxic sites that pose ongoing health risks (such as\nnosebleeds\nor respiratory issues) to nearby residents and can potentially lead to hazardous events like eruptions of toxic sludge. Critics argue that, similar to the situation in\nEcuador\n, regulatory oversight in these areas appears insufficient to prevent these practices or hold the company accountable for remediation.\nCritics argue that these incidents reflect a broader business model employed by Chevron and other large\nresource extraction\ncompanies. This alleged model involves entering countries, extracting resources as cheaply as possible (often with minimal environmental safeguards), dumping waste products to cut costs, and leaving local communities to deal with the long-term environmental and health consequences. The company is accused of using its significant financial and legal resources (employing thousands of lawyers and spending billions on litigation in the Ecuador case) to overwhelm opponents, delay legal proceedings, influence governments, and avoid accountability for damages caused by its operations globally.\nSee also\n[\nedit\n]\nCompanies portal\nChevron U.S.A., Inc. v.
