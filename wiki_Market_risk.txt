Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_risk\n==================================================\n\nRisks arising from movements in market variables\nMarket risk\nis   the\nrisk\nof losses in positions arising from movements in market variables like prices and\nvolatility\n.\nThere is no unique classification as each classification may refer to different aspects of market risk. Nevertheless, the most commonly used types of market risk are:\nEquity risk\n, the risk that\nstock\nor\nstock indices\n(e.g.\nEuro Stoxx 50\n, etc.) prices or their\nimplied volatility\nwill change.\nInterest rate risk\n, the risk that\ninterest rates\n(e.g.\nLibor\n,\nEuribor\n, etc.) or their implied volatility will change.\nCurrency risk\n, the risk that foreign exchange rates (e.g.\nEUR/USD\n,\nEUR/GBP\n, etc.) or their implied volatility will change.\nCommodity risk\n, the risk that\ncommodity\nprices (e.g.\ncorn\n,\ncrude oil\n) or their implied volatility will change.\nMargining risk\nresults from uncertain future cash outflows due to\nmargin\ncalls covering adverse value changes of a given position.\nShape risk\nHolding period risk\nBasis risk\nThe\ncapital requirement\nfor market risk is addressed under a revised framework known as "\nFundamental Review of the Trading Book\n" (FRTB).\nRisk management\n[\nedit\n]\nAll businesses take risks based on two factors: the probability an adverse circumstance will come about and the cost of such adverse circumstance.\nRisk management\nis then the study of how to control risks and balance the possibility of gains.
For a discussion of the practice of (market) risk management in banks, investment firms, and corporates more generally see\nFinancial risk management §\nApplication\n.\nMeasuring the potential loss amount due to market risk\n[\nedit\n]\nAs with other forms of risk, the potential loss amount due to market risk may be measured in several ways or conventions. Traditionally, one convention is to use\nvalue at risk\n(VaR). The conventions of using VaR are well established and accepted in the short-term risk management practice.\nHowever, VaR contains a number of limiting assumptions that constrain its accuracy.  The first assumption is that the composition of the portfolio measured remains unchanged over the specified period.  Over short time horizons, this limiting assumption is often regarded as reasonable. However, over longer time horizons, many of the positions in the portfolio may have been changed.  The VaR of the unchanged portfolio is no longer relevant.  Other problematic issues with VaR is that it is not\nsub-additive\n, and therefore not a\ncoherent risk measure\n.\nAs a result, other suggestions for measuring market risk is conditional value-at-risk (CVaR) that is coherent for general loss distributions, including discrete distributions and is sub-additive.\nThe\nvariance covariance\nand\nhistorical simulation\napproach to calculating VaR assumes that historical correlations are stable and will not change in the future or breakdown under times of market stress.  However these assumptions are inappropriate as during periods of high volatility and market turbulence, historical correlations tend to break down.  Intuitively, this is evident during a financial crisis where all industry sectors experience a significant increase in correlations, as opposed to an upward trending market.  This phenomenon is also known as asymmetric correlations or asymmetric dependence.  Rather than using the historical simulation, Monte-Carlo simulations with well-specified multivariate models are an excellent alternative. For example, to improve the estimation of the variance-covariance matrix, one can generate a forecast of asset distributions via Monte-Carlo simulation based upon the Gaussian copula and well-specified marginals.\nAllowing the modelling process to allow for empirical characteristics in stock returns such as auto-regression, asymmetric volatility, skewness, and kurtosis is important. Not accounting for these attributes lead to severe estimation error in the correlation and variance-covariance that have negative biases (as much as 70% of the true values).\nEstimation of VaR or CVaR for large portfolios of assets using the variance-covariance matrix may be inappropriate if the underlying returns distributions exhibit asymmetric dependence. In such scenarios, vine copulas that allow for asymmetric dependence (e.g., Clayton, Rotated Gumbel) across portfolios of assets are most appropriate in the calculation of tail risk using VaR or CVaR.\nBesides, care has to be taken regarding the intervening cash flow, embedded options, changes in floating rate interest rates of the financial positions in the portfolio.  They cannot be ignored if their impact can be large.\nMarket exposure\n[\nedit\n]\nIn\nfinance\n,\nmarket exposure\n(or\nexposure\n) is a measure of the\nproportion\nof money invested in the same\nindustry sector\n. For example, a\nstock portfolio\nwith a total worth of $500,000, with $100,000 in\nsemiconductor\nindustry stocks, would have a 20% exposure in "chip" stocks.\nRegulatory views\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nBasel Committee\nset revised minimum\ncapital requirements\nfor market risk in January 2016.\nThese revisions, the\n"Fundamental Review of the Trading Book"\n, address deficiencies relating to the existing\nInternal models\nand\nStandardised approach\nfor the calculation of market-risk capital, and in particular discuss the following:\nBoundary between the "\nTrading book\n" and the "\nBanking book\n"\nUse of\nvalue at risk\nvs.\nexpected shortfall\nto measure of risk under stress\nThe risk of\nmarket illiquidity\nUse in annual reports of U.S. corporations\n[\nedit\n]\nIn the\nUnited States\n, a section on market risk is mandated by the\nSEC\nin all annual reports submitted on\nForm 10-K\n. The company must detail how its results may depend directly on financial markets. This is designed to show, for example, an investor who believes he is investing in a normal milk company, that the company is also carrying out non-dairy activities such as investing in complex derivatives or foreign exchange futures.\nMarket risk for physical investments\n[\nedit\n]\nPhysical investments face market risks as well, for example\nreal capital\nsuch as real estate can lose market value and cost components such as fuel costs can fluctuate with market prices. On the other hand, some investments in physical capital can reduce risk and the value of the risk reduction can be estimated with financial calculation methods, just as market risk in financial markets is estimated. For example\nenergy efficiency\ninvestments, in addition to reducing fuel costs, reduce exposure fuel price risk. As less fuel is consumed, a smaller cost component is susceptible to fluctuations in fuel prices. The value of this risk reduction can be calculated using the Tuominen-Seppänen method\nand its value has been shown to be approximately 10% compared to direct cost savings for a typical energy efficient building.\nSee also\n[\nedit\n]\nSystemic risk\nCost risk\nDemand risk\nValuation risk\nRisk modeling\nRisk attitude\nModern portfolio theory\nRisk return ratio\nFinancial risk management §\nBanking\nFundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB)\nInternal models approach (market risk)\nStandardized approach (market risk)\nReferences\n[\nedit\n]\n↑\nBank for International Settlements: A glossary of terms used in payments and settlement systems\n↑\nArtzner, P.; Delbaen, F.; Eber, J.; Heath, D.
