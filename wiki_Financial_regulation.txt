Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_regulation\n==================================================\n\nRules or restrictions for financial institutions\nFinancial regulation\nis a broad set of policies that apply to the financial sector in most jurisdictions, justified by two main features of finance:\nsystemic risk\n, which implies that the failure of financial firms involves public interest considerations; and\ninformation asymmetry\n, which justifies curbs on\nfreedom of contract\nin selected areas of financial services, particularly those that involve retail clients and/or\nprincipal–agent problems\n. An integral part of financial regulation is the supervision of designated financial firms and markets by specialized authorities such as\nsecurities commissions\nand\nbank supervisors\n.\nIn some jurisdictions, certain aspects of financial supervision are delegated to\nself-regulatory organizations\n. Financial regulation forms one of three legal categories which constitutes the content of\nfinancial law\n, the other two being market practices and\ncase law\n.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nFinancial regulation has ancient roots, often evolving in response to economic crises or the need to standardize commerce. The\nCode of Hammurabi\n(c. 1700 BCE) contained standards for lending and the regulation of interest.\nIn the Roman Empire, banking was expanded beyond temples into private and state roles. This led to the creation of distinct financial institutions subject to administrative oversight.\nDuring the medieval period, financial regulation in Europe was heavily influenced by religious restrictions on\nusury\n(the charging of interest), which shaped the development of early merchant banks in Italy.\nIn the\nearly modern period\n, the Dutch were the pioneers in financial regulation.\nThe first recorded ban (regulation) on\nshort selling\nwas enacted by the Dutch authorities as early as 1610.\nAims of regulation\n[\nedit\n]\nThe objectives of financial regulators are usually:\nmarket confidence – to maintain confidence in the financial system\nfinancial stability – contributing to the protection and enhancement of stability of the financial system\nconsumer protection\n– securing the appropriate degree of protection for consumers.\nreduce financial crime\nregulate foreign participation\nStructure of supervision\n[\nedit\n]\nActs empower organizations, government or non-government, to monitor activities and enforce actions.\nThere are various setups and combinations in place for the financial regulatory structure around the globe.\nSecurities market regulation\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nSecurities commission\nExchange acts ensure that trading on the floor of exchanges is conducted in a proper manner. Most prominent the pricing process, execution and settlement of trades, direct and efficient trade monitoring.\nFinancial regulators ensure that listed companies and market participants comply with various regulations under the trading acts. The trading acts demands that listed companies publish regular financial reports, ad hoc notifications or directors' dealings. Whereas market participants are required to publish major shareholder notifications. The objective of monitoring compliance by listed companies with their disclosure requirements is to ensure that investors have access to essential and adequate information for making an informed assessment of listed companies and their securities.\nAsset management supervision or investment acts ensures the frictionless operation of those vehicles.\nSupervision of banks and financial services providers\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nBank regulation\nBanking acts lay down rules for banks which they have to observe when they are being established and when they are carrying on their business. These rules are designed to prevent unwelcome developments that might disrupt the smooth functioning of the banking system. Thus ensuring a strong and efficient banking system.\nFinancial supervisory authorities\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nList of financial supervisory authorities by country\nSee also\n[\nedit\n]\nBank regulation\nFinance\nFinancial economics §\nFinancial markets\nFinancial ethics\nFinancial regulation in India\nFinancial repression\nGlobal financial system\nGroup of Thirty\nInsurance law\nInternational Organization of Securities Commissions\nInternational Centre for Financial Regulation\nLabEx ReFi - European Laboratory on Financial Regulation\nMacroprudential regulation\nMicroprudential regulation\nRegulatory capture\nRegulatory economics\nSecurities commission\nCommodity market §\nRegulation of commodity markets\nVirtual currency law in the United States\nReferences\n[\nedit\n]\n↑\nJoanna Benjamin 'Financial Law' Oxford University Press\n↑\n"The Code of Hammurabi"\n.
