For other uses, see\nJ & J (disambiguation)\nand\nJNJ (disambiguation)\n.\nJohnson & Johnson\n(\nJ&J\n) is an American\nmultinational\npharmaceutical\n,\nbiotechnology\n, and\nmedical technologies\ncorporation headquartered in\nNew Brunswick, New Jersey\n. The company is ranked No. 48 on the 2025\nFortune\n500\nlist of the largest United States corporations.\nIn 2025, the company was ranked 42nd in the\nForbes\nGlobal 2000\n.\nAlong with\nMicrosoft\n, Johnson & Johnson is one of only two U.S.-based companies that has a prime\ncredit rating\nof AAA from\nS&P Global\n.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\n1873–1885: Before Johnson & Johnson\n[\nedit\n]\nRobert Wood Johnson\nbegan his professional training at age 16 as a pharmaceutical apprentice at an apothecary run by his mother's cousin, James G. Wood, in Poughkeepsie, New York.\nJohnson co-founded his own company with\nGeorge J. Seabury\nin 1873. The New York-based Seabury & Johnson became known for its medicated plasters.\nRobert Wood Johnson represented the company at the\nCentennial Exposition\nin 1876.\nThere he heard\nJoseph Lister\n's explanation of a new procedure: antiseptic surgery.\nJohnson parted ways with his business partner Seabury in 1885.\n1886: Founding of Johnson & Johnson\n[\nedit\n]\nRobert Wood Johnson, one of the company's founders\nRobert Wood Johnson joined his brothers,\nJames Wood Johnson\nand\nEdward Mead Johnson\n, and created a line of ready-to-use sterile surgical dressings in 1886. They founded Johnson & Johnson in 1886\nwith 14 employees, eight women and six men.\nJohnson & Johnson opened its first factory building in the old Janeway and Carpenter factory on Neilson Street in New Brunswick, New Jersey.\nThey manufactured sterile surgical supplies, household products, and medical guides.\nThose products initially featured a logo that resembled the signature of James Wood Johnson.\nRobert Wood Johnson served as the first president of the company.\n1887–1942: Early history\n[\nedit\n]\nEarly corrosive sublimate cotton packaging with the signature logo, c. 1887\nThe company sold medicated plasters such as Johnson & Johnson's Black Perfect Taffeta Court Plaster\nand also manufactured the world's first sterile surgical products, including sutures, absorbent cotton, and gauze.\nThe company published "Modern Methods of Antiseptic Wound Treatment", a guide on how to do sterile surgery using its products, and in 1888, distributed 85,000 copies to doctors and pharmacists across the United States.\nThe manual was translated into three languages and distributed worldwide.\nThe first commercial\nfirst aid kit\nwas designed in 1888 to support railroad construction workers, who were often hundreds of miles from medical care.\nThe kits included antiseptic emergency supplies and directions for field use. In 1901, the company published the\nHandbook of First Aid\n, a guide on applying first aid.\nIn 1889, the company hired pharmacist\nFred Kilmer\nas its first scientific director, who led its scientific research and wrote educational manuals.\nKilmer's first achievement as scientific director was developing the industrial sterilization process.\nHe was employed at the company until 1934.\nJohnson & Johnson had more than 400 employees and 14 buildings by 1894.\nIn 1894, the company began producing Johnson's Baby Powder, the company's first baby product.\nThe company introduced the world's first maternity kit in 1894 to aid at-home births, called Dr. Simpson's Maternity Packet, presumably after Scottish\nobstetrician\nJames Young Simpson\n. The kit included a washcloth; safety pins; sterile sutures, sponges, and gauze; antiseptic soap; an obstetric sheet and ligatures; flannel to wrap the baby in; and a chart for keeping birth records.\nThe products were later marketed separately, including "Lister's Towels", the world's first mass-produced sanitary napkins.\nKilmer wrote "Hygiene in Maternity", an instructional guide for mothers before and after delivery.\nIn 1904, the company expanded its baby care products with "Lister's Sanitary Diapers", a diaper product for infants.\nDuring the\nSpanish–American War\n, Johnson & Johnson developed and donated 300,000 packaged compressed surgical dressings for soldiers in the field\nand created a trauma stretcher for field medics. The company donated its products in disaster relief efforts of the\n1900 Galveston hurricane\nand the\n1906 San Francisco earthquake\n.\nJohnson & Johnson vaccinated all of its employees against\nsmallpox\nduring the 1901 smallpox epidemic. The firm employed more than 1,200 people by 1910.\nWomen accounted for half of the company's workforce and led a quarter of its departments.\nRobert Wood Johnson died in 1910, and he was succeeded as president of the company by his brother James Wood Johnson.\nDuring\nWorld War I\n, Johnson & Johnson factories ran round-the-clock shifts to supply sterile\ndressings\n,\ngauze\n, and\nsurgical sutures\nto the Allied forces and European hospitals.\nIn 1916, the company acquired Chicopee Manufacturing Company in\nChicopee Falls, Massachusetts\n, to meet demand.\nNear the end of World War I, the\nSpanish flu\nbroke out. The company invented and distributed an epidemic mask that helped prevent the spread of the flu.\nIn 1919, Johnson & Johnson opened the Gilmour Plant near Montreal, its first factory outside the United States,\nwhich produced surgical products for international customers. In 1924 the company's first overseas manufacturing facility was opened in\nSlough\n, England.\nIn 1920, Earle Dickson combined two Johnson & Johnson products, adhesive tape and gauze, to create the first commercial adhesive bandage. Band-Aid Brand Adhesive Bandages began sales the following year.\nIn 1921, the company released Johnson's Baby Soap.\nNamed after its Massachusetts facility, Johnson & Johnson built a textile mill and company town, Chicopee, outside Gainesville, Georgia.\nIn the 1930s, the company expanded operations to Argentina, Brazil, Mexico, and South Africa.\nIn 1931, Johnson & Johnson introduced the first prescription contraceptive gel marketed as Ortho-Gynol.\nRobert Wood Johnson II\nbecame president of the company in 1932.\nDuring the\nGreat Depression\n, Johnson & Johnson kept all its workers employed and raised wages by 5%.\nIn 1933, Robert Wood Johnson II wrote a letter to\nFranklin D. Roosevelt\n, calling for a federal law to increase wages and reduce hours for all American workers.\nThe company also opened a new facility in Chicago during that period.\nJohnson wrote and distributed "Try Reality: A Discussion of Hours, Wages, and The Industrial Future" to persuade business leaders to follow his lead, advocating that business is more than profit and that companies have responsibilities to consumers, employees, and society. In "Try Reality", the section titled "An Industrial Philosophy" would later become the company's credo.\nIn 1935, Johnson's Baby Oil was added to its line of baby products.\nBoth male and female Johnson & Johnson employees were drafted and enlisted during World War II.\nThe company ensured no one would lose their job when they returned home.\nRobert Wood Johnson II was appointed head of the Smaller War Plants Corporation in Washington, D.C. His work ensured U.S. factories with under 500 employees were awarded government contracts.\n1943: Credo and IPO\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 1943, as the company was preparing for its\ninitial public offering\n(IPO), Robert Wood Johnson wrote what the company called "Our Credo",\na defining document that has been used to guide the company's decisions over the years.\nThe company became a\npublic company\nvia an\ninitial public offering\nin 1944.\nIn 1943,\nVesta Stoudt\nidentified a need for waterproof tape for ammunition boxes in\nWorld War Two\n. She wrote to\nFranklin D. Roosevelt\nwith the idea; the president commissioned Revolite, a subsidiary of Johnson & Johnson at the time, to develop and manufacture a cloth-based adhesive tape, later\nduct tape\n.\n1944–1999: Acquisitions and international expansion\n[\nedit\n]\nVariation of logo used from the company's establishment until September 14, 2023.\nIn 1944, the company began selling\nJohnson's Baby\nLotion. The same year, the company established Ethicon Suture Laboratories. In 1947, G. F. Merson Ltd. was acquired to expand the company's suture business in the United Kingdom. The company was rebranded and absorbed into\nEthicon\n.\nJohnson & Johnson\nchairman of the board\n, Robert Wood Johnson, published\nOr Forfeit Freedom\n, in 1947. Micro-point surgical needles and sutures allowed for advances in modern vision surgery.\nIn 1956, the company opened its first Asia-based operating company in the Philippines.\nJohnson & Johnson opened its first operating company in India in 1957.\nIn 1959, Johnson & Johnson acquired\nMcNeil Consumer Healthcare\n.\nA year later, the company sold Tylenol for the first time without a prescription.\nIn the same year, Cilag Chemie joined Johnson & Johnson as Cilag.\nIn 1961, Johnson & Johnson acquired\nJanssen Pharmaceuticals\n, which had been founded in 1953 by Belgian scientist\nPaul Janssen\n,\nthe inventor of\nFentanyl\n.\nIn 1963,\nPhilip B. Hofmann\nsucceeded Robert Wood Johnson as chairman and CEO. He was the first non-Johnson family member to become chief executive. The vaccine prevented Rh hemolytic disease in newborns.\nIn 1969, Ortho Diagnostics, a company subsidiary, launched the Sickledex Tube Test for detecting anemia.\nThe same year, the FDA approved the Johnson & Johnson arterial graft.\nIn 1971, the company launched Hapindex Diagnostic Test, a rapid\nhepatitis B\ntest for blood donors. The test was developed to prevent the spread of hepatitis B through blood transfusions.\nIn the 1970s, Johnson & Johnson hired Henry N. Cobb from Pei Cobb Freed & Partners to design its new headquarters.\nThe firm designed Johnson & Johnson Plaza across the railroad tracks from the older section of the Johnson & Johnson campus.\nIn 1973,\nRichard Sellars\nbecame chairman and CEO of Johnson & Johnson.\nIn 1976,\nJames E. Burke\nbecame the company's chairman and CEO.\nDuring Burke's tenure, he managed the\n1982 Tylenol tampering incident\n. It became a case study on crisis management. Under his leadership, the company recalled 31 million bottles of Tylenol, relaunched the product with a triple tamper-evident seal, and urged consumers not to use if tampered with. These practices became the pharmaceutical and packaged food industry norm.\nJohnson & Johnson opened operating companies in China and Egypt in 1985.\nIn 1987,\nAcuvue\ncontact lenses\nbecame the first disposable contact lenses available to consumers. The lenses lasted up to one week, reducing the cost of contact lenses. In the same year, the company launched One Touch, a blood glucose monitoring system.\nIn 1989,\nRalph S. Larsen\nwas appointed chairman and CEO of the company.\nAfter the\ndissolution of the Soviet Union\n, Johnson & Johnson expanded into eastern Europe. By 1991, the company had a presence in Hungary, Russia, the\nCzech Republic\n, and Poland. In the 1990s, the company acquired\nClean & Clear\n,\nNeutrogena\n,\nMotrin\n, and\nAveeno\n.\nIn 1997, Johnson & Johnson acquired Biosense Webster, based in\nHaifa\n.\nDePuy\nwas acquired by Johnson & Johnson in 1998 for $3.5 billion.\n2000–2019\n[\nedit\n]\nWilliam C. This means that the U.S. did not agree to any interpretation of the 1949 Geneva Conventions that would overrule Johnson & Johnson's trademark. The American Red Cross continues to recognize the validity of Johnson & Johnson's trademark.\nIn August 2007, Johnson & Johnson filed a lawsuit against the American Red Cross, demanding that the charity halt the use of the red cross symbol on products it sells to the public, though the company takes no issue with the charity's use of the mark for nonprofit purposes.\nIn May 2008, the judge in the case dismissed most of Johnson & Johnson's claims, and, a month later, the two organizations settled the case, allowing both parties to use the symbol.\nBoston Scientific lawsuits\n[\nedit\n]\nSince 2003, Johnson & Johnson and\nBoston Scientific\nhave both claimed that the other had infringed on their patents covering\nheart stent\nmedical devices.
