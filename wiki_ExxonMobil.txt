As the largest U.S.-based oil and gas company, ExxonMobil is the\neighth-largest company by revenue in the U.S.\nand\n13th-largest in the world\n. It is also the largest investor-owned oil company in the world.\nThe company has been widely criticized and sued, mostly for environmental incidents and\nits history of climate change denial\nagainst the scientific consensus that fossil fuels significantly contribute to\nglobal warming\n.\nThe company is responsible for many oil spills, the largest and most notable of which was the 1989\nExxon Valdez\noil spill\nin\nAlaska\n, considered to be one of the\nworld's worst oil spills\nin terms of environmental damage.\nThe company has also been accused of human rights violations and exerting excessive influence on\nAmerican foreign policy\nand developing countries.\nIn 2024, ExxonMobil was responsible for 610 Mt of CO\n2\nemissions, which was 1.58% of global CO\n2\nemissions.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nHistory of ExxonMobil\nOriginally,\nVacuum Oil Company\nwas founded in 1866. Vacuum Oil later was acquired by\nStandard Oil\nin 1879, divested from Standard in 1911 with\nits legally-mandated breakup\n, and merged by the Standard Oil Company of New York (Socony), later known as\nMobil\n, in 1931. After the 1911 breakup, Standard Oil continued to exist through its New Jersey subsidiary (Jersey Standard), and retained its name in much of the eastern United States. Jersey Standard grew by acquiring\nHumble Oil\nin the 1930s and became the dominant oil company on the world stage.\nThe company's lack of ownership over the Standard Oil name across the United States, however, prompted a name change to unify all of its brands under one name, choosing Exxon in 1972 instead of continuing to use the three distinct brands\nEsso\n,\nEnco\n, and\nHumble Oil\n.\nIn 1998, the two companies agreed to merge and form ExxonMobil, with the deal closing on November 30, 1999. The new company's name containing both of the trade names of its immediate predecessors. The authors of the paper concluded: "Yet, whereas academic and government scientists worked to communicate what they knew to the public, ExxonMobil worked to deny it."\nBetween the 1980s and 2014, ExxonMobil was a notable denier of climate change, though the company officially changed its position in 2014 to acknowledge the existence of climate change. ExxonMobil's prolonged response incited the creation of the\nExxon Knew\nmovement, which aims to hold the company accountable for various climate-related incidents. ExxonMobil has used its own website to attack\nExxon Knew\n.\nIn December 2022,\nU.S. House Oversight and Reform Committee\nChair\nCarolyn Maloney\nand\nU.S. House Oversight Environment Subcommittee\nChair\nRo Khanna\nsent a memorandum to all House Oversight and Reform Committee members summarizing additional findings from the committee's investigation into the\nfossil fuel industry disinformation\ncampaign to obscure the role of fossil fuels in causing global warming. The most widely publicized oil spill was the 1989\nValdez\noil spill\n, where an Exxon tanker discharged approximately 11 million U.S. gallons (42,000 m3) of oil into\nPrince William Sound\n,\noiling\n1,300 miles (2,100\nkm)\nof the remote Alaskan coastline. The spill remains the second largest in American history, only trailing BP's\nDeepwater Horizon\nspill\nin the\nGulf of Mexico\n.\nExxonMobil was also responsible for various other oil spills across the world. Some of Exxon's largest and most notable oil spills in the United States include long-lasting oil leaks totaling into an estimated 30 million gallon spill into New York City's\nNewtown Creek\nover the course of a century by Exxon and other Standard Oil predecessors,\na 2011 oil spill which leaked 1,500 barrels of oil into the\nYellowstone River\n(resulting in about $135 million in damages),\nand a 2012 1,900 barrel (80,000 gallon) spill from the company's\nBaton Rouge Refinery\nin the rivers of\nPointe Coupee Parish, Louisiana\n.\nExxonMobil's actives in Louisiana in particular, especially its Baton Rouge Refinery, have given the area the nickname of\nCancer Alley\n. The company's activities, along with other operations and refineries in the area, have been the source of increased cancer infections, lower air quality, and as seen by some, potential environmental racism committed by the company.\nIn May 2021, ExxonMobil topped the Plastic Waste Makers Index report published by the Minderoo Foundation of 20 petrochemical companies that manufactured 55 percent of the\nsingle-use plastic waste\nin the world in 2019 (which were part of a larger group of 100 petrochemical companies that manufactured 90 percent of the waste),\nwhile in April 2022, California Attorney General\nRob Bonta\nissued a subpoena to ExxonMobil for information related to the company's role in overstating the effectiveness of\nplastic recycling\nin reducing plastic pollution as part of an industry campaign to promote plastic usage.\nOn September 23, 2024,\nCalifornia Attorney General\nRob Bonta\nfiled a lawsuit in\nSan Francisco County Superior Court\nagainst ExxonMobil on behalf of the state of\nCalifornia\n, alleging that the company carried out a "decades-long campaign of deception" and misled the public on the merits of plastic recycling;
