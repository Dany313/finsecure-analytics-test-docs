Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credit_risk\n==================================================\n\nRisk that a borrower or counterparty fails to meet financial obligations\nCredit risk\nis the chance that a\nborrower\ndoes not repay a\nloan\nor fulfill a loan obligation.\nFor\nlenders\nthe risk includes late or lost\ninterest\nand\nprincipal\npayment, leading to disrupted\ncash flows\nand increased\ncollection costs\n. The loss may be complete or partial. In an\nefficient market\n, higher levels of credit\nrisk\nwill be associated with higher borrowing costs.  Because of this, measures of borrowing costs such as\nyield spreads\ncan be used to infer credit risk levels based on assessments by\nmarket participants\n.\nLosses can arise in a number of circumstances,\nfor example:\nA consumer may fail to make a payment due on a\nmortgage loan\n, credit card,\nline of credit\n, or other loan.\nA company is unable to repay asset-secured fixed or\nfloating charge\ndebt.\nA business or consumer does not pay a\ntrade invoice\nwhen due.\nA business does not pay an employee's earned wages when due.\nA business or government\nbond\nissuer does not make a payment on a\ncoupon\nor principal payment when due.\nAn\ninsolvent\ninsurance company does not pay a\npolicy\nobligation.\nAn insolvent bank will not return funds to a depositor.\nA government grants\nbankruptcy\nprotection to an insolvent consumer or business.\nTo reduce the lender's credit risk, the lender may perform a\ncredit check\non the prospective borrower, may require the borrower to take out appropriate insurance, such as\nmortgage insurance\n, or seek\nsecurity\nover some assets of the borrower or a\nguarantee\nfrom a third party. The lender can also take out insurance against the risk or on-sell the debt to another company. In general, the higher the risk, the higher will be the\ninterest rate\nthat the debtor will be asked to pay on the debt. Credit risk mainly arises when borrowers are unable or unwilling to pay.\nTypes\n[\nedit\n]\nA credit risk can be of the following types:\nCredit default risk\n– The risk of loss arising from a debtor being unlikely to pay its loan obligations in full or the debtor is more than 90 days past due on any material credit obligation; default risk may impact all credit-sensitive transactions, including loans, securities and\nderivatives\n.\nConcentration risk\n– The risk associated with any single exposure or group of exposures with the potential to produce large enough losses to threaten a bank's core operations. It may arise in the form of single-name concentration or industry concentration.\nCountry risk\n– The risk of loss arising from a sovereign state freezing foreign currency payments (transfer/conversion risk) or when it defaults on its obligations (\nsovereign risk\n); this type of risk is prominently associated with the country's macroeconomic performance and its political stability.\nAssessment\n[\nedit\n]\nMain articles:\nCredit analysis\nand\nConsumer credit risk\nSignificant resources and sophisticated programs are used to analyze and manage risk.\nSome companies run a credit risk department whose job is to assess the financial health of their customers, and extend credit (or not) accordingly. They may use in-house programs to advise on avoiding, reducing and transferring risk. They also use the third party provided intelligence. Nationally recognized statistical rating organizations provide such information for a fee.\nFor large companies with liquidly traded\ncorporate bonds\nor\ncredit default swaps\n, bond\nyield spreads\nand credit default swap spreads indicate market participants assessments of credit risk and may be used as a reference point to price loans or trigger collateral calls.\nMost lenders employ their models (\ncredit scorecards\n) to rank potential and existing customers according to risk, and then apply appropriate strategies.\nWith products such as unsecured personal loans or mortgages, lenders charge a higher price for higher-risk customers and vice versa.\nWith revolving products such as credit cards and overdrafts, the risk is controlled through the setting of credit limits. Some products also require\ncollateral\n, usually an asset that is pledged to secure the repayment of the loan.\nCredit scoring models also form part of the framework used by banks or lending institutions to grant credit to clients.\nFor corporate and commercial borrowers, these models generally have qualitative and quantitative sections outlining various aspects of the risk including, but not limited to, operating experience, management expertise, asset quality, and leverage and\nliquidity ratios\n, respectively. Once this information has been fully reviewed by credit officers and credit committees, the lender provides the funds subject to the terms and conditions presented within the contract (as outlined above).\nSovereign risk\n[\nedit\n]\nSovereign credit risk\nis the risk of a government being unwilling or unable to meet its loan obligations, or reneging on loans it guarantees. Many countries have faced sovereign risk in the\nGreat Recession\n. The existence of such risk means that creditors should take a two-stage decision process when deciding to lend to a firm based in a foreign country. Firstly one should consider the sovereign risk quality of the country and then consider the firm's credit quality.\nSix macroeconomic variables that affect the probability of\nsovereign debt\nrescheduling are:\nDebt service ratio\nImport ratio\nInvestment ratio\nVariance of export revenue\nDomestic money supply growth\nGDP growth rate\nThe probability of rescheduling is an increasing function of debt service ratio, import ratio, the variance of export revenue and domestic money supply growth.\nThe likelihood of rescheduling is a decreasing function of investment ratio due to future economic productivity gains. Debt rescheduling likelihood can increase if the investment ratio rises as the foreign country could become less dependent on its external creditors and so be less concerned about receiving credit from these countries/investors.\nCounterparty risk\n[\nedit\n]\nA counterparty risk, also known as a\nsettlement risk\nor\ncounterparty credit risk\n(CCR), is a risk that a\ncounterparty\nwill not pay as obligated on a\nbond\n,\nderivative\n,\ninsurance policy\n, or other contract.\nFinancial institutions or other transaction counterparties may\nhedge\nor take out\ncredit insurance\nor, particularly in the context of derivatives, require the posting of collateral. Offsetting counterparty risk is not always possible, e.g. because of temporary liquidity issues or longer-term systemic reasons.\nFurther, counterparty risk increases due to positively correlated risk factors; accounting for this correlation between portfolio risk factors and counterparty default in risk management methodology is not trivial.\nThe\ncapital requirement\nhere is calculated using SA-CCR, the\nstandardized approach for counterparty credit risk\n. This framework replaced both non-internal model approaches – Current Exposure Method (CEM) and Standardised Method (SM).\nMitigation\n[\nedit\n]\nLenders mitigate credit risk in a number of ways, including:\nRisk-based pricing\n– Lenders may charge a higher\ninterest rate\nto borrowers who are more likely to default, a practice called\nrisk-based pricing\n. Lenders consider factors relating to the loan such as\nloan purpose\n,\ncredit rating\n, and\nloan-to-value ratio\nand estimates the effect on yield (\ncredit spread\n).\nCovenants\n– Lenders may write stipulations on the borrower, called\ncovenants\n, into loan agreements, such as:\nPeriodically report its financial condition,\nRefrain from paying\ndividends\n,\nrepurchasing shares\n, borrowing further, or other specific, voluntary actions that negatively affect the company's financial position, and\nRepay the loan in full, at the lender's request, in certain events such as changes in the borrower's\ndebt-to-equity ratio\nor\ninterest coverage ratio\n.\nCredit insurance\nand\ncredit derivatives\n– Lenders and\nbond\nholders may\nhedge\ntheir credit risk by purchasing\ncredit insurance\nor\ncredit derivatives\n. These contracts transfer the risk from the lender to the seller (insurer) in exchange for payment. The most common credit derivative is the\ncredit default swap\n.\nTightening\n– Lenders can reduce credit risk by reducing the amount of credit extended, either in total or to certain borrowers. For example, a\ndistributor\nselling its products to a troubled\nretailer\nmay attempt to lessen credit risk by reducing payment terms from\nnet 30\nto\nnet 15\n.\nDiversification\n– Lenders to a small number of borrowers (or kinds of borrower) face a high degree of\nunsystematic\ncredit risk, called\nconcentration risk\n.\nLenders reduce this risk by\ndiversifying\nthe borrower pool.\nDeposit insurance\n– Governments may establish\ndeposit insurance\nto guarantee bank deposits in the event of insolvency and to encourage consumers to hold their savings in the banking system instead of in cash.\nRelated  initialisms\n[\nedit\n]\nACPM\nActive credit portfolio management\nCCR\nCounterparty credit risk\nCE\nCredit exposure\nCVA\nCredit valuation adjustment\nDVA\nDebit valuation adjustment
