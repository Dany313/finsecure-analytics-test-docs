Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basel_III\n==================================================\n\nBanking regulation framework\nBasel III\nis the third of three\nBasel Accords\n, a framework that sets international standards and minimums for bank\ncapital requirements\n,\nstress tests\n,\nliquidity regulations\n, and\nleverage\n, with the goal of mitigating the risk of\nbank runs\nand\nbank failures\n. It was developed in response to the deficiencies in\nfinancial regulation\nrevealed by the\n2008 financial crisis\nand builds upon the standards of\nBasel II\n, introduced in 2004, and\nBasel I\n, introduced in 1988.\nThe Basel III requirements were published by the\nBasel Committee on Banking Supervision\nin 2010,\nand began to be implemented in major countries in 2012.\nImplementation of the\nFundamental Review of the Trading Book\n(FRTB), published and revised between 2013 and 2019, has been completed only in some countries and is scheduled to be completed in others in 2025 and 2026.\nImplementation of the\nBasel III: Finalising post-crisis reforms\n(also known as Basel 3.1 or Basel III Endgame), introduced in 2017, was extended several times, and will be phased-in by 2028.\nKey principles and requirements\n[\nedit\n]\nSee also:\nCapital requirement\n,\nCapital adequacy ratio\n, and\nList of bank stress tests\nCommon Equity Tier 1 (CET1) capital requirements\n[\nedit\n]\nCET1\nRWAs\n=\nCET1\nratio\n{\displaystyle {\frac {\mbox{CET1}}{\mbox{RWAs}}}={\mbox{CET1}}\;{\textrm {ratio}}}\nBasel III requires banks to have a minimum CET1 ratio (Common\nTier 1 capital\ndivided by\nrisk-weighted assets\n(RWAs)) at all times of:\n4.5%\nPlus:\nA mandatory "capital conservation buffer" or "stress capital buffer requirement", equivalent to at least 2.5% of risk-weighted assets, but could be higher based on results from\nstress tests\n, as determined by national regulators.\nPlus:\nIf necessary, as determined by national regulators, a "\ncounter-cyclical\nbuffer" of up to an additional 2.5% of RWA as capital during periods of high credit growth. This must be met by CET1 capital.\nIn the U.S., an additional 1% is required for globally\nsystemically important financial institutions\n.\nIt also requires minimum\nTier 1 capital\nof 6% at all times (beginning in 2015).\nCommon\nTier 1 capital\ncomprises shareholders equity (including\naudited\nprofits), less deductions of accounting reserve that are not believed to be loss absorbing "today", including goodwill and other intangible assets. To prevent the potential of double-counting of capital across the economy, bank's holdings of other bank shares are also deducted.\nTier 2 capital requirements\n[\nedit\n]\nTier 2 capital\n+\nTier 1 capital\nis required to be above 8%.\nLeverage ratio requirements\n[\nedit\n]\nLeverage ratio is calculated by dividing Tier 1 capital by the bank's leverage exposure. The leverage exposure is the sum of the exposures of all on-balance sheet assets, 'add-ons' for derivative exposures and securities financing transactions (SFTs), and credit conversion factors for off-balance sheet items.\nBasel III introduced a minimum leverage ratio of 3%.\nTier 1 Capital\nTotal exposure\n≥\n3\n%\n{\displaystyle {\frac {\mbox{Tier 1 Capital}}{\mbox{Total exposure}}}\geq 3\%}\nThe U.S. established another ratio, the supplemental leverage ratio, defined as\nTier 1 capital\ndivided by total assets. It is required to be above 3.0%.\nA minimum leverage ratio of 5% is required for large banks and\nsystemically important financial institutions\n.\nDue to the\nCOVID-19 pandemic\n, from April 2020 until 31 March 2021, for financial institutions with more than $250\nbillion in consolidated assets, the calculation excluded U.S. Treasury securities and deposits at Federal Reserve Banks.\nIn the EU, the minimum bank leverage ratio is the same 3% as required by Basel III.\nThe UK requires a minimum leverage ratio, for banks with deposits greater than £50 billion, of 3.25%. This higher minimum reflects the PRA's differing treatment of the leverage ratio, which excludes central bank reserves in 'Total exposure' of the calculation.\nLiquidity requirements\n[\nedit\n]\nBasel III introduced two required liquidity/funding ratios.\nLiquidity coverage ratio\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nliquidity coverage ratio\nrequires banks to hold sufficient high-quality liquid assets to cover its total net cash outflows over 30 days under a stressed scenario. This was implemented because some adequately-capitalized banks faced difficulties because of poor liquidity management.\nThe LCR consists of two parts: the numerator is the value of HQLA, and the denominator consists of the total net cash outflows over a specified stress period (total expected cash outflows minus total expected cash inflows).\nMathematically it is expressed as follows:\nLCR\n=\nHigh quality liquid assets\nTotal net liquidity outflows over 30 days\n≥\n100\n%\n{\displaystyle {\text{LCR}}={\frac {\text{High quality liquid assets}}{\text{Total net liquidity outflows over 30 days}}}\geq 100\%}\nRegulators can allow banks to dip below their required liquidity levels per the liquidity coverage ratio during periods of stress.\nLiquidity coverage ratio requirements for U.S. banks\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2014, the\nFederal Reserve Board of Governors\napproved a U.S. version of the liquidity coverage ratio,\nwhich had more stringent definitions of HQLA and total net cash outflows. Certain privately issued\nmortgage backed securities\nare included in HQLA under Basel III but not under the U.S. rule. Bonds and securities issued by financial institutions, which can become illiquid during a\nfinancial crisis\n, are not eligible under the U.S. rule. The rule is also modified for banks that do not have at least $250 billion in total assets or at least $10 billion in on-balance sheet foreign exposure.\nNet stable funding ratio\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nNet stable funding ratio\nThe\nNet stable funding ratio\nrequires banks to hold sufficient stable funding to exceed the required amount of stable funding over a one-year period of extended stress.\nNSFR\n=\nAvailable amount of stable funding\nRequired amount of stable funding\n>\n100\n%\n{\displaystyle {\text{NSFR}}={\frac {\text{Available amount of stable funding}}{\text{Required amount of stable funding }}}>100\%}\nCounterparty risk: CCPs and SA-CCR\n[\nedit\n]\nA new framework for exposures to\nCCPs\nwas introduced in 2017.\nThe\nstandardised approach for counterparty credit risk\n(SA-CCR), which replaced the\nCurrent exposure method\n, became effective in 2017.\nSA-CCR is used to measure the\npotential future exposure\nof derivative transactions in the leverage exposure measure and non-modelled Risk Weighted Asset calculations.\nCapital requirements for equity investments in funds\n[\nedit\n]\nCapital requirements for equity investments in\nhedge funds\n, managed funds, and\ninvestment funds\nwere introduced in 2017. The framework requires banks to take account of a fund's leverage when determining risk-based capital requirements associated with the investment and more appropriately reflecting the risk of the fund's underlying investments, including the use of a 1,250% risk weight for situations in which there is not sufficient transparency.\nLimiting large exposure to external and internal counterparties\n[\nedit\n]\nA framework for limiting large exposure to external and internal counterparties was implemented in 2018.\nIn the UK, as of 2024, the\nBank of England\nwas in the process of implementing the Basel III framework on large exposures.\nCapital standards for securitisations\n[\nedit\n]\nA revised securitisation framework, effective in 2018, aims to address shortcomings in the Basel II securitisation framework and to strengthen the capital standards for securitisations held on bank balance sheets.\nThe frameworks addresses the calculation of minimum capital needs for securitisation exposures.\nBasel III reclassifies physical gold from a Tier 3 asset to a Tier 1 asset.\nInterest rate risk in the banking book\n[\nedit\n]\nNew standards for "interest rate risk in the\nbanking book\n" (IRRBB) became effective in 2023. Banks are required to calculate their exposures based on "\neconomic value\nof equity" (EVE) and "\nnet interest income\n" (NII) under a set of prescribed\ninterest rate\nshock scenarios.\nThe standards thereby deal with the risks associated with a change in interest rates, including\ninterest rate gaps\n,\nbasis risk\n,\nyield curve risk\n, and\noption risk\n.\nThe bank's exposure to IRRBB is then equal to the largest negative change in EVE across all scenarios - in essence, the theoretical risk to the economic value of a bank's equity from a change in interest rates.\nIRRBB falls under\nPillar II\n.\nFundamental Review of the Trading Book\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nFundamental Review of the Trading Book\nFollowing a\nFundamental Review of the Trading Book\n, minimum capital requirements for market risk in the trading book are based on a better calibrated standardised approach or internal model approval (IMA) for an\nexpected shortfall\nmeasure rather than, under Basel II,\nvalue at risk\n.\nBasel III: Finalising post-crisis reforms\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nBasel III: Finalising post-crisis reforms\nstandards cover further reforms in six areas:\nstandardised approach for credit risk\n(SA-CR)\ninternal ratings based approach\n(IRB) for credit risk\nCredit valuation adjustment\nrisk (the process through which counterparty credit is valued, priced and hedged - using a standardised approach);\nOperational risk\n- A standardised approach for operational risk for a bank based on income and historical losses\nOutput floor - Replaces Basel II output floor with a more robust risk-sensitive floor and disclosure requirements\nFinalised Leverage ratio framework - buffer for global systemically important banks, definitions and requirements, exposure measures for on-balance sheet exposures, derivatives, securities financing transactions and off-balance sheet items.\nOther principles and requirements\n[\nedit\n]\nThe quality, consistency, and transparency of the capital base was raised.\nTier 1 capital\n: the predominant form of Tier 1 capital must be common shares and retained earnings. This is subject to prudential deductions, including goodwill and intangible assets.\nTier 2 capital\n: supplementary capital, however, the instruments were harmonised.\nTier 3 capital was eliminated.\nThe risk coverage of the capital framework was strengthened.\nPromoted more integrated management of market and counterparty credit risk\nAdded the\ncredit valuation adjustment\n–risk due to deterioration in counterparty's credit rating\nStrengthened the capital requirements for counterparty\ncredit exposures arising from banks' derivatives\n, repo and\nsecurities financing\ntransactions\nRaised the capital buffers backing these exposures\nReduced\nprocyclicality\nand\nProvided additional incentives to move\nOTC derivative contracts\nto qualifying central counterparties (probably\nclearing houses\n). Where a bank acts as a clearing member of a central counterparty for its own purposes, a risk weight of 2% must be applied to the bank’s trade exposure to the central counterparty.\nProvided incentives to strengthen the\nrisk management\nof counterparty credit exposures\nRaised counterparty credit risk management standards by including\nwrong way risk\nA series of measures was introduced to promote the buildup of capital buffers in good times that can be drawn upon in periods of stress ("Reducing procyclicality and promoting countercyclical buffers").\nMeasures to address procyclicality:\nDampen excess cyclicality of the minimum capital requirement;\nPromoted more forward looking provisions;\nConserved capital to build buffers at individual banks and the banking sector that can be used in stress; and\nAchieved the broader\nmacroprudential\ngoal of protecting the banking sector from periods of excess credit growth.\nRequirement to use long-term data horizons to estimate probabilities of default\ndownturn loss given default\nestimates, recommended in Basel II, to become mandatory\nImproved\ncalibration of the risk functions, which convert loss estimates into regulatory capital requirements.\nBanks must conduct\nstress tests\nthat include scenarios of widening\nyield spreads\nin\nrecessions\n.\nStronger provisioning practices (forward-looking provisioning):\nAdvocates a change in the accounting standards towards an expected loss (EL) approach (usually,\nEL amount\n:=\nLGD\n*\nPD\n*\nEAD\n).\nU.S. modifications\n[\nedit\n]\nIn the U.S., Basel III applies not only to banks but also to all institutions with more than US$50 billion in assets:\n"Risk-based capital and leverage requirements" including first annual capital plans, conduct\nstress tests\n, and\ncapital adequacy\n"including a tier one common risk-based capital ratio greater than 5 percent, under both expected and stressed conditions"\nMarket liquidity\n, first based on the United States' own interagency liquidity risk-management guidance issued in March 2010 that require liquidity\nstress tests\nand set internal quantitative limits.\nThe\nFederal Reserve Board\nitself conducts stress tests annually using three economic and financial market scenarios. Institutions are encouraged to use at least five scenarios reflecting improbable events, and especially those considered impossible by management, but no standards apply to extreme scenarios. Only a summary of the three official Fed scenarios including company-specific information is made public but one or more internal company-run stress tests must be run each year with summaries published.\nSingle-counterparty credit limits to cut credit exposure of a covered financial firm to a single counterparty as a percentage of the firm's regulatory capital. Credit exposure between the largest financial companies is subject to a tighter limit.\nEarly remediation requirements to ensure that financial weaknesses are addressed at an early stage. One or more triggers for remediation—such as capital levels, stress test results, and risk-management weaknesses. Required actions vary based on the severity of the situation, but include restrictions on growth, capital distributions, and executive compensation, as well as capital raising or asset sales.\nTimelines\n[\nedit\n]\nImplementation by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision\n[\nedit\n]\nOn 15 April 2014, the\nBasel Committee on Banking Supervision\n(BCBS) released the final version of its "Supervisory Framework for Measuring and Controlling Large Exposures" (SFLE) that builds on longstanding BCBS guidance on credit exposure concentrations.\nOn 11 March 2016, the\nBasel Committee on Banking Supervision\nreleased the second of three proposals on public disclosure of regulatory metrics and qualitative data by banking institutions. The proposal requires disclosures on market risk to be more granular for both the standardized approach and regulatory approval of internal models.\nIn January 2013, the BCBS extended not only the implementation schedule to 2019, but broadened the definition of liquid assets.\nIn December 2017, the implementation of the market risk framework was delayed from 2019 to 2022.\nImplementation of the\nBasel III: Finalising post-crisis reforms\n, the market risk framework, and the revised Pillar 3 disclosure requirements were extended several times and will be phased-in by 2028.\nCapital requirements timeline\n[\nedit\n]\nLeverage ratio timeline\n[\nedit\n]\nLiquidity requirements timeline\n[\nedit\n]\nCountry-specific timelines of implementation\n[\nedit\n]\nThe\nFederal Reserve\nimplemented the Basel III standards in the U.S., with some modifications, via a proposal first published in 2011.\nFinal rules on the liquidity coverage ratio were published in 2014.\nThe\nimplementing act\nof the Basel III agreements in the\nEuropean Union\nwas\nDirective 2013/36/EU (CRD IV) and Regulation (EU) No. 575/2013\non prudential requirements for credit institutions and investment firms (CRR), which was approved in 2013 and replaced the Capital Requirements Directives (2006/48 and 2006/49).\nImpact\n[\nedit\n]\nProjected macroeconomic impact\n[\nedit\n]\nAn\nOECD\nstudy, released on 17 February 2011, projected that all else equal, the medium-term impact of Basel III implementation on\neconomic growth\nwould be in the range of −0.05% to −0.15% per year due to increased bank lending spreads of 15 to as much as 50\nbasis points\n. The study hypothesized that the effect can be negated by a decrease in monetary policy rates of 30 to 80\nbasis points\nIn June 2024, a study by PwC projected that implemented of the Basel III Endgame requirements would reduce economic growth in the U.S. by 56 basis points via reduced returns to bank shareholders and increased costs to consumers and businesses.\nIn the United States, higher capital requirements resulted in contractions in trading operations and the number of personnel employed on trading floors.\nCriticism\n[\nedit\n]\nAcademics criticized Basel III for continuing to allow large banks to calculate credit risk using internal models and for setting overall minimum capital requirements too low.\nOpaque treatment of all derivatives contracts is also criticized. While institutions have many legitimate ("hedging", "insurance") risk reduction reasons to deal in derivatives, the Basel III accords:\ntreat insurance buyers and sellers equally even though sellers take on more concentrated risks (literally purchasing them) which they are then expected to offset correctly without regulation\ndo not require organizations to investigate correlations of all internal risks they own\ndo not tax or charge institutions for the systematic or aggressive externalization or conflicted marketing of risk—other than requiring an orderly unravelling of derivatives in a crisis and stricter record keeping\nSince derivatives present major unknowns in a crisis these are seen as major failings by some critics\ncausing several to claim that the "\ntoo big to fail\n" status remains with respect to major derivatives dealers who aggressively took on risk of an event they did not believe would happen—but did. As Basel III does not absolutely require extreme scenarios that management flatly rejects to be included in stress testing this remains a vulnerability.\nThe Heritage Foundation\nargued that capitalization regulation is inherently fruitless due to these and similar problems and—despite an opposite ideological view of regulation—agree that "too big to fail" persists.\nBasel III was also criticized as negatively affecting the stability of the financial system by increasing incentives of banks to game the regulatory framework.\nNotwithstanding the enhancement introduced by the Basel III standard, it argued that "markets often fail to discipline large banks to hold prudent capital levels and make sound investment decisions".\nIn comments published in October 2012, the\nAmerican Bankers Association\n, community banks organized in the\nIndependent Community Bankers of America\n, and\nDemocratic Party\nSenators\nBen Cardin\nand\nBarbara Mikulski\nand Representatives\nChris Van Hollen\nand\nElijah Cummings\nof Maryland, said that the Basel III proposals would hurt small banks by increasing their capital holdings dramatically on mortgage and small business loans.\nRobert Reich\n, former\nUnited States Secretary of Labor\nand Professor of Public Policy at the\nUniversity of California, Berkeley\n, has argued that Basel III did not go far enough to regulate banks since, he believed, inadequate regulation was a cause of the\n2008 financial crisis\nand remains an unresolved issue despite the severity of the impact of the\nGreat Recession\n.\nIn 2019,\nMichael Burry\ncriticized Basel III for what he characterizes as "more or less remov[ing]\nprice discovery\nfrom the\ncredit markets\n, meaning risk does not have an accurate pricing mechanism in\ninterest rates\nanymore."\nThe\nInstitute of International Finance\na Washington, D.C.–based, 450-member banking\ntrade association\n, argued against the implementation of the accords, claiming it would hurt banks and economic growth and add to the paper burden and risk inhibition by banks.\nFurther reading\n[\nedit\n]\nCecchetti, Stephen, Jeremy Kress, and Kermit Schoenholtz. 2025.
