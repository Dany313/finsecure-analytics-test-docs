Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroeconomics\n==================================================\n\nStudy of an economy as a whole\nNot to be confused with\nMicroeconomics\n.\nProduction and national income: Macroeconomics takes a big-picture view of the entire economy, including examining the roles of, and relationships between, firms, households and governments, and the different types of markets, such as the financial market and the labour market.\nMacroeconomics\nis a branch of\neconomics\nthat deals with the performance, structure, behavior, and decision-making of an\neconomy\nas a whole.\nThis includes regional, national, and\nglobal economies\n.\nMacroeconomists study aggregate measures of the economy, such as\noutput\nor\ngross domestic product\n(GDP),\nnational income\n,\nunemployment\n,\ninflation\n,\nconsumption\n,\nsaving\n,\ninvestment\n, or trade.\nMacroeconomics is primarily focused on questions that help to understand aggregate variables in relation to long-run\neconomic growth\n.\nMacroeconomics and\nmicroeconomics\nare the two most general\nfields\nin economics.\nGiven macroeconomists focus on large-scale phenomena, or aggregate variables, they differ significantly from microeconomists who study markets and decision making at a smaller level of analysis, such as firms or consumers. This divide is institutionalized in the field of economics, given the differences in both\nmethods\nand outcomes of interest.\nMacroeconomics is further divided into topics based on the time frame of analysis:\nshort-term\nfluctuations over the\nbusiness cycle\n,\nmedium-term\ndeterminants of aggregate variables, such as unemployment, that are unaffected by short-term shocks, and\nlong-term\neconomic growth. The field also includes analysis of\nmonetary\nand\nfiscal policies\n, particularly where they target\nstabilization\nof certain indicators or the rate of economic growth.\nMacroeconomics, as a separate field of research and study, is generally recognized to have begun in 1936, when\nJohn Maynard Keynes\npublished his\nThe General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money\n, but its intellectual predecessors are much older.\nSwedish economist\nKnut Wicksell\nwrote the book\nInterest and Prices\n(1898), translated into English in 1936, is considered to be the pioneer of macroeconomics, while Keynes who introduced\nnational income accounting\nand various related concepts can be said to be the founding father of macroeconomics as a formal discipline. Since\nWorld War II\n, various macroeconomic schools of thought like\nKeynesians\n,\nmonetarists\n,\nnew classical\nand\nnew Keynesian economists\nhave made contributions to the development of the\nmainstream research\n.\nBasic concepts\n[\nedit\n]\nMacroeconomics encompasses a variety of concepts and variables, but above all, the three central macroeconomic variables are output, unemployment, and inflation.\nBesides, the time horizon varies for different types of macroeconomic topics, and this distinction is crucial for many research and policy debates.\nA further important dimension is that of an economy's openness, economic theory distinguishing sharply between\nclosed economies\nand\nopen economies\n.\nIt is usual to distinguish between three time horizons in macroeconomics, each having its own focus on, e.g., the determination of output:\nthe short run (e.g., a few years): Focus is on\nbusiness cycle\nfluctuations and changes in\naggregate demand\n, which often drive them.\nStabilization policies\nlike\nmonetary policy\nor\nfiscal policy\nare relevant in this time frame\nthe medium run (e.g., a decade): Over the medium run, the economy tends to an output level determined by supply factors like the capital stock, the technology level, and the labor force, and unemployment tends to revert to its structural (or "natural") level. These factors move slowly, so that it is a reasonable approximation to take them as given on a medium-term time scale, though\nlabour market policies\nand\ncompetition policy\nare instruments that may influence the economy's structures and hence also the medium-run equilibrium\nthe long run (e.g., a couple of decades or more): On this time scale, emphasis is on the determinants of long-run\neconomic growth\nlike\naccumulation\nof human and physical capital, technological innovations, and\ndemographic changes\n. Potential policies to influence these developments include\neducation reform\n, incentives to change\nsaving rates\n, or incentives to increase\nR&D\nactivities.\nOutput and income\n[\nedit\n]\nNational\noutput\nis the total amount of everything a country produces in a given period of time. Everything that is produced and sold generates an equal amount of income. The total\nnet output\nof the economy is usually measured as GDP. Adding net\nfactor incomes\nfrom abroad to GDP produces\ngross national income\n(GNI), which measures the total income of all residents in the economy. In most countries, the difference between GDP and GNI is modest so that GDP can be treated as the total income of all inhabitants as well; however, in some countries, e.g., those with very large\nnet foreign assets\n(or debt), the difference may be considerable.\nAdvances in technology, accumulation of machinery and other\ncapital\n, and better education and\nhuman capital\n, are all factors that lead to increased economic output over time. However, output does not always increase consistently over time.\nBusiness cycles\ncan cause short-term drops in output called\nrecessions\n. Economists look for\nmacroeconomic policies\nthat prevent economies from slipping into either\nrecessions\nor\noverheating\nand that lead to higher\nproductivity\nlevels and\nstandards of living\n.\nUnemployment\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nUnemployment\nA chart using US data showing the relationship between economic growth and unemployment expressed by\nOkun's law\n. The relationship demonstrates cyclical unemployment. High short-run GDP growth leads to a lower unemployment rate.\nThe amount of\nunemployment\nin an economy is measured by the unemployment rate, i.e., the percentage of persons in the\nlabor force\nwho do not have a job, but who are actively looking for one. People who are retired, pursuing education, or\ndiscouraged from seeking work\ndue to a lack of job prospects are not part of the labor force and, consequently, are not counted as unemployed either.\nUnemployment has a short-run cyclical component which depends on the business cycle, and a more permanent structural component, which can be loosely thought of as the average unemployment rate in an economy over extended periods,\nand which is often termed the\nnatural\nor structural\nrate of unemployment.\nCyclical unemployment\noccurs when growth stagnates.\nOkun's law\nrepresents the empirical relationship between unemployment and short-run GDP growth.\nThe original version of Okun's law states that a 3% increase in output would lead to a 1% decrease in unemployment.\nThe structural or\nnatural rate of unemployment\nis the level of unemployment that will occur in a medium-run equilibrium, i.e., a situation with a cyclical unemployment rate of zero. There may be several reasons why there is some positive unemployment level even in a cyclically neutral situation, which all have their foundation in some\nmarket failure\n:\nSearch unemployment\n(also called frictional unemployment) occurs when workers and firms are heterogeneous, and there is\nimperfect information\n, generally causing a time-consuming\nsearch and matching process\nwhen filling a job vacancy in a firm, during which the prospective worker will often be unemployed.\nSectoral shifts and other reasons for a changed demand from firms for workers with particular skills and characteristics, which occur continually in a changing economy, may also cause more search unemployment because of increased mismatch.\nEfficiency wage\nmodels are labor market models in which firms choose not to lower wages to the level where supply equals demand because the lower wages would lower employees' efficiency levels\nTrade unions\n, which are important actors in the labor market in some countries, may exercise\nmarket power\nto keep wages over the market-clearing level for the benefit of their members, even at the cost of some unemployment\nLegal\nminimum wages\nmay prevent the wage from falling to a market-clearing level, causing unemployment among low-skilled (and low-paid) workers.\nIn the case of employers having some\nmonopsony power\n, however, employment effects may have the opposite sign.\nInflation and deflation\n[\nedit\n]\nChanges in the ten-year moving averages of price level and growth in money supply (using the measure of M2, the supply of hard currency and money held in most types of bank accounts) in the US from 1880 to 2016. Over the long run, the two series show a clear positive correlation.\nA general price increase across the entire economy is called\ninflation\n. When prices decrease, there is\ndeflation\n. Economists measure these changes in prices with\nprice indexes\n. Inflation will rise when an economy overheats and grows too quickly. Similarly, a declining economy can lead to decreasing inflation and, in some cases, deflation.\nChanges in the inflation rate may result from several factors. Too much\naggregate demand\nin the economy will cause an\noverheating\n, raising inflation rates via the\nPhillips curve\nbecause of a tight labor market leading to large wage increases which will be\ntransmitted\nto increases in the price of the products of employers. Too little aggregate demand will have the opposite effect of creating more unemployment and lower wages, thereby decreasing inflation. Aggregate\nsupply shocks\nwill also affect inflation, e.g., the\noil crises of the 1970s\nand the\n2021–2023 global energy crisis\n. Changes in inflation may also impact the formation of\ninflation expectations\n, creating a self-fulfilling inflationary or deflationary spiral.\nThe\nmonetarist\nquantity theory of money\nholds that changes in the price level are directly caused by changes in the\nmoney supply\n.\nCentral bankers\nconducting\nmonetary policy\nusually have as a main priority to avoid too high inflation, typically by adjusting interest rates. High inflation, as well as deflation, can lead to increased uncertainty and other negative consequences, particularly when inflation (or deflation) is unexpected. Consequently, most central banks aim for a positive, but stable and not very high inflation level.\nWhereas there is empirical evidence that there is a long-run positive\ncorrelation\nbetween the growth rate of the money stock and the rate of inflation, the quantity theory has proved unreliable in the short- and medium-run time horizon relevant to monetary policy and is abandoned as a practical guideline by most central banks today.\nGDP Measurement\n[\nedit\n]\nEquation Using Expenditure Approach\n[\nedit\n]\nOne way to capture GDP, or total net output, is the expenditure method. Conversely, a GDP deflator below 100 indicates deflation.\nMeasuring the supply of money\n[\nedit\n]\nTwo common ways of determining the\ntotal money supply\nin an economy are M1 and M2. M2 consists of M1 plus a few other things. M1 is money that is\nliquid.\nLiquid refers to a financial asset that can be quickly converted into cash without significant loss of value. This obviously includes cash, coins, checking account deposits, and similar items. M2, however, includes time deposits, saving accounts, and money market\nmutual funds\n, which are not as liquid, in its measurement. It is important to understand the money supply, as it affects interest rates and plays a central role in monetary policy.\nThe\nmoney multiplier equation\nshows how a bank can expand the money supply by taking in deposits and making loans. The\nmoney supply reserve multiplier equation\nis:\nMoney multiplier\n= 1 /\nreserve requirement ratio\nThe reserve requirement in this equation represents the proportion of deposits that the bank must keep in reserve to meet customer withdrawals. That proportion of money is based on the deposits made at the bank. So, if the reserve requirement is .20 (20%), then the money multiplier is five. This means that a $5 deposit would lead to a $25 increase in the money supply. This is because of the cycle in which the bank keeps a portion of the deposit (in our example, 20%) and lends out the remainder each time. These new spendable bank deposits are counted in the money supply even though the amount of physical currency did not change. So, while the physical amount of currency would still be $5, the amount of spendable money would be $25.\nOpen economies\n[\nedit\n]\nOpen economy\nmacroeconomics deals with the consequences of\ninternational trade\nin\ngoods\n,\nfinancial assets\n, and possibly\nfactor markets\nsuch as\nlabor migration\nand the international relocation of firms (physical capital). It explores what determines\nimport\n,\nexport\n, the\nbalance of trade\n, and over longer horizons, the accumulation of\nnet foreign assets\n. An important topic is the role of\nexchange rates\nand the pros and cons of maintaining a\nfixed exchange rate\nsystem or even a\ncurrency union\nlike the\nEconomic and Monetary Union of the European Union\n, drawing on the research literature on\noptimum currency areas\n.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nHistory of macroeconomic thought\nJohn Maynard Keynes\nis considered the initiator of macroeconomics when he published his work\nThe General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money\nin 1936.\nMacroeconomics, as a separate field of research and study, is generally recognized to have begun with the publication of\nJohn Maynard Keynes\n's\nThe General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money\nin 1936.\nThe terms "macrodynamics" and "macroanalysis" were introduced by\nRagnar Frisch\nin 1933, and Lawrence Klein in 1946 used the word "macroeconomics" itself in a journal title in 1946.\nBut naturally, several of the themes which are central to macroeconomic research had been discussed by thoughtful economists and other writers long before 1936.\nBefore Keynes\n[\nedit\n]\nIn particular, macroeconomic questions before Keynes were the topic of the two long-standing traditions of\nbusiness cycle theory\nand\nmonetary theory\n.\nWilliam Stanley Jevons\nwas one of the pioneers of the first tradition, whereas the\nquantity theory of money\n, labelled the oldest surviving theory in economics, as an example of the second was described already in the 16th century by\nMartín de Azpilcueta\nand later discussed by personalities like\nJohn Locke\nand\nDavid Hume\n. In the first decades of the 20th century, monetary theory was dominated by the eminent economists\nAlfred Marshall\n,\nKnut Wicksell\n, and\nIrving Fisher\n.\nKeynes and Keynesian economics\n[\nedit\n]\nWhen the Great Depression struck, the reigning economists had difficulty explaining how goods could go unsold and workers could be left unemployed. In the prevailing\nneoclassical economics\nparadigm, prices and wages would fall until the market cleared, with all goods and labor sold. Keynes, in his main work, the\nGeneral Theory\n, initiated what is known as the\nKeynesian Revolution\n. He offered a new interpretation of events and a whole intellectual framework - a novel theory of economics that explained why markets might not clear, which would evolve into a school of thought known as\nKeynesian economics\n, also called Keynesianism or Keynesian theory.\nIn Keynes' theory,\naggregate demand\n- by Keynes called "effective demand" - was key to determining output. Even if Keynes conceded that output might eventually return to a medium-run equilibrium (or "potential") level, the process would be slow at best. Keynes coined the term\nliquidity preference\n(his preferred name for what is also known as\nmoney demand\n) and explained how monetary policy might affect aggregate demand, at the same time offering clear policy recommendations for an active role of fiscal policy in stabilizing aggregate demand and hence output and employment. In addition, he explained how the\nmultiplier effect\nwould magnify a small decrease in consumption or investment, causing declines throughout the economy, and noted the role that uncertainty and\nanimal spirits\ncan play.\nThe generation following Keynes combined the macroeconomics of the\nGeneral Theory\nwith neoclassical microeconomics to create the\nneoclassical synthesis\n. By the 1950s, most economists had accepted the synthesis view of the macroeconomy.\nEconomists like\nPaul Samuelson\n,\nFranco Modigliani\n,\nJames Tobin\n, and\nRobert Solow\ndeveloped formal Keynesian models and contributed formal theories of consumption, investment, and money demand that fleshed out the Keynesian framework.\nMonetarism\n[\nedit\n]\nMilton Friedman\nupdated the quantity theory of money to include a role for money demand. He argued that the role of money in the economy was sufficient to explain the\nGreat Depression\n, and that aggregate-demand-oriented explanations were unnecessary. Friedman also argued that monetary policy was more effective than fiscal policy; however, Friedman doubted the government's ability to "fine-tune" the economy with monetary policy. He generally favored a policy of steady growth in the money supply rather than frequent intervention.\nFriedman also challenged the original simple\nPhillips curve\nrelationship between inflation and unemployment. Friedman and\nEdmund Phelps\n(who was not a monetarist) proposed an "augmented" version of the Phillips curve that excluded the possibility of a stable, long-run tradeoff between inflation and unemployment.\nWhen the\noil shocks\nof the 1970s created a high unemployment and high inflation, Friedman and Phelps were vindicated.\nMonetarism\nwas particularly influential in the early 1980s, but fell out of favor when central banks found the results disappointing when trying to target money supply instead of interest rates as monetarists recommended, concluding that the relationships between money growth, inflation, and real GDP growth are too unstable to be useful in practical monetary policy making.\nNew classical economics\n[\nedit\n]\nNew classical macroeconomics\nfurther challenged the Keynesian school. A central development in new classical thought came with\nRobert Lucas\n's introduction of\nrational expectations\ninto macroeconomics. Before Lucas, economists had generally used\nadaptive expectations\n, in which agents were assumed to look at the recent past to form expectations about the future. Under rational expectations, agents are assumed to be more sophisticated.\nConsumers will not simply assume a 2% inflation rate just because that has been the average the past few years; they will look at current monetary policy and economic conditions to make an informed forecast. In the new classical models with rational expectations, monetary policy only had a limited impact.\nLucas also made an\ninfluential critique\nof Keynesian empirical models. He argued that forecasting models based on empirical relationships would continue to produce the same predictions even as the underlying data-generating model changed. He advocated models based on fundamental economic theory (i.e., having an explicit\nmicroeconomic foundation\n) that would, in principle, be structurally accurate as economies changed.\nFollowing Lucas's critique, new classical economists, led by\nEdward C. Prescott\nand\nFinn E. Kydland\n, developed\nreal business cycle\n(RBC) models of the macroeconomy. RBC models were created by combining fundamental equations from neo-classical microeconomics to make quantitative models. To generate macroeconomic fluctuations, RBC models explained recessions and unemployment as changes in technology rather than in the markets for goods or money. Critics of RBC models argue that technological changes, which typically diffuse slowly throughout the economy, could hardly generate the large short-run output fluctuations that we observe. In addition, there is strong empirical evidence that monetary policy does affect real economic activity, and the idea that technological regress can explain recent recessions seems implausible.\nDespite criticism of the realism in the RBC models, they have been very influential in\neconomic methodology\nby providing the first examples of\ngeneral equilibrium\nmodels based on\nmicroeconomic foundations\nand a specification of underlying shocks that aim to explain the main features of macroeconomic fluctuations, not only qualitatively, but also quantitatively. In this way, they were forerunners of the later DSGE models.\nNew Keynesian response\n[\nedit\n]\nNew Keynesian\neconomists responded to the new classical school by adopting rational expectations and focusing on developing microfounded models immune to the Lucas critique. Like classical models, new classical models assumed that prices would adjust perfectly and that monetary policy would only lead to price changes. New Keynesian models investigated sources of\nsticky prices and wages\ndue to\nimperfect competition\n,\nwhich would not adjust, allowing monetary policy to impact quantities instead of prices.\nStanley Fischer\nand\nJohn B. Taylor\nproduced early work in this area by showing that monetary policy could be effective even in rational-expectations models when contracts locked in workers' wages. Other new Keynesian economists, including\nOlivier Blanchard\n,\nJanet Yellen\n,\nJulio Rotemberg\n,\nGreg Mankiw\n,\nDavid Romer\n, and\nMichael Woodford\n, expanded on this work and demonstrated other cases where various market imperfections caused inflexible prices and wages leading in turn to monetary and fiscal policy having real effects. Other researchers focused on imperfections in labor markets, developing models of\nefficiency wages\nor\nsearch and matching\n(SAM) models, or imperfections in credit markets like\nBen Bernanke\n.\nBy the late 1990s, economists had reached a rough consensus.\nThe market imperfections and nominal rigidities of new Keynesian theory were combined with rational expectations and the RBC methodology to produce a new and popular type of models called\ndynamic stochastic general equilibrium\n(DSGE) models. The fusion of elements from different schools of thought has been dubbed the\nnew neoclassical synthesis\n.\nThese models are now used by many central banks and are a core part of contemporary macroeconomics.\n2008 financial crisis\n[\nedit\n]\nThe 2008 financial crisis and the subsequent\nGreat Recession\nwere the first events to lead to a major reassessment of macroeconomics. This field had generally neglected the specific role of\nfinancial institutions\nin the economy. After the crisis, macroeconomic researchers have turned their attention to several new directions:\nthe financial system and the nature of macro-financial linkages and frictions, studying leverage, liquidity and complexity problems in the financial sector, the use of\nmacroprudential tools\nand the dangers of an\nunsustainable\npublic debt,\nincreased emphasis on\nempirical work\nas part of the so-called\ncredibility revolution\nin economics, using improved methods to distinguish between\ncorrelation and causality\nto improve future policy discussions,\ninterest in understanding the importance of\nheterogeneity\namong the economic agents, leading among other examples to the construction of heterogeneous agent new Keynesian models (\nHANK models\n), which may potentially also improve understanding of the impact of macroeconomics on the\nincome distribution\n,\nunderstanding the implications of integrating the findings of the increasingly useful\nbehavioral economics\nliterature into macroeconomics\nand behavioral finance.\nGrowth models\n[\nedit\n]\nResearch in the economics of the determinants behind long-run\neconomic growth\nhas followed its own course.\nThe\nHarrod-Domar\nmodel from the 1940s attempted to build a long-run growth model inspired by Keynesian demand-driven considerations.\nThe\nSolow–Swan model\nworked out by\nRobert Solow\nand, independently,\nTrevor Swan\nin the 1950s achieved more long-lasting success, however, and is still today a common textbook model for explaining economic growth in the long-run.\nThe model operates with a\nproduction function\nwhere national output is the product of two inputs: capital and labor. The Solow model assumes that labor and capital are used at constant rates without the fluctuations in unemployment and capital utilization commonly seen in business cycles.\nIn this model, increases in output, i.e., economic growth, can only occur because of an increase in the capital stock, a larger population, or technological advancements that lead to higher productivity (\ntotal factor productivity\n). An increase in the savings rate leads to a temporary increase in output as the economy builds more capital. However, eventually the depreciation rate will limit capital expansion: savings will be used to replace depreciated capital, leaving none to pay for additional expansion. Solow's model suggests that economic growth, measured by output per capita, depends solely on technological advances that enhance productivity.\nThe Solow model can be interpreted as a special case of the more general\nRamsey growth model\n, where households' savings rates are not constant as in the Solow model, but derived from an explicit intertemporal\nutility function\n.\nIn the 1980s and 1990s,\nendogenous growth theory\narose to challenge the neoclassical growth theory of Ramsey and Solow. The earliest work tackling the former question of scarcity was presented in\nWilliam Jevons\n's 1865 book,\nThe Coal Question\n, in which he postulates a paradox whereby productivity improvements may accelerate scarcity in a growing economy.\nDuring the oil crises of the 1970s, when scarcity problems of natural resources were high on the public agenda, economists like\nJoseph Stiglitz\nand\nRobert Solow\nintroduced\nnon-renewable resources\ninto neoclassical growth models to study the possibilities of maintaining growth in living standards under these conditions.\nIn 2006, economist\nNicholas Stern\npublished the first comprehensive analysis of the global damages to the economy as a result of projected climate change scenarios in\nThe Stern Review.\nThis paper was the first to develop a model to assess the impact of slow, on-set climate change on GDP. More recently, the issue of\nclimate change\nand the possibilities of a\nsustainable development\nare examined in so-called\nintegrated assessment models\n(IAMs), pioneered by\nWilliam Nordhaus\n.\nThe use of IAMs is now widespread in\nclimate economics\n, and is used to get both disaggregated, regional assessment of damages, or global damages, under different policy scenarios.\nMore theoretically, macroeconomic models in\nenvironmental economics\nmodel a system in which production takes natural resources such as land, water, or energy as inputs. In this case, one example of an integrated model would replace the\ncircular flow of income\ndiagram may be replaced by a more complex flow diagram reflecting the input of solar energy, which sustains natural inputs and\nenvironmental services\nwhich are then used as units of\nproduction\n, such as the early work of\nHerman Daly\n.\nOnce consumed, natural inputs pass out of the economy as pollution and waste. The potential of an environment to provide services and materials is referred to as an "environment's source function", and this function is depleted as resources are consumed or pollution contaminates the resources. This discipline includes the aforementioned\necological economics\n, modern monetary theory,\nMarxian economics\n, and other schools of thought that build on theoretical traditions beyond neoclassical or Keynesian works. For example, heterodox economists see the driver of economic instability as price flexibility rather than\nprice stickiness\n, and therefore develop models with different assumptions and structures.\nMacroeconomic policy\n[\nedit\n]\nThe division into various time frames of macroeconomic research leads to a parallel division of macroeconomic policies into short-run policies aimed at mitigating the harmful consequences of business cycles (known as\nstabilization policy\n) and medium- and long-run policies targeted at improving the structural levels of macroeconomic variables.\nStabilization policy is usually implemented through two sets of tools: fiscal and monetary policy. Both forms of policy are used to\nstabilize the economy\n, i.e. limiting the effects of the business cycle by conducting expansive policy when the economy is in a\nrecession\nor contractive policy in the case of\noverheating\n.\nStructural policies may include labour market policies that aim to change the structural unemployment rate, or policies that affect long-run propensities to save, invest, or engage in education, research, and development.\nMonetary policy\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nMonetary policy\nCentral banks\nconduct monetary policy mainly by adjusting short-term\ninterest rates\n.\nThe actual method through which the interest rate is changed differs from central bank to central bank, but typically the implementation happens either directly via administratively changing the central bank's own offered interest rates or indirectly via\nopen market operations\n.\nVia the\nmonetary transmission mechanism\n, interest rate changes affect\ninvestment\n,\nconsumption\n,\nasset prices\nlike listed companies' shares prices and\nhouse prices\n, and through\nexchange rate\nreactions\nexport\nand\nimport\n. In this way,\naggregate demand\n,\nemployment\n, and ultimately inflation are affected.\nExpansionary monetary policy lowers interest rates, increasing economic activity, whereas contractionary monetary policy raises interest rates. In a fixed exchange rate system, interest rate decisions, together with direct intervention by central banks in exchange rate dynamics, are major tools for controlling the exchange rate.\nIn developed countries, most central banks follow\ninflation targeting\n, focusing on keeping medium-term inflation close to an explicit target, say 2%, or within an explicit range. This includes the\nFederal Reserve\nand the\nEuropean Central Bank\n, which are generally considered to follow a strategy very close to inflation targeting, even though they do not officially label themselves as inflation targeters.\nIn practice, an official inflation targeting often leaves room for the central bank to also help stabilize\noutput\nand employment, a strategy known as "flexible inflation targeting".\nMost\nemerging economies\nfocus their monetary policy on maintaining a\nfixed exchange rate\nregime, aligning their currency with one or more foreign currencies, typically the\nUS dollar\nor the\neuro\n.\nConventional monetary policy can be ineffective in situations such as a\nliquidity trap\n. When nominal interest rates are near zero, central banks cannot loosen monetary policy through conventional means. In that situation, they may use unconventional monetary policy such as\nquantitative easing\nto help stabilize output. Quantity easing can be implemented by buying not only government bonds, but also other assets such as corporate bonds, stocks, and other securities. This allows lower interest rates for a broader class of assets beyond government bonds. A similar strategy is to lower long-term interest rates by buying long-term bonds and selling short-term bonds to create a flat\nyield curve\n, known in the US as\nOperation Twist\n.\nFiscal policy\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nFiscal policy\nFiscal policy is the use of the government's revenue (\ntaxes\n) and\nexpenditure\nas instruments to influence the economy.\nFor example, if the economy is producing less than\npotential output\n, government spending can be used to employ idle resources and boost output, or taxes could be lowered to boost private consumption, which has a similar effect. Government spending or tax cuts do not have to make up for the entire\noutput gap\n. There is a\nmultiplier effect\nthat affects the impact of government spending. For instance, when the government pays for a bridge, the project not only adds the bridge's value to output but also enables bridge workers to increase their consumption and investment, helping close the output gap.\nThe effects of fiscal policy can be limited by partial or full\ncrowding out\n. When the government undertakes spending projects, it limits the resources available to the\nprivate sector\n. Full crowding out occurs in the extreme case when government spending replaces private-sector output rather than adding to the economy's output. A crowding-out effect may also occur if government spending raises interest rates, thereby limiting investment.\nSome fiscal policy is implemented through\nautomatic stabilizers\nwithout any active decisions by politicians. Automatic stabilizers do not suffer from the policy lags of\ndiscretionary fiscal policy\n. Automatic stabilizers use conventional fiscal mechanisms, but take effect as soon as the economy takes a downturn: spending on unemployment benefits automatically increases when unemployment rises, and\ntax revenue\ndecreases, which shelters private income and consumption from part of the fall in market income.\nComparison of fiscal and monetary policy\n[\nedit\n]\nThere is a consensus that both monetary and fiscal instruments may affect demand and activity in the short run (i.e., over the business cycle).\nEconomists usually favor monetary over fiscal policy to mitigate moderate fluctuations, however, because it has two major advantages.\nFirst, monetary policy is generally implemented by independent central banks instead of the political institutions that control fiscal policy. Independent central banks are less likely to be subject to political pressure to pursue overly expansionary policies. Second, monetary policy may suffer shorter\ninside lags\nand\noutside lags\nthan fiscal policy.\nThere are some exceptions, however: Firstly, in the case of a major shock, monetary stabilization policy may not be sufficient and should be supplemented by active fiscal stabilization.\nSecondly, in the case of a very low interest level, the economy may be in a\nliquidity trap\nin which monetary policy becomes ineffective, which makes fiscal policy the more potent tool to stabilize the economy.\nThirdly, in regimes where monetary policy is tied to fulfilling other targets, in particular\nfixed exchange rate\nregimes, the central bank cannot simultaneously adjust its interest rates to mitigate domestic business cycle fluctuations, making fiscal policy the only usable tool for such countries.\nMacroeconomic models\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nMacroeconomic model\nMacroeconomic teaching, research, and informed debates normally evolve around formal (\ndiagrammatic\nor\nequational\n)\nmacroeconomic models\nto clarify assumptions and show their consequences precisely. Models include simple theoretical models, often containing only a few equations, used in teaching and research to highlight key basic principles, and larger applied quantitative models used by e.g. governments, central banks, think tanks and international organisations to predict effects of changes in economic policy or other\nexogenous factors\nor as a basis for making\neconomic forecasting\n.\nWell-known specific theoretical models include short-term models like the\nKeynesian cross\n, the\nIS–LM model\nand the\nMundell–Fleming model\n, medium-term models like the\nAD–AS model\n, building upon a\nPhillips curve\n, and long-term growth models like the\nSolow–Swan\nmodel, the\nRamsey–Cass–Koopmans model\nand\nPeter Diamond\n's\noverlapping generations model\n. Quantitative models include early\nlarge-scale macroeconometric model\n, the new classical\nreal business cycle models\n, microfounded\ncomputable general equilibrium\n(CGE) models used for medium-term (structural) questions like international trade or tax reforms,\nDynamic stochastic general equilibrium\n(DSGE) models used to analyze business cycles, not least in many central banks, or\nintegrated assessment\nmodels like\nDICE\n.\nSpecific models\n[\nedit\n]\nIS–LM model\n[\nedit\n]\nIn this example of a traditional IS–LM chart, the IS curve moves to the right, causing higher interest rates (i) and expansion in the "real" economy (real GDP, or Y).\nThe\nIS–LM\nmodel, invented by\nJohn Hicks\nin 1936, gives the underpinnings of aggregate demand (itself discussed below). It answers the question "At any given price level, what is the quantity of goods demanded?" The graphic model shows combinations of interest rates and output that ensure equilibrium in both the goods and money markets under the model's assumptions.\nThe goods market is modeled as giving equality between investment and public and private saving (IS), and the money market is modeled as giving equilibrium between the\nmoney supply\nand\nliquidity preference\n(equivalent to money demand).\nThe IS curve consists of the points (combinations of income and interest rate) at which, for a given interest rate, investment equals public and private saving, given output.\nThe IS curve is downward sloping because output and the interest rate have an inverse relationship in the goods market: as output increases, more income is saved, which means interest rates must be lower to spur enough investment to match saving.\nThe traditional LM curve is upward-sloping because the interest rate and output are positively related in the money market. As income (identical to output in a closed economy) increases, the demand for money rises, leading to a higher interest rate to offset the incipient rise in money demand.\nThe IS-LM model is often used in elementary textbooks to demonstrate the effects of monetary and fiscal policy, though it ignores many complexities of most modern macroeconomic models.\nA problem related to the LM curve is that modern central banks largely ignore the money supply in determining policy, contrary to the model's basic assumptions.\nIn some modern textbooks, consequently, the traditional IS-LM model has been modified by replacing the traditional LM curve with an assumption that the central bank determines the interest rate of the economy directly.\nAD-AS model\n[\nedit\n]\nA traditional AD–AS diagram showing a shift in AD, and the AS curve becoming inelastic beyond potential output\nThe\nAD–AS model\nis a common textbook model for explaining the macroeconomy.\nThe original version of the model shows the price level and level of real output given the equilibrium in\naggregate demand\nand\naggregate supply\n. The aggregate demand curve's downward slope means that more output is demanded at lower price levels.\nThe downward slope can be explained as the result of three effects: the\nPigou or real balance effect\n, which states that as real prices fall, real wealth increases, resulting in higher consumer demand of goods; the\nKeynes or interest rate effect\n, which states that as prices fall, the demand for money decreases, causing interest rates to decline and borrowing for investment and consumption to increase; and the net export effect, which states that as prices rise, domestic goods become comparatively more expensive to foreign consumers, leading to a decline in exports.\nIn many representations of the AD–AS model, the aggregate supply curve is horizontal at low levels of output; it becomes inelastic near the point of\npotential output\n, which corresponds with\nfull employment\n.\nSince the economy cannot produce beyond the potential output, any AD expansion will lead to higher price levels instead of higher output.\nIn modern textbooks, the AD–AS model is often presented slightly differently, however, in a diagram showing not the price level, but the inflation rate along the vertical axis,\nmaking it easier to relate the diagram to real-world policy discussions.\nIn this framework, the AD curve is downward sloping because higher inflation will cause the central bank, which is assumed to follow an\ninflation target\n, to raise the interest rate, which will dampen economic activity, hence reducing output. The AS curve is upward-sloping, consistent with a standard modern\nPhillips curve\nview, in which higher economic activity lowers unemployment, leading to higher wage growth and, in turn, higher inflation.\nSee also\n[\nedit\n]\nBusiness and economics portal\nMicroeconomics\nBusiness cycle accounting\nEconomic development\nGrowth accounting\nReferences\n[\nedit\n]\n↑\nSamuelson, Robert\n(2020).\n"Goodbye, readers, and good luck — you'll need it"\n.\nThe Washington Post\n.\nThis article was an opinion piece expressing despondency in the field shortly before his retirement, but it is still a good summary.\n↑\n"What is macroeconomics?"\n.\nBoard of Governors of the Federal Reserve System\n.
