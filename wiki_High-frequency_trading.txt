Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-frequency_trading\n==================================================\n\nType of algorithmic trading\nHigh-frequency trading\n(\nHFT\n) is a type of\nalgorithmic\nautomated trading system\nin\nfinance\ncharacterized by high speeds, high turnover rates, and high order-to-trade ratios that leverages high-frequency financial data and electronic trading tools.\nWhile there is no single definition of HFT, among its key attributes are highly sophisticated algorithms, co-location, and very short-term investment horizons in trading\nsecurities\n.\nHFT uses proprietary trading strategies carried out by computers to move in and out of\npositions\nin seconds or fractions of a second.\nIn 2016, HFT on average initiated 10–40% of trading volume in equities, and 10–15% of volume in foreign exchange and commodities.\nHigh-frequency traders move in and out of short-term positions at high volumes and high speeds aiming to capture sometimes a fraction of a cent in profit on every trade.\nHFT firms do not consume significant amounts of capital, accumulate positions or hold their portfolios overnight.\nAs a result, HFT has a potential\nSharpe ratio\n(a measure of reward to risk) tens of times higher than traditional\nbuy-and-hold\nstrategies.\nHigh-frequency traders typically compete against other HFTs, rather than long-term investors.\nHFT firms compensate for low profit margins by executing extremely high volumes of trades, often numbering in the millions.\nA substantial body of research argues that HFT and electronic trading pose new types of challenges to the financial system.\nAlgorithmic and high-frequency traders were both found to have contributed to\nvolatility\nin the\nFlash Crash of May 6, 2010\n, when high-frequency liquidity providers rapidly withdrew from the market.\nSeveral European countries have proposed curtailing or banning HFT due to concerns about volatility.\nOther complaints against HFT include the argument that some HFT firms scrape profits from investors when\nindex funds\nrebalance their portfolios.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nThe rapid-fire computer-based HFT developed gradually since 1983 after NASDAQ introduced a purely electronic form of trading.\nAt the turn of the 21st century, HFT trades had an execution time of several seconds, whereas by 2010 this had decreased to\nmilli\n- and even\nmicroseconds\n.\nAt that time, high-frequency trading was still a little-known topic outside the financial sector, with an article published by the\nNew York Times\nin July 2009 being one of the first to bring the subject to the public's attention.\nOn September 2, 2013,\nItaly\nbecame the world's first country to introduce a tax specifically targeted at HFT, charging a levy of 0.02% on equity transactions lasting less than 0.5 seconds.\nMarket growth\n[\nedit\n]\nIn the early 2000s, high-frequency trading still accounted for fewer than 10% of equity orders, but this proportion was soon to begin rapid growth. According to data from the NYSE, trading volume grew by about 164% between 2005 and 2009 for which high-frequency trading might be accounted.\nAs of the first quarter in 2009, total assets under management for hedge funds with high-frequency trading strategies were $141\nbillion, down about 21% from their peak before the worst of the\n2008 financial crisis\n,\nalthough most of the largest HFTs are actually LLCs owned by a small number of investors.  The high-frequency strategy was first made popular by\nRenaissance Technologies\nwho use both HFT and quantitative aspects in their trading. Many high-frequency firms are\nmarket makers\nand provide liquidity to the market which lowers volatility and helps narrow\nbid–offer spreads\n, making trading and investing cheaper for other market participants.\nMarket share\n[\nedit\n]\nIn the United States in 2009, high-frequency trading firms represented 2% of the approximately 20,000 firms operating today, but accounted for 73% of all equity order volume.\nThe major U.S. high-frequency trading firms include\nJane Street Capital\n,\nHudson River Trading\n,\nSusquehanna International Group\n,\nXTX Markets\n,\nVirtu Financial\n,\nTower Research Capital\n,\nIMC\n,\nTradebot\n,\nAkuna Capital\nand\nCitadel LLC\n.\nThe\nBank of England\nestimates similar percentages for the 2010 UK market share, also suggesting that in Europe HFT accounts for about 40% of equity orders volume and for Asia about 5–10%, with potential for rapid growth.\nBy value, HFT was estimated in 2010 by consultancy\nTabb Group\nto make up 56% of equity trades in the US and 38% in Europe.\nAs HFT strategies become more widely used, it can be more difficult to deploy them profitably. According to an estimate from Frederi Viens of\nPurdue University\n, profits from HFT in the U.S. has been declining from an estimated peak of $5bn in 2009, to about $1.25bn in 2012.\nThough the percentage of volume attributed to HFT has fallen in the\nequity markets\n, it has remained prevalent in the\nfutures markets\n. According to a study in 2010 by Aite Group, about a quarter of major global futures volume came from professional high-frequency traders.\nIn 2012, according to a study by the TABB Group, HFT accounted for more than 60 percent of all futures market volume in 2012 on U.S. exchanges.\nStrategies\n[\nedit\n]\nHigh-frequency trading is\nquantitative trading\nthat is characterized by short portfolio holding periods.\nAll portfolio-allocation decisions are made by computerized quantitative models. The success of high-frequency trading strategies is largely driven by their ability to simultaneously process large volumes of information, something ordinary human traders cannot do. Specific algorithms are closely guarded by their owners.   Many practical algorithms are in fact quite simple arbitrages which could previously have been performed at lower frequency—competition tends to occur through who can execute them the fastest rather than who can create new breakthrough algorithms.\nThe common types of high-frequency trading include several types of market-making, event arbitrage, statistical arbitrage, and latency arbitrage.\nMost high-frequency trading strategies are not fraudulent, but instead exploit minute deviations from market equilibrium.\nMarket making\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nMarket maker\nAccording to SEC:\nA "market maker" is a firm that stands ready to buy and sell a particular stock on a regular and continuous basis at a publicly quoted price. You'll most often hear about market makers in the context of the\nNasdaq\nor other "over the counter" (OTC) markets. Market makers that stand ready to buy and sell stocks listed on an exchange, such as the\nNew York Stock Exchange\n, are called "third market makers". Many OTC stocks have more than one market-maker.

Market-makers generally must be ready to buy and sell at least 100 shares of a stock they make a market in. As a result, a large order from an investor may have to be filled by a number of market-makers at potentially different prices.\nThere can be a significant overlap between a "market maker" and "HFT firm".  HFT firms characterize their business as "Market making" – a set of high-frequency trading strategies that involve placing a limit order to sell (or offer) or a buy limit order (or bid) in order to earn the bid-ask spread. By doing so, market makers provide a counterpart to incoming market orders. Although the role of market maker was traditionally fulfilled by specialist firms, this class of strategy is now implemented by a large range of investors, thanks to wide adoption of\ndirect market access\n. As pointed out by empirical studies,\nthis renewed competition among liquidity providers causes reduced effective market spreads, and therefore reduced indirect costs for final investors."  A crucial distinction is that true market makers don't exit the market at their discretion and are committed not to, where HFT firms are under no similar commitment.\nSome high-frequency trading firms use market making as their primary strategy.\nAutomated Trading Desk (ATD), which was bought by\nCitigroup\nin July 2007, has been an active market maker, accounting for about 6% of total volume on both the NASDAQ and the New York Stock Exchange.\nIn May 2016,\nCitadel LLC\nbought assets of ATD from Citigroup. Building up market making strategies typically involves precise modeling of the target\nmarket microstructure\ntogether with\nstochastic control\ntechniques.\nThese strategies appear intimately related to the entry of new electronic venues. Academic study of Chi-X's entry into the European equity market reveals that its launch coincided with a large HFT that made markets using both the incumbent market,\nNYSE\n-\nEuronext\n, and the new market, Chi-X. The study shows that the new market provided ideal conditions for HFT market-making, low fees (i.e., rebates for quotes that led to execution) and a fast system, yet the HFT was equally active in the incumbent market to offload nonzero positions. New market entry and HFT arrival are further shown to coincide with a significant improvement in liquidity supply.\nQuote stuffing\n[\nedit\n]\nFurther information:\nQuote stuffing\nQuote stuffing is a form of abusive market manipulation that has been employed by high-frequency traders (HFT) and is subject to disciplinary action. It involves quickly entering and withdrawing a large number of orders in an attempt to flood the market, creating confusion in the market and trading opportunities for high-frequency traders.\nTicker tape trading\n[\nedit\n]\nFor other uses, see\nTicker tape (disambiguation)\n.\nMuch information happens to be unwittingly embedded in market data, such as quotes and volumes. By observing a flow of quotes, computers are capable of extracting information that has not yet crossed the news screens. Since all quote and volume information is public, such strategies are fully compliant with all the applicable laws.\nFilter trading is one of the more primitive high-frequency trading strategies that involves monitoring large amounts of stocks for significant or unusual price changes or volume activity. This includes trading on announcements, news, or other event criteria. Software would then generate a buy or sell order depending on the nature of the event being looked for.\nTick trading often aims to recognize the beginnings of large orders being placed in the market.  For example, a large order from a pension fund to buy will take place over several hours or even days, and will cause a rise in price due to increased\ndemand\n. An arbitrageur can try to spot this happening, buy up the security, then profit from selling back to the pension fund. This strategy has become more difficult since the introduction of dedicated trade execution companies in the 2000s which provide optimal trading for pension and other funds, specifically designed to remove the arbitrage opportunity.\nStatistical arbitrage\n[\nedit\n]\nAnother set of high-frequency trading strategies are strategies that exploit predictable temporary deviations from stable statistical relationships among securities.\nStatistical arbitrage\nat high frequencies is actively used in all liquid securities, including equities, bonds, futures, foreign exchange, etc. Such strategies may also involve classical arbitrage strategies, such as covered\ninterest rate parity\nin the\nforeign exchange market\n, which gives a relationship between the prices of a domestic bond, a bond denominated in a foreign currency, the spot price of the currency, and the price of a\nforward contract\non the currency. High-frequency trading allows similar arbitrages using models of greater complexity involving many more than four securities.\nThe TABB Group estimates that annual aggregate profits of high-frequency arbitrage strategies exceeded US$21\nbillion in 2009,\nalthough the Purdue study estimates the profits for all high frequency trading were US$5\nbillion in 2009.\nIndex arbitrage\n[\nedit\n]\nIndex arbitrage\nexploits\nindex tracker\nfunds which are bound to buy and sell large volumes of securities in proportion to their changing weights in indices.   If a HFT firm is able to access and process information which predicts these changes before the tracker funds do so, they can buy up securities in advance of the trackers and sell them on to them at a profit.\nNews-based trading\n[\nedit\n]\nCompany news in electronic text format is available from many sources including commercial providers like\nBloomberg\n, public news websites, and Twitter feeds. Automated systems can identify company names, keywords and sometimes semantics to make news-based trades before human traders can process the news.\nLow-latency strategies\n[\nedit\n]\nA separate, "naïve" class of high-frequency trading strategies relies exclusively on\nultra-low latency direct market access\ntechnology. In these strategies, computer scientists rely on speed to gain minuscule advantages in arbitraging price discrepancies in some particular security trading simultaneously on disparate markets.\nAnother aspect of\nlow latency\nstrategy has been the switch from\nfiber optic\nto\nmicrowave\nand\nshortwave\ntechnology for long distance networking. The switch to microwave transmission was because microwaves traveling in air suffer a less than 1% speed reduction compared to light traveling in a vacuum, whereas with conventional fiber optics light travels over 30% slower.\nEspecially since 2011, companies involved in HFT have massively invested in microwaves infrastructure to transmit data across key connections such as the one between\nNew York City\nand\nChicago\nbut also between\nLondon\nand\nFrankfurt\n, going through Belgium thanks to a network of former US army antennas.\nHowever, microwave transmission requires\nline-of-sight propagation\n, which is difficult over long distances, driving some HFT firms to use shortwave radio instead.\nShortwave radio signals can be transmitted over a longer distance, but carry less information; in 2020, a hedge fund partner quoted in\nBloomberg News\nsaid that shortwave\nbandwidth\nis insufficient for transmitting full\norder book\nfeeds for low-latency strategies.\nFirms have also looked into using\nsatellites\nto transmit market data.\nOrder properties strategies\n[\nedit\n]\nHigh-frequency trading strategies may use properties derived from market data feeds to identify orders that are posted at sub-optimal prices. Such orders may offer a profit to their counterparties that high-frequency traders can try to obtain. Examples of these features include the age of an order\nor the sizes of displayed orders.\nTracking important order properties may also allow trading strategies to have a more accurate prediction of the future price of a security.\nEffects\n[\nedit\n]\nThe effects of algorithmic and high-frequency trading are the subject of ongoing research. High frequency trading causes regulatory concerns as a contributor to market fragility.\nRegulators claim these practices contributed to volatility in the May 6, 2010, Flash Crash\nand find that risk controls are much less stringent for faster trades.\nMembers of the financial industry generally claim high-frequency trading substantially improves market liquidity,\nnarrows\nbid–offer spread\n, lowers volatility and makes trading and investing cheaper for other market participants.\nAn academic study\nfound that, for large-cap stocks and in quiescent markets during periods of "generally rising stock prices", high-frequency trading lowers the cost of trading and increases the informativeness of quotes;\nhowever, it found "no significant effects for smaller-cap stocks",\nand "it remains an open question whether algorithmic trading and algorithmic liquidity supply are equally beneficial in more turbulent or declining markets. ...algorithmic liquidity suppliers may simply turn off their machines when markets spike downward."\nIn September 2011, market data vendor\nNanex LLC\npublished a report stating the contrary. They looked at the amount of quote traffic compared to the value of trade transactions over 4 and half years and saw a 10-fold decrease in efficiency.\nNanex's owner is an outspoken detractor of high-frequency trading.\nMany discussions about HFT focus solely on the frequency aspect of the algorithms and not on their decision-making logic (which is typically kept secret by the companies that develop them). This makes it difficult for observers to pre-identify market scenarios where HFT will dampen or amplify price fluctuations. The growing quote traffic compared to trade value could indicate that more firms are trying to profit from cross-market arbitrage techniques that do not add significant value through increased liquidity when measured globally.\nMore fully automated markets such as\nNASDAQ\n, Direct Edge, and BATS, in the US, gained market share from less automated markets such as the\nNYSE\n.\nEconomies of scale\nin electronic trading contributed to lowering commissions and trade processing fees, and contributed to international mergers and consolidation of\nfinancial exchanges\n.\nThe speeds of computer connections, measured in milliseconds or microseconds, have become important.\nCompetition is developing among exchanges for the fastest processing times for completing trades. For example, in 2009 the\nLondon Stock Exchange\nbought a technology firm called MillenniumIT and announced plans to implement its Millennium Exchange platform\nwhich they claim has an average latency of 126 microseconds.\nThis allows sub-millisecond resolution\ntimestamping\nof the\norder book\n.\nOff-the-shelf software\ncurrently allows for nanoseconds resolution of timestamps using a\nGPS\nclock with 100 nanoseconds precision.\nSpending on computers and software in the financial industry increased to $26.4\nbillion in 2005.\nMay 6, 2010 flash crash\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\n2010 flash crash\nThe brief but dramatic stock market crash of May 6, 2010, was initially thought to have been caused by high-frequency trading.\nThe\nDow Jones Industrial Average\nplunged to its largest intraday point loss, but not percentage loss,\nin history, only to recover much of those losses within minutes.\nIn the aftermath of the crash, several organizations argued that high-frequency trading was not to blame, and may even have been a major factor in minimizing and partially reversing the Flash Crash.\nCME Group\n, a large\nfutures exchange\n, stated that, insofar as stock index futures traded on CME Group were concerned, its investigation had found no support for the notion that high-frequency trading was related to the crash, and actually stated it had a market stabilizing effect.\nHowever, after almost five months of investigations, the\nU.S. Securities and Exchange Commission\n(\nSEC\n) and the\nCommodity Futures Trading Commission\n(\nCFTC\n) issued a joint report identifying the cause that set off the sequence of events leading to the Flash Crash\nand concluding that the actions of high-frequency trading firms contributed to volatility during the crash.\nThe report found that the cause was a single sale of $4.1\nbillion in futures contracts by a mutual fund, identified as\nWaddell & Reed Financial\n, in an aggressive attempt to hedge its investment position.\nThe joint report also found that "high-frequency traders quickly magnified the impact of the mutual fund's selling."\nThe joint report "portrayed a market so fragmented and fragile that a single large trade could send stocks into a sudden spiral", that a large mutual fund firm "chose to sell a big number of futures contracts using a computer program that essentially ended up wiping out available buyers in the market", that as a result high-frequency firms "were also aggressively selling the E-mini contracts", contributing to rapid price declines.\nThe joint report also noted "HFTs began to quickly buy and then resell contracts to each other – generating a 'hot-potato' volume effect as the same positions were passed rapidly back and forth."\nThe combined sales by Waddell and high-frequency firms quickly drove "the E-mini price down 3% in just four minutes".\nAs prices in the futures market fell, there was a spillover into the equities markets where "the liquidity in the market evaporated because the automated systems used by most firms to keep pace with the market paused" and scaled back their trading or withdrew from the markets altogether.\nThe joint report then noted that "Automatic computerized traders on the stock market shut down as they detected the sharp rise in buying and selling."\nAs computerized high-frequency traders exited the stock market, the resulting lack of liquidity "...caused shares of some prominent companies like\nProcter & Gamble\nand\nAccenture\nto trade down as low as a penny or as high as $100,000".\nWhile some firms exited the market, high-frequency firms that remained in the market exacerbated price declines because they "'escalated their aggressive selling' during the downdraft".\nIn the years following the flash crash, academic researchers and experts from the CFTC pointed to high-frequency trading as just one component of the complex current U.S. market structure that led to the events of May 6, 2010.\nGranularity and accuracy\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2015 the Paris-based regulator of the 28-nation European Union, the\nEuropean Securities and Markets Authority\n, proposed time standards to span the EU, that would more accurately synchronize trading clocks "to within a nanosecond, or one-billionth of a second" to refine regulation of gateway-to-gateway latency time—"the speed at which trading venues acknowledge an order after receiving a trade request". Using these more detailed time-stamps, regulators would be better able to distinguish the order in which trade requests are received and executed, to identify market abuse and prevent potential manipulation of European securities markets by traders using advanced, powerful, fast computers and networks. The fastest technologies give traders an advantage over other "slower" investors as they can change prices of the securities they trade.\nRisks and controversy\n[\nedit\n]\nAccording to author\nWalter Mattli\n, the ability of regulators to enforce the rules has greatly declined since 2005 with the passing of the\nRegulation National Market System\n(Reg NMS) by the SEC. As a result, the\nNYSE\n's quasi monopoly role as a stock rule maker was undermined and turned the stock exchange into one of many globally operating exchanges. The market then became more fractured and granular, as did the regulatory bodies, and since stock exchanges had turned into entities also seeking to maximize profits, the one with the most lenient regulators were rewarded, and oversight over traders' activities was lost. This fragmentation has greatly benefitted HFT.\nHigh-frequency trading comprises many different types of algorithms.\nVarious studies reported that certain types of market-making high-frequency trading reduces volatility and does not pose a systemic risk,\nand lowers transaction costs for retail investors,\nwithout impacting long term investors.\nOther studies, summarized in Aldridge, Krawciw, 2017\nfind that high-frequency trading strategies known as "aggressive" erode liquidity and cause volatility.\nHigh-frequency trading has been the subject of intense public focus and debate since the May 6, 2010, Flash Crash.\nAt least one Nobel Prize–winning economist,\nMichael Spence\n, believes that HFT should be banned.\nA\nworking paper\nfound "the presence of high frequency trading has significantly mitigated the frequency and severity of end-of-day price dislocation".\nIn their joint report on the 2010 Flash Crash, the\nSEC\nand the\nCFTC\nstated that "market makers and other liquidity providers widened their quote spreads, others reduced offered liquidity, and a significant number withdrew completely from the markets"\nduring the flash crash.\nPoliticians, regulators, scholars, journalists and market participants have all raised concerns on both sides of the Atlantic.\nThis has led to discussion of whether high-frequency market makers should be subject to various kinds of regulations.\nIn a September 22, 2010, speech, SEC chairperson\nMary Schapiro\nsignaled that US authorities were considering the introduction of regulations targeted at HFT. She said, "high frequency trading firms have a tremendous capacity to affect the stability and integrity of the equity markets. Currently, however, high frequency trading firms are subject to very little in the way of obligations either to protect that stability by promoting reasonable price continuity in tough times, or to refrain from exacerbating price volatility."\nShe proposed regulation that would require high-frequency traders to stay active in volatile markets.\nA later SEC chair\nMary Jo White\npushed back against claims that high-frequency traders have an inherent benefit in the markets.\nSEC associate director Gregg Berman suggested that the current debate over HFT lacks perspective. In an April 2014 speech, Berman argued: "It's much more than just the automation of quotes and cancels, in spite of the seemingly exclusive fixation on this topic by much of the media and various outspoken market pundits. (...) I worry that it may be too narrowly focused and myopic."\nThe Chicago Federal Reserve letter of October 2012, titled "How to keep markets safe in an era of high-speed trading", reports on the results of a survey of several dozen financial industry professionals including traders, brokers, and exchanges.\nIt found that\nrisk controls were poorer in high-frequency trading, because of competitive time pressure to execute trades without the more extensive safety checks normally used in slower trades.\n"some firms do not have stringent processes for the development, testing, and deployment of code used in their trading algorithms."\n"out-of-control algorithms were more common than anticipated prior to the study and that there were no clear patterns as to their cause."\nThe\nCFA Institute\n, a global association of investment professionals, advocated for reforms regarding high-frequency trading,\nincluding:\nPromoting robust internal risk management procedures and controls over the\nalgorithms\nand strategies employed by HFT firms.\nTrading venues should disclose their fee structure to all market participants.\nRegulators should address\nmarket manipulation\nand other threats to the integrity of markets, regardless of the underlying mechanism, and not try to intervene in the trading process or to restrict certain types of trading activities.\nFlash trading\n[\nedit\n]\nExchanges offered a type of order called a "Flash" order (on NASDAQ, it was called "Bolt" on the Bats stock exchange) that allowed an order to lock the market (post at the same price as an order on the other side of the\norder book\n) for a small amount of time (5 milliseconds).  This order type was available to all participants but since HFT's adapted to the changes in market structure more quickly than others, they were able to use it to "jump the queue" and place their orders before other order types were allowed to trade at the given price.  Currently, the majority of exchanges do not offer flash trading, or have discontinued it. By March 2011, the\nNASDAQ\n,\nBATS\n, and Direct Edge exchanges had all ceased offering its Competition for Price Improvement functionality (widely referred to as "flash technology/trading").\nViolations and fines\n[\nedit\n]\nRegulation and enforcement\n[\nedit\n]\nSee also:\nRegulation of algorithms\nIn March 2012, regulators fined Octeg LLC, the equities market-making unit of high-frequency trading firm\nGetco LLC\n, for $450,000. Regulators stated the HFT firm ignored dozens of error messages before its computers sent millions of unintended orders to the market. According to Nasdaq CEO\nRobert Greifeld\n"the regulator shouldn't have approved IEX without changing the rules that required quotes to be immediately visible". The IEX speed bump—or trading slowdown—is 350\nmicroseconds\n, which the SEC ruled was within the "immediately visible" parameter. The slowdown promises to\nimpede HST ability\n"often [to]\ncancel dozens\nof orders for every trade they make".\nOutside of US equities, several notable spot foreign exchange (FX) trading platforms—including ParFX,\nEBS Market,\nand Refinitiv FXall\n—have implemented their own "speed bumps" to curb or otherwise limit HFT activity. Unlike the IEX fixed length delay that retains the temporal ordering of messages as they are received by the platform, the spot FX platforms' speed bumps reorder messages so the first message received is not necessarily that processed for matching first.
