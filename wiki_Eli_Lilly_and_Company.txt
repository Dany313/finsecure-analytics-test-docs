Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eli_Lilly_and_Company\n==================================================\n\nAmerican pharmaceutical company\nFor other uses, see\nEli Lilly (disambiguation)\n.\nDavid Ricks, CEO of Lilly since 2016\nEli Lilly and Company\n,\ndoing business as\nLilly\n, is an American multinational\npharmaceutical\ncompany headquartered in\nIndianapolis\n, Indiana, with offices in 18 countries. Its products are sold in approximately 125 countries. The company was founded in 1876 by\nEli Lilly\n, a pharmaceutical\nchemist\nand\nUnion army\nveteran during the\nAmerican Civil War\nfor whom the company was later named.\nThe company is ranked 4th on the\nlist of largest biomedical companies by revenue\nand is the most valuable pharmaceutical company worldwide.\nIt is ranked 100th on the\nFortune\n500\nand 138th on the\nForbes\nGlobal 2000\n.\nIn November 2025, the company reached a\n$1 trillion market capitalization\n, the first health-care company in the world to do so.\nThe company's primary products are\ntirzepatide\n(Mounjaro and Zepbound) for the treatment of\ntype\n2 diabetes\n,\nweight loss\n, and\nobstructive sleep apnea\n(56% of 2025 revenues);\nabemaciclib\n(Verzenio) for the treatment of advanced or metastatic\nbreast cancers\n(9% of 2025 revenues);\ndulaglutide\n(Trulicity) for the treatment of type\n2 diabetes (7% of 2025 revenues);\nixekizumab\n(Taltz) for the treatment of\nautoimmune diseases\n(6% of 2025 revenues); and\nempagliflozin\n(Jardiance) for the treatment of type\n2 diabetes (5% of 2025 revenues). Products that have a minor contribution to revenues include\nInsulin lispro\n(Humalog), a modified type of medical\ninsulin\n;\nramucirumab\n(Cyramza), a fully human\nmonoclonal antibody\nused for the treatment of cancer;\nbaricitinib\n(Olumiant), an\nimmunomodulatory medication\nused for the treatment of\nrheumatoid arthritis\n,\nalopecia areata\n, and\nCOVID-19\n;\ninsulin\n(Humulin) to treat\nhigh blood glucose\n; and\ngalcanezumab\n(Emgality), a humanized\nmonoclonal antibody\nused for the prevention of\nmigraines\n.\nIn 2024, 67% of the company's revenues came from the United States, 18% came from Europe, 3% came from Japan, and 3% came from China.\nLilly was the first company to mass-produce both the\npolio vaccine\n, developed in 1955 by\nJonas Salk\n, and\ninsulin\n.\nIt was one of the first pharmaceutical companies to produce human\ninsulin\nusing\nrecombinant DNA\n, including Humulin (\ninsulin medication\n), Humalog (\ninsulin lispro\n), and the first approved\nbiosimilar\ninsulin product in the US, Basaglar (\ninsulin glargine\n).\nIt achieved commercial success with\nmajor depressive disorder\ndrugs Prozac (\nfluoxetine\n) (1986), Cymbalta (\nduloxetine\n) (2004), and its\nantipsychotic\nmedication Zyprexa (\nolanzapine\n) (1996), all of which are now off patent and are manufactured by others as\ngeneric drugs\n. Lilly turned over operation of the family business to his son, Josiah, who ran the company for the next several decades. Col. Lilly remained active in civic affairs and assisted a number of local organizations, including the Commercial Club of Indianapolis, which later became the Indianapolis Chamber of Commerce,\nand the\nCharity Organization Society\n, a forerunner to the Family Services Association of Central Indiana, an organization supported by\nUnited Way of America\n.\nJosiah's sons, Eli and Joe, were also philanthropists who supported numerous cultural and educational organizations.\n19th century\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 1878, Lilly hired his brother, James, as his first full-time salesman, and the subsequent sales team marketed the company's drugs nationally.\nBy 1879, the company had grown to $48,000 in sales.\nThe company moved its Indianapolis headquarters from Pearl Street to larger quarters at 36 South\nMeridian Street\n. In 1881, the company moved to its current headquarters in Indianapolis's south-side industrial area, and the company later purchased additional facilities for research and production. The same year, Lilly incorporated the business as Eli Lilly and Company, elected a board of directors, and issued stock to family members and close associates.\nLilly's first innovative product was\ngelatin\n-coating for pills and capsules. The company's other early innovations included fruit flavorings and sugarcoated pills, which made the medicines easier to swallow.\nIn 1882, Colonel Lilly's only son,\nJosiah K. Lilly Sr.\n(J. K.), a pharmaceutical chemist, graduated from the\nPhiladelphia College of Pharmacy\nin\nPhiladelphia\n, and returned to Indianapolis to join the family business as a superintendent of its laboratory.\nIn 1883, the company contracted to mix and sell Succus Alteran, its first widely successful product and one its best sellers. The product was marketed as a "blood purifier" and as a treatment for\nsyphilis\n, some types of\nrheumatism\n, and skin diseases such as\neczema\nand\npsoriasis\n.\nBy the late 1880s, Colonel Lilly was one of the Indianapolis area's leading businessmen, and the company had over 100 employees and $200,000 in annual sales.\nIn 1890, Colonel Lilly fully turned over the day-to-day management of the business to J. K., who ran the company for 34 years. The 1890s were a tumultuous decade economically, but the company flourished.\nIn 1894, Lilly purchased a manufacturing plant to be used solely for creating capsules. The company also made several technological advances in the manufacturing process, including automating its capsule production. Over the next few years the company annually created tens of millions of capsules and pills.\nIn 1898, Lilly's son, J. K. Lilly, inherited the company and became its president following Colonel Lilly's death. At the time of Colonel Lilly's death, the company had a product line of 2,005 items and annual sales of more than $300,000.\nColonel Lilly was a pioneer in the modern pharmaceutical industry, with many of his early innovations later becoming standard practice. His ethical reforms in a trade that was marked by outlandish claims of miracle medicines began a period of rapid advancement in the development of medicinal drugs.\nJ. K. Lilly continued to advocate for federal regulation on medicines.\nAs the Lilly company grew, other businesses set up operations near the plant on Indianapolis's near south side. The area developed into one of the city's major business and industrial hubs. Lilly's production, manufacturing, research, and administrative operations in Indianapolis eventually occupied a complex of more than two dozen buildings, which covered 15-block area, in addition to its production plants along Kentucky Avenue.\nIn addition to Colonel Lilly, his brother, James, and son, Josiah (J. K.), the company employed other Lilly family. Colonel Lilly's cousin, Evan Lilly, was hired as a bookkeeper.\nLilly's grandsons,\nEli\nand\nJosiah Jr.\n(Joe) joined the company from a young age.\nUnder J. K.'s leadership, the company introduced scientific management concepts, organized the company's research department, increased its sales force, and began international distribution of its products. For the rest of the late 19th century, Lilly operated in Indianapolis and the surrounding area as many other pharmaceutical businesses did, manufacturing and selling "sugar-coated pills, fluid extracts, elixirs, and syrups".\nThe company used plants for its raw materials and produced its products by hand. One historian noted, "Although the Indianapolis firm was more careful in making and promoting drugs than the patent medicine men of the era, the company remained ambivalent about scientific research."\n1900s\n[\nedit\n]\nAn assortment of Lilly's\nthroat lozenges\nfrom a 1906 sales book\nJosiah K. K. Lilly oversaw a large expansion of the company, and it reached annual sales of $1 million.\nFollowing the\n1906 San Francisco earthquake\n, the company sent much needed medicine to support recovery efforts.\nBefore and after\nWorld War I\n, the company experienced rapid growth,\nincluding expanded manufacturing facilities at its McCarty Street plant, which improved production capacity with a new Science Building (Building 14), opened in 1911, and a new capsule plant (Building 15) in 1913.\nIn 1913, the company began construction of\nLilly Biological Laboratories\n, a research and manufacturing plant on 150 acres near\nGreenfield, Indiana\n.\nAfter\nWorld War I\n, the company's expanded production facilities and introduction of new management methods set the stage for Lilly's next crucial phase\n—\nits "aggressive entry into scientific research and development."\nThe first big step came in 1919 when Josiah Lilly hired biochemist\nGeorge Henry Alexander Clowes\nas director of biochemical research.\nClowes had extensive medical research expertise and links to the scientific research community, which led to the company's collaborations with researchers in the US and elsewhere.\nClowes's first major collaboration with researchers who developed\ninsulin\nat the\nUniversity of Toronto\nsignificantly impacted the company's future.\nLilly's success with insulin production secured the company's position as a leading research-based pharmaceutical manufacturer, allowing it to attract and hire more research scientists and to collaborate with other universities in additional medical research.\nIn addition to development of new medicines, the company achieved several technological advances, including automation of its production facilities. Lilly was also an innovator in pill capsule manufacturing. It was among the first manufacturers to insert medications into empty gelatin capsules, which provided a more exact dosage.\nLilly manufactured capsules for its own needs and sold its excess capacity to others.\nIn 1917,\nScientific American\ndescribed Lilly as "the largest capsule factory in the world" and reported that the company was "capable of producing 2.5 million capsules a day".\nOne of Lilly's early innovations was fruit flavoring for medicines and sugar-coated pills to make their medicines easier to swallow.\nOver the next few years, the company created tens of millions of capsules and pills annually.\nOther advances improved plant efficiency and eliminated production errors. In 1909,\nEli Lilly\n, grandson of the company's founder, introduced a method for blueprinting manufacturing tickets,\nwhich created multiples copies of a drug formula and helped eliminate manufacturing and transcription errors.\nIn 1919, Josiah hired biochemist\nGeorge Henry Alexander Clowes\nas director of biochemical research.\nIn the 1920s, Eli introduced the new concept of straight-line production to the pharmaceutical industry, where raw materials entered at one end of the facility and the finished product came out the other end, in the company's manufacturing process.\nThis new process led to an increase in output and profit for the company.\nUnder Eli's supervision, the design for Building 22, a new five-floor plant that opened in Indianapolis in 1926, implemented the straight-line concept to improve production efficiency and lower production costs.\nOne historian noted, "It was probably the most sophisticated production system in the American pharmaceutical industry."\nThis more efficient manufacturing process also allowed the company to hire a regular workforce. Instead of recalling workers at peak times and laying them off when production demand fell, Lilly's regular workforce produced less-costly medicines in off-peak times using the same manufacturing facilities.\nDuring the 1920s, the introduction of new products brought the company financial success.\nIn 1921, three\nUniversity of Toronto\nscientists,\nJohn Macleod\n,\nFrederick Banting\n, and\nCharles Best\n, were working on the development of\ninsulin\nfor treatment of diabetes.\nClowes proposed a collaboration with the researchers in December 1921, and then again March and May 1922. The researchers were hesitant to work with a commercial drug firm, particularly since they had the\nConnaught Laboratories\n' non-commercial facilities at hand. But as limits were reached at the scale to which Connaught could produce insulin, Clowes and Eli Lilly met with the researchers in 1922 to negotiate an agreement with the University of Toronto scientists to mass-produce insulin.\nThe collaboration greatly accelerated the large-scale production of the extract.\nIn 1923, the company began selling Iletin, the company's tradename for the first commercially available insulin product in the US for the treatment of diabetes.\nNumerous objections were registered by the Insulin Committee of the University of Toronto in regard to Lilly's use of the term "Iletin", although production continued under this name and the objection was later dropped "as a concession".\nAlso in 1923, Banting and Macleod were awarded the\nNobel Prize\nfor their research, which they subsequently shared with co-discoverers\nCharles Best\nand\nJames Collip\n.\nInsulin, "the most important drug" in the company's history, did "more than any other" to make Lilly "one of the major pharmaceutical manufacturers in the world."\nEli Lilly and Company enjoyed an effective monopoly on the sale of insulin in the US for almost two years, until the first of the new American licensees, Frederick Stearns & Co., entered the market in June 1924.\nThe success of insulin enabled the company to attract scientists and, with them, make more medical advances. By the company's 50th anniversary in 1926, its sales had reached $9 million and it was producing over 2,800 products.\nIn 1928, Lilly introduced Liver Extract 343 for the treatment of\npernicious anemia\n, a blood disorder, in a joint venture with two\nHarvard University\nscientists,\nGeorge Minot\nand\nWilliam P. Murphy\n. The same year, Eli Lilly, eldest grandson of Col. Lilly who had joined the company in 1909, was named as the company's president, succeeding his father, who remained as chairman of the board until 1948. In his early years at the company, Eli was especially interested in improving production efficiency and introduced a number of labor-saving devices.
