Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merck_%26_Co.\n==================================================\n\nAmerican multinational pharmaceutical company\nNot to be confused with the\nMerck Group\n(Merck KGaA), a distinct but historically related company based in Germany.\nMerck & Co., Inc.\nis an American\nmultinational\npharmaceutical\ncompany headquartered in\nRahway, New Jersey\n. The company does business as\nMerck Sharp & Dohme\nor\nMSD\noutside the United States and Canada. The company is ranked fifth on the\nlist of largest biomedical companies by revenue\n.\nThe company's revenues are primarily from cancer treatments, vaccines, and animal health products. In 2024, 46% of the company's revenue, or $29.5 billion, came from sales of Keytruda (\npembrolizumab\n), a\nPD-1 inhibitor\nused to treat various types of\ncancers\n, and 13% of the company's revenue, or $8.6 billion, came from sales of\nGardasil\n, an\nHPV vaccine\n.\nIn addition, 9% of the company's revenue, or $5.8 billion, came from the sales of animal health products.\nThe company is ranked 65th on the\nFortune\n500\nand 76th on the\nForbes\nGlobal 2000\n.\nIn 1891, Merck & Co. was established as the American affiliate of the\nMerck Group\n, founded by the\nMerck family\n, and the companies are still in trademark disputes in several countries over the right to use the name "Merck".\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\nRoots and early history\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nMerck Group\nThe Angel Pharmacy in\nDarmstadt\n, the beginning of the\nMerck Group\nMerck & Co. traces its origins to its former German parent company the\nMerck Group\n, which was established by the\nMerck family\nin 1668 when\nFriedrich Jacob Merck\npurchased a pharmacy in\nDarmstadt\n.\nIn 1827, Merck Group evolved from a pharmacy to a drug manufacturer company with the commercial manufacture of\nmorphine\n.\nMerck perfected the chemical process of deriving morphine from\nopium\nand later introduced\ncocaine\n, used to treat sinus problems and to add to beverages to boost energy levels.\nIn 1887 a German-born, long-time Merck employee, Theodore Weicker, went to the United States to represent Merck Group.\nIn 1891, with $200,000 received from E. Merck, Weicker started Merck & Co., with headquarters in lower Manhattan. That year George Merck, the 23-year-old son of the then head of E. Merck (and grandson of the founder) joined Weicker in New York.\nMerck & Co. operated from 1891 to 1917 as the US subsidiary of the\nMerck Group\n.\nNationalization\n[\nedit\n]\nAfter the U.S. entered\nWorld War I\n, due to its German connections, Merck & Co. was the subject of\nexpropriation\nunder the\nTrading with the Enemy Act of 1917\n.\nThe government seized 80 percent of the shares owned by the German parent company and sold it.\nIn 1919, George F. Merck (head of the American branch of the Merck family), in partnership with\nGoldman Sachs\nand\nLehman Brothers\n, bought the company back at a U.S. government auction for $3.5 million, but Merck & Co. remained a separate company from its former German parent.\nMerck & Co. holds the trademark rights to the "Merck" name in the United States and Canada, while its former parent company retains the rights in the rest of the world; the right to use the Merck name was the subject of litigation between the two companies in 2016.\nIn 1925,\nGeorge W. Merck\nsucceeded his father George F. Merck as president. In 1927, the corporation merged with the Powers-\nWeightman\n-Rosengarten Company, a Philadelphia quinine manufacturer. George Merck remained president and Frederic Rosengarten became chairman of the board.\nIn 1929,\nH. K. Mulford Company\nmerged with Sharp and Dohme, Inc. and brought vaccine technology, including immunization of cavalry horses in World War I and delivery of a diphtheria antitoxin to Merck & Co.\nIn 1943,\nstreptomycin\nwas discovered during a Merck-funded research program in\nSelman Waksman's\nlaboratory at\nRutgers University\n. It became the first effective treatment for\ntuberculosis\n. At the time of its discovery,\nsanatoriums\nfor the isolation of tuberculosis-infected people were a ubiquitous feature of cities in developed countries, with 50% dying within 5 years of admission.\nAlthough Merck's agreement with Rutgers gave it exclusive rights to streptomycin, at Waksman's request the company renegotiated the agreement, returning the rights to the university in exchange for a royalty. Merck & Co. closed this facility in July 2010 but remerged in 2011 as Merck Canada.\nMaurice Hilleman\n, a scientist at Merck, developed the first\nmumps vaccine\nin 1967,\nthe first\nrubella vaccine\nin 1969,\nand the first trivalent measles, mumps, rubella (\nMMR vaccine\n) in 1971.\nThe incidence of rubella-associated birth defects fell from up to 10,000 per year in the U.S. to zero in the aftermath of the rubella vaccine's development.\nHilleman also developed the first\nHepatitis B vaccine\nand the first\nvaricella vaccine\n, for chickenpox.\nThe company was incorporated in New Jersey in 1970.\nJohn J. The donation program has significantly reduced the incidence of the disease.\nLawsuits and controversies\n[\nedit\n]\nHeart attacks after use of Vioxx\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 1999, the\nU.S. Food and Drug Administration\n(FDA) approved Vioxx (known generically as\nrofecoxib\n), a Merck product for treating\narthritis\n. Vioxx was designed as a selective inhibitor of the enzyme\ncyclooxygenase-2\n. Such compounds were expected to cause less gastrointestinal bleeding than older\nanti-inflammatory\ndrugs such as\nnaproxen\n, which were associated with 20,000 hospitalizations and 2000 deaths each year.\nVioxx became one of the most prescribed drugs in history.\nThereafter, studies by Merck and by others found an increased risk of heart attack associated with\nVioxx\nuse when compared with naproxen. Merck adjusted the labeling of Vioxx to reflect possible cardiovascular risks in 2002.\nOn September 23, 2004, Merck received information about results from a\nclinical trial\nit was conducting that included findings of increased risk of heart attacks among Vioxx users who had been using the medication for over eighteen months.\nOn September 28, 2004, Merck notified the FDA that it was voluntarily withdrawing Vioxx from the market, and it publicly announced the withdrawal on September 30. An analysis for the period 1999–2004, based on U.S. Medical Expenditure Survey data, reported that Vioxx was associated with 46,783 heart attacks in the US, and along with the other popular\nCOX-2 inhibitor\nCelebrex\n, an estimated 26,603 deaths from both.\nAbout 50,000 people sued Merck, claiming they or their family members had suffered medical problems such as heart attacks or strokes after taking Vioxx.\nIn November 2007, Merck agreed to pay $4.85 billion to settle most of the pending Vioxx lawsuits.\nThe settlement required that claimants provide medical and pharmacy records confirming the occurrence of a heart attack, ischemic stroke, or sudden cardiac death; the receipt of at least 30 Vioxx pills within 60 days prior to the injury or death; and confirmation of Vioxx being used within 14 days of the Vioxx-related event.\nThe settlement was generally viewed by industry analysts and investors as a victory for Merck, considering that original estimates of Merck's liability reached between $10 billion and $25 billion.\nAs of mid-2008, when the plaintiff class had reached the threshold percentage required by Merck to go through with the settlement, plaintiffs had prevailed in only three of the twenty cases that had reached juries, all with relatively small awards.\nMerck has refused to consider compensation for Vioxx victims and their families outside the US.
