For the protest song, see\nUnited Health\n(song)\n.\nUnitedHealth Group Incorporated\nis an American\nmultinational\nfor-profit\ncompany based in\nEden Prairie, Minnesota\n, specializing in\nhealth insurance\nand health care services. Selling insurance products under\nUnitedHealthcare\n, and health care services under the\nOptum\nbrand, it is the\nworld's seventh-largest company by revenue\nand the largest health care company by revenue. The company is ranked seventh on the 2025\nFortune\nGlobal 500\n.\nUnitedHealth Group had a\nmarket capitalization\nof more than $300 billion as of year-end 2025.\nThe company is the largest health insurer in the United States, insuring over 50 million people,\nand operates globally.\nUnitedHealth Group has faced numerous\ninvestigations, lawsuits, and fines, including\nSEC\nenforcement for stock option backdating,\nMedicare\nover-billing, unfair claims practices, mental health treatment denials, and\nanticompetitive\nbehavior.\nHistory\n[\nedit\n]\n1970s–1990s\n[\nedit\n]\nUnitedHealth Group originated in late 1974, when Minnesota-based Charter Med Incorporated was founded by\nRichard Taylor Burke\n. It originally processed claims for doctors at the Hennepin County Medical Society.\nUnitedHealthcare Corporation was founded in 1977 to purchase Charter Med and create a network-based health plan for seniors.\nIt became a publicly traded company in 1984 and changed its name to UnitedHealth Group in 1998.\nIn 1988, UnitedHealthcare started its first\npharmacy benefit management\n, through its\nDiversified Pharmaceutical Services\nsubsidiary. It managed pharmacy benefits delivered both through retail pharmacies and mail. The subsidiary was sold to\nSmithKline Beecham\nin 1994 for $2.3 billion.\nIn 1994, UnitedHealthcare acquired Ramsey-HMO, a Florida insurer.\nIn 1995, the company acquired The MetraHealth Companies Inc. for $1.65 billion. MetraHealth was a privately held company formed by combining the group healthcare operations of\nThe Travelers Companies\nand\nMetLife\n. In 1996, UnitedHealthcare acquired HealthWise of America, which operated HMOs in Arkansas, Maryland, Kentucky and Tennessee.\nIn 1998, the company was reorganized as the\nholding company\nfor hitherto independent companies UnitedHealthcare, Ovations, Uniprise, Specialized Care Services, and Ingenix and rebranded as "UnitedHealth Group". Also in 1998, UnitedHealth Group acquired HealthPartners of Arizona, operator of Arizona's largest\nAHCCCS\nprovider.\n2000s\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2001, EverCare, a UnitedHealth Group subsidiary, merged with LifeMark Health Plans\nIn 2002, UnitedHealth Group acquired GeoAccess and Medicaid insurance company AmeriChoice.\nIn 2003, UnitedHealth Group acquired Mid Atlantic Medical Services, an insurer serving Maryland, Washington D.C., Virginia, Delaware and West Virginia.\nAlso in 2003, UnitedHealth Group acquired Golden Rule Financial, a provider of health savings accounts.\nOn July 21, 2003, Exante Bank started operating in Salt Lake City, Utah, as a Utah state-chartered industrial loan corporation. It changed its name to OptumHealth Bank in 2008\nand to\nOptum Bank\nin 2012.\nIn April 2004, UnitedHealth Group acquired Touchpoint Health Plan, a Wisconsin health plan.\nIn July 2004, the company acquired\nOxford Health Plans\n.\nIn December 2005, the company acquired\nPacifiCare Health Systems\n.\nIt agreed to divest parts of PacifiCare's commercial health insurance business in\nTucson, Arizona\nand\nBoulder, Colorado\nto satisfy\nantitrust\nregulator concerns, and also agreed to end its network access agreement with\nBlue Shield of California\n. The Tucson business was sold to Cigna.\nThe company acquired Prescription Solutions, another pharmacy benefits manager, as part of its acquisition of PacifiCare Health Systems.\nThis business was later rebranded OptumRx.\nIn February 2006, the company acquired\nJohn Deere\nHealth Care.\nThe same year,\nWilliam W. McGuire\nstepped down as chairman and director due to his involvement in the employee stock options scandal.\nHe was replaced by CEO\nStephen Hemsley\nwho had served as president and COO and a member of the board of directors. McGuire's exit compensation from UnitedHealth was anticipated to be $1.1 billion,\nbut he only received $618 million after returning $420 million in stock options.\nIn February 2008, the company acquired Sierra Health Services for $2.6 billion.\nAs part of the transaction, to obtain regulatory approval, 25,000 customers were sold to\nHumana\n.\nIn July 2009, UnitedHealth Group agreed to acquire\nHealth Net\n's Northeast licensed subsidiaries for up to $570 million in payments spread out over two years.\n2010s\n[\nedit\n]\nThrough 2010 and into 2011, senior executives of the company met monthly with executives of other health insurers to limit the effect of the health care reform law.\nIn July 2010, Ingenix acquired Picis Clinical Solutions, Inc., a health information provider for the high-acuity areas of hospitals.\nIn 2011, Logistics Health, Inc. of\nLa Crosse, Wisconsin\n, was acquired by OptumHealth.\nIn September 2014, the office buildings where LHI is based were sold to UnitedHealth Group for $45 million.\nIn February 2012, the company acquired XLHealth, a sponsor of\nMedicare Advantage\nhealth plans with a primary focus on medicare recipients with special needs such as those with chronic illness and those eligible for Medicaid ("dual eligibles").\nIn October 2012, UnitedHealth Group and Amil Participações, one of the biggest Brazilian health insurance companies, completed the first phase of their merger.\nFormer LHI (now Optum Serve) buildings in\nLa Crosse\nIn February 2014,\nOptum\nsecured a\nmajority stake\nin the Washington, D.C.–based startup Audax Health.\nAudax's CEO, Grant Verstandig, continued running the firm alongside\nCOO\nDavid Ko.\nIn October 2014, Optum Health acquired the health services unit of\nAlere\nfor $600 million cash.\nIn March 2015, it was announced that\nCatamaranRx\nwould be acquired by OptumRx.\nIn April 2016, the company announced it was pulling out of all but a "handful" of state healthcare exchanges provided under\nAffordable Care Act\nand will continue to sell only in three states in 2017.\nIn 2017, UnitedHealth's Optum unit acquired Rally Health,\na company started by Audax Health's executives.\nPrior to acquisition, in 2015, UnitedHealth supported Rally Health as a majority investor,\nand through enrolling more than 5 million UnitedHealth policy holders in Rally Health's flagship product, Rally.\nThe close relations between UnitedHealth, Audax Health and Rally Health follows a close personal relationship between Grant Verstandig (Audax and Rally) and UnitedHealth's\nPresident\nand\nCFO\nat the time, David Wichmann.\nIn June 2019, UnitedHealth's\nOptum\ndivision acquired DaVita Medical Group from\nDaVita, Inc.\nfor $4.3 billion.\nThat year, the company also agreed to acquire Equian for $3.2 billion.\nOn June 19, 2019, UnitedHealth acquired the online\npatient community platform\nPatientsLikeMe\nfor an undisclosed amount and it will be incorporated into UnitedHealth Group's research division.\nIn November 2019,\nAndrew Witty\nwas named president of UnitedHealth Group, in addition to his role as chief executive of the company's Optum division.\n2020s–present\n[\nedit\n]\nUnitedHealth announced in March 2022 that it would acquire LHC Group for $5.4 billion. The deal will expand its home health capabilities by combining LHC's services with UnitedHealth's Optum unit.\nIn 2022, UnitedHealth Group said there would be no out-of-pocket costs for\nalbuterol\n,\nepinephrine\n,\nglucagon\n,\ninsulin\n, and\nnaloxone\nfor fully insured members starting in 2023. The discounts were guaranteed for less than a quarter of UnitedHealthcare's membership and did not extend to those enrolled in UHC's\nMedicare Advantage\nand\nMedicaid\nplans.\nIn 2023, UnitedHealthGroup announced it would move its headquarters from Minnetonka to\nEden Prairie, Minnesota\n.\nIn February 2024, UnitedHealth Group completed the sale of its operations in Brazil.\nIn February 2022, UnitedHealth announced the acquisition of\nChange Healthcare\n, the largest health payments platform in the US, which the\nUS Justice Department\ntried to block on antitrust grounds; the sale went through by September.\n18 months later, in February 2024, the subsidiary was brought completely down by the\n2024 Change Healthcare ransomware attack\n, and the Justice Department announced that it was opening a new antitrust and\nMedicare\novercharging probe.\nDespite making a ransomware payment, services remained down or incomplete for months. The CEO, Andrew Witty, was called before the\nUS House\nand\nUS Senate\nto testify about the cyberattack and DoJ concerns.\nIn late April 2024, the Senate held a hearing concerning the cyberattack and UnitedHealth's response. Andrew Witty went before the Senate stating that there needed to be a strong digital security system in place along with an adequate backup plan. UnitedHealth revealed the hackers did gain access to patient information, but the Senate was informed the company is not yet aware of the extent of the data breach.\nIn June 2026, UnitedHealth Group announced plans to invest $3 billion in\nartificial intelligence\nacross\n2026\nand\n2027\n, with roughly $1.5 billion allocated for 2026. Company executives said the initiative was already generating an approximately two-to-one return by automating tasks such as\ncustomer service\n, clinical record summarization,\nfraud detection\n, and\nappointment scheduling\n, with about one-third of the spending directed toward software products within\nOptumInsight\n.\nKilling of Brian Thompson\n[\nedit\n]\nPolice outside the corporate headquarters in\nMinnetonka, Minnesota\n, on December 8, 2024\nOn December 4, 2024, Brian Thompson, the CEO of UnitedHealth Group's insurance\nsubsidiary\nUnitedHealthcare, was\nkilled in a shooting in New York City\n.\nThe shooting occurred outside the\nNew York Hilton Midtown\n, where UnitedHealth Group was hosting an investor event.\nReactions to the shooting on social media were defined by resentment and criticism of UnitedHealthcare, the\nAmerican health insurance system\n, and widespread disdain towards Thompson.\nPolice later arrested a suspect,\nLuigi Mangione\n, and charged him with murder.\nTim Noel joined UnitedHealthcare in 2007\nand succeeded Brian Thompson as the CEO. Prior to becoming CEO, Noel was in charge of UnitedHealthcare's\nMedicare Advantage\nplans.\nFurther events\n[\nedit\n]\nIn November 2025,\nScott Gottlieb\n, the former\ncommissioner\nof the\nFood and Drug Administration\n, joined UnitedHealth Group's board of directors.\nOrganizational structure\n[\nedit\n]\nUnitedHealth Group has about 2700 subsidiaries and affiliates\nworldwide,\nthe highest profile being Optum and UnitedHealthcare.\nOptum\n[\nedit\n]\nMain article:\nOptum\nFormed in 2011, Optum says that it provides "data and analytics, pharmacy care services, population health, healthcare delivery, and healthcare operations".\nIt is organized into three businesses:\nOptumHealth – provides primary and secondary care.\nOptumInsight – provides data analytics, technology, and operations services.\nOptumRX – provides pharmacy services.\nUnitedHealthcare\n[\nedit\n]\n"UnitedHealthcare" redirects here. Cash flows from operations were $24.2 billion.\nLobbying and political activity\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2009, according to\nOpenSecrets\n, people affiliated with UnitedHealth Group gave $4.77\nmillion to political candidates and groups.\nThe\nAffordable Care Act\nwas being discussed in Congress at the time and was subsequently passed in the first quarter of 2010. In 2010 UnitedHealth Group hired nine different\nlobbying\nfirms to work on its behalf.\nIn addition, its corporate\npolitical action committee\n(PAC), called United for Health, spent an additional $1\nmillion on lobbying activities.\nUnitedHealth Group subsequently spent $4.11\nmillion to $4.75\nmillion from 2011 to 2021.\nThe Lewin Group\n[\nedit\n]\nThe Lewin Group is a policy research and consulting firm that provides an economic analysis of health care and human services issues and policies.\nThe organization has existed for about 40 years and has maintained a nonpartisan reputation through the many ownership changes that have occurred over that time.\nThe Lewin Group was purchased in 2007 by Ingenix, a subsidiary of UnitedHealth Group, but alleges editorial and analytical "independence" from UnitedHealth Group, its parent company.\nThe Lewin Group discloses its ownership in its reports and on its website. While the Lewin Group does not advocate for or against any legislation, both\nDemocratic\nand\nRepublican\npoliticians frequently cite the firm's studies to argue for and against various\nU.S. healthcare reform\nproposals.\nFor example, Democratic Senator\nRon Wyden\nuses Lewin Group estimates to cite the feasibility of his\nHealthy Americans Act\n.\nFormer U.S. Representative\nEric Cantor\n, the former House Republican\nWhip\n, has referred to the organization as "the nonpartisan Lewin Group" in arguing against government-funded health insurance proposals.\nSeveral Lewin studies have been used to argue both for and against the inclusion of a\npublic option\nin national health reform. Lewin clients who often cite its findings include\nThe Commonwealth Fund\n, which, in 2009, held up a Lewin study it commissioned\nto advocate for a public plan. The report showed that legislative proposals would achieve nearly universal coverage and "estimated that a public plan could offer small businesses insurance that is at least 9 percent cheaper than current small-business policies."\nHowever, the Lewin Group has acknowledged that clients sometimes choose to keep the results of studies they have commissioned private. Indeed, Lewin Group Vice President John Sheils told\nThe Washington Post\nthat "sometimes studies come out that don't show exactly what the client wants to see. And in those instances, they have [the] option to bury the study."\nCriticism and controversies\n[\nedit\n]\nSEC investigation\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2006, the\nU.S. Securities and Exchange Commission\n(SEC) began investigating the conduct of UnitedHealth Group's management and directors, for\nbackdating of stock options\n. Investigations were also started by the\nInternal Revenue Service\nand prosecutors in the U.S. attorney's office for the Southern District of New York, who subpoenaed documents from the company. The investigations came to light after a series of probing stories in the\nWall Street Journal\nin May 2006, discussing the apparent backdating of hundreds of millions of dollars' worth of\nstock options\nby UHC management. The backdating apparently occurred with the knowledge and approval of the directors, according to the\nJournal\n. Major shareholders have filed lawsuits accusing former\nNew Jersey\ngovernor\nThomas Kean\nand UHC's other directors of failing in their fiduciary duty.\nOn October 15, 2006, CEO\nWilliam W. McGuire\nwas forced to resign, and relinquish hundreds of millions of dollars in stock options.\nOn December 6, 2007, the SEC announced a settlement under which McGuire was to repay $468\nmillion, including a $7\nmillion civil penalty, as a partial settlement of the backdating prosecution. He was also barred from serving as an officer or director of a public company for ten years.\nThis was the first time in which the little-used "\nclawback\n" provision under the\nSarbanes-Oxley Act\nwas used against an individual by the SEC. The SEC continued its investigations even after it in 2008 settled legal actions against both UnitedHealth Group itself and its former general counsel.\nAmerican Chiropractic Association lawsuit\n[\nedit\n]\nIn June 2006, the\nAmerican Chiropractic Association\nfiled a national class-action lawsuit against the American Chiropractic Network (ACN), which is owned by UnitedHealth Group and administers\nchiropractic\nbenefits, and against UnitedHealth Group itself, for alleged practices in violation of the federal\nRacketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act\n(RICO).\nThe Missouri Department of Insurance, Financial Institutions and Professional Registration determined that UnitedHealth Group violated state insurance laws, so levied $536,000 in fines and ordered more than 50,000 cases re-opened; any improperly denied claims must have been reimbursed with interest for chiropractors and patients.\nOptumInsight,\na.k.a.\nIngenix lawsuit\n[\nedit\n]\nIn February 2008, New York State Attorney General\nAndrew Cuomo\nannounced an industry-wide investigation into a scheme by health insurers to defraud consumers by manipulating reasonable and customary rates. The announcement included a statement that Cuomo intended "to file suit against Ingenix, Inc., its parent UnitedHealth Group, and three additional subsidiaries." Cuomo asserted that his investigation found that rates found in a database of health care charges maintained by Ingenix were lower than what he determined was the actual cost of certain medical expenses. Cuomo said this inappropriately allowed health insurance companies to deny a portion of provider claims, thereby pushing costs down to members.\nOn January 13, 2009, Ingenix announced an agreement with the New York State attorney settling the probe into the independence of the health pricing database. Under the settlement, UnitedHealth Group and Ingenix would pay $50\nmillion to finance a new, non-profit entity that would develop a new healthcare pricing database. Ingenix would discontinue its medical pricing databases when the new entity makes its product available. The company acknowledged the appearance of a conflict of interest but admitted no wrongdoing.\nOn January 15, 2009, UnitedHealth Group announced a $350\nmillion settlement of three class action lawsuits filed in Federal court by the\nAmerican Medical Association\n, UnitedHealth Group members, healthcare providers, and state medical societies for not paying out-of-network benefits. This settlement came two days after a similar settlement with Cuomo.\nOn October 27, 2009, Cuomo announced the creation of\nFAIR Health\n, an independent, non-profit organization that would develop a nationwide database for consumer reimbursement, as well as a website where consumers would be able to compare prices before choosing doctors. To fund FAIR Health, the Attorney General's office secured nearly $100\nmillion from insurers such as\nAetna\n, UnitedHealth Group, and\nAnthem Inc.\nPacifiCare fine in California\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2008, the\nCalifornia Department of Insurance\ntook action against UnitedHealthcare's subsidiary\nPacifiCare Health Systems\n, acquired in 2005, ultimately fining UnitedHealthcare around $173\nmillion for an estimated over 900,000 violations of the Unfair Insurance Practices Act; by 2019, the case was still being disputed in court, with the possibility of affirming $91\nmillion in penalties.\nMedicare overbilling lawsuit\n[\nedit\n]\nA\nwhistleblower\nlawsuit filed in 2011 charged UnitedHealth Group's data analytics division with assisting in defrauding\nMedicare\nby boosting risk adjustment scores from\nMedicare Advantage\ncompanies. The suit alleged that UnitedHealth Group subsidiary Ingenix (now OptumInsight) "defrauded the United States of hundreds of millions – and likely billions – of dollars." Former UnitedHealth executive Benjamin Poehling brought the suit under the\nFalse Claims Act\n. The government said it would proceed on claims against two healthcare companies, UnitedHealth and its Texas subsidiary WellMed Medical Management. In February 2017, a federal judge unsealed the suit after the\nDepartment of Justice\nannounced it would join the case.\nCMS fine over Medicare Part D\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2017, the\nCenters for Medicare and Medicaid Services\n(CMS) fined UnitedHealthcare $2.5\nmillion after discovering issues in\nMedicare Part D\nleading to delays or denials in a 2016 audit.\n2018 New Jersey fine\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2018, the\nNew Jersey Department of Banking and Insurance\nfined UnitedHealthcare $2.5\nmillion due to various compliance issues; this was the largest fine against a licensee in nine years.\nRichard Cole, and others v. UnitedHealthcare\n[\nedit\n]\nOn April 29, 2019, Judge\nRobert N. Scola Jr.\nof the\nUnited States District Court for the Southern District of Florida\n, a\ncancer survivor\n,\nrecused\nhimself from a case against UnitedHealthcare, stating that the company's denial of treatment was "immoral and barbaric", and that his opinions regarding the company would prevent him from "deciding this case fairly and impartially."\nMental health treatment lawsuit\n[\nedit\n]\nAs reported by openminds.com:\nOn September 13, 2019, three addiction and mental health treatment centers sued United Behavioral Health (UBH), UnitedHealthcare's mental health subsidiary. The centers alleged that UBH wrongfully denied $5\nmillion in behavioral health treatment claims for self-insured and fully insured employer health plans for residential and outpatient treatment from 2011 to 2017. The case involves claims for 157 employer group health plan members, including children.\nPennsylvania lawsuit\n[\nedit\n]\nIn 2019, UnitedHealthcare paid a $1\nmillion penalty to settle Pennsylvania regulators' allegations that the company violated state and federal laws when paying medical claims, particularly for patients seeking treatment for autism and substance use disorders. The regulators also compelled the company to pay restitution for wrongly denied or delayed claims and to spend $800,000 on an outreach campaign to notify consumers of their mental health and substance use disorder benefits.\nDispute with TeamHealth\n[\nedit\n]\nIn late 2021, Tennessee-based physician network\nTeamHealth\n, a subsidiary of the private equity company\nBlackstone Inc.\n, sued UnitedHealthcare in the 8th District Court of\nClark County, Nevada\n, alleging the insurer underpaid claims to three of TeamHealth's Nevada-based affiliates. In November 2021, the jury unanimously found United guilty of "oppression, fraud, and malice" in its conduct and awarded TeamHealth $2.65\nmillion in compensatory damages. In December, the jury reconvened to determine punitive damages and awarded TeamHealth $60\nmillion. TeamHealth plans to pursue similar legal action against United and other insurers in\nNew Jersey\n,\nPennsylvania\n,\nNew York\n,\nFlorida\n,\nOklahoma\n, and\nTexas\n.\nMedicare Advantage overbilling\n[\nedit\n]\nAn October 2021\nNew York Times\nreport identified UnitedHealth in a list of\nMedicare\ninsurers accused of over-billing. According to the Inspector General, a\nwhistleblower\ncame forward, so the U.S. government went after UnitedHealth for over-billing Medicare. Executives at UnitedHealth Group told workers to mine old medical records for more illnesses, to identify diagnoses of serious diseases that might have never existed, inflating bills paid by the federal government's Medicare Advantage program.\nA study by the Kaiser Family Foundation found that in 2021,\nMedicare Advantage\nprograms provided insurers with double the\ngross margin\nof insurance for individuals, groups, or Medicaid Managed Care.\nIn July 2024, the\nWall Street Journal\nconcluded that UnitedHealth was the worst offender among private insurers who made dubious diagnoses in their clients in order to trigger large payments from the government's\nMedicare Advantage\nprogram. The patients often did not receive any treatment for those insurer-added diagnoses. The report, based on Medicare data obtained from the federal government under a research agreement, calculated that diagnoses added by UnitedHealth for diseases patients had never been treated for had yielded $8.7\nbillion in payments to the company in 2021 – over half of its net income of $17\nbillion for that year.\nIn July 2025, the\nWall Street Journal\nreported that\nUnited States Department of Justice\nofficials were interviewing former UnitedHealth employees in connection with the investigation,\nand later that month, UnitedHealth confirmed that it was under criminal and civil federal investigation for its Medicare practices.\nThe company said it cooperated with those probes after reaching out to the DOJ.\nChange Healthcare acquisition\n[\nedit\n]\nIn February 2022, the\nUnited States Department of Justice\nsued to stop UnitedHealth Group's $8 billion acquisition of\nChange Healthcare\n, arguing that the deal would give UnitedHealth access to its competitors' data and ultimately push up healthcare costs. The Justice Department said that UnitedHealth knew that access to claims would give it a view into rival health plans at\nHumana\n,\nAnthem Inc\n,\nCVS Health\n,\nAetna\n, and\nCigna\n.\nA U.S. judge rejected the department's bid in September. Following the completion of the acquisition in October 2022, the parties agreed that the appeal would be voluntarily dismissed, with no reasons provided by the Justice Department for dropping the appeal.\nInsider trading lawsuit\n[\nedit\n]\nIn May of 2024, the City of Hollywood Firefighters Pension Fund sued UnitedHealth Group for alleged\ninsider trading\n, claiming that then-Board Chair and future chairman and CEO Stephen Hemsley and then-UnitedHealthcare CEO Brian Thompson had sold millions of dollars worth of shares in UHG since October 2023 with foreknowledge that the\nUnited States Department of Justice\nwere investigating them.\nUse of algorithmic AI model to deny claims\n[\nedit\n]\nUnitedHealth Group has faced a lawsuit over its use of an algorithmic AI model created by naviHealth, which UnitedHealth acquired in 2020, to deny claims. The lawsuit alleged that the model, named\nnH Predict\n, had a 90% error rate. A spokesperson for UnitedHealth denied that they use the model to deny claims when questioned by\nThe Guardian\n.\nSecret payments to nursing homes\n[\nedit\n]\nIn May 2025,\nThe Guardian\npublished an investigative report which uncovered a systemic effort by UnitedHealth to secretly pay nursing homes bonuses to deny hospital transfers for nursing home residents who needed\nimmediate hospital care\n.\nOther tactics uncovered by\nThe Guardian\ninclude UnitedHealth managers pressuring nurse practitioners to persuade members to sign onto\nDo not resuscitate\nagreements and payments for\nleaking\nconfidential patient data to UnitedHealth sales teams.\nIn June, UnitedHealth sued\nThe Guardian\nfor defamation over the findings of their article.\nCampaign against critics\n[\nedit\n]\nIn July 2025, a\nNew York Times\ninvestigation revealed that UnitedHealth Group, with the help of the law firm Clare Locke, had made legal threats against journalists, social media users and healthcare professionals who criticized their policies and practices.
