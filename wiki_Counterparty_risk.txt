Source URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterparty_risk\n==================================================\n\nRisk that a party fails to deliver\nSettlement risk\n, also known as\ndelivery risk\nor\ncounterparty risk\n, is the\nrisk\nthat a\ncounterparty\n(or intermediary agent) fails to deliver a\nsecurity\nor its\nvalue\nin cash as per agreement after the  first party has delivered the security or cash value.\nThe term covers factors incidental to the settlement process that may suspend or prevent a trade from completing, even should the parties themselves be in agreement, act in\ngood faith\n, and are otherwise competent to perform.\nThe term applies only to risks inherent to the settlement method of a particular transaction. Broader risks of trading such as\npolitical risk\nor\nsystemic risk\nmay interrupt markets and prevent settlement, but these are not settlement risk\nper se\n.\nHerstatt risk\n[\nedit\n]\nOne form of settlement risk is foreign exchange settlement risk or cross-currency settlement risk, sometimes called Herstatt risk after a notable incident in which the\nGerman bank\nfailed due to trading risks.\nOn 26 June 1974, the bank's license was withdrawn by German regulators at the end of the banking day (4:30pm local time) because of a lack of capital to cover liabilities that were due. Some banks had undertaken foreign exchange transactions with Herstatt and had already paid\nDeutsche Marks\nto the bank during the day, believing they would receive US dollars later that day in the US from Herstatt's US\nnostro\n. But after 3:30 pm in Germany and 10:30 am in New York, Herstatt stopped all dollar payments to counterparties, leaving the counterparties unable to collect their payment.\nThe closing of\nDrexel Burnham Lambert\nin 1990 was prevented from causing similar problems because the\nBank of England\nhad set up a special scheme which ensured that payments were completed.\nThe collapse of\nBarings Bank\nin 1995 resulted in minor losses for counterparties in the\nforeign exchange market\nbecause of a specific complexity in the\nECU\nclearing system.\nMitigation\n[\nedit\n]\nSettlement risk may be mitigated through various techniques, including:\nLimits on the amount of transactions to settle with each counterparty on a given day to avoid developing a large exposure to a single counterparty;\nSettlement through a\nclearing house\nto reduce exposure and liquidity required by\nnetting\ntransactions (bilateral or multilateral);\nRequirement of initial margin deposits (also called "collateral deposits") and variation margin to provide a safety buffer;\nProvide independent valuation of trades and collateral and monitor the creditworthiness of the parties to enhance transparency and risk management;\nSet up a guarantee fund that can be used to cover losses that exceed a defaulting member's collateral on deposit;\nSettlement through a\ncentral counterparty clearing\nto mutualize or transfer the settlement risk (cf.\nnovation\n);\nSettlement via\nDelivery versus payment\nor\nPayment versus payment\nmechanism;\nSettling foreign exchange via a\nspecial-purpose entity\n, such as the\nCLS Group\n;\nSettling via a cryptographic consensus system, such as a\nblockchain\n.\nReferences\n[\nedit\n]\n↑\n"Settlement Risk: Definition, 2 Main Types, and How To Reduce It"\n.\nInvestopedia\n.
